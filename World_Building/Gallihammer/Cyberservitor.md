# Cyberservitor

## Overview

A cyberservitor is any individual or construct fitted with cybernetic components to serve a defined function. The term covers an enormous range: from simple automated units built around a processing core with no capacity for independent thought, to augmented sapients who retain full cognitive function and personhood while inhabiting bodies that have been substantially or radically altered from their original form by mechanical, electronic, or synthetic integration.

What unites them is that they were made. A cyberservitor's capabilities did not emerge through natural development — they were designed, installed, and calibrated by a practitioner who had a purpose in mind. Everything else varies.

---

## The Spectrum

### Purpose-Built Units

At one end of the spectrum are cyberservitors with no origin in a sapient subject — constructs assembled from mechanical and electronic components around a processing architecture to perform specific functions. These have existed since the earliest eras of industrial toolmaking, and many of the techniques for producing them are among the better-preserved fragments of older engineering knowledge, having been useful enough that multiple independent technical lineages maintained them.

Common categories include:

**Labour units** — built for strength, endurance, or specific physical capacities. Often based on heavy-frame chassis with modifications for environmental tolerance and longevity. Found in communities with access to a cyberwright, used for tasks that would otherwise require many human workers or specialised equipment.

**Sentry and pursuit units** — fitted with acute sensor arrays, rapid actuators, and threat-response programming. More dangerous to produce than labour units because the systems that make them effective as sentries make them dangerous to handle. Quality varies enormously by practitioner.

**Industrial and environmental units** — purpose-built for specific operating conditions, specific inputs, specific outputs. Older civilisations produced extraordinary variety in this category; modern practitioners work with fragments of that knowledge. A cyberwright who knows how to produce a reliable industrial unit for a specific environment is extremely valuable to that environment.

**Medical units** — the most technically demanding of the non-sapient categories. Constructs that can diagnose, stabilise, or treat specific threats to human biology through mechanical or electronic intervention. Many older medical units have been running without maintenance for generations and have drifted far from their original parameters. Some remain useful. Some are now threats.

Purpose-built units exist on a sliding scale of complexity and capacity. Simple ones have no more cognitive function than the processing architecture they were assembled around, sometimes less. More complex ones — sentries, pursuit units, certain medical constructs — require enough behavioural flexibility that something resembling problem-solving becomes necessary. Practitioners debate at what point along this scale the term "intelligence" becomes applicable and what obligations, if any, that creates.

### Modified Sapients

At the other end of the spectrum are individuals who began as sapient beings — humans, anthros, or members of other peoples — and were augmented by cybernetic work into substantially different configurations while retaining their cognitive capacity.

This is the most ethically charged territory in the entire craft.

The augmentation of a sapient subject is technically demanding in ways that purpose-built units are not. A sapient body does not passively accept radical mechanical integration. The subject's own nervous system, their immune response, their proprioception and sense of self generate interference that the practitioner must account for throughout the procedure. The psychological reality of the subject — their expectations, their fears, their will — affects the integration process in ways that are not fully understood and cannot be fully controlled.

Augmentations that succeed produce individuals who are genuinely changed. The form is different. The physical and perceptual capacities are different. What persists — in almost every recorded case — is the person. The continuity of identity through radical physical modification is one of the most-discussed topics in cyberwright knowledge traditions, and the consensus is consistent enough to be considered settled: the person who emerges is the person who went in. They may be profoundly changed by the experience. They are not a different person.

This matters because it means that what has been done to an augmented sapient has been done to a person, and the question of what that person consented to — fully, partially, or under circumstances that compromised their ability to consent freely — does not dissolve because the augmentation is complete.

## Consent and Its Complications

The question of consent runs through every discussion of sapient cyberservitors. Most cyberwrights who work in this area have developed explicit positions on it. Those positions vary.

### Genuine Consent

Genuine consent — an individual who understands what is being proposed, has the capacity to refuse without serious consequence, and chooses the augmentation because they want it — is the clearest case. It exists. Some augmentations are sought, desired, requested by the subject for reasons that belong entirely to them. The cyberwright's role in these cases is technical: producing the outcome the subject wants as safely and completely as possible.

The complication is that "understands what is being proposed" is difficult to guarantee for an augmentation that has no precedent in the subject's experience. A cyberwright can explain. They can show prior cases if they exist. They can run simulations, display schematics, let the subject handle the components that will become part of them. The subject can consent to their understanding of what will happen. What they cannot do is consent from experience of the outcome, because they do not yet have it. Cyberwrights who take consent seriously work to bridge this gap; it cannot be fully closed.

### Consent Under Pressure

Most consent to augmentation happens under circumstances that are not ideal. People who seek radical cybernetic modification typically do so because something in their current situation makes it preferable — economic desperation, community obligation, a role they need to fill that cannot be filled otherwise, a body that was damaged beyond what organic medicine can repair, a situation they cannot escape by other means.

Whether consent under these conditions is genuine consent is a question cyberwrights answer differently. Some hold that consent is consent regardless of circumstances, that to refuse to perform augmentations for people in difficult situations is to deny them agency over their own bodies in the name of protecting them from themselves. Others hold that genuine consent requires conditions that make refusal genuinely possible, and that performing augmentations for people who cannot meaningfully refuse is participation in their exploitation regardless of what contracts they sign.

Most practitioners work somewhere between these positions, making judgments case by case, and most are privately uncertain whether their judgments are right.

### Coerced and Non-Consensual Augmentation

Augmentation performed on an unwilling subject, or on a subject whose consent was manufactured through deception, threat, or incapacity, is the clearest ethical violation in the craft. Most cyberwright knowledge traditions name it explicitly. The existence of naming it as a violation does not mean it does not happen.

Practitioners who perform non-consensual augmentations exist. They tend to operate for powerful clients — factions, corporations, warlords, institutions with the resources to commission their work and the reach to protect them from consequences. The augmented individuals produced by this work are usually not described as cyberservitors in public contexts. They are described in other terms that obscure what was done to them.

## Function and Form

Sapient cyberservitors are typically augmented for specific functional purposes. The range is broad.

**Labour and physical roles** — augmentations that substantially increase physical capacity, endurance, or environmental tolerance. Reinforced skeletal structures, powered musculature, sealed systems for hostile environments. The subject's cognitive function is largely unchanged; their body is fitted for work that would otherwise require equipment or multiple individuals.

**Sensory and reconnaissance roles** — augmentations that extend or alter perceptual capacity. Enhanced optics, broadband hearing, electromagnetic spectrum awareness, chemical analysis arrays. Subjects in these roles often retain high cognitive function; what has changed is how they perceive the world.

**Interface and processing roles** — augmentations oriented toward direct interaction with electronic systems, networks, or other constructs. Neural bridges, data processing implants, communication arrays. The subject becomes a point of contact between sapient decision-making and machine infrastructure. The ethics of this category are heavily debated because the boundary between the person's thoughts and the system's outputs can become difficult to locate.

**Combat roles** — augmentations oriented toward the capacity to do harm. Integrated weapons, armour plating, threat-assessment systems, reflex accelerators. The most contentious category because the augmentation's purpose is unambiguous and the question of who controls the deployment of that capacity — the subject, the client, the practitioner, the system itself — is rarely resolved cleanly.

**Aesthetic and social roles** — augmentations that serve symbolic, cultural, or relational functions within a specific community. These exist in communities that have developed cultural frameworks around cybernetic modification, where certain configurations carry specific meaning.

## Extensiveness of Augmentation

Cybernetic augmentation exists on a spectrum of invasiveness that parallels — but does not map neatly onto — the biological reshaping spectrum described in the Bioservitor entry. The key structural difference is that cybernetic augmentation is additive and modular where biological reshaping is holistic. A biomancer transforms the subject's body as a continuous system. A cyberwright removes, replaces, and installs components. This means cybernetic augmentation can be performed incrementally in a way that biological reshaping generally cannot — a subject can receive a single implant and return for more later, building toward a configuration that would be too radical to attempt in a single procedure. It also means the boundary between "augmented person" and "construct with a biological core" is crossed gradually, and the people involved may not agree on when it was crossed.

### Utility-Level Augmentation

The lightest tier — functional tools installed to support the subject's existing role without fundamentally altering their body plan. A neural interface jack for data access and communication. Enhanced optics for precision work. A mechadendrite utility rig for additional manipulation. Respiratory filters for hostile-atmosphere tolerance. Subdermal reinforcement at stress points.

At this level, the subject is recognisably themselves. The augmentations are visible but clearly additions — tools worn in the body rather than replacements for it. Removal is straightforward. The subject's organic systems remain primary; the cybernetics supplement them. Most subjects at this tier would not be described as cyberservitors in casual usage — they are augmented persons, workers with implants, people who carry some hardware. The term attaches more firmly as the augmentation deepens.

### Protective-Level Augmentation

The next tier — modifications that alter the subject's biological tolerances beyond what their organic systems could achieve. Rad-hardened internals. Vacuum-sealed respiratory replacement. Thermal regulation systems that operate independently of the subject's metabolism. Reinforced skeletal structures capable of absorbing impacts that would shatter unmodified bone. Sealed skin grafts — synthetic or composite — that replace vulnerable organic tissue over critical areas.

At this level, the subject's body is no longer purely organic but retains its original architecture. They look like themselves, modified. The augmentations are survival equipment — installed because the subject's work requires tolerances their biology cannot provide. Removal is possible but more involved than utility-level; some protective augmentations require the organic systems they replaced to be regrown or substituted, which means reversal requires a biomancer or a chirugen with biological reconstruction capability, not just a cyberwright with a wrench.

### Enhancement-Level Augmentation

The tier where the augmentation begins to define the subject rather than support them. Cognitive accelerators that alter processing speed. Reflex systems that bypass conscious decision-making for specific response patterns. Sensory arrays that replace organic perception with something broader, sharper, or fundamentally different — electromagnetic spectrum awareness, sonar, chemical analysis through contact. Powered musculature that replaces organic muscle groups entirely. Limb replacement with purpose-built alternatives that outperform the originals by design.

At this level, the subject's capabilities exceed baseline biology in ways that are not merely incremental. They perceive differently. They move differently. They interact with the world through systems that have no organic analogue. The original body plan is still present — they have a recognisable head, torso, limbs — but the systems inside are increasingly mechanical. Removal at this tier is a major procedure. Some of the organic systems the augmentations replaced no longer exist; they were removed during installation. Getting back to baseline requires not just extraction but reconstruction, and the reconstruction may not be perfect.

### Integration-Level Augmentation

The deep end. The subject's body is more mechanical than organic. Limbs are fully prosthetic. Internal organs are supplemented or replaced by mechanical equivalents. Neural architecture has been modified extensively — not just interface jacks but processing systems woven into the brain's own structure, altering how the subject thinks without (in successful cases) altering who they are. The remaining organic tissue — brain, portions of the nervous system, whatever biological components the cyberwright determined were necessary to preserve — exists within a mechanical framework that supports it.

At this level, the subject is as much construct as person. Their body plan may still be humanoid, or it may have been restructured to suit a specific function — additional limbs, non-standard proportions, configurations that prioritise capability over resemblance to the original form. The subject's experience of the world is substantially mediated by their hardware; their senses, their movement, their interaction with their environment all pass through mechanical systems that are now primary rather than supplementary.

Removal at this tier is not meaningfully possible without equivalent reconstruction. The organic body that existed before the augmentation is gone — not transformed, as in biological reshaping, but physically absent. The tissue was removed, discarded, or allowed to atrophy as mechanical systems took over its functions. Reversal would require building a new organic body from scratch and transplanting the surviving biological components into it. This is theoretically possible with sufficient biomantic or chirugen capability. In practice, it is rare, expensive, and produces results that feel like inhabiting a third body — not the original, not the augmented form, but something new that is neither.

### Total Conversion

The far end of the spectrum. The subject has been reduced to their brain — and sometimes only the functional core of their brain — encased in a life-support capsule and installed in a fully mechanical chassis. Everything else is gone. The body that walked into the cyberwright's workshop does not exist in any form. What remains is a mind in a machine, operating a body that was built rather than born.

Total conversion chassis vary enormously by purpose. Combat frames. Industrial platforms. Mobile sensor arrays. Maintenance rigs designed to operate inside infrastructure. The chassis is chosen for the role; the brain provides the judgment, the adaptability, the capacity for initiative that no processing architecture has reliably replicated. Some total conversion subjects retain full cognitive function — memory, personality, the capacity for independent thought and emotional response. Some have had cognitive function selectively reduced: memory pruned, personality dampened, obedience architectures installed. Some have been reduced to biological processing cores running operational algorithms in an armoured shell, aware in some technical sense but no longer persons in any meaningful one.

The ethics of total conversion follow predictably from the consent framework described earlier in this document, with the additional weight that total conversion is functionally irreversible. The brain cannot be put back into a body that no longer exists. Cloning or regrowing an organic body and reinstalling the brain is theoretically possible but represents a reshaping so radical that the result is a new organism with the original mind installed — closer to the bioservitor model of "a person installed in an organism" than to any meaningful concept of reversal. The subject who undergoes total conversion is making a permanent decision, or having one made for them. The distinction between those two cases is the entire ethical landscape of the practice.

## Life After Augmentation

What a sapient cyberservitor's life looks like after augmentation depends heavily on the augmentation itself and the circumstances around it.

**Physical adjustment** takes time that varies with the extent of the change. A subject whose body has been substantially altered must relearn things — movement, perception, the boundary between their own body and the hardware integrated into it — that they previously did without thought. Phantom feedback from removed organic components, unexpected sensory data from new systems, the constant low awareness of mechanisms operating inside them that are not under conscious control. Most cyberwrights who work with sapient subjects maintain some ongoing involvement through this period, both because their technical knowledge is useful for addressing complications and because the adjustment period is when subjects are most vulnerable to decisions made without full information about how things will settle.

**Maintenance** is a reality that has no parallel in the bioservitor context. Cybernetic components require upkeep — power, calibration, replacement of worn parts, software updates for processing systems. This creates an ongoing dependency on technical infrastructure and on practitioners with the knowledge to service the specific systems installed. A sapient cyberservitor whose augmentations degrade without maintenance faces consequences that range from discomfort to incapacity to death, depending on how deeply integrated the systems are. The question of who is responsible for maintenance — the subject, the practitioner, the client who commissioned the work — is one of the most practically urgent in the field and one of the least consistently resolved.

**Social position** is determined more by the circumstances of the augmentation than the augmentation itself. A subject who was augmented with community support and in a respected role has a different social position from one who was augmented under economic desperation with no formal status in their new configuration. The augmentation's legibility — whether the surrounding community has frameworks for understanding what the subject now is — matters enormously. Visible cybernetics provoke responses that internal augmentations do not, regardless of which is more radical.

**Psychological continuity** is consistently present. The person who was augmented is the person who remains. This is sometimes a comfort and sometimes not. A subject who understood what they were agreeing to and chose it carries their identity through the change and integrates it. A subject who was deceived, coerced, or who consented without understanding carries the memory of who they were before into a body fitted with someone else's hardware for someone else's purposes. Both outcomes follow from the same basic fact — the person persists — and arrive at very different places.

## Reversal and Its Limits

Reversal of cybernetic augmentation is fundamentally more difficult than reversal of biological reshaping. This is the single most important practical distinction between the two disciplines, and it shapes everything downstream — consent discussions, programme design, the subject's long-term relationship with their augmented form, and the power dynamics between subject and practitioner.

A bioservitor's body, however radically transformed, remains a continuous biological system. Reversal is another transformation — the biomancer reshapes the organism back toward its original configuration, or toward a functional baseline, using the same techniques that produced the current form. The raw material is present. The body is the medium, and the medium persists through the change.

A cyberservitor's augmented body is not continuous in the same way. Cybernetic augmentation works by removal and replacement. Organic tissue is excised to make room for mechanical components. Limbs are amputated and substituted. Organs are extracted and their functions assumed by engineered systems. The original biological material is not transformed — it is gone. Discarded, recycled, or allowed to atrophy and be absorbed as the mechanical replacement took over.

This means reversal cannot simply undo what was done. There is nothing to reshape back. Reversal requires *reconstruction* — growing, cloning, or fabricating new organic tissue to replace the mechanical components being removed, and then integrating that new tissue into a body that has adapted around the hardware. This is not cyberwright work. It requires a biomancer, a chirugen with biological reconstruction capability, or both working in concert with the cyberwright who understands the existing installation.

### The Tier Problem

Reversal difficulty scales with augmentation depth, but not linearly. There are thresholds.

**Utility and protective level:** Reversal is feasible and relatively straightforward. The organic systems that were supplemented or partially replaced can usually recover once the hardware is removed, possibly with medical support. Scars, minor functional differences, and phantom sensations from removed implants are common but manageable. The subject gets most of themselves back.

**Enhancement level:** Reversal is a major medical undertaking. Organic systems that were fully replaced must be reconstructed. Muscle groups, sensory organs, skeletal structures — these must be regrown or fabricated, and the body must relearn how to use them. The result is functional but imperfect. The subject returns to a baseline that works but doesn't feel quite like the original — proportions slightly off, responses slightly different, a persistent awareness that this is the reconstructed version. The original is in a waste processor somewhere.

**Integration level:** Reversal is theoretically possible but practically rare. The amount of organic reconstruction required approaches a full biological reshaping in scope and complexity. The subject would essentially need a new body built around their surviving biological components — brain, portions of the nervous system, whatever the cyberwright preserved. The result is not reversal in any honest sense. It is a second transformation: from augmented person to biological person, with all the adjustment, disorientation, and identity disruption that implies. Most subjects at integration level who want to change their situation pursue reconfiguration — a different cybernetic setup — rather than full reversal, because at least the reconfigured body is operating on familiar principles.

**Total conversion:** Reversal is a category error. There is no body to reverse to. The brain exists in a life-support capsule inside a mechanical chassis. Reinstalling it in a biological body means growing or building that body from scratch — a full bioservitor-grade reshaping. The person who emerges will be inhabiting their third body (original, chassis, reconstruction) and the reconstruction will have no physical continuity with either predecessor. Some total conversion subjects pursue this. Most who consider it decide that they have already crossed a line that cannot be meaningfully uncrossed, and focus instead on ensuring their current chassis serves them well.

### Comparative Advantage of Biological Reshaping

This asymmetry — that biological reshaping is reversible in ways that cybernetic augmentation is not — has significant implications for institutional programmes that offer both pathways. A programme that reshapes workers biologically can credibly promise reversal at end of term. A programme that augments workers cybernetically can promise reversal only up to a certain depth of augmentation, and beyond that depth the promise becomes increasingly hollow.

This does not make cybernetic augmentation worse. It makes it different, with different consequences that honest practitioners and honest programmes acknowledge at intake. Cybernetic augmentation offers modularity, upgradeability, and precision that biological reshaping cannot match. A cyberwright can swap a component, upgrade a system, reconfigure a loadout — changes that would require a full reshaping procedure in the biological context. The trade-off is that the deeper the augmentation goes, the more permanent it becomes, and the harder the road back.

## In-World Perception

Most communities without direct experience of cyberservitors respond to them along two axes: the visible augmentation and the knowledge that the augmentation was installed. The visible hardware triggers responses that vary by the modification — some provoke fear, some fascination, some revulsion, some neither. The knowledge that it was installed adds a layer that is distinct from any response to the hardware itself: someone built this into a person, and that someone is somewhere.

Communities with experience of cyberservitors — typically because they have access to a cyberwright and have had augmentations performed in their midst — tend to develop more differentiated responses. The augmented individual's social position stabilises around what they do and who they are, not just what they look like. This happens faster in communities that had frameworks for thinking about modification before they encountered it and slower in communities that did not.

The presence of coerced cyberservitors in a community's history, or in the histories of adjacent communities, reliably produces more fearful and less differentiated responses to all cyberservitors regardless of the circumstances of their augmentation. The inability to tell, from outside, whether an augmented individual chose their configuration produces a generalised wariness that individual relationships can overcome but community-level perception rarely does. Cybernetic augmentation carries an additional dimension of suspicion absent from biological transformation: the hardware can be observed, examined, potentially interfaced with or overridden from outside. The fear that an augmented individual is not fully in control of their own systems — that they could be accessed, commanded, or shut down by whoever built or maintains their components — is persistent and not entirely unfounded.

## Relationship to the Cyberwright

The relationship between a cyberwright and a sapient cyberservitor they have augmented varies from practitioner to practitioner and case to case. It is always present, in some form, whether acknowledged or not.

The augmentation creates a connection that is at minimum technical — the practitioner knows the subject's systems in detail that the subject may not have access to, holds schematics and calibration data that may be necessary for the subject's ongoing function — and often more than that. A practitioner who worked closely with a subject through a long augmentation process knows them in a way that is difficult to describe to people who have not experienced it from either side. Whether this creates obligation is a matter the practitioner must decide for themselves.

The technical dependency is sharper here than in the biomantic equivalent. A bioservitor's transformed body, once stable, is largely self-sustaining. A cyberservitor's augmented body contains components that will eventually require service, replacement, or recalibration, and the practitioner who installed them is often the only person who fully understands how they interact with the subject's biology. This gives the cyberwright a form of ongoing leverage — or ongoing responsibility, depending on how they choose to see it — that does not naturally diminish over time.

Practitioners who decline to think about it tend to produce worse outcomes for their subjects, both during augmentation and after. This is the consensus of cyberwright knowledge traditions across multiple independent lineages. It is not universally followed.
