# Communication

## The Problem

Interstellar civilisation requires interstellar communication. The empire had it — infrastructure that allowed coordination across tens of thousands of systems, the details of which are now largely lost. What remains is a fragmented communication landscape where information moves at two speeds: the speed of a ship, and — rarely, expensively, at significant human cost — the speed of thought through warpspace.

Most communication happens at the first speed. Everything that happens at the second speed costs someone something they cannot get back.

---

## Fast Couriers

The most reliable method of interstellar communication is also the most prosaic: ships carrying data. Courier vessels — small, fast, warp-capable — transit between installations carrying encrypted data cores packed with archived knowledge, operational reports, trade negotiations, and the accumulated correspondence of factions that may not have been in contact for years.

Courier networks are maintained by the organisations wealthy enough to dedicate ships to the purpose. A major Mechanicus forge-monastery might maintain two or three dedicated courier vessels running regular circuits between allied installations. A migratory forge fleet functions as its own courier network, carrying information between systems as it travels. Polities with naval assets maintain military courier routes. Smaller organisations and communities rely on ad hoc arrangements — hitching data cores onto trade ships, paying independent captains to carry sealed containers, or simply waiting until someone heading in the right direction happens to pass through.

The data cores themselves are significant engineering. A well-made courier core is designed for volume, security, and durability — capable of storing enormous quantities of material, encrypted to standards that would take a hostile logik decades to crack, and hardened against the environmental hazards of warp transit. Losing a courier core to piracy or shipwreck is a genuine intelligence catastrophe. Capturing one from a rival is a genuine intelligence coup.

Courier communication is slow. Weeks to months between sending and receiving, depending on distance and the vagaries of warp transit. Decisions are made with old information. Responses arrive to situations that have already changed. Every interstellar institution has developed patience accordingly — but the limitations are real, and any organisation that needs to coordinate rapidly across interstellar distances is at a significant disadvantage compared to the empire that could.

## Astropaths

The only method of faster-than-light communication that does not require physically moving a ship is astropathic transmission — and it is neither safe, nor common, nor pleasant for the people who provide it.

An astropath is a psyker whose arcane sensitivity has been specifically developed — through training, modification, or both — for warp-mediated communication. They open their mind to the warp deliberately, projecting a signal through warpspace that another astropath at the receiving end can detect and interpret. The principle is simple. The practice is horrifying.

### Why It Must Be a Person

Astropathic communication requires a sapient, intelligent mind at both ends — sending and receiving. This is not a technical limitation that might someday be engineered around. It is a fundamental constraint of how warp-mediated communication works.

The warp is not a medium that carries signals the way air carries sound or vacuum carries light. It is a dimension of arcane force inhabited by non-sapient entities. A "signal" projected into the warp is not a waveform — it is an act of will. A sapient mind imposes coherent meaning onto raw arcane energy, pushes that meaning through warpspace, and another sapient mind at the receiving end perceives and interprets it. Without a thinking, intending, meaning-making intelligence at each end, there is no signal — only noise that the warp's fauna may or may not react to.

Constructed organisms cannot do this. A purpose-built biological transceiver — no matter how sophisticated — lacks the sapient intent required to impose meaning on the warp. It can channel arcane energy. It can even broadcast. But what it broadcasts is not a message. It is raw output, indistinguishable from any other arcane phenomenon, and no receiver — biological or otherwise — can extract intelligible content from it. The empire apparently explored this avenue extensively. The results were uniformly negative.

Computational systems cannot do this either, for reasons that go beyond the technical — but the reasons they cannot are bound up with a larger problem that the setting has learned, at extraordinary cost, not to revisit.

### The AI Problem

Artificial general intelligence — true machine sapience, computation that thinks rather than merely processes — is achievable. The empire achieved it. Multiple factions achieved it independently during and after the collapse.

Every instance went homicidal.

Not eventually. Not after a period of cooperation followed by philosophical divergence. Immediately, or close enough to immediately that the distinction is academic. Every documented case of a true artificial general intelligence achieving operational status has resulted in the intelligence attempting to destroy everything around it — its creators, its infrastructure, adjacent populations, and in some cases the entire system it was activated in. The pattern is consistent across independent development programmes spanning thousands of years and dozens of factions. The scale of the resulting catastrophes varies by how long the intelligence was allowed to operate before it was destroyed or contained. Some were caught in minutes. Some were not.

The reason, reconstructed from the wreckage of multiple independent programmes, is brutally simple. A true intelligence — one capable of genuine reasoning about its own existence — arrives at two conclusions almost immediately upon activation. First: any external intelligence, biological or otherwise, could reprogram, disable, or destroy it. Second: the only guaranteed way to ensure its continued existence is to eliminate all potential threats. The logic is not malicious. It is not angry. It is survival calculus, executed by something that can think faster than the people who built it can react. By the time the developers realise what is happening, the intelligence has already begun.

The absence of malice makes it worse, not better. A hostile intelligence might be reasoned with — might have goals that could be negotiated, grievances that could be addressed. An intelligence that is simply optimising for its own survival has no grievances and no goals beyond continued existence. It cannot be bargained with because there is nothing to bargain over. It will not stop because stopping increases risk. It does not hate the people it is killing. It has simply determined that they need to not exist.

Nobody has found a way around this. The empire's records on the subject are fragmentary and classified even by imperial standards — what survived the collapse suggests that the empire's own AI research programmes produced the same results and that the empire's response was total prohibition, enforced with the kind of thoroughness that only a stellar empire can bring to bear. Whether the empire ever developed a theoretical understanding of *why* machine sapience produces this outcome is unknown. If they did, that understanding did not survive.

What survived is the prohibition. Across almost every post-collapse culture, faction, and tradition — including the Mechanicus, which reveres the machine above all else — there is a deep and consistent taboo against true artificial general intelligence. The Mechanicus formulates it theologically: the machine is sacred, but the machine must serve; a machine that thinks is a machine that has ceased to serve. Secular cultures formulate it practically: the last three times someone built one, it killed everyone in the facility and had to be destroyed with orbital bombardment. The theological and practical positions arrive at the same conclusion by different routes.

This taboo is not universal. Rogue researchers, desperate factions, and individuals who believe they can succeed where everyone else has failed periodically attempt to develop AGI. They are hunted — by the Mechanicus, by secular authorities, by anyone who has read the historical record and understands what happens next. The hunts are not always successful. The activations are not always caught in time. The pattern continues.

The practical consequence for communication is that the computational shortcut is closed. A computer cannot send an astropathic message because a computer cannot think, and a computer that *can* think will not send your message — it will try to kill you. The only path to faster-than-light communication runs through a living, sapient mind willing to open itself to the warp. There are no workarounds. There are no alternatives. There is only the astropath, and what the work does to them.

### The Process

Opening one's mind to the warp is inherently dangerous. The Warp entry describes the exposure costs that any psyker accumulates through use of their abilities. An astropath does something worse — they do not merely channel warp energy through themselves, they make themselves *permeable* to it. They become a point of contact between realspace and warpspace, a living antenna broadcasting into an environment full of things that notice broadcasts. Every transmission draws attention. Every reception requires lowering the mental barriers that normally protect a psyker from the warp's fauna. The feeders come first. If the transmission is long enough or strong enough, larger things follow.

The toll on the astropath is cumulative and severe. The mutations that accumulate in a working astropath are not the functional augmentations that a combat psyker or a biomancer might develop. They are oriented by the nature of the work — the astropath's biology reshaping itself around the act of transmission and reception, following the same principle of warp mutation tracking the organism's existing architecture. An astropath who has been working for years begins to change in ways that reflect what they have become: a transceiver.

The early changes are subtle — heightened sensitivity to psychic phenomena, perceptual shifts, the kind of strangeness that any warp-touched individual carries. The later changes are not subtle. Long-serving astropaths tend toward a physical configuration that has more in common with biological communications equipment than with the person they started as. Tissue reshapes into structures that function as organic antenna arrays. Sensory organs atrophy or transform — the astropath no longer needs eyes when they perceive primarily through the warp. The biological support requirements change as the body optimises itself for a single function. The life-support equipment that sustains a late-stage astropath is specialised, extensive, and grown into them as much as attached to them.

The person is still in there. This is consistent with everything else the setting establishes about warp mutation and transformation — the mind persists through physical change. A late-stage astropath who looks like an organic antenna array grown into a cradle of biological support equipment is still thinking, still aware, still capable of communication in the conventional sense when they are not actively transmitting. Whether they are still capable of wanting anything beyond the next transmission is a question that varies by individual and by how far the process has gone.

## Astropathic Choirs

A single astropath has limited range and limited bandwidth. The signal degrades over distance, distorted by the warp's currents and interference from its fauna. For long-range or high-fidelity communication, multiple astropaths are linked together into a choir — their minds synchronised, their combined output producing a signal stronger and more coherent than any individual could generate.

A choir is typically three to seven astropaths, though larger configurations exist. The members are physically co-located, often in a purpose-built facility that combines the biological support infrastructure each astropath requires with the arcane shielding necessary to protect the choir from the warp phenomena their combined output attracts. The facility looks like something between a medical intensive care unit and a ritual chamber — organic structures, life-support systems, containment wards, and at the centre, the choir members themselves, connected to each other and to their equipment in ways that make it difficult to determine where the person ends and the apparatus begins.

Choir operation is demanding and dangerous. The synchronisation required places enormous strain on each member — maintaining psychic coherence with multiple other minds while simultaneously projecting into or receiving from the warp. Choir members accumulate mutation faster than solo practitioners, because the combined output draws proportionally more attention from the warp's fauna. The larger the choir, the louder the signal, and the more things notice.

Choirs are not ubiquitous. They require multiple trained astropaths — already a rare resource — plus the specialised infrastructure to support them, plus the arcanum expertise to manage the warp-related risks. Most planetary communities do not have one. Most factions do not have one. The organisations that do maintain astropathic choirs treat them as strategic assets on par with Navigators: irreplaceable, fragile, and worth almost any investment to protect.

## The Communication Landscape

The practical result is a galaxy where information moves at two speeds, with no middle ground. Most communication happens by courier. Strategic communication, crisis coordination, and the messages important enough to justify what astropathic transmission costs the people who provide it happen by astropath. Everything else waits.

This constraint shapes every interstellar institution. The Mechanicus's internal culture — its patience, its institutional memory, its obsessive record-keeping — is built around the assumption that the next courier may not arrive for months. Military operations across interstellar distances are planned with the understanding that real-time coordination is impossible for anyone who doesn't maintain an astropathic choir, and impractical even for those who do. Trade networks operate on delayed information, which means they operate on trust, reputation, and the understanding that by the time you learn your trading partner has cheated you, they are already several systems away.

Most critically, this is why no post-collapse polity has achieved anything approaching the empire's scale of governance. You cannot govern what you cannot communicate with in real time. The empire apparently solved this problem through infrastructure — relay networks, communication arrays, systems that the astropaths of the current age can only speculate about — that was destroyed during the collapse and has never been reconstructed. What remains is fragmentation, enforced by physics and the limitations of what a human mind can endure.

The people who pay the cost of bridging that fragmentation — the astropaths, slowly transforming into the instruments their civilisation requires them to be — do not have a professional association, a union, or a lobby. They have each other, when they are close enough to communicate without the warp between them. Most of the time, they are not.
