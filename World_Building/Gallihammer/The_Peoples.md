# The Peoples

## What Happened

The empire that built itself across ten thousand systems was, at its core, a human civilisation. The people who crewed the ships, governed the colonies, and fought the wars were human — baseline, unmodified, recognisably the same species that had crawled out of Africa a hundred thousand years earlier. The empire's engineers could reshape biology at will. They did, extensively. But the governing species remained baseline human throughout.

Then the empire ended, and the environment stopped being managed.

What followed was not a single event but a sustained process — twenty to thirty thousand years of environmental pressure operating on populations that no longer had access to the medical infrastructure, genetic monitoring, or atmospheric controls that had kept them baseline. The collapse wars had saturated Earth with radiation, chemical weapons residue, bioweapon contamination, and the accumulated toxic output of an industrial civilisation that had built at planetary scale and then burned at planetary scale. The atmosphere was wrong. The water was wrong. The soil was wrong in ways that varied by region and by what had been done to that region during the wars.

Populations that survived did so by adapting. Adaptation, over twenty thousand years in a toxic environment, produces divergence. The divergence was not directed. Nobody was steering it. It was evolution doing what evolution does when selection pressure is high and consistent — pushing populations toward whatever configuration lets them survive in the specific hellscape they happen to occupy.

The result, thirty to forty thousand years later, is a species that has fractured into distinct strains. Not separate species in the strict biological sense — interbreeding remains possible at most boundaries, though fertility and viability decrease with divergence distance. But the differences are real, stable, and old enough that the people who carry them do not think of themselves as variants of a single thing. They think of themselves as peoples.

## The Divergent Strains

### Humans

Baseline humans — or what passes for baseline after thirty thousand years of background mutation — remain the most numerous strain. They are also the most variable, because "baseline" in the post-collapse context means "not divergent enough to be classified as something else." A human from one region may differ from a human from another region in ways that would have been striking in the pre-collapse era — skin density, metabolic efficiency, toxin tolerance, sensory range — but the differences fall within a band that the surrounding cultures read as "human" rather than as something else.

This is a social designation as much as a biological one. The line between "human with adaptations" and "something other than human" is drawn differently by different communities, and where it falls has more to do with local politics, local history, and local prejudice than with any objective biological threshold. A population that has developed visible radiation-hardening — thickened skin, unusual pigmentation, eyes that process light differently — might be considered human in a region where that adaptation is common and something else entirely in a region where it is not.

Humans dominate politically in most regions not because they are superior but because they are numerous, because they occupy the widest range of environments, and because most of the power structures that survived the collapse were human-majority and have stayed that way through institutional inertia. This dominance is not universal. There are regions where other strains hold power, and regions where the concept of a dominant strain is meaningless because survival requires everyone and nobody has the luxury of hierarchy.

### Elves

The people called elves are the product of environments where the selection pressure favoured longevity, sensory acuity, and metabolic efficiency over raw physical robustness. The regions that produced them tend to share common features: lower ambient radiation but higher chemical toxicity, environments where survival depended on detecting threats early and outlasting them rather than overpowering them.

The result, after twenty thousand years, is a strain characterised by extended lifespans, heightened senses, lighter builds optimised for caloric efficiency, and a general physiological orientation toward endurance rather than burst capacity. An elf does not hit harder than a human. An elf outlasts a human — metabolically, perceptually, temporally. Their cardiovascular systems are more efficient. Their cellular repair mechanisms are more robust. Their sensory processing captures detail that baseline human neurology filters out.

The longevity is the most socially significant trait. Elven lifespans extend to several centuries under favourable conditions — not indefinitely, but long enough that an individual elf accumulates experience and institutional memory on a scale that no human can match. This has produced cultures with very long time horizons, very stable knowledge traditions, and a relationship to change that humans frequently misread as conservatism or arrogance. It is neither. It is the perspective of someone who has watched human communities cycle through three generations of leadership while they were in the middle of a single project.

Some elven communities remember things they probably shouldn't — fragments of pre-collapse or early post-collapse knowledge maintained through oral tradition chains that stretch back millennia, viable only because the people carrying them live long enough for the chains to hold. Whether these memories are accurate is debatable. That they exist at all is remarkable.

There is almost certainly an early genetic stabilisation event in elven history — a point, likely within the first few thousand years of divergence, where someone with bioengineering capability recognised what was happening and cleaned up the worst instabilities in the emerging phenotype. The evidence is indirect: elven genetics are unusually clean for a naturally divergent strain, with fewer of the accumulated error and redundancy that characterise other post-collapse populations. Whoever did the cleanup — a surviving biomancer lineage, a Mechanicus faction, an imperial holdout with functioning laboratories — is not recorded. The elves themselves have mythologised the event into various creation narratives, none of which are accurate and all of which contain the same kernel: someone made us what we are. They did. Just not the way the stories say.

### Dwarves

Dwarves are what happens when a population spends twenty thousand years underground.

The post-collapse surface was, in many regions, not survivable. The people who retreated into the deep infrastructure — the empire's underground complexes, mine networks, transit systems, the layered subterranean architecture that imperial construction had driven through continental bedrock — survived. The surface killed. The deep sheltered. Over millennia, the populations that stayed below adapted to the environment that kept them alive.

The adaptations are comprehensive. Denser bone and muscle structure, a consequence of sustained selection in environments with variable gravity, confined spaces, and physical labour requirements. Stockier builds that conserve heat in thermally stable but cool underground environments. Vision adapted to low light — not blindness in bright conditions, but a perceptual system optimised for dim, consistent illumination rather than the variable light of the surface. Enhanced spatial awareness, a sense of three-dimensional orientation that surface-dwelling strains do not develop to the same degree. Metabolic tolerance for mineral-heavy water and food sources that would cause problems in baseline human biology.

Dwarven communities know the deep ruins better than anyone. They have been living in them, expanding them, maintaining them, and fighting the things that also live in them for longer than most surface cultures have existed. This gives them access to pre-collapse technology and infrastructure that surface-dwellers can only reach through expeditions — expeditions that the dwarves regard with the mixture of amusement and concern that any established resident feels toward tourists wandering into dangerous territory without adequate preparation.

The relationship with what lives in the deep is complex. The bioweapons, the mutated fauna, the things that have had thirty thousand years to evolve in sealed underground ecosystems — dwarven communities coexist with all of it. Coexistence does not mean peace. It means a working knowledge of what is down there, where it lives, what it does, and how to avoid provoking it. Dwarven communities that have survived this long have survived because they learned. The communities that didn't learn are the ruins that other dwarves now tell cautionary stories about.

Like the elves, dwarven genetics show signs of early stabilisation — the adaptation pattern is too clean, too coherent for purely undirected evolution in such a variable environment. Someone, at some point, looked at what the underground populations were becoming and helped the process along. The dwarves are generally less mythological about this than the elves. Their cultural memory tends toward the pragmatic: someone fixed us. Good. Back to work.

### Orcs

Orcs are the descendants of populations that survived the worst surface environments — the heavily irradiated zones, the bioweapon-saturated wastelands, the regions where the collapse wars had done the most damage and left the most residual lethality. Where elves adapted to detect and avoid threats, and dwarves adapted by retreating underground, the populations that became orcs adapted by becoming harder to kill.

The result is a strain optimised for physical resilience at the expense of nearly everything else. Denser musculature. Accelerated healing that is crude but effective — an orc's body closes wounds and fights infection with a brute-force metabolic response that a biomancer would describe as elegantly inelegant. Higher pain tolerance. Toxin resistance that lets them eat things that would hospitalise a baseline human and live in environments that would kill one. Shorter lifespans — the metabolic cost of all that resilience is paid in longevity, and orc populations cycle through generations faster than any other major strain.

The shorter generational cycle has cultural consequences. Where elven societies think in centuries, orc societies think in decades. Institutional knowledge is harder to maintain. Traditions are more fluid, more responsive to immediate conditions, less anchored to the deep past. This is frequently misread by longer-lived peoples as cultural simplicity or lack of sophistication. It is neither. It is adaptation to a timescale. An orc culture that can reinvent itself every three generations has survived conditions that would have destroyed a culture built for stability.

The warrior-culture reputation is not baseless but is badly understood from outside. Orc populations did not become martial by choice or by some inherent disposition toward violence. They became martial because the environments they occupied were actively trying to kill them — not metaphorically, literally. The bioweapon fauna, the radiation storms, the competing survival groups, the things in the ruins. Communities that could not fight did not survive. The martial traditions that developed are survival traditions first, and the distinction between "warrior culture" and "culture that has been at war with its environment for twenty thousand years" is important.

The empire's soldier-caste modification programmes likely contributed to the orc phenotype's emergence. Whether this means orc populations descended from modified soldiers or simply that the same environmental pressures that the empire designed its soldiers to withstand also shaped the populations living in those environments is debated. Both are probably true in different regions. The genetic evidence, where it has been examined, suggests both natural adaptation and traces of old engineering, intertwined too thoroughly to separate.

The early stabilisation that is evident in elven and dwarven genetics is less clean in orcs. Whatever cleanup was done — if it was done — was either less thorough, less successful, or done by someone with less capability. Orc genetics carry more accumulated error, more redundancy, more variation between regional populations. This makes them, paradoxically, both more robust and more variable than other strains. An orc population transplanted to a new environment adapts faster than almost any other strain. The cost is that more individuals fail to adapt, and the failures are harsher.

### Halflings

Halflings are the generalists — the strain that adapted not to any single extreme environment but to the spaces between them. Where other strains were shaped by sustained pressure in one direction, the populations that became halflings were shaped by variability itself. They occupied the margins, the transitional zones, the places where conditions changed frequently and survival depended on flexibility rather than specialisation.

The result is a strain characterised by small, metabolically efficient builds, rapid maturation, high fertility, and an extraordinary capacity for physiological adjustment to changing conditions. A halfling population transplanted to a new environment shows measurable adaptation within two to three generations — faster than any other strain except orcs, and with significantly fewer casualties in the process. They are not as strong as orcs, not as long-lived as elves, not as specialised as dwarves. They are adaptable in a way that none of the others match.

The small build is an efficiency adaptation, not a limitation. Smaller bodies require fewer calories, generate less heat, fit into spaces that larger strains cannot access, and are less conspicuous in environments where being noticed is dangerous. Halfling communities that have occupied the same territory for millennia tend to be deeply integrated into their environment in ways that larger strains find difficult to replicate — knowing every passage, every food source, every hiding place, every route that avoids whatever the local threats are.

Halflings are found everywhere. In the cracks between orc warlord territories, in the margins of elven forest-holds, in the upper levels of dwarven underground complexes, in human cities, in places nobody else bothers to go because there isn't enough there to support a larger population. This ubiquity makes them easy to overlook and hard to dislodge. Communities that underestimate halfling populations based on their size tend to discover, eventually, that the halflings know the territory better than anyone else and have been there longer than anyone realised.

### Anthros

Anthros are a different category from the other divergent strains. They were not produced by environmental pressure. They were made.

The empire's bioengineering capability was not used exclusively for serious purposes. It was used for whatever purposes the people who controlled it decided to pursue, and those purposes included — at various points, in various programmes, under various degrees of institutional oversight — the production of human-animal hybrid organisms. The motivations were mixed. Some were utilitarian: a minotaur-type organism built for industrial labour or security work, combining human cognitive capacity with bovine mass and strength. Some were aesthetic: organisms designed to appeal to specific tastes, incorporating feline, vulpine, lapine, or other animal characteristics into a human frame for reasons that were, at root, fetishistic. The empire was enormous, long-lived, and contained multitudes, and some of those multitudes wanted catgirls. The empire's bioengineers, or at least some of them, obliged.

The distinction between "utility anthro" and "aesthetic anthro" was always blurry. A feline-type designed as a companion also needed to function as an organism — to eat, to sleep, to navigate physical space, to interact with other people. A minotaur-type designed for heavy labour also had a face that someone had made design decisions about. The empire's anthro programmes produced organisms that were, from their inception, a mixture of engineering purpose and aesthetic choice, and pretending that the aesthetic component was incidental is dishonest about the history.

What the empire did not do — or at least, did not consistently do — was plan for what would happen to these populations after the empire ended.

There were enough of them. That is the critical fact. Across the empire's tens of thousands of systems, the anthro programmes had produced sufficient numbers of sufficient variety to constitute sustainable breeding populations. When the infrastructure collapsed, they did not disappear. They survived, reproduced, and — over the millennia that followed — evolved.

The early post-collapse period was the most dangerous. Anthro populations in the immediate aftermath of the empire's fall faced the same environmental pressures as everyone else, plus additional challenges specific to their origin. The original engineering had prioritised form over function in many strains — decorative features that looked appealing in a controlled imperial environment but had no survival value in a collapsing biosphere. Claws that were shaped for aesthetics but couldn't cut. Eyes that were striking but processed light less efficiently than baseline human vision. Pelts that were beautiful but provided inadequate thermal regulation. Builds that had been designed for a specific look rather than for the mechanical demands of actual physical survival.

The ones that survived the first few thousand years were the ones whose decorative features happened to have functional potential — or whose descendants' decorative features mutated, under environmental pressure, into something with actual teeth. The feline claws that were ornamental in the empire are not ornamental now. Twenty thousand years of selection pressure in environments where things try to kill you has turned them into weapons. The enhanced hearing that was charming in a companion is not charming now — it is a survival advantage that the lineages without it did not pass on. The eyes that were designed to be large and appealing have, in many feline and vulpine strains, developed genuine low-light acuity because the populations that couldn't see in the dark died in it.

This process was not uniform. Different anthro strains in different environments diverged along different lines. A feline strain that survived in forest environments developed differently from one that survived in urban ruins. A canine strain that attached itself to human communities and filled security roles developed differently from one that went feral in the wastelands. The result is enormous variety — dozens of recognisable anthro types, each with regional variants, each carrying the double legacy of imperial design and post-collapse adaptation.

Early genetic stabilisation played a role here too, and it was arguably more critical for anthros than for any other strain. The original imperial engineering had produced organisms that worked, but that had not been designed with multi-generational genetic stability as a priority — they were products, maintained by the infrastructure that produced them, and that infrastructure was gone. Without intervention, many anthro lineages would have degraded within a few generations as the engineered genetic architecture accumulated errors without the correction mechanisms the empire had maintained.

Someone — multiple someones, in multiple places, over a period of centuries to millennia — did the cleanup work. Stabilised the genomes. Removed the worst inherited fragilities. Ensured that the populations could reproduce reliably without ongoing technical support. Who did this work varied by region: surviving biomancer lineages, early Mechanicus biologis factions, individuals with enough imperial knowledge to recognise what was happening and enough capability to intervene. The work was not coordinated and was not consistent. Some anthro strains were stabilised thoroughly and early. Others were stabilised partially or late. A few were never stabilised at all, and those lineages either adapted on their own — messily, with high attrition — or didn't.

The result, thirty thousand years on, is that anthro populations are as established, as genetically stable, and as culturally developed as any other strain. The imperial origin is history — known, in the broad strokes, by most anthro communities, mythologised in some, ignored in others. The fetishistic component of that origin is acknowledged with varying degrees of comfort. Some anthro cultures have integrated it into their identity narratives with a pragmatic shrug: yes, someone made our ancestors to be pretty. Our ancestors survived the apocalypse. We are here. The aesthetic choices of dead empire-era perverts are not the most important thing about us. Other cultures find the origin more fraught, particularly in communities where the utilitarian anthro types — the ones designed for labour, for security, for roles that were always about function — look at the aesthetic types and see a different relationship with the empire's legacy.

## Inter-Strain Relations

### The General Pattern

Relations between the peoples follow patterns that would be familiar to any student of human history, because the peoples are — beneath the divergence — still recognisably products of the same original species. They trade. They fight. They coexist, sometimes comfortably, sometimes not. They interbreed at the margins, producing offspring that may or may not be fertile depending on the divergence distance. They build communities that are sometimes mixed and sometimes homogeneous, and the reasons for either have more to do with local history and local economics than with any inherent compatibility or incompatibility.

The biological differences are real. The social weight placed on those differences varies enormously by region, by community, and by circumstance. A frontier settlement where everyone is needed tends toward functional integration regardless of strain. A stable, wealthy community with surplus resources tends toward stratification along whatever lines are locally salient, and strain is usually one of them.

### The Knowledge Question

The peoples do not, in general, know their own origins with accuracy. The divergence happened over timescales that exceed any continuous cultural memory. What most communities have are origin myths — stories that explain why they are the way they are, cast in whatever theological or mythological framework their culture has built.

Elves tend to frame their emergence as an elevation — a refinement of the human form, sometimes attributed to divine favour, sometimes to the land itself shaping them. Dwarves tend to frame theirs as a practical matter — they went underground because the surface was death, and they became what the underground required. Orcs tend to frame theirs as a trial — they survived what others couldn't, and they carry the marks of that survival. Halflings tend not to frame it at all — they are simply here, as they have always been, fitting into whatever space is available.

Anthros have the most complicated relationship with their origins because the evidence of engineering is, in many cases, still visible. The animal characteristics are not something that could plausibly have emerged through undirected evolution alone, and most anthro communities know this at some level. The specific knowledge — that they were made, that some of them were made for purposes that had more to do with someone's desires than with any functional need — varies in how it is held. Some communities preserve it as history. Some have buried it under mythology. Some have reframed it entirely: we were made, yes, but we made ourselves into what we are now. The empire created us. We created what we became after.

### Interbreeding and the Boundaries

Interbreeding between strains is possible but variable. The closer two strains are on the divergence tree, the more viable the offspring. Human-elf crosses produce fertile offspring reliably. Human-orc crosses produce fertile offspring with somewhat reduced reliability. Human-dwarf crosses are viable but less common, partly biological and partly cultural. Crosses between the more divergent strains — elf-orc, dwarf-anthro — decrease in viability and fertility as the genetic distance increases.

Anthro-baseline crosses are a special case. Because anthro genetics include non-human animal DNA from the original engineering, crosses with non-anthro strains are less predictable. The offspring may express animal characteristics, may not, may express them in unexpected combinations. Anthro-anthro crosses between different animal types are similarly variable. None of this prevents the crossings from happening — people are people, and attraction does not check genetic compatibility charts. It does mean that mixed-strain communities develop practical knowledge about which combinations work, which don't, and what to expect.

Communities that maintain biomancers sometimes employ them specifically for this purpose — stabilising mixed-strain pregnancies, addressing viability issues, ensuring that the genetic conversation between two divergent genomes produces something healthy. This is among the most in-demand and least discussed applications of biomantic work.

## The Strain That Isn't

Not everything that walks on two legs in the post-collapse world fits neatly into the strain categories above. Mutations accumulate. Radiation does things. Bioweapon contamination does things. Warp exposure does things. And occasionally, in places where multiple pressures converge, the result is something that is not a variant of any existing strain but something genuinely new.

These emergent populations are rare, usually small, and frequently short-lived — either because the mutation set that produced them is not genetically stable, or because the surrounding communities regard them as threats and respond accordingly. The ones that persist long enough to establish themselves become local phenomena — the people who live in that valley, the community in the deep ruins that nobody visits, the settlement on the edge of the dead zone whose residents look wrong in ways that are hard to articulate.

Whether these emergent populations represent the future of human divergence or simply the noise at the edges of the process is a question that nobody has the timescale to answer. The process is ongoing. The environment is still toxic. The pressures are still operating. Twenty thousand years from now, the peoples may look different again.

The only certainty is that they will still be arguing about who is really human.
