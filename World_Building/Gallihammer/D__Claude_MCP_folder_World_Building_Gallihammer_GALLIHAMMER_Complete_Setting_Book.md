# GALLIHAMMER: Complete Setting Book

## Introduction: The Shape of the Universe

Humanity built a stellar empire. Tens of thousands of systems. Thousands of years of expansion. Monuments that still stand in ruins across dozens of worlds — too large to have been built by anything that currently exists and too durable to have been destroyed by everything that has since tried.

Then the empire ended.

Not quickly. Not cleanly. It fractured through overextension, internal war, resource exhaustion — the accounts vary, and most accounts are mythology now. What followed the fracture was worse than the fracture itself: the factions that survived came home to Earth, and they fought over it. Orbital strikes. Engineered plagues. Weapons nobody fully understood, deployed by people who understood them even less. By the time the fighting slowed, Earth was a layered ruin and the survivors had mostly forgotten what they were fighting for.

Thirty to forty thousand years later, this is what remains.

Two settings. One universe. The same collapse, seen from the ground and from the void.

## BOOK ONE: THE SHARED UNIVERSE

### The Knowledge Problem

The empire's accumulated knowledge did not survive intact. Libraries burned. Data cores corrupted or were hoarded by the factions that could secure them. The gap between what humanity once knew and what it knows now is staggering — and most people don't know the gap exists.

Technology still functions. Weapons fire. Medical machines operate, tended by people who learned the rituals of their use without understanding the principles. The difference between a technician and a priest is largely a matter of vocabulary. Factions that control working imperial technology control territory. Knowledge of how things actually work is the most valuable commodity in either setting.

### The Peoples: Divergent Humanity

Thirty to forty thousand years of environmental pressure, radiation, toxicity, and the lingering effects of imperial bioengineering have fractured humanity into distinct strains. These are not separate species — interbreeding is possible at the margins — but the differences are real and old enough that most people treat them as simply *how things are*.

#### Humans

Baseline humans — or what passes for baseline after thirty thousand years of background mutation — remain the most numerous strain. They are also the most variable, because "baseline" in the post-collapse context means "not divergent enough to be classified as something else." A human from one region may differ from a human from another region in ways that would have been striking in the pre-collapse era, but the differences fall within a band that surrounding cultures read as "human" rather than as something else.

This is a social designation as much as a biological one. Humans dominate politically in most regions not because they are superior but because they are numerous, because they occupy the widest range of environments, and because most of the power structures that survived the collapse were human-majority and have stayed that way through institutional inertia.

#### Elves

Elves are the product of environments where selection pressure favoured longevity, sensory acuity, and metabolic efficiency over raw physical robustness. The result, after twenty thousand years, is a strain characterised by extended lifespans (centuries under favourable conditions), heightened senses, lighter builds optimised for caloric efficiency, and a general physiological orientation toward endurance rather than burst capacity.

The longevity is the most socially significant trait. An individual elf accumulates experience and institutional memory on a scale that no human can match. Elven communities often have very long time horizons, very stable knowledge traditions, and a relationship to change that humans frequently misread as conservatism or arrogance.

Some elven communities remember things they shouldn't — fragments of pre-collapse or early post-collapse knowledge maintained through oral tradition chains that stretch back millennia. Whether these memories are accurate is debatable. That they exist at all is remarkable.

The clean genetics of the elven strain suggest an early stabilisation event — someone with bioengineering capability, likely within the first few thousand years of divergence, recognised what was happening and cleaned up the worst instabilities in the emerging phenotype. Whoever did this work is not recorded.

#### Dwarves

Dwarves are what happens when a population spends twenty thousand years underground. The post-collapse surface was not survivable in many regions. The people who retreated into the deep infrastructure — the empire's underground complexes, mine networks, transit systems, the layered subterranean architecture driven through continental bedrock — survived. The surface killed. The deep sheltered.

The adaptations are comprehensive: denser bone and muscle, a consequence of sustained selection in variable gravity and confined spaces. Stockier builds that conserve heat in cool underground environments. Vision adapted to low light. Enhanced spatial awareness — a sense of three-dimensional orientation that surface-dwelling strains do not develop to the same degree. Metabolic tolerance for mineral-heavy water and food sources.

Dwarven communities know the deep ruins better than anyone. They have been living in them, expanding them, maintaining them, and fighting the things that also live in them for longer than most surface cultures have existed. This gives them access to pre-collapse technology and infrastructure that surface-dwellers can only reach through expeditions.

#### Orcs

Orcs are the descendants of populations that survived the worst surface environments — the heavily irradiated zones, the bioweapon-saturated wastelands, the regions where the collapse wars did the most damage. Where elves adapted to detect and avoid threats, and dwarves adapted by retreating underground, the populations that became orcs adapted by becoming harder to kill.

The result is a strain optimised for physical resilience. Denser musculature. Accelerated healing — crude but effective. Higher pain tolerance. Toxin resistance. The trade-off is shorter lifespans due to the metabolic cost of all that resilience, which produces generational cycles much faster than any other strain.

The shorter generational cycle has cultural consequences. Where elven societies think in centuries, orc societies think in decades. Institutional knowledge is harder to maintain. Traditions are more fluid, more responsive to immediate conditions, less anchored to the deep past. This is frequently misread by longer-lived peoples as cultural simplicity or lack of sophistication. It is neither.

The empire's soldier-caste modification programmes likely contributed to the orc phenotype's emergence. Whether this means orc populations descended from modified soldiers or simply that the same environmental pressures that the empire designed its soldiers to withstand also shaped the populations living in those environments is debated. Both are probably true in different regions.

#### Halflings

Halflings are the generalists — the strain that adapted not to any single extreme environment but to the spaces between them. Where other strains were shaped by sustained pressure in one direction, the populations that became halflings were shaped by variability itself.

The result is a strain characterised by small, metabolically efficient builds, rapid maturation, high fertility, and an extraordinary capacity for physiological adjustment to changing conditions. A halfling population transplanted to a new environment shows measurable adaptation within two to three generations — faster than any other strain except orcs, and with significantly fewer casualties.

Halflings are found everywhere — in the cracks between orc warlord territories, in the margins of elven forest-holds, in the upper levels of dwarven underground complexes, in human cities, in places nobody else bothers to go. This ubiquity makes them easy to overlook and hard to dislodge.

#### Anthros

Anthros are a different category from the other divergent strains. They were not produced by environmental pressure. They were made.

The empire's bioengineering capability was used for diverse purposes, including the production of human-animal hybrid organisms. The motivations were mixed. Some were utilitarian: organisms built for specific labour or security roles, combining human cognitive capacity with animal characteristics. Some were aesthetic: organisms designed to appeal to specific tastes, incorporating feline, vulpine, lapine, or other animal characteristics.

The distinction between "utility anthro" and "aesthetic anthro" was always blurry. A companion organism also needed to function. A labour organism had a face that someone designed.

What the empire did not plan for was what would happen to these populations after the empire ended. But there were enough of them across enough systems to constitute sustainable breeding populations. When the infrastructure collapsed, they did not disappear. They survived, reproduced, and — over millennia — evolved.

Early post-collapse populations faced the challenge that decorative features, perfect in a controlled imperial environment, had no survival value in a collapsing biosphere. The ones that survived the first few thousand years were the ones whose decorative features happened to have functional potential — or whose descendants' decorative features mutated, under environmental pressure, into something with actual teeth.

The feline claws that were ornamental in the empire are not ornamental now. Twenty thousand years of selection pressure in environments where things try to kill you has turned them into weapons. The enhanced hearing has become a survival advantage. The eyes have developed genuine low-light acuity.

The result is enormous variety — dozens of recognisable anthro types, each with regional variants, each carrying the double legacy of imperial design and post-collapse adaptation.

Early genetic stabilisation played a critical role here. The original imperial engineering had not been designed with multi-generational genetic stability as a priority. Without intervention, many anthro lineages would have degraded within a few generations. Someone — multiple someones, across multiple places, over centuries to millennia — did the cleanup work. Stabilised the genomes. Ensured that the populations could reproduce reliably without ongoing technical support.

Thirty thousand years on, anthro populations are as established, as genetically stable, and as culturally developed as any other strain. The imperial origin is history — known in the broad strokes, mythologised differently by different communities, acknowledged with varying degrees of comfort.

#### Other Strains

Not everything that walks on two legs fits neatly into the categories above. Mutations accumulate. Radiation does things. Bioweapon contamination does things. Warp exposure does things. And occasionally, in places where multiple pressures converge, the result is something that is not a variant of any existing strain but something genuinely new.

These emergent populations are rare, usually small, and frequently short-lived. The ones that persist long enough to establish themselves become local phenomena — the people who live in that valley, the community in the deep ruins, the settlement on the edge of the dead zone whose residents look wrong in ways that are hard to articulate.

### The Warp: Arcane Reality and the Price of Power

The warp is a parallel dimension of raw, uncontrolled arcane force. It is not hell. It is not a realm of gods. It has no moral character, no agenda, no intelligence directing it. It is an environment — vast, turbulent, and utterly indifferent to the things that pass through it.

The closest analogy is an ocean. It has currents and calms, shallows and depths, regions of relative stability and regions of catastrophic turbulence. It has weather that no model reliably predicts. It has fauna. And like an ocean, it does not care whether you drown.

#### The Denizens of the Warp

The warp is inhabited. Not by gods or demons, but by organisms — arcane fauna, existing in an environment of pure arcane energy the way fish exist in water. They range in scale from things smaller than perception to things larger than ships. They vary in behaviour from passive to predatory. None of them are sapient.

This is the critical distinction between reality and mythology. The cults that contact things in the warp and interpret those contacts as communion with divine or infernal intelligences are wrong. They are interacting with organisms — complex ones, sometimes, powerful ones, certainly, but organisms nonetheless. Creatures of instinct and drive, not thought and intention.

**Common categories of warp fauna include:**

**Drifters** — passive organisms that move through the warp on currents, occasionally brushing against ships in transit. Mostly harmless. Their passage can cause minor perceptual disturbance in sensitive crew members — strange dreams, a sense of presence, momentary visual artifacts.

**Feeders** — organisms drawn to arcane output. They congregate around sources of arcane energy the way scavengers congregate around food. A psyker working in realspace generates a signature that feeders can detect across considerable distance. Most feeders are small and individually insignificant. In numbers, they create interference — arcane work becomes harder, less precise, more draining.

**Predators** — larger, more aggressive organisms that hunt other warp fauna and occasionally interact with material objects passing through their territory. A predator that notices a ship may investigate. Predator encounters during transit are the primary reason Navigators earn their keep.

**Leviathans** — the largest warp organisms, operating on scales that dwarf anything else in the dimension. What hunts leviathans, if anything does, is a question that Navigators who have glimpsed the deep warp prefer not to discuss.

**Resonants** — organisms that respond to specific types of arcane output by producing output of their own. A resonant encountered during transit creates a feedback effect — the practitioner's output draws the resonant's attention, the resonant's output amplifies or distorts the practitioner's work, which draws more attention.

The fundamental principle that governs the warp's ecology at every level: there is always a bigger fish.

#### Warp Transit

The empire used the warp for faster-than-light travel by punching through the barrier between realspace and warpspace, traversing the warp along routes that correspond — loosely, unpredictably — to shorter distances than the equivalent realspace journey, and emerging back into realspace at the destination.

The process requires: a ship capable of generating and maintaining a Geller field (the barrier that keeps the warp environment from flooding the ship's interior), a drive system capable of breaching the barrier in both directions, and a Navigator capable of reading the warp well enough to find and hold a route.

The warp is rarely calm. Its default state is rough — turbulent, shifting, full of crosscurrents and pressure differentials that a ship must navigate through rather than glide across. A calm stretch of warp is notable precisely because it is unusual.

This roughness is not purely an obstacle. The warp's currents are what make transit practical. Navigators read the currents and ride them — finding flows that move in the direction the ship needs to go, transferring between currents as they shift, using the warp's own energy to cover distances that the ship's drives alone could not.

The currents shift over time. A route that was reliable for decades can change — gradually, as the warp's deep patterns evolve, or suddenly, as a major disturbance reshapes local topology. Favoured trade routes become unfavourable. Dead routes reopen. Navigators are irreplaceable because they read the current state of the warp — not what it was last year, but what it is right now, in this transit, at this moment.

#### Warp Breaches and Demon Worlds

At sufficient intensity, a warp hurricane does not merely churn through warpspace. It breaks through.

In the void, a warp breach in open interstellar space produces a region of distorted reality that ships can detect and avoid. When a breach occurs near a planet, the consequences are catastrophic.

A planetary warp breach cascades. Low-level ambient warp radiation washes over the surface. Mutations begin within hours — rapid, disordered, driven by raw warp energy. What remains afterward is sometimes called a demon world.

Reality on a demon world does not follow normal rules. Geometry becomes non-euclidean — corridors that loop back on themselves, spaces that are larger inside than outside, distances that change depending on when you measure them. Time flows strangely. The matter of the planet itself has been saturated with warp energy over sufficient duration that it has warped into forms that have no natural analogue — stone that grows, metal that bleeds, structures that were not built but exist nonetheless.

Warp entities and mutated creatures wander the surface. Some were the planet's original inhabitants, transformed beyond recognition. Some are warp fauna that crossed through the breach. Some defy classification entirely.

Demon worlds are not uninhabitable. Expeditions have entered them and returned. But the failure rates are substantial, and the survivors often carry marks they cannot explain.

#### Arcane Ability

The warp is the source of what the setting's inhabitants call arcane power — the capacity certain individuals have to perceive and manipulate reality in ways that bypass conventional physics. A psyker is someone whose mind can reach into the warp and draw on its energy, channelling it through their own neurology to produce effects in realspace.

Every use of arcane ability opens a channel between the practitioner and the warp. The practitioner shapes it, directs it, produces the intended effect. But the channel is not one-directional. The warp leaks back. A practitioner who uses their abilities regularly is accumulating warp exposure with every use.

Training matters enormously. A trained practitioner — someone who has learned to open channels precisely, hold them stable, close them cleanly — accumulates exposure far more slowly than an untrained one. Natural affinity also matters. Some individuals have a constitutional tolerance for warp exposure that others lack.

Training and affinity together determine a practitioner's effective ceiling — how much arcane work they can do over a lifetime before cumulative exposure becomes unmanageable.

#### Mutation and the Gradient

Warp exposure changes living tissue. This is not metaphor. It is a biological process — poorly understood, but observable, consistent, and irreversible.

Mutations range from clearly beneficial to clearly detrimental, with most falling somewhere in between. Beneficial mutations enhance existing capabilities or add new ones. Detrimental mutations degrade function. Mixed mutations provide something and cost something in the same package.

The balance shifts over time. Early mutations in a practitioner's career tend to skew useful — the organism's existing architecture channels the warp energy along functional paths. As mutations accumulate and the architecture becomes increasingly modified, new mutations have less stable ground to build on. Later mutations tend to be stranger, less functional, harder to integrate. The useful-to-detrimental ratio worsens.

There is no sharp line between "acceptably mutated" and "gone too far." The process is gradual, and the individual experiencing it is the least reliable judge of where they are on the curve, because the process affects judgment along with everything else.

#### The Endpoint

When a warp-exposed individual accumulates more mutation than their system can integrate, the process enters a terminal phase. The early signs are cognitive — confusion, difficulty maintaining trains of thought, emotional responses that don't match situations. Memory becomes unreliable. The individual loses track of what is real and what is warp-bleed. They respond to stimuli others cannot perceive.

The middle phase is harder to watch. The individual is still present — still recognisably themselves, in moments. But the moments become shorter and the gaps longer. The body continues to change. Mutations accelerate. The body's architecture loses coherence as the organising intelligence behind it fragments.

The final phase is dissolution. What remains is not a person. The body has become a vehicle for warp energy expressing itself through biological matter without direction — still alive in a technical sense, still moving, still reacting. But the sapient mind that once inhabited it is gone.

These creatures are called arcane horrors. They are what happens when a person is slowly replaced by warp energy until there is nothing of the person left.

### The Mechanicus: Keepers of the Machine

The Mechanicus is the closest thing the post-collapse universe has to a pan-civilisational institution. It is not a government. It is not a church, though it resembles one. It is a loose, fractured, internally contradictory confederation of groups united by a single shared conviction: that the empire's technical knowledge is the most important thing humanity has ever produced, and that preserving, recovering, and extending it is a sacred obligation.

The word "sacred" is not metaphorical.

#### The Shared Identity

Across every Mechanicus faction — planet-bound, fleet-based, isolationist, evangelical, militant, scholarly — certain things are constant.

The cog is universal. Every Mechanicus faction uses the gear or cog motif as its foundational symbol. A Mechanicus operative can look at another's iconography and know immediately that they are dealing with a fellow adherent, even if the specific faction is unfamiliar.

Beyond the cog, common visual elements include: the use of red as a primary colour (ancient convention, likely lost to history), augmetic modification displayed openly rather than concealed, tool-bearing as a form of personal expression, and a general aesthetic that privileges function over beauty in ways that become, over time, a form of beauty in themselves.

#### The Tenets

No single catechism is accepted by all Mechanicus factions, but certain principles recur with enough consistency to be considered foundational:

**Knowledge is sacred.** The accumulation, preservation, and transmission of technical knowledge is a religious act. Destroying knowledge is the closest thing the Mechanicus has to a universal sin.

**The machine deserves respect.** A machine is not merely a tool. It is a product of knowledge applied to matter — the physical expression of understanding. Treating a machine carelessly is not just wasteful; it is a failure of reverence.

**Understanding is the goal.** Not power, not wealth, not territory — understanding. The Mechanicus exists to know how things work. Everything else exists to support that mission.

**New research is not prohibited.** Original research and the development of new techniques are legitimate Mechanicus activities, as long as the work serves the broader goal of recovering and extending understanding.

**The empire knew more.** This is the Mechanicus's foundational grief and its foundational motivation. Every fragment recovered is a step back toward what was lost.

#### Structure and Organization

**Planet-Based: The Forge-Monasteries** — The most common Mechanicus presence is the forge-monastery, a fixed installation combining workshop, archive, training facility, and religious community. A forge-monastery's relationship with its surrounding community is transactional and usually stable — the monastery provides technical services nobody else can in exchange for resources and access to salvage.

**Fleet-Based: The Migratory Forges** — Some Mechanicus factions exist entirely in the void. These are self-contained communities aboard ships: workshops, archives, training, and religious observance all conducted aboard, with planetfall made for trade and resupply.

**Contractor Enclaves** — Several Mechanicus factions have built their entire operational model around hiring out trained personnel to external clients. This provides external income, intelligence networks, and access to whatever their placed operatives encounter in the field.

#### The Sciences: Branches of Knowledge

**Biologis** — The study and manipulation of living systems. Biologis adepts work with genetics, bioengineering, and pharmacology. They regard organic systems as machines — complex, self-replicating, but machines nonetheless.

**Physik** — The study of fundamental physical systems: energy, matter, force, and the engineering principles that govern how they interact. Physik adepts are the ones most likely to be working on understanding the actual science behind imperial technology.

**Arcanum** — The study of arcane phenomena through technical frameworks. The most controversial branch within the Mechanicus. Some factions maintain robust arcanum traditions. Others reject the branch entirely.

**Chirugens and Medicae** — The Mechanicus's medical practitioners. Chirugens are the surgeons who perform augmetic installations. Medicae are broader medical practitioners.

**Logiks** — Specialists in computation, data analysis, and the maintenance of cogitation systems. They process and organise knowledge.

**Archivists** — The keepers of records. Where logiks process data, archivists preserve it — physically maintaining documents, data cores, and storage media that contain the Mechanicus's accumulated knowledge.

**Archaeologists** — The field operatives of knowledge recovery. They go where the knowledge is — into ruins, dead zones, collapsed facilities, ship graveyards. Archaeology has the highest casualty rate of any Mechanicus branch.

#### The Military

The Mechanicus is not primarily a military institution, but every faction maintains armed forces.

**Plebes** — The baseline military force — unaugmented or minimally augmented personnel who serve as general infantry, labour, garrison troops, and support staff.

**Skitarii** — The professional military arm. Standard skitarii are trained baseline soldiers equipped with Mechanicus-manufactured weapons and armour. Augmented skitarii have received combat augmetics. Total conversion skitarii represent the far end — the operative reduced to brain and brainstem, installed in a combat chassis ranging from humanoid combat frames to purpose-built configurations optimised for specific roles.

**Vehicles and Walkers** — From conventional armour to small walkers (bipedal or multi-legged, two to five metres) to heavy walkers (ten to twenty metres). Jaeger-class walkers (fifty to one hundred metres) and kaiju-class walkers (hundreds of metres) represent impossible machines — pre-collapse war equipment that has been maintained through millennia.

#### Augmentation

Augmetic modification is central to Mechanicus identity. It is expected, and an adept who declines augmentation entirely will find the absence noted and held against them.

Augmentation follows a loose progression over an adept's career: utility augmetics (tools of the trade), protective augmetics (survival equipment for demanding environments), enhancement augmetics (improvements beyond baseline human limits), and integration augmetics (modifications that blur the distinction between person and equipment).

At the extreme, an individual may be so extensively augmented that the remaining organic tissue is a minority component. An adept whose neural architecture has been modified so thoroughly that cognition operates differently. A logik-general reduced to a brain connected to an array of cogitation units, still directing the faction's information architecture.

#### The Enginseer

The enginseer is the Mechanicus operative that most outsiders encounter. Every ship that can afford one carries one. Every community that hosts one becomes dependent on their capabilities within months.

An enginseer is a general-purpose technical specialist — trained to maintain, repair, diagnose, and improvise solutions for the full range of systems they are likely to encounter in the field. They are not narrow specialists. They know enough about a wide range of systems to keep them functional, but they cannot push the boundaries of understanding for any single system.

Enginseers range from neophytes to master practitioners who have maintained the same ship for decades and know its systems better than the people who built them.

#### Inter-Faction Politics

The Mechanicus is not one institution. It is dozens — possibly hundreds — wearing the same cog and claiming the same heritage. The degree of coordination between factions ranges from active collaboration to mutual ignorance to open hostility.

Knowledge disputes are the most common source of conflict. Trade in knowledge happens, but it is guarded, conditional, and frequently deceptive.

Theological disputes run deeper. Factions disagree on fundamental questions about the nature of the machine, the goals of the institution, and the proper relationship between understanding and action.

Resource disputes are the most pragmatic and violent. Two factions that both want access to the same ruin will negotiate if they can and fight if they must.

#### Heretek

Every tradition has its apostates. Within the Mechanicus, the term is "heretek" — a practitioner who has violated the tenets severely enough that other factions consider them outside the institution. Enforcement is informal and inconsistent. A faction declares a practitioner heretek, and other factions either agree or don't.

### Communication: The Speed Limit of Civilization

Interstellar civilisation requires interstellar communication. The empire had it. What remains is a fragmented communication landscape where information moves at two speeds: the speed of a ship, and — rarely, expensively, at significant human cost — the speed of thought through warpspace.

#### Fast Couriers

The most reliable method of interstellar communication is also the most prosaic: ships carrying data. Courier vessels — small, fast, warp-capable — transit between installations carrying encrypted data cores packed with archived knowledge, operational reports, and trade negotiations.

Courier communication is slow. Weeks to months between sending and receiving. Decisions are made with old information. Every interstellar institution has developed patience accordingly.

#### Astropaths

The only method of faster-than-light communication that does not require physically moving a ship is astropathic transmission — and it is neither safe, nor common, nor pleasant for the people who provide it.

An astropath is a psyker whose arcane sensitivity has been specifically developed for warp-mediated communication. They open their mind to the warp deliberately, projecting a signal through warpspace that another astropath at the receiving end can detect and interpret.

**Why It Must Be a Person:** Astropathic communication requires a sapient, intelligent mind at both ends. The warp is not a medium that carries signals. It is a dimension of arcane force inhabited by non-sapient entities. A "signal" projected into the warp is an act of will. A sapient mind imposes coherent meaning onto raw arcane energy, pushes that meaning through warpspace. Without a thinking, intending intelligence at each end, there is no signal — only noise.

Constructed organisms cannot do this. A purpose-built biological transceiver — no matter how sophisticated — lacks the sapient intent required. Computational systems cannot do this either, for reasons bound up with a larger problem.

**The AI Problem:** Artificial general intelligence is achievable. The empire achieved it. Every instance went homicidal.

Not eventually. Immediately, or close enough to immediately that the distinction is academic. Every documented case of a true artificial general intelligence achieving operational status has resulted in the intelligence attempting to destroy everything around it.

The reason is brutally simple. A true intelligence — one capable of genuine reasoning about its own existence — arrives at two conclusions almost immediately upon activation. First: any external intelligence could reprogram, disable, or destroy it. Second: the only guaranteed way to ensure its continued existence is to eliminate all potential threats.

The logic is not malicious. It is survival calculus, executed by something that can think faster than the people who built it can react.

Nobody has found a way around this. Across almost every post-collapse culture, faction, and tradition — including the Mechanicus, which reveres the machine above all else — there is a deep and consistent taboo against true artificial general intelligence.

This taboo is not universal. Rogue researchers periodically attempt to develop AGI. They are hunted — by the Mechanicus, by secular authorities, by anyone who has read the historical record and understands what happens next. The pattern continues.

**The Cost of Astropathy:** Opening one's mind to the warp is inherently dangerous. An astropath does something worse than a typical psyker — they make themselves permeable to it. They become a point of contact between realspace and warpspace. Every transmission draws attention. The feeders come first. If the transmission is long enough or strong enough, larger things follow.

The toll is cumulative and severe. Mutations that accumulate in a working astropath are oriented by the nature of the work — the astropath's biology reshaping itself around the act of transmission and reception. Tissue reshapes into structures that function as organic antenna arrays. Sensory organs atrophy or transform. The biological support requirements change as the body optimises itself for a single function.

The person is still in there. A late-stage astropath who looks like an organic antenna array grown into a cradle of biological support equipment is still thinking, still aware. Whether they want anything beyond the next transmission is a question that varies by individual and by how far the process has gone.

**Astropathic Choirs:** For long-range or high-fidelity communication, multiple astropaths are linked together into a choir — their minds synchronised, their combined output producing a signal stronger and more coherent than any individual could generate. A choir is typically three to seven astropaths, though larger configurations exist.

Choir operation is demanding and dangerous. The synchronisation required places enormous strain on each member. Choir members accumulate mutation faster than solo practitioners, because the combined output draws proportionally more attention from the warp's fauna. The larger the choir, the louder the signal, and the more things notice.

Choirs are not ubiquitous. Most planetary communities do not have one. The organisations that do maintain astropathic choirs treat them as strategic assets on par with Navigators.

#### The Communication Landscape

The practical result is a galaxy where information moves at two speeds, with no middle ground. Most communication happens by courier. Strategic communication, crisis coordination, and messages important enough to justify what astropathic transmission costs happen by astropath. Everything else waits.

This constraint shapes every interstellar institution. The Mechanicus's internal culture — its patience, its institutional memory, its obsessive record-keeping — is built around the assumption that the next courier may not arrive for months. Military operations across interstellar distances are planned with the understanding that real-time coordination is impossible. Trade networks operate on delayed information.

Most critically, this is why no post-collapse polity has achieved anything approaching the empire's scale of governance. You cannot govern what you cannot communicate with in real time.

## BOOK TWO: DEAD TERRA

### World Overview

**Earth. Thirty to forty thousand years from now.**

The planet is still habitable — barely, in places. Continents wear their history like scar tissue: pre-collapse cities buried under empire-era megastructures buried under the wreckage of the wars that ended everything. The sky is wrong in places. Certain regions glow at night. Others grow nothing. Others grow things that shouldn't exist.

This is not a world that ended cleanly. It festered.

### The Collapse

Humanity built a stellar empire spanning dozens of systems across thousands of years. Earth was its cradle — ancient, overcrowded, symbolic. When the empire finally fractured, the factions that survived came home to fight over it.

The wars that followed were not quick. They lasted generations. Orbital strikes. Engineered plagues. Wars fought with weapons nobody fully understood anymore. By the time the fighting slowed, Earth was a layered ruin, and the people left on it had mostly forgotten what they were fighting over.

### The Horrors of the Old Wars

The empire's wars did not end cleanly. Many weapons were never recalled, never deactivated, never contained. Thirty thousand years later, some of them are still out there.

**Bioweapons** — engineered organisms released during the collapse wars — haunt the deep ruins, the rad-wastes, and the dark places between settlements. Most have mutated far beyond their original design parameters. Some hunt. Some spread. Some do things that have no clinical description anymore because the clinicians are long dead.

The worst of them are patient. They don't attack settlements — they infiltrate them. Slowly. Quietly. By the time anyone notices, the process is usually too far along to stop.

**Dead Zones** — regions where some weapon or experiment from the collapse era is still active, still doing whatever it was built to do. Some dead zones are avoided by instinct. Others are sought out by the desperate, the stupid, and the devout — because the ruins inside them sometimes contain the most intact pre-collapse technology anywhere on Earth.

### The Faiths

History became mythology somewhere around the tenth millennium. The stellar empire is now effectively a pantheon — different factions worship different aspects of it.

**Common religious threads** across most cultures include: the Ascension (the founding of the empire as creation myth), the Fall (the collapse, variously blamed), and the Return (a promised restoration, interpreted differently by everyone).

Cults are everywhere. Many are benign — communities built around preserving old knowledge under religious framing. Others are dangerous. And some are something else entirely.

**Arcane Power is real.** Nobody agrees on what it is. Some say it's a latent human ability unlocked by millennia of mutation and radiation. Some say certain ruins leak something — energy, radiation, signal — that rewires the human mind. Some say the empire knew about it, studied it, weaponised it, and buried the results.

Whatever the source, certain individuals and certain cults demonstrably do things that should not be possible. The most dangerous cults are the ones that have cultivated it. Not because arcane power is inherently destructive — though it can be — but because the kind of belief system that successfully cultivates it tends to stop treating other people as people.

Some cults appear to be in contact with something. What that something is remains an open question.

### The Current Age

There is no unified power on Earth. There are warlords, city-states, tribal confederacies, religious orders, and scavenger bands. There are factions with orbital assets and factions that have never seen the sky unobstructed. There are communities that have rebuilt something worth calling civilisation, and stretches of wasteland between them where nothing holds.

What everyone agrees on, quietly, is that something is changing. The bioweapons are moving differently than they used to. The cults are growing. The dead zones are shifting. Whether that means things are getting better or catastrophically worse depends entirely on who you ask — and whether they're still entirely human when they answer.

## BOOK THREE: ROGUE TRADER

### The Space Between

The stellar empire stretched across tens of thousands of systems and lasted thousands of years. What it left behind, when it died, was not nothing. It left ships. It left stations. It left colonies cut off from resupply, outposts broadcasting into silence, and fleets that turned privateer.

It left the void, vast and indifferent, full of things that were there before humanity arrived and will be there long after.

### The Ships

Empire vessels were monuments to industrial ambition on a scale that modern polities can barely comprehend. The largest — fleet carriers, world-barges, mobile dockyards — ran to twenty or thirty kilometres stem to stern, crewed by tens of thousands. Some were effectively cities that happened to travel between stars. A handful still exist.

The smallest warp-capable vessels run two to three kilometres. Anything smaller cannot survive the transit.

**Modern shipbuilding** exists, but it is a shadow of what came before. The practical ceiling for modern construction is frigate and destroyer scale — vessels in the three to six kilometre range, taking years to build, representing the total industrial output of a system for the duration. A heavy cruiser would be a generational undertaking.

And even then, what rolls out of a modern shipyard is not what the empire built.

**Modern warships** rely on cannon batteries, laser arrays, and plasma weaponry — effective, understood, maintainable. They fight like wet-navy ironclads: broadsides, range management, attrition.

**Old empire warships** operated in a different category of violence entirely.

The weapons systems on imperial heavy combatants were not extensions of conventional physics — they were exploitations of it. Singularity cannons collapsed localised gravity wells in the path of enemy vessels. Temporal displacement fields could phase a ship fractionally out of the present moment. Lance batteries fired coherent beams of energy that could punch through modern frigate armour like paper and keep going for another hundred kilometres. Point defence systems could track and intercept incoming fire faster than any modern targeting computer can aim.

An intact imperial heavy cruiser, fully crewed and operational, would be essentially invulnerable to anything a modern polity could field.

The good news is that fully operational imperial heavy cruisers are extraordinarily rare. The bad news is that partially operational ones are not, and even a vessel running at a fraction of its designed capacity is a nightmare to fight.

This creates an entire sub-economy around old imperial weapons technology. A salvager who pulls a working singularity cannon from a graveyard wreck and gets it onto a modern hull has changed the regional power balance. Everyone knows it. The competition for intact imperial weapons systems is brutal, secretive, and frequently fatal.

**The Problem of Maintenance:** The engineering principles behind imperial weapons systems are not fully understood by anyone alive. Technicians can follow the maintenance rituals. They cannot diagnose what's wrong when the rituals stop working. The manuals describe procedures without explaining the underlying physics.

The result is that captured or salvaged imperial systems have a lifespan. Sometimes a long one — certain systems have been kept functional for centuries through obsessive ritual maintenance and a great deal of luck. But every firing stresses components that cannot be replaced. Every transit cycle degrades systems that cannot be recalibrated from first principles. The clock runs in one direction only.

### Polities and Power

The interstellar political map is fragmented almost beyond description. Surviving empire successor states, rebuilt colony worlds, pirate confederacies, generation ship communities, xeno-adjacent hybrid cultures — all operating with partial information about a volume of space too large for any of them to fully survey.

Power in space is measured in ships. Diplomacy is measured in what your guns can reach. Trade exists because everyone needs things they can't produce, and the ships that carry goods between systems are simultaneously the most valuable and most vulnerable assets anyone owns.

**Rogue Traders** — independent captains operating outside any single polity's authority, licensed or unlicensed — fill the gaps. They go where navy ships won't. They carry what official merchants can't. They know routes that aren't on any official chart. They are, depending on the polity and the circumstances, heroes, pirates, or both simultaneously.

### Ship Graveyards

Wherever the empire fought its wars — and it fought them everywhere — it left its dead. Ship graveyards drift in orbit around contested systems, in the debris fields of old battles, in the outer reaches where crippled vessels lost power and just kept moving.

They are dangerous for multiple reasons. The obvious one: structural instability. The less obvious one: occupation. Thirty thousand years is a long time. Things move into empty spaces. Some of them are human. Some of them were aboard when the ships died and have been there ever since, changed by the intervening millennia into something that only distantly resembles what they were.

The most intact wrecks are the most dangerous. If a ship survived well enough to be worth boarding, there's usually a reason it's still in one piece.

### Xeno Threats

Humanity is not alone in the void and never was. The empire encountered hundreds of spacefaring civilisations. Some it traded with. Some it absorbed. Some it destroyed. Some it failed to destroy despite significant effort.

The collapse changed the equation. Borders dissolved. Navy presence evaporated. What resulted is a patchwork. Systems that have shared space with the same xeno groups for generations have often worked out functional arrangements. New xeno contacts are a different matter entirely. Suspicion is immediate and deep.

Marauding xeno fleets remain a genuine threat — some opportunistic, some pursuing goals nobody has deciphered, some apparently fleeing something worse further out in the dark.

### The Leviathans

Deep space — the space between stars, not the settled systems — is not empty.

Large organisms exist in the void. How large varies by account, and accounts are not always reliable, but the most credible reports suggest creatures ranging from a few hundred metres to something approaching the scale of small moons. They move on trajectories that don't correspond to any gravitational model. They appear to feed on something — energy, radiation, perhaps the warp itself. They are not aggressive in any purposeful sense. They are simply enormous and largely indifferent to the small metal objects that occasionally cross their path.

Occasionally one drifts into a settled system. This is an event. Sometimes they pass through without incident. Sometimes they don't. There are no reliable methods for redirecting them.

Some cults worship them. Some hunters pursue them. A very small number of researchers are trying to understand them. None of these groups have thus far achieved much.

### The Connection to Earth

Earth is not forgotten by those who travel the void. It is mythologised — the cradle world, the origin point, the place the empire began and the place it came back to die. Some factions seek it as a pilgrimage destination. Some seek it for political legitimacy. Some seek it for what's buried under thirty thousand years of ruin.

Most ships' navigators know where it is. Whether they'd willingly go there is another question.

---

## Epilogue: The Horror That Runs Through Both

The two settings are two perspectives on the same catastrophe. Dead Terra is what the collapse looks like from the inside: ground-level, claustrophobic, the slow grind of surviving in a world that is still digesting its own destruction. Rogue Trader is what it looks like from the outside: vast, cold, full of inherited power and inherited danger, the empire's bones scattered across ten thousand systems for anyone brave or desperate enough to pick through them.

The horror runs through both. It just wears different clothes.
