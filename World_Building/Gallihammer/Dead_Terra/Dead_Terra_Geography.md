# Dead Terra — World Geography

## The Shape of the World

Earth is recognisable, if you know what to look for. The continents are where they always were. The mountain ranges still stand — some taller now from tectonic stress, some reduced to rubble by orbital strikes. The old names survive in corrupted forms in enough places that a scholar with access to the right ruins could still draw a map and label it correctly.

What has changed is the water.

Roughly half the planet's ocean volume is gone. The precise mechanism is debated: orbital bombardment vaporising shallow seas, weapons that broke the hydrological cycle at critical points, deliberate engineering by factions that wanted new land and didn't care what the long-term consequences were. The result is the same regardless of cause. The shallow seas are gone. The deep ocean basins still hold water, but the coastlines have retreated hundreds of kilometres in most places, and what was seafloor is now terrain.

This is the most consequential geographic change in Earth's history since the supercontinent split. Everything built on what used to be coastline is now inland. Port cities sit stranded above exposed shelf terrain. New land bridges connect continents that were once separated. The dried basins — salt flats, chemical deserts, plains of exposed sediment — are among the most distinctive and hostile environments on the planet.

Added to this: the craters. Orbital strikes during the collapse wars left permanent marks on the landscape. Some are kilometres wide. Some have filled with toxic water, chemical residue, or stranger things. A handful are inhabited. Most are avoided.

## The Physical Changes — Overview

### Ocean Drainage

**Seas now fully dry:**
- The Mediterranean — gone entirely. See *The Great Basin* below.
- The North Sea and Baltic — gone. The island of Albia is now a peninsula attached to the European mainland via exposed shelf plain.
- The Bering region — expanded dramatically. The old land bridge is now a territory in its own right, hundreds of kilometres wide.
- The South China Sea's shallow shelf — gone, creating vast new lowland connecting Southeast Asia's island chains into a near-continuous landmass. This is called the New Shore.
- The Persian Gulf — dried. The Arabian Peninsula connects directly to the Iranian plateau.
- The Red Sea — reduced to a salt depression, shallow and toxic.
- The Hudson Bay — gone. Northern Merica is one continuous landmass.
- The Caribbean shelf — largely exposed, connecting former islands to each other and to the continental shores.

**Deep oceans — reduced but present:**
- The Atlantic is still there, narrowed. Crossing it is still an ocean voyage.
- The deep Pacific holds water but the coastlines have retreated. The Pacific is still vast.
- The Indian Ocean has contracted, but remains navigable where it persists.

### What the Drainage Created

**New terrain types:**
- *Salt flats* — former shallow seabeds now crusted with mineral deposits. Blinding white in direct sun. Hostile to most life. Some support extreme organisms.
- *Shelf plains* — exposed continental shelf, usually flat, sometimes with old seafloor geology visible. Often rich in mineral deposits from millennia of accumulation. Also often laced with the remnants of sunken pre-collapse infrastructure — cables, pipelines, the foundations of things built for underwater deployment.
- *Stranded coast* — the old shoreline, now hundreds of metres above the waterline. Former port cities, beach infrastructure, and coastal settlements sitting above terrain that was once sea floor.
- *Relic waterways* — rivers that once emptied into shallow seas now terminate in dried basins or run underground. Some have carved new channels through exposed terrain.

### The Craters

Named sites of confirmed orbital or high-yield strike impact. Dozens more exist, unnamed and unexplored.

**The Wound** — Central Europa. Approximately 40km wide, irregular, the centre holds a permanent chemical lake of toxic runoff. The surrounding blast zone is elevated and glassy in places from impact heat. A major landmark and navigation point. Several settlements exist on the crater rim. Cults treat it as sacred ground.

**Ural Scar** — Eastern Ursh, Ural range. A long trench rather than a circular crater, suggesting an angled kinetic impactor rather than an explosive warhead. Approximately 600km long, 2km wide at its widest. A natural boundary line that several regional warlords treat as a border.

**The Panpacifik Hollow** — Former coastal zone, now several hundred kilometres inland due to ocean drainage. One of the largest confirmed impact sites — over 80km diameter. Heavily irradiated. The ruins of what was likely a major pre-collapse city sit at its edges.

**Merica Scar** — Midlands of northern Merica. Multiple overlapping craters suggesting a sustained bombardment campaign rather than a single strike. The region is still geologically unstable — minor tremors common, ground periodically shifting.

**The Afrik Eye** — Central Nordafrik, almost perfectly circular. 30km diameter. Unusually, it is not a dead zone — some unknown effect has made the interior of the crater fertile, and a permanent settlement exists there. Widely considered strange. Several theories exist.

## The Regions

### The Europan Reaches

**Former territory:** Western and Central Europe.

**Character:** The most densely rebuilt region on Earth. The Europan Reaches have been continuously inhabited, fought over, destroyed, and rebuilt since before the empire. Every major city in this region is a layered core — pre-collapse streets buried under empire-era megastructures buried under the wreckage of the collapse wars buried under whatever the survivors built on top. Digging anywhere in an Europan city means cutting through civilisation.

The terrain varies. The northern coastal zones — where the North Sea once was — are now exposed shelf plains stretching out toward what remains of the Atlantic. The Alps and Pyrenees still stand. The Rhine, Danube, and other major river systems persist, though their courses have shifted in places. The southern edge of the region is defined by the Great Basin — the dried Mediterranean — and the relationship between the Reaches and the Basin is the defining geographic and political fact of this part of the world.

**The city-cores:** Names have corrupted over thirty thousand years but remain recognisable: Romass (the Eternal Ruin), Parisc, Frankenhalt, Iberis. Each is a layered site where ten or twenty civilisations have built on top of each other. The deepest levels are sealed off in most cases — the technology, organisms, and conditions down there are unknown.

**Warlord density:** High. The Europan Reaches are the most politically fractured region on Earth — small territories, contested borders, constant flux. Every warlord wants the city-cores; few hold them cleanly.

**Relationship to the Basin:** Control of access points to the Great Basin — the stable crossing routes down the Basin walls — is among the most contested resources in the Reaches. The Basin floor has things in it that people want.

---

### Albia

**Former territory:** The British Isles and the exposed North Sea shelf connecting them to the European mainland.

**Character:** Albia was an island culture for most of recorded history. It is not one anymore — the North Sea drained within the last few thousand years and the adjustment has not been comfortable. The exposed shelf that now connects Albia to the Europan mainland is flat, mineral-rich, and contested. Albia's interior cultures treat the new land as frontier territory; the continental powers treat it as an extension of their sphere.

The island itself retains a distinct character. Heavy industrial ruins from the empire era. A tradition of underground living in the network of collapsed tunnel systems beneath the old cities. A reputation for insularity that the geographic change has not resolved.

**Landmarks:** The ruins of what was clearly once an enormous coastal city (formerly London) now sit several kilometres from the Atlantic coast, elevated on what is now a low ridge above the exposed shelf. The surviving structures are among the more intact pre-collapse ruins in the region — partly because of the island's relative isolation during the collapse wars, partly because several major underground complexes survived intact and have been continuously occupied.

### The Urshic Steppe

**Former territory:** Russia, Central Asia, Siberia, the former Caspian and Aral sea basins.

**Character:** Vast. The Urshic Steppe is the largest continuous geographic region on Earth. Travelling from the western edge to the eastern fringe is a journey of months. The climate ranges from arctic in the north to semi-arid in the south, with the dried Caspian basin — a chemical wasteland of salt and mineral residue — as the defining interior feature.

Population is low relative to area. Nomadic cultures cover enormous territories. Fixed settlements cluster around reliable water sources, defensible terrain, or significant ruin sites. Warlords who control the Steppe tend toward confederation models — direct control of a few key sites, loose overlordship of the nomadic groups in between.

**The Dried Caspian:** Called the White Scar by most Urshic cultures. Roughly 1,200km long and up to 400km wide, the bottom sitting hundreds of metres below the surrounding terrain. The floor is mineral-heavy, toxic in places, and crossed by the remnants of pre-collapse infrastructure built on what was then the seafloor. Exploration parties go down. Not all return. Those that do report intact structures and active bioweapon-organisms doing whatever they were built to do, undisturbed, for tens of thousands of years.

**Siberia:** The northern reaches are cold enough to be self-limiting. The permafrost contains preserved ruins and preserved organisms from every era of Earth's history. Some of what is preserved has begun to thaw.

### The Great Basin

**Not a political region — a geographic feature, noted here because of its scale.**

The Great Basin is the dried Mediterranean. Approximately 3,700km long, 800km wide at its widest, sitting at its deepest point nearly 5km below the surrounding terrain. It is the most dramatic single geographic feature on Earth.

The edges drop gradually in most places, though there are sections where the drop is steep enough to be impassable without specific routes. Former island chains now exist as isolated highland ranges rising from the Basin floor — the Inland Mountains. Some are inhabited.

**The floor:** Salt flat in most places. In the deepest sections, residual water has pooled into brine pits of extremely high salinity. The floor is not uniformly dead — extremophile organisms thrive, and in some areas old industrial outflows have left chemical landscapes supporting novel ecosystems. Pre-collapse coastal infrastructure — ports, underwater facilities, pipelines — sits exposed on the floor, preserved in places by the salt.

**Access:** Control of the "drops" — stable descent routes down the Basin walls — is economically and militarily significant. A warlord who controls a major drop controls the trade between the Europan Reaches and the Nordafrik Expanse.

**Cultural status:** The Basin appears in the mythology of every culture whose territory borders it. The deepest sections are considered cursed. The cults that operate in the Basin interior are varied and, in several cases, not entirely human.

### The Nordafrik Expanse

**Former territory:** North Africa, the Sahara, the Sahel.

**Character:** Defined by the Basin to the north and the transition to the Afrik Interior to the south. Before the collapse this was already one of the driest inhabited regions on Earth. The Basin's drainage removed the moderating influence of the Mediterranean on the regional climate. What survives is hardy — culture built around water management, long-distance trade, and navigating extreme environments.

The Nile still runs. Communities along it are among the oldest continuously inhabited places on Earth. The delta region, no longer coastal, is now an inland marsh system supporting a dense population.

**The Basin frontier:** The southern edge of the Great Basin, where it borders Nordafrik territory, is a contested zone. Several Nordafrik factions have established footholds on the Basin floor and treat it as sovereign territory. This conflicts directly with Europan factions doing the same from the north.

**What the empire left:** The pyramid complexes and associated ruins — now several hundred kilometres from the Basin edge — are among the most intact pre-collapse structures on Earth. They are inhabited. Who lives in them, and what they do there, is considered dangerous knowledge.

### Achaemenia

**Former territory:** Middle East, Arabian Peninsula, Persia, the dried Persian Gulf.

**Character:** Extreme heat, extreme ruin density, extreme religious significance. The region sits at the crossroads between the Europan/Basin sphere and the Indic Belt and has been fought over for the entirety of human history. The collapse wars were particularly intense here. The craters are numerous. The dead zones are significant.

**The Dried Gulf:** The former Persian Gulf is now a below-sea-level basin approximately 1,000km long and 200km wide. The floor is hot and mineral-saturated. The edges contain pre-collapse port facilities and resource infrastructure being actively excavated. The Arabian Peninsula is now land-connected to the Iranian plateau — a single continuous territory where there was once a water boundary.

**Cult density:** Achaemenia has more active religious organisations per square kilometre than any other region. The oldest sites are considered sacrosanct even by factions otherwise hostile to each other. Violating certain ruins in Achaemenia is one of the few things that reliably causes temporary cross-faction alliances.

**Arcane concentration:** Several biomancer lineages trace their knowledge through chains back to the empire era. The region is one of the more reliable sources of preserved bioengineering knowledge in a religious framework.

---

### The Indic Belt

**Former territory:** Indian subcontinent, Sri Lanka, surrounding regions.

**Character:** Dense. The Indic Belt supported enormous populations before the collapse and supports the largest surviving population of any single region now. The land is productive, the monsoon cycle persists, the river systems are intact. The result is a region of many city-states in close proximity, constant low-level competition, and sophisticated political culture built around managing that competition. More treaties and agreements are active in the Indic Belt at any given time than in any other region. More are also being broken.

**Biomancy:** The Indic Belt has the strongest tradition of open biomancer practice on Earth. Several established knowledge institutions — operating under religious framing — have preserved and developed bioengineering techniques across multiple generations. The region is the primary source of trained biomancers for most of the eastern world.

**City-cores:** Enormous — millions of inhabitants in some cases, built on layered ruins going back to some of the earliest human settlements anywhere on Earth. The deepest levels of the oldest cities have not been explored in living memory.

---

### Yndonesh and the New Shore

**Former territory:** Southeast Asia, Indonesian archipelago, the exposed South China Sea shelf.

**Character:** Two things imperfectly combined — the old island cultures of the former Indonesian and Philippine archipelagos, now connected by exposed shelf terrain, and the New Shore: the vast lowland that used to be the South China Sea floor, now exposed and only partially explored.

**The New Shore:** Flat, low, hot, still geologically unstable in sections. The largest frontier territory on Earth. Every major faction bordering it claims portions. None controls it fully.

What the New Shore contains: extensive pre-collapse seafloor infrastructure, unique geological formations, and biological organisms that evolved in the deep seafloor environment and are now exposed to surface conditions for the first time. Some are harmless. Others are not.

---

### The Panpacifik

**Former territory:** East Asia, Korean peninsula, Japanese archipelago, eastern Pacific coastline.

**Character:** The Panpacifik contains what were, before the collapse, the most densely industrialised territories on Earth. The ruins reflect this — enormous city-cores, extensive underground infrastructure, and the highest density of pre-collapse technology still in partial operation of any region. The dominant political form is the city-state: single urban centres of enormous population with controlled hinterlands, conducting relations with more ceremony than most of the rest of the world would recognise as functional.

**The Japanese highlands:** Retains distinct cultural traditions that have survived in modified form across tens of thousands of years. Preserves some of the oldest continuously maintained knowledge traditions in the Panpacifik.

**Pacific remnant:** What remains of the Pacific Ocean is still vast. Rogue Trader routes sometimes touch at Pacific-facing ports. The ocean-facing city-states have the most consistent contact with what comes from off-planet.

---

### Merica

**Former territory:** North America, Central America, South America.

**Character:** Two places that happen to share a continental mass.

**Northern Merica:** Large and complicated by the Merica Scar, which rendered a significant portion of the interior geologically unstable. The Hudson Bay is gone. The eastern and western coastal regions support significant city-states; the interior is sparsely populated and controlled by nomadic confederacies. The Bering Corridor connects northern Merica to the Urshic Steppe — a wide, cold land bridge that has been continuously trafficked since it opened. The Corridor itself is contested territory, with fortified positions on both ends and a no-man's land in the middle.

**Southern Merica:** Warmer, wetter. The rainforest territories in the equatorial zone are among the least explored regions on Earth — the combination of terrain, humidity, and aggressive indigenous biology makes sustained occupation difficult. The southern cone is more temperate and more settled.

---

### The Afrik Interior

**Former territory:** Sub-Saharan Africa.

**Character:** The oldest continuously inhabited region on Earth and, paradoxically, the least scarred by the empire's legacy. The collapse wars were fought most intensely in regions of highest strategic value — the Afrik Interior's value to the empire was as a resource extraction zone, not a population centre. The orbital strikes landed elsewhere.

The result: pre-collapse culture has survived in forms more continuous than almost anywhere else. The empire-era ruins are present but not dominant. Some communities can trace their cultural practices back further than any equivalent tradition in the Europan Reaches.

**Biological diversity:** The highest density of native organism diversity on Earth. The disease ecology is intense — communities have built their entire social structure around managing a pathogen load that would devastate outsiders.

**Relationship to outsiders:** The Afrik Interior has a long experience of being exploited by outside powers and a sophisticated cultural memory of it. Resistance to outside encroachment is not primarily military — it is social, informational, and ecological. The Interior has been protecting itself from outsiders for longer than most outsiders have existed as distinct polities.

### Australis

**Former territory:** Australia, Tasmania, and the exposed Timor and Arafura sea shelves.

**Character:** Australis was already one of the harshest inhabited continents on Earth before the collapse. The interior was arid. The biology was ancient, isolated, and aggressive in ways that surprised every outside group that ever arrived expecting something manageable. Thirty to forty thousand years of additional isolation and additional aridity have not improved the situation. The interior of Australis is one of the most inhospitable non-dead-zone environments on Earth — not because of weapons residue or engineered organisms, but because the land itself has had thirty millennia to perfect the art of killing things that don't belong there.

The collapse wars touched Australis lightly relative to most regions. The continent was not a major strategic target — no significant imperial infrastructure, no critical population centres by empire standards, no orbital assets worth striking. The wars happened elsewhere. Australis simply continued being itself, with diminishing outside contact as the trade networks that had reached it during the empire era collapsed along with everything else.

**The ocean drainage:** The Timor Sea and Arafura Sea — the shallow waters separating Australis from Yndonesh and New Guinea — are now significantly reduced or in places fully dry. This has created an imperfect land connection northward: not a clean corridor, but a broken series of exposed shelf flats, salt marshes, and shallow remnant water that can be crossed with sufficient knowledge and preparation. Australis is no longer fully isolated. The cultures that have developed in the north of the continent have known about this for thousands of years. They have mixed feelings about it.

**The people:** Australis has human populations distributed primarily around the continental coast and the river systems of the eastern and southeastern regions. The interior is inhabited in the sense that people cross it and survive in it, but permanent settlement requires knowledge that takes generations to develop and is not transferable without that inheritance. The cultural traditions of Australis are among the oldest continuous knowledge systems on Earth — predating the empire by enough that the empire's rise and fall registers in their oral history as a distant event that happened to other people.

The biological knowledge embedded in those traditions is extraordinary. Australis has some of the most dangerous native organisms on Earth and some of the most useful, and the communities that have lived among them for tens of thousands of years know the difference in detail that no outside observer has ever successfully catalogued. This knowledge is not freely shared. Outside factions that have attempted to extract it have generally found that the people of Australis are significantly better at operating in their environment than anyone else is, which gives them considerable advantage when they choose to be unwelcoming.

**Relationship to Yndonesh:** The partial land connection northward has produced cautious, irregular contact between Australis's northern cultures and Yndonesh factions probing southward along the New Shore. Trade occurs. Population movement occurs, in both directions. Full integration has not occurred and the northern Australis cultures are not pursuing it. The Yndonesh factions most interested in southern expansion regard Australis as a frontier opportunity; the Australis cultures regard the Yndonesh expansion as a familiar kind of problem.

**Dead zones:** Australis has fewer dead zones than most inhabited regions. What it has instead are several areas of extreme native biological activity — regions where the local ecosystem is dense enough, aggressive enough, and sufficiently unfamiliar to outsiders that the practical effect is similar to a dead zone: entry is possible, exit is not guaranteed, and the things that live there are operating on a logic that outsiders cannot read. Whether any of these are partially the result of old bioweapon interference with native organisms is unknown. The question has been raised.

### The Thaw — Antarctica

**Former territory:** Antarctica. The continent at the bottom of the world, formerly under kilometres of ice.

**Character:** Antarctica is unlike every other region on Earth in one fundamental way: it has no deep human history. Every other continent has been inhabited for tens or hundreds of thousands of years, layered with the accumulated weight of settlement, culture, and survival. Antarctica was ice. The empire reached it, eventually — the ice had been retreating since the pre-collapse period, accelerated by centuries of industrial warming and then by weapons effects during the early collapse era — and built on it during the later empire period, when the coastal fringes and then the interior began to become accessible. Those empire-era installations are the oldest human structures on the continent. There is nothing beneath them.

Thirty to forty thousand years of further warming — both natural continuation and the ongoing effects of whatever the collapse wars did to the atmosphere — have deglaciated most of the continent. What remains of the ice is concentrated in the highest interior elevations. The rest is terrain: exposed rock, mineral-heavy plains, river systems that formed as the melt progressed, coastal zones that have been accessible for long enough to develop something approaching a functional ecosystem.

Antarctica is cold. Not impassably cold in most of the habitable zones — the coastal regions in particular have temperatures comparable to historical Scandinavia or northern Canada — but cold enough to shape everything. The growing season is short. The weather is severe. The geography is dramatic in the way that recently deglaciated landscapes always are: raw, sharp, unweathered, still being carved by the rivers that are only a few thousand years old.

**What the ice left behind:** When the ice retreated, it uncovered everything that was under it. This includes: the bedrock geology of a continent that has been sealed under ice for millions of years, which means mineral deposits that have never been touched; the preserved remains of organisms from every era of Earth's pre-glacial history; and, most significantly, the empire-era installations built on the early coastal fringe, which in some cases were sealed by subsequent ice advance and then re-exposed by the final melt. Some of these installations are intact. Their contents have not been assessed by anyone currently alive.

**Current habitation:** Sparse. The coastal zones support human settlement — mostly groups that moved south from the former Southern Ocean islands (now partially exposed shelf) or down from the tip of Southern Merica across what is now a much narrower Drake Passage. These communities are young by the standards of most regions — a few thousand years of continuous habitation at most — and are still in the process of developing the accumulated knowledge of how to live in this specific environment.

The interior is largely unexplored. Expeditions have mapped the major terrain features and returned with reports of intact empire-era infrastructure in the deep interior — structures that were built during the peak of imperial capability and then sealed when the ice advanced over them, now re-exposed. None of these expeditions has fully investigated what they found. The distances involved, the weather, and the things that several expeditions reported encountering without being able to adequately describe have discouraged follow-up.

**Strategic significance:** Antarctica's mineral wealth is substantial — the geology has never been exploited and the deposits are extensive. Its empire-era sealed installations are potentially the most intact pre-collapse technology on Earth, precisely because they were sealed and isolated. And the Drake Passage narrowing has made it accessible from Southern Merica in a way it never was historically, which has brought it into the active awareness of Merican factions that previously had no reason to care about the southern continent.

The result is a slow-building contest for the Thaw. No single faction controls it. Several are in the early stages of establishing presence. The communities already there are aware of what is coming and are making decisions accordingly.

**The sealed installations:** What the empire built at the bottom of the world, during the period when Antarctica was first becoming accessible, and what it considered worth building there, are questions that the sealed installations could answer. The expeditions that found them have not provided adequate reports. This may be because the conditions made proper documentation impossible. It may be because what they found was difficult to describe. It may be because some of the expeditions that went in did not all come back out, and the ones who did were not in a condition to write clearly.

## Navigation and Connectivity

### Major Overland Routes

- **The Basin Floor Crossing** — the Great Basin can be crossed with the right preparation and knowledge of stable routes. Cuts travel time between Nordafrik and the Europan Reaches dramatically. Extremely hazardous.
- **The Bering Corridor** — connects Merica to the Urshic Steppe. The primary route by which Urshic and Merican cultures interact.
- **The Gulf Plain** — the dried Persian Gulf floor connects Arabian routes directly to Iranian plateau routes. Historically a point of conflict.
- **The Yndonesh Land Routes** — the exposed South China Sea shelf connects formerly island cultures into a continuous path. Still being mapped.
- **The Timor Crossing** — the partially exposed Timor and Arafura shelves create a broken but passable connection between Yndonesh and northern Australis. Requires local knowledge. Not a clean route.

### Sea Travel

The remaining oceans are still navigable but significantly reduced. Ship-based trade is the primary means of long-distance commerce for coastline-adjacent settlements, ranging from basic sail and oar to rare vessels using partially intact imperial propulsion technology.

### The Rogue Trader Connection

Off-planet contact touches at a handful of known points: major Pacific-facing ports in the Panpacifik, certain Atlantic-facing points in the Europan Reaches, and a few sites in other regions where orbital infrastructure remnants make contact feasible. Their owners know exactly how valuable these contact points are.

## Cartographic Note

No complete map of the current Earth exists. The geography has changed enough — and knowledge networks have fragmented sufficiently — that no single source covers all regions accurately. What does exist: regional maps of varying quality; fragmentary imperial-era maps reflecting the world as it was, not as it is; trade route charts accurate along routes and blank everywhere else; cult documents describing sacred sites.

The Afrik Eye crater is reported to contain a library-cult that has made mapping a religious practice for thousands of years. Whether their archives are accessible or accurate is unknown.
