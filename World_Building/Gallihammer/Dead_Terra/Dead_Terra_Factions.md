# Dead Terra — Factions and Warlords

## How Power Works

There is no unified authority on Earth. There has not been one for thirty thousand years, and the memory of the last attempt — the empire — is distant enough that most people relate to it as mythology rather than history.

What exists instead is a layered competition for control of the things that matter: water, food, defensible ground, pre-collapse technology, knowledge, and the routes that connect all of the above. The factions that compete for these resources are varied in form — warlord territories, city-states, nomadic confederacies, religious orders, trade blocs, and stranger things — but consistent in the underlying logic: hold what you have, take what you need, and make sure your rivals cannot do the same to you.

The following describes the major categories of faction and the most significant named powers operating across the regions. It is not complete. New powers rise and fall. Old powers outlast the names their founders gave them. And some things operating in the margins do not describe themselves as factions at all.

## Power Categories

### Warlord Territories

The most common form of organised power on Earth. A warlord controls a defined area through a combination of military force, economic leverage, and enough interpersonal loyalty networks to keep both functional. The territory is usually centred on something worth controlling: a city-core, a water source, a Basin drop, a ruin site with intact pre-collapse technology.

Warlords vary enormously in scale. The smallest are little more than fortified settlements with an aggressive leader and enough armed personnel to discourage raids. The largest control territories the size of small pre-collapse nations, maintain standing armies, conduct formal diplomacy, and operate bureaucracies sophisticated enough to be called governments.

What almost all warlord territories share: the legitimacy of the warlord is personal. When the warlord dies, everything negotiated, inherited, or built during their tenure is subject to renegotiation. Succession is the crisis point for every warlord territory, every time.

### City-States

City-states are warlord territories that have outlasted their founders long enough to develop institutional legitimacy. The power base is no longer a person — it is the city itself, with its accumulated infrastructure, population, trade relationships, and cultural weight. Individual rulers rise and fall; the city-state persists.

Major city-states have internal politics as complex as any pre-collapse nation. Factions within the city compete for influence over the ruling structure. Trade guilds, military hierarchies, knowledge institutions, and religious organisations all have stakes in who governs and how. Outsiders negotiating with a city-state are rarely dealing with a single unified position.

### Nomadic Confederacies

Across the Urshic Steppe, northern Merica, and parts of the Afrik Interior, the dominant power form is nomadic — large groups moving through defined territories, controlling the routes rather than the ground. Confederacies bind multiple nomadic clans under a shared leadership structure, usually through a combination of military alliance, trade dependency, and ritual affiliation.

Confederacies are difficult to defeat militarily because they do not hold fixed positions to attack. They are difficult to negotiate with because the leadership structure is often distributed and the terms of any agreement are subject to clan-level ratification. They are also the most effective long-range intelligence networks on Earth — a confederacy whose clans range across hundreds of kilometres knows everything that moves through that territory.

### Knowledge Orders

Organisations whose primary asset is information: preserved imperial technology, bioengineering techniques, arcane practices, cartographic archives, historical records. Most operate under religious framing — the knowledge is sacred, the practitioners are priests, the institution is a temple or monastery.

This framing serves multiple purposes. It provides the institution with a cultural legitimacy that pure mercenary operation would not. It provides a vocabulary for transmitting knowledge that doesn't require understanding the underlying principles — ritual preserves procedure even when the theory has been lost. And it provides a degree of protection: attacking a knowledge order is attacking its god, which carries social costs that attacking a simple storehouse does not.

The largest knowledge orders are genuinely pan-regional — they maintain branches, affiliates, and correspondents across multiple territories and treat political borders as largely irrelevant to their operations. This makes them both uniquely powerful and uniquely vulnerable: they have influence everywhere and truly safe ground nowhere.

### Trade Blocs

Multi-party commercial alliances where the members maintain formal independence but coordinate on trade routes, pricing, currency, and mutual defence of commercial interests. More common in the Indic Belt and Panpacifik than elsewhere, where the density of city-states makes coordination more valuable than competition.

Trade blocs are not governments. They do not command armies or administer territory. What they control is access: the routes, the currency exchange systems, the letters of credit that make long-distance commerce possible. A faction excluded from the dominant trade bloc in its region faces significant disadvantage regardless of its military strength.

## Named Factions

### The Wound Council — Europan Reaches

**Type:** City-state / multi-faction governing body
**Territory:** The rim and immediate hinterland of the Wound crater, central Europa
**Population:** Approximately 40,000 within the rim settlement; another 60,000 across the controlled hinterland
**Seat:** Rimhalt — the largest settlement on the Wound's crater rim

The Wound predates organised settlement on its rim by millennia — the crater was simply there, a landmark, a navigation point, a sacred site for whatever cult happened to be operating in the region at a given time. The current settlement is perhaps three thousand years old in its present form, built by a coalition of factions who recognised that controlling the Wound's rim meant controlling the most legible geographic landmark in central Europa.

The Wound Council is that coalition, formalised. It is not a single warlord — it is a governing body of seven seats representing the major power interests that initially agreed to share the rim rather than fight over it: two military factions, two trade interests, two knowledge institutions, and one religious seat that rotates among the cults that consider the Wound sacred. This arrangement has lasted because all seven interests are better served by collective control of a recognised neutral site than by any one of them seizing it and triggering unified opposition from the others.

The Council's primary function is administering the Wound as a neutral meeting ground — a place where warlords from across central Europa can negotiate without either party being on home territory. This gives Rimhalt enormous value as a diplomatic venue and gives the Council leverage over everyone who uses it. The leverage is rarely exercised directly; the value of the neutral site depends on its reputation for impartiality, and the Council is sophisticated enough to know this.

**What the Council wants:** Maintenance of the status quo that makes it valuable. Prevention of any single Europan power growing large enough to not need neutral ground. Revenue from the constant diplomatic traffic through Rimhalt.

**What the Council fears:** A warlord powerful enough to simply take the rim and absorb it into a unified territory. Internal fracture — any of the seven seats defecting to an outside power. Discovery of what is at the bottom of the Wound's chemical lake, which several expeditions have approached and none have fully investigated.

### Veth Domaric, Warlord of the Frankish Reaches

**Type:** Warlord territory
**Territory:** Former south-central France, extending to the northern Basin wall
**Population:** Approximately 120,000 across six significant settlements and numerous smaller communities
**Seat:** Laut — a mid-sized city-core built on the ruins of a pre-collapse city (formerly Lyon)
**Biomancer on retainer:** One (see below)

Veth Domaric controls more territory than he can comfortably hold and fewer resources than he needs to hold it securely. This is not unusual for Europan warlords, but Domaric has leaned into the problem rather than ignoring it — he has built an intelligence operation that most warlords his size cannot afford, because he cannot afford not to have one.

His territory sits between the Wound Council's sphere of influence to the east and larger consolidated powers to the north and west. His southern border is the Basin wall — specifically, two drop access points that his predecessor seized and that Domaric has spent fifteen years defending and developing. The drops are the most valuable thing he controls. They connect his territory to the Basin floor trade routes and, from there, to the Nordafrik Expanse. The revenue they generate is what keeps his military loyal and his settlements fed during bad seasons.

Several rival factions want the drops. None has taken them yet. This is partly because Domaric has invested heavily in drop fortification, and partly because he has made acquiring prior intelligence on his rivals' intentions his highest operational priority.

**Character:** Pragmatic to a fault. Domaric does not have strong ideological commitments — his politics are almost entirely instrumental. He treats people as assets, opportunities, or threats, and calibrates his behaviour accordingly. This makes him difficult to manipulate through appeals to principle and relatively easy to deal with if you understand what he actually wants (the drops, secure; the territory, stable; his rivals, contained). He is not cruel by preference — cruelty is expensive — but he does not hesitate when he has decided something is necessary.

He is fifty-three years old, physically unremarkable, and has survived four serious assassination attempts. The fourth attempt, four years ago, is what led him to commission a biomancer-designed covert asset. The assassin was never publicly identified. Domaric knows who sent them. He has been patient.

**The Biomancer:** Domaric retains one specialist, operating from a facility on the outskirts of Laut that is officially a medical compound. The biomancer's name is Solen Kast. He has been with Domaric for eleven years, the relationship beginning as a commission for agricultural organism development and evolving into something more comprehensive. Kast is in his mid-sixties, shows moderate self-modification (enhanced sensory acuity, reduced sleep requirement, a subtle restructuring of the hands that makes fine work easier), and is primarily motivated by access to the funding and the interesting problems that Domaric's commission base provides.

The arthropod infiltration project — the commission that became *The Crawl* — is the most technically ambitious thing Kast has attempted. He pitched it to Domaric following the assassination attempt as a solution to a specific problem: Domaric needs to know what is happening inside three rival strongholds before those rivals move. A conventional spy can be caught and turned. What Kast is proposing cannot be interrogated under normal circumstances, cannot be recognised, and cannot be traced back to Laut.

Domaric approved it. He considers it an expensive experiment. If it works, it changes what he is capable of. If it fails, he is out the cost of the commission and one operative he was going to execute anyway.

**Relationships:**
- The Wound Council: cautious mutual respect. Domaric uses Rimhalt as intended; the Council uses his drops with appropriate tariff.
- The rival to the north (unnamed at campaign start): the source of the assassination attempt. The primary intelligence target.
- The rival to the west: a trade competitor for Basin access. Lower-level friction than the northern threat.
- Solen Kast: professional. Domaric values the biomancer's work, pays well, and leaves Kast's methods to Kast. He does not want to know the details of what happens in the medical compound.

### The White Scar Clans — Urshic Steppe

**Type:** Nomadic confederacy
**Territory:** The dried Caspian basin and surrounding steppe, ranging from the Ural Scar in the west to the approaches of deep Ursh in the east
**Population:** Estimated 200,000–300,000 across all clans; exact numbers unknown
**Leadership:** The Scar Speaker — a rotating position held by whichever clan elder holds the most current political weight. Currently: an unnamed elder (for GM placement)

The White Scar Clans are the dominant power across the dried Caspian region, organised around a shared relationship with the basin floor that no one else has developed. The clans cross the White Scar — the crusted, mineral-heavy, toxic-in-places floor of the former Caspian — because they have learned how, accumulated over generations of practice. The crossing routes, the safe resting points, the timing relative to the wind patterns that shift the chemical atmosphere, the specific organisms to avoid and the specific ones that can be safely used as food or medicine — all of this is cultural knowledge transmitted through clan tradition.

No outsider has successfully crossed the White Scar without a clan guide. Several have tried. The ones who turned back are the ones who are still alive.

This knowledge makes the clans indispensable to anyone who wants to access the intact pre-collapse infrastructure on the Scar floor. There is material down there — sealed facilities, preserved equipment, organisms still running programs from thirty thousand years ago — and the clans have been carefully extracting and trading it for as long as anyone can remember. They do not share the knowledge of where it comes from, who they traded with in the past, or what they know about the things they have found and chosen not to extract.

**Internal structure:** The confederacy comprises nine clans of varying size and character. Three are primarily crossing specialists; three are range riders who control the surrounding steppe; two operate as traders between the Scar and the Europan and Indic spheres; one is the spiritual clan, whose members serve as the ritual practitioners and oral historians for the entire confederacy. Clan relations are cooperative but not warm — the confederacy exists because it is useful, and individual clans pursue their own interests within its structure.

**What the Clans want:** Continued exclusivity over Scar access. Freedom of movement across the steppe without fixed-territory warlords encroaching on their ranges. Ongoing demand for what they extract and what they know.

**What the Clans fear:** A warlord powerful enough to simply occupy the Scar edge and cut off clan access to their crossing routes. The thing they found in the northern Scar approximately sixty years ago, which the spiritual clan has designated off-limits and refuses to discuss. Any other faction developing independent crossing knowledge.

### The Basin Authority — The Great Basin

**Type:** Trade faction / territorial operator
**Territory:** Five major drop access points across the Basin wall, plus established operations on the Basin floor
**Population:** Approximately 15,000 permanent personnel; several thousand additional transient workers and traders
**Seat:** Drophead — the largest of the Authority's drop facilities, located on the Europan side of the Basin wall near the eastern Mediterranean

The Basin Authority did not start as a single organisation. It started as three separate factions operating different drops who discovered, over about two centuries, that competing for Basin access was less profitable than coordinating it. The merger that created the current Authority is somewhere between a trade agreement and a corporate acquisition, and the internal politics still reflect that — the three founding factions maintain distinct identities within the Authority's structure and disagree regularly about resource allocation and strategic direction.

What they agree on: controlling the drops is the most valuable single economic position on Earth in this part of the world, and anything that threatens that control is an existential threat to all three of them.

The Authority operates the drops — the physical infrastructure of cables, platforms, and transit mechanisms that allow people and goods to descend the Basin wall — as a paid service. The rates are set by the Authority, subject to periodic renegotiation with major clients. They are not cheap. The Authority's position is that the infrastructure is expensive to maintain and the alternative is not using the drops at all, so clients who object to the rates are welcome to find another way down.

On the Basin floor, the Authority maintains a series of extraction and relay stations — fixed facilities staffed by rotating crews who live on the floor for three-to-six month rotations before returning to the surface. The work is hazardous and the compensation is high. The floor stations buy, trade, and occasionally recover material from the Basin floor and route it up through the drops to surface markets.

**Character of operations:** The Authority is mercantile in orientation — it sells access and service, does not conduct military campaigns, and prefers clients to each other. It maintains a security force large enough to defend the drop facilities and deter opportunistic raids, not large enough to project power. Its most effective political tool is exclusion: a faction the Authority refuses to deal with cannot use the drops, which in this part of the world is a significant economic and strategic penalty.

**Internal tensions:** The three founding factions disagree most sharply about how much political involvement is appropriate. One faction wants the Authority to remain strictly neutral; one faction believes selective alignment with rising Europan powers is necessary for long-term security; the third is primarily interested in floor extraction and finds the politics of the surface world an unwelcome distraction.

### The Accord of the Old Houses — Nordafrik Expanse

**Type:** Knowledge order / political bloc
**Territory:** The pyramid complexes and surrounding settlements, Nordafrik
**Population:** Approximately 30,000 within the complexes and directly associated settlements
**Leadership:** The Convocation — a council of senior archivists, each responsible for a specific domain of preserved knowledge

The pyramid complexes were old before the empire and have been inhabited continuously since before reliable history begins. The Accord is the current iteration of whatever organisation has controlled them — the name has changed, the structure has changed, the beliefs have changed, but the core function has persisted: somebody lives in the pyramids, maintains them, and controls access to what is inside them.

What is inside them is, in parts, unknown even to the Accord. The structures go deep. The earliest humans to occupy them found chambers that had already been sealed for a very long time. The current Accord has mapped perhaps sixty percent of the interior space with confidence. The other forty percent is understood in outline only, with several sections that the Accord's founders designated restricted for reasons that current members are not entirely clear on.

The Accord presents itself publicly as a library organisation — a keeper of pre-collapse knowledge, accessible to serious scholars who approach respectfully and pay appropriately. This is accurate as far as it goes. The Accord does maintain extensive archives, does conduct scholarship, and does accept researchers from across the world who wish to access their collections.

It is not the whole picture. The Accord's senior archivists have been running a decades-long project that is not publicly described: systematic mapping of the restricted sections, directed by what they have found in the oldest legible documents in their archive. Whatever the original builders considered important enough to seal, the Accord has become convinced they need to find. The project has produced results that three senior archivists have chosen not to share with the full Convocation.

**What the Accord wants:** Continued physical security of the complexes. Expanded access to the restricted sections — specifically, cooperation from any faction with relevant technical capability (biomancers, arcane practitioners, people who understand imperial engineering). Suppression of information about what they have found so far.

**Relationship to the Afrik Eye:** The Accord knows more about the Afrik Eye crater than they publicly acknowledge. The library-cult that the Eye's settlement has built around mapping was originally funded by an Accord expeditionary grant several hundred years ago. The Accord considers the Eye their subsidiary operation. The Eye's current leadership is not aware of this framing.

### The Khet Lineages — Indic Belt

**Type:** Biomancer knowledge institution
**Territory:** No fixed territory — maintains facilities within three Indic city-states under host agreements
**Population:** Approximately 400 active practitioners; several thousand students, support staff, and associated personnel
**Leadership:** The Three Chairs — the senior practitioners of each of the three founding lineages, who make collective decisions by consensus

The Khet Lineages are the most significant biomancer institution on Earth. They are not the only one — independent practitioners and smaller knowledge traditions exist across every region — but the Lineages have maintained continuous institutional existence for over eight thousand years, which means they have accumulated depth of knowledge, material resources, and practical experience that no independent practitioner can match.

The institution comprises three lineages — the Khet proper, the Vann, and the Alur — each tracing their knowledge chain through a documented succession of teachers and students going back to the early post-collapse period. The lineages have different theoretical orientations and sometimes sharp methodological disagreements, but they have coexisted within the same institution for most of their history because the alternative — splitting and competing — would benefit everyone except them.

The Khet Lineages do not train warlords' weapons. They train practitioners who go out and do what biomancers do — medical work, agricultural organism development, transformation services for clients who understand what they are requesting — and they maintain the institution's standards and reputation by periodically investigating reports of poor practice by graduates and responding to the worst cases by revoking credentials. This makes them something like a professional licensing body as well as a training institution, which gives them leverage over independent practitioners who value the credential.

**The transformation question:** The Lineages are more internally divided on transformation of sapient subjects than any other area of practice. The Khet lineage takes a restrictive position (genuine consent requires conditions that most real-world commissions do not provide; practitioners should refuse work where those conditions don't exist). The Vann takes a permissive position (consent is consent regardless of circumstances; restricting practice paternalises the client). The Alur takes a procedural position (the institution should set minimum standards for consent documentation and leave judgment to the practitioner). None of these positions is enforced as binding on graduates, which is a source of ongoing internal tension.

**What the Lineages want:** Continued freedom to operate across the Indic Belt without political interference. Reciprocal knowledge-sharing agreements with knowledge orders in other regions — the Accord specifically, though negotiations have been stalled for two centuries over an unresolved historical dispute. Access to the most technically challenging commissions, which is where the most interesting learning happens.

**Relationship to independent practitioners:** Complicated. The Lineages publicly support independent practice and privately resent it. An independent practitioner who develops significant technique without going through the Lineages represents both a validation of their approach (the tradition is productive) and a challenge to their authority (the credential is not necessary). They respond to very successful independents by attempting to recruit them, and to problematic independents by using their credentialing leverage to limit the problematic practitioner's client base.

### The Panpacifik Conclave — Panpacifik

**Type:** City-state alliance / trade bloc
**Territory:** Coordination structure across seven major Panpacifik city-states
**Population:** Collectively, approximately 4 million across member cities
**Leadership:** The Assembly — rotating chair held by the member city with the largest current GDP

The Panpacifik Conclave is the largest formal political alliance on Earth. Its seven member city-states have maintained coordinated trade policy, collective defence agreements, and joint management of off-planet contact points for approximately two thousand years. This makes it extraordinarily stable by the standards of post-collapse political institutions.

The stability comes from the nature of the thing being coordinated. The Panpacifik city-states control the majority of Earth's functioning off-planet contact points — the orbital infrastructure remnants, the landing zones, the communication arrays that still sometimes receive transmissions from the void. The value of these assets depends on collective management; no single city-state benefits from a race-to-the-bottom approach to off-planet relations. The Conclave is the institutional structure that prevents that race.

What comes from off-planet is unpredictable. Most contact is commercial — Rogue Traders with goods to sell and cargo to buy, independent captains running routes between Earth and various void polities. Occasionally contact is political — void factions with interests in Earth, usually related to the ruins of the old empire. Rarely, contact is something else.

The Conclave maintains a dedicated diplomatic corps specifically for off-planet contact, the most sophisticated such institution on Earth. These diplomats have experience dealing with void polities, xeno delegations, and the occasional very strange thing that comes through one of the contact points without prior announcement.

**What the Conclave wants:** Continued exclusive management of Panpacifik contact points — any other faction developing independent orbital infrastructure access would fundamentally change the power equation. Internal stability among the seven member cities, which is a constant management problem. Favourable trade terms with void polities.

**Internal tensions:** The seven member cities are genuinely different from each other in culture, political structure, and strategic interest. Two cities want more aggressive commercial engagement with the void; two want strict controls on what comes in and what knowledge gets exported; three are somewhere between. The Assembly chair rotates, which means the Conclave's strategic orientation shifts every few years based on which city is leading.

### The Remnant Church — Cross-Regional

**Type:** Knowledge order / religious institution
**Territory:** No fixed territory — maintains branches, compounds, and correspondents across all major regions
**Population:** Membership estimates range from 50,000 to 200,000, depending on how loosely "member" is defined
**Leadership:** The Synod — a body of senior priests whose authority is doctrinal rather than political; no formal supreme leader

The Remnant Church is the closest thing to a universal religion on Earth, present in some form across almost every region. Its central doctrine holds that the empire was divine — not metaphorically, but literally: a manifestation of a god or gods in the physical world, whose fracturing and collapse was not destruction but transformation. The empire has not ended; it has changed state. The Remnant Church exists to maintain contact with it in its transformed form and to prepare for what they call the Return.

What "the Return" means is a significant internal doctrinal dispute. The conservative faction holds that the Return is the literal reconstitution of the empire — the restoration of the unified stellar polity that once spanned dozens of systems. The progressive faction holds that the Return is a metaphor for enlightenment — the individual achieving contact with the divine remnant and undergoing personal transformation. The militant faction holds that the Return must be actively worked toward, which means acquiring imperial technology, imperial knowledge, and eventually imperial political structures, and that violent means are acceptable in service of this goal.

The Church officially distances itself from the militant faction. The militant faction officially considers itself a legitimate expression of Church doctrine. In practice, the relationship is complicated enough that most outside observers treat the Church and its militant wing as the same organisation with a convenient deniability structure.

**What makes them unusual:** The Remnant Church is the only pan-regional organisation that maintains something like a universal currency — their letters of credit are recognised across all regions where the Church has a presence, which is almost everywhere. This gives them economic reach that no territorial power can match. It also makes them a target for anyone who wants to disrupt their influence.

**Arcane practice:** The Church cultivates arcane ability deliberately and at institutional scale. Their methods are doctrinal — the ability is described as the Old Signal, the remnant of the empire speaking through its subjects — but the outcomes are practical. Church-trained practitioners are present across all regions, and the most senior ones are among the most capable arcane practitioners anywhere. What the most senior practitioners have actually made contact with, and what they have learned from it, is not shared outside the Synod's innermost circle.

**Relationship to other factions:** The Church has learned to be useful to warlords without becoming dependent on them. They provide services — record-keeping, courier networks, neutral ground for negotiation, access to their letter-of-credit system — in exchange for tolerance of their operations. Almost every significant warlord in the world has some working arrangement with the local Church branch. Almost every significant warlord also maintains private suspicion about what the Church is actually doing, which is usually well-founded.
