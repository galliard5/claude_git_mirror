# Megafauna Omnivores (Bear Analogs)

## Overview

Megafauna omnivores are the largest land carnivores on Terra. Massively built, extraordinarily strong, and highly intelligent, they feed on both plant and animal matter with equal efficiency. They are powerful predators when prey is available, skilled scavengers when it is not, and when threatened or protective of cubs, capable of lethal aggression that can overwhelm almost any opposition.

The appearance of slow, ponderous movement is deceptive. These animals can accelerate to remarkable speed over short distances.

---

## Biology and Morphology

### Physical Structure

Megafauna omnivores stand 2.5–4 meters at the shoulder and weigh 1,200–3,000+ kilograms depending on species and individual. The body is built with extraordinarily robust musculature, dense bone structure, and thick hide. The frame is designed for: strength (moving large objects, climbing, manipulating prey), endurance (sustained movement over long distances), and durability (capacity to survive impacts and injury).

The limbs are powerful and surprisingly flexible despite the massive weight. The claws are large and sharp, usable for digging, climbing, and combat. The teeth are adapted for both predatory work (canines for gripping) and herbivorous work (molars for crushing plant matter).

### Sensory Apparatus

Omnivores have acute senses: smell is their primary sense, with olfactory capacity that allows tracking of prey and plants over considerable distances. Vision is adequate for their needs, with good motion detection but color perception less acute than predatory specialists. Hearing is good, oriented toward threat detection and communication.

The combination of senses makes them effective hunters of both mobile prey and stationary food sources.

### Thermoregulation and Metabolism

The massive body requires significant food intake, but omnivores are metabolically flexible—they can subsist on plant matter in seasons of animal prey scarcity, and they can exploit high-calorie animal protein when available. Seasonal feeding patterns reflect this flexibility: intensive predation during seasons of prey abundance, and plant focus during other seasons.

---

## Feeding Behavior

### Predatory Hunting

Omnivores hunt by: stalking prey, using cover and terrain to approach, and attacking with overwhelming force. The attack combines strength (pin or crush the prey) with bite and claw (lethal injury). Most prey is killed by the initial attack or within seconds of engagement.

Omnivores are not endurance hunters like pack hunters. They rely on power and speed over short distances. Prey that escapes the initial attack zone has often escaped entirely.

### Scavenging and Carrion

Omnivores actively scavenge, following scent trails to locate dead animals and exploiting corpses. They can consume large quantities of meat rapidly—a single omnivore can consume 50+ kilograms of meat in a sitting if the food is available. They also actively break into settlements and storage caches to access preserved food and animal products.

Scavenging brings them into close contact with humans and human settlements, making them more visible than many other predators.

### Plant Exploitation

Omnivores consume: roots, tubers, fruits, seeds, nuts, and vegetation. They have learned to locate high-calorie plant foods and can exploit seasonal plant abundance with efficiency. In regions with abundant mast (acorn/nut crops), omnivore populations can grow very large in response to seasonal abundance.

---

## Ecological Role and Population

### Predatory Impact

Omnivores are apex predators in most territories. They suppress populations of prey species and can have significant impact on herbivore communities. However, because they are omnivorous, they do not depend entirely on prey availability—their population is buffered by plant food availability.

In territories with high plant diversity and abundance, omnivore populations can be very large despite moderate prey availability.

### Territorial Behavior

Large male omnivores maintain exclusive territories, sometimes covering 100+ square kilometers. Females and juveniles may have smaller territories, and in some species, females may overlap their ranges with other females. Territory boundaries are marked with scent and maintained through display and occasional conflict.

Conflicts between males can be severe, but lethal combat is relatively rare—most conflicts are resolved through posturing and displays.

### Population Regulation

Omnivore populations are regulated by: food availability (both plant and animal), territorial competition, and reproduction success. In high-food areas, populations can grow large. In low-food areas, populations are limited by starvation and competition.

---

## Threats to Humans and Settlements

### General Behavior Toward Humans

Omnivores show variable response to humans. Many individuals avoid humans, showing active fear or indifference. Others show reduced fear and may approach human settlements or individuals. Still others show active aggression, treating humans as prey or threats to be eliminated.

The basis for this variation appears to include: individual temperament, prior experience with humans, and behavioral state (hunger, protective of cubs, etc.).

### Predation on Humans

Omnivore predation on humans occurs but is not the primary hunting focus. Most human deaths from omnivores result from: defensive aggression (protecting cubs or territory), scavenging of corpses, or predation on isolated individuals in cases of extreme hunger.

Risk is highest during seasons of food scarcity and highest for isolated individuals.

### Settlement Attacks

Omnivores actively attack settlements to access food stores. A determined omnivore can break through crude fortifications and access stored grain, meat, and other provisions. Attacks can result in property damage, livestock loss, and human casualties.

---

## Settlement Integration and Defense

### Fortification Strategies

Settlements in omnivore territories employ: reinforced structures capable of withstanding omnivore impact, elevated food storage out of reach, and perimeter fencing. Active defense typically includes: trained guard animals, human sentries, and weapons capable of wounding omnivores (range weapons are essential—close combat with an omnivore is nearly invariably fatal).

### Seasonal Adaptation

Many settlements in omnivore territories practice seasonal food caching and intensified fortification during seasons of scarcity when omnivore desperation is highest.

### Professional Hunting

Some settlements maintain professional omnivore hunters. Hunting is dangerous—omnivores can inflict lethal injury rapidly, and tracking a wounded omnivore is particularly hazardous. However, successful hunters can reduce settlement predation pressure.

---

## Behavior and Intelligence

### Problem-Solving Capability

Omnivores show significant intelligence. They learn to: open containers, solve spatial puzzles, remember locations of food sources across seasons, and recognize individual humans. An omnivore that has successfully raided a settlement will return repeatedly and may show escalating sophistication in breaking through fortifications.

### Temperament and Personality

Individual omnivores show significant personality variation. Some are naturally cautious and fear-responsive; others are bold and aggressive. Some are intelligent problem-solvers; others are more rigid in behavior. Interactions with humans vary based on individual personality as much as circumstance.

### Learned Behavior and Tradition

Evidence suggests that behavior can be transmitted between omnivores—particularly from mothers to cubs. Cubs raised by omnivores that successfully raid settlements show higher propensity to attack settlements themselves. This creates lineages of "problem omnivores" that persist across generations.

---

## Interaction with Other Predators

### Relationship to Pack Hunters

Pack hunters avoid conflict with omnivores. A pack coordinated enough might potentially injure a young omnivore, but healthy adult omnivores are dangerous opposition. Most packs maintain distance from omnivores.

### Predation by Humans and Other Species

Adult omnivores have few natural predators. Packs may prey on young omnivores; other large predators may do so as well. These interactions are rare and represent minimal population impact.

### Scavenging Competition

Omnivores compete with Formica and other scavengers for carrion. An omnivore on a fresh kill will exclude most competing scavengers through sheer presence and intimidation.

---

## Reproduction and Population Dynamics

### Breeding and Maternal Behavior

Females breed at intervals of 2–3 years typically. Litters are small (1–4 cubs), and maternal investment is intensive and long—cubs remain with mothers for 1–3 years depending on species. Maternal omnivores are extremely defensive and present a serious threat to anything approaching cubs.

### Longevity and Life History

Omnivores can live 25–40+ years in the wild, with significant individual variation. Older individuals show evidence of experience and learned behavior, suggesting that knowledge accumulates across a long lifespan.

---

## Limits and Uncertainties

### Geographic Distribution

The full extent of omnivore distribution is not completely mapped. They appear to be present across most of Terra, though species diversity varies by region.

### Species Diversity

Multiple species of omnivore exist, showing significant variation in size, aggression, and feeding preferences. Generalized descriptions apply to most species, but significant variation exists.

### Hibernation and Dormancy Behavior

In cold climates, many omnivores enter extended dormancy during winter months. The physiology and duration of this dormancy varies by species, location, and individual condition.

---

## Relationship to Biomancers

Omnivores are presumed to be Terra-native lifeforms, though their size and predatory efficiency has led to speculation about imperial origins. No definitive evidence of manipulation has been established.

Some biomancers have attempted domestication of omnivores with limited success. The intelligence and predatory response of these animals make reliable control difficult.

---

## Cultural and Psychological Impact

### Symbolic Significance

Omnivores feature prominently in many Terra cultures as symbols of: power, strength, wisdom, and the wild. Hunting omnivores is a high-status activity in many warrior-oriented cultures.

### Respect and Fear

Communities that coexist with omnivores typically maintain strong frameworks of respect and fear, recognizing the animal as a powerful force that deserves caution and strategic thinking.

---

## Final Assessment

Megafauna omnivores are the most individually dangerous predators on Terra—a single animal can inflict lethal injury with minimal effort. However, they are not the most systemic threat. Their omnivorous nature means they can be avoided through relocation, and their intelligence means they can sometimes be deterred or even negotiated with (through maintaining distance and avoiding provocation).

Settlements that thrive near omnivores are settlements that have learned to fortify against incursion, provide no easy access to food, and respect the animal's space and autonomy. The omnivore does not seek conflict with humans—it seeks food and security. A settlement that makes itself a poor food source becomes progressively less interesting to the omnivore.
