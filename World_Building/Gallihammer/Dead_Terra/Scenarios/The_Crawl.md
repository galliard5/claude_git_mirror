# The Crawl

**Campaign:** Dead Terra — Remade Origin
**Setting:** Dead Terra (post-collapse Earth)
**Framework:** Remade Origin Framework
**Estimated Duration:** Open-ended (origin sequence ~2-3 sessions, then open world)
**Tone:** Gritty, psychological, espionage thriller with body horror undertones

---

## Overview

A warlord's personal assassin — an anthro operative who has done the quiet, deniable work for years — gets caught on a job. The warlord can't protect him without exposing the connection, so the sentence is death. Publicly. Cleanly. Case closed.

Except the warlord still needs his knife. And a biomancer the warlord retains has been pitching a project: an infiltration-form bioservitor, purpose-built for access-based espionage. Something that can move through infrastructure — vents, walls, drainage, the layered ruin-structure under every settlement in Dead Terra. Something with a mind sharp enough to gather intelligence and a body designed to go where no one else can.

The warlord offers the condemned man a choice: real death, or this. The execution is faked. The assassin is delivered to the biomancer's workshop. The campaign begins in the prep room, the day the procedure starts.

**Hook:** The PC has already made the choice. He's standing in the biomancer's workspace, knowing what's about to happen to him. There is no going back. The only question is what comes next.

**Core Conflict:** A man remade into a weapon for someone who already burned him once. The leash is chemical — a drug dependency the PC believes is fatal to break. The truth is more complicated. The campaign follows the PC through transformation, deployment, the slow discovery that the cage has a crack in it, and whatever he does with that knowledge.

**Resolution:** Open-ended. The PC's arc depends on whether he discovers the flaw, whether he escapes, and what he builds (or destroys) after.

## Origin Sequence (Remade Origin Framework)

### 1. Capture / Acquisition

**Method:** Criminal sentencing under coerced consent.

The PC was the warlord's personal covert operative — assassinations, intelligence gathering, the kind of work that doesn't appear on any official record. He was good at it. He got caught on a job — not through incompetence but through circumstance (the details are a Session Zero variable: betrayed by an informant, target had unexpected security, bad luck, or the warlord set him up deliberately).

The warlord could not intervene without revealing the connection. The PC was sentenced to death by whatever local authority holds jurisdiction. The warlord then arranged the execution to be faked — the body that was buried or burned was not the PC. The PC was delivered to the biomancer's workshop instead.

The "choice" was presented as voluntary: transformation or actual death. The PC chose transformation. Whether that constitutes consent is a question the biomancer has opinions about but kept to himself.

**Session Zero Variables:**
- PC's name and anthro subtype (what animal strain?)
- How long the PC worked for the warlord before the arrest
- What the failed job was — who was the target, why it mattered
- Whether the PC suspects the warlord burned him deliberately

### 2. Commission

**Patron:** [WARLORD NAME — Session Zero variable]

A regional warlord with enough territory and resources to retain a biomancer and commission custom work. Controls a settlement or cluster of settlements, has rivals, has enemies, has the kind of problems that require intelligence before they require violence.

**The commission specifications:**
- **Form:** Arthropod body plan. Multi-limbed, segmented, capable of navigating infrastructure — vents, wall cavities, drainage systems, the layered ruin-structure that underlies most Dead Terra settlements. Must be able to climb any surface, compress through structural gaps, operate in darkness and confined spaces.
- **Cognizance:** Full. The patron wants an operative, not an animal. The form must retain complete cognitive function — memory, judgment, tactical assessment, language comprehension. This is the entire point: a thinking infiltrator that can assess what it finds, prioritise intelligence, and make kill/no-kill decisions independently in the field.
- **Combat capability:** Secondary but present. The form must be able to eliminate a target quietly when required. Not a frontline combatant — an assassin's toolkit built into the body. Venomous delivery, speed, the ability to strike from positions no one expects an attack from.
- **Control:** Chemical dependency. The warlord wants an absolute leash — something that guarantees the asset returns. The biomancer proposed a metabolic dependency on a compound administered at regular intervals. The warlord was told that withdrawal is fatal within 72 hours. The PC was told the same thing.
- **Sensory suite:** Rebuilt entirely. The form doesn't see or hear the way the PC used to. Vibration sensing through substrate contact. Chemoreception — reading air composition, detecting biological signatures. Possibly limited visual capacity in a different spectrum. The PC will perceive the world in ways that have no relationship to his old senses, but the information he gets may be *better* for the infiltration role.

**Patron motivation for cognizance:** Pragmatic. The warlord sees this as an upgrade to an asset he already owned. The PC was already his knife; now the knife fits into places it couldn't before. The warlord does not think of this as cruelty. He thinks of it as problem-solving.

### 3. Reshaping

**Specialist:** [BIOMANCER NAME — Session Zero variable]

A curious craftsman. The biomancer finds this commission technically fascinating — an infiltration-optimised arthropod form with full cognitive retention is a genuine design challenge, and he's been thinking about it for longer than the warlord knows. The PC is the first live subject for a concept the biomancer has been refining on paper and in smaller experiments for years.

**The biomancer's workspace** is cluttered with the accumulated evidence of long practice — jars, cultures, specimens, equipment that has been modified past recognition. It smells wrong in a way the PC can't name. The biomancer himself shows signs of self-modification: subtle things, features that don't quite track to any single race, senses that seem sharper than they should be. He has been doing this work for a long time.

**The procedure:**
- **Duration:** Days. The framework suggests hours to weeks; this is a complex transformation that requires staged work. The biomancer will not rush it.
- **Consciousness:** The PC is conscious for prep and early stages. Sedated for the most intensive restructuring. Brought back to consciousness periodically so the biomancer can assess cognitive function and make adjustments. The moments of awareness during the process are disorienting — the body is wrong and getting wronger, the senses are shifting, nothing maps to what the PC expects.
- **Staged process:** The biomancer works in phases. Skeletal restructuring first (or rather, skeletal *dissolution* — the arthropod form doesn't use an internal skeleton). Then tissue reshaping, limb formation, sensory organ development, integument (the chitin). The chemical dependency is introduced during the process, woven into the form's metabolism as it develops.
- **The biomancer's demeanour:** Professional, absorbed, occasionally conversational in the way someone is conversational while doing something that occupies most of their attention. He talks to the PC during conscious phases — partly to assess cognitive function, partly because he's genuinely interested in the subject's experience. He asks questions a craftsman asks: *How does this feel? Can you sense the difference? What are you perceiving right now?* He is not cruel. He is not kind. He is fascinated.

### 4. The Flaw

**Type:** Chemical dependency — fabricated lethality.

The biomancer built the drug dependency exactly as commissioned. The compound is real. The dependency is real. The mild euphoria is real. What is not real is the lethality of withdrawal.

The warlord was told: *Without the compound administered every 48-72 hours, the subject's modified metabolism will cascade into systemic failure. Death within 72 hours of the last dose, no exceptions.* The PC was told the same thing during the transformation process, presented as a simple biological fact of the new form.

**The truth:** Withdrawal is severe but survivable. The symptoms are genuine and brutal — metabolic distress, sensory disruption, pain, temporary loss of some of the form's capabilities. It feels like dying. It is not dying. The form's biology will stabilise after a withdrawal period of approximately 5-7 days. Full capability returns within 2 weeks.

**Why the biomancer did it:** Not conscience. Not sympathy. Engineering philosophy. A leash that kills the asset on failure is a *bad leash* — it means any supply disruption, any logistics failure, any accident destroys something the biomancer spent days of exhausting work creating. The craftsman builds in tolerances. He builds the dependency the client asked for, and he makes it survivable because that's better design. He doesn't tell the client about the tolerance because the client didn't ask for it and wouldn't appreciate it.

The biomancer may or may not feel anything about the PC's situation. What he definitely feels is that his work should not be destroyed by a missed supply shipment.

**Discovery:** The PC does not know the truth at campaign start. Discovery of the flaw is a potential campaign arc — it could come through accidental supply interruption (a mission runs long, a shipment is delayed), through information from another biomancer or someone who understands the biology, or through the PC's own growing awareness that the dependency doesn't behave quite the way he was told it would.

### 5. Escape

**Not yet determined.** The campaign begins pre-transformation. Escape is a future event whose shape depends on play. Possible vectors per the framework:

- **Active escape:** The PC discovers the flaw (survival through withdrawal) and deliberately breaks the leash by enduring the withdrawal period away from the warlord's supply.
- **Passive failure:** Extended deployment degrades the conditioning. The PC begins to question.
- **Catastrophic event:** The warlord's territory is attacked, supply lines are cut, the PC is stranded without his dose and survives what should have killed him.
- **Discarding:** The warlord considers a mission failed and writes off the asset, not knowing the PC survived.

## The Form — Design Notes

### Body Plan

Arthropod. Multi-segmented, multi-limbed, no internal skeleton. The form uses an exoskeletal structure (chitin-analogue integument) with enough flexibility at segment joints to compress through structural gaps. Not a direct copy of any natural arthropod — the biomancer designed from function, not from reference.

**Key physical characteristics:**
- **Size:** Roughly dog-sized at full extension, but capable of compressing significantly. The segmented body can fold, flatten, and reconfigure to fit through spaces much smaller than its full profile suggests.
- **Limbs:** Multiple pairs — enough for rapid, silent movement across any surface including ceilings and vertical faces. Some limbs are specialised: gripping, cutting, manipulating small objects. Fine manipulation is possible but different from hands — the PC will need to relearn how to interact with physical objects.
- **Integument:** Chitin-analogue. Hard enough for protection, with flexible joints. Coloration is a Session Zero variable — could be dark/matte for concealment, could shift based on environment (chromatophore-adjacent capability the biomancer may have built in).
- **No recognisable face.** The form has sensory organs but they do not map to eyes-nose-mouth. The PC perceives through antennae, vibration-sensing plates, chemoreceptors. There is nothing to make eye contact with. This is deliberate — the warlord specified a form that would not be recognised as containing a person.

### Sensory Suite

The PC's old senses are gone. Replaced with:
- **Vibration sensing:** Through substrate contact (floor, wall, any solid surface). Effectively replaces hearing and provides spatial awareness through solid structures. The PC can feel footsteps through walls, sense machinery through floors, detect structural stress in the material he's moving through.
- **Chemoreception:** Air sampling through specialised antennae. Detects biological signatures (people nearby, their approximate number, possibly emotional state via stress hormones), chemical composition (atmosphere quality, toxins, accelerants), and environmental information. Replaces smell and adds capabilities beyond it.
- **Limited visual capacity:** Not eyes as the PC knew them. Light-sensitive structures that provide motion detection and gross spatial awareness in open environments. Possibly extended into infrared or ultraviolet. Detail is poor compared to the PC's old vision; the form relies on vibration and chemoreception as primary senses.

### Combat Capability

Secondary to infiltration but present:
- **Venom delivery:** One or more limbs equipped with envenomation capability. The biomancer designed multiple compounds — paralytic, lethal, and possibly a sedative variant. Delivery is through puncture, silent, fast.
- **Speed and ambush:** The form can strike from concealment with speed that exceeds human reaction time. Ceilings, wall cavities, behind structural elements — the attack comes from a direction the target is not watching.
- **Cutting/gripping:** Limb structures capable of severing soft targets. Not designed for sustained combat — designed for a single decisive action and immediate withdrawal.
- **Durability:** The chitin provides moderate physical protection but the form is not built to take hits. If a fight goes past the first few seconds, the form is in the wrong fight.

### The Drug

**Name:** [Session Zero variable — the warlord or biomancer's term for it]

**Administration:** Liquid or injectable compound, provided by the warlord's people at regular intervals (every 48-72 hours).

**Effects when taken:** Mild euphoria, metabolic stabilisation, a sense of physical wellbeing. The high is subtle — not incapacitating, not strongly pleasurable, just... a quiet rightness. Enough to notice its absence.

**Perceived withdrawal (what the PC believes):** Systemic metabolic failure. Organ shutdown. Death within 72 hours.

**Actual withdrawal:** Severe metabolic distress (cramping, tremors, sensory disruption). Loss of some capabilities (venom production slows, chitin integrity may temporarily weaken, senses become unreliable). Intense discomfort verging on agony for the first 48-72 hours. Gradual stabilisation over days 3-5. Near-full recovery by day 7-10. Complete capability restoration by day 14.

**The experience of withdrawal** should feel like the PC is dying, especially in the early hours. Everything the warlord told him appears to be true. The moment when the dying *stops* — when the body stabilises despite everything the PC was told would happen — is the moment the leash breaks.

## Key NPCs

### The Warlord — [NAME TBD]

**Role:** Patron. The PC's owner, employer, and the person who arranged his fake execution.

- *Motivation:* Pragmatic power consolidation. The warlord has rivals, enemies, and territory to protect. An infiltration-form bioservitor is an intelligence asset no one else in the region has. The warlord sees the PC as an investment, not a person.
- *Relationship to PC:* Pre-existing. The PC has worked for the warlord for years. There may be something resembling mutual respect buried under the power dynamic — or there may not. The warlord burned the PC when it was convenient and offered transformation as a favour. Whether he sees the irony is a Session Zero question.
- *Patron motivation for cognizance:* Pragmatic. A thinking infiltrator is worth more than a biological drone. The warlord does not consider the ethical dimension.
- *Pursuit tendency:* If the PC escapes, the warlord will pursue — not obsessively, but methodically. The PC knows too much about the warlord's operations, and a loose bioservitor with full cognizance and assassination capability is a threat that cannot be ignored.

### The Biomancer — [NAME TBD]

**Role:** Specialist. The craftsman who performs the transformation.

- *Motivation:* Technical fascination. This commission is the most interesting design problem he's had in years. An infiltration-optimised arthropod with full cognitive retention pushes his capabilities in directions he finds genuinely exciting. The PC is a subject, a project, and — during conscious phases — a source of data about how the experience feels from the inside.
- *Attitude:* Curious craftsman. Not cruel, not kind. Professional in a way that is both reassuring and unsettling. He cares about the quality of his work more than he cares about the PC's feelings about it. He will answer questions honestly when they're about the procedure. He will deflect questions about the ethics because they're not interesting to him.
- *The flaw:* Built in the survivable withdrawal out of engineering philosophy, not conscience. May or may not resurface later in the campaign. If he does, his interest in the PC will be professional — how is the form performing? What has the PC discovered about its capabilities? Has the design held up?
- *Self-modification:* Visible. The biomancer has been practicing long enough that he doesn't look entirely like whatever he started as. This should be apparent to the PC during their interactions.

### The Handler — [NAME TBD, Optional]

**Role:** The warlord's representative who manages the PC's deployments and administers the drug.

- *Motivation:* Follows orders. May have personal feelings about the PC (respect, contempt, discomfort, indifference) that colour the operational relationship.
- *Function:* Provides mission briefings, administers the drug, debriefs the PC after deployments. The handler is the PC's primary human contact during active service. The quality of this relationship matters.

## Key Locations

### The Biomancer's Workshop

Where the campaign begins. A fixed workspace in or near the warlord's territory — possibly in an old ruin the biomancer has claimed, possibly in a facility the warlord provides. Cluttered, smells wrong, filled with the accumulated evidence of years of practice. This is where the transformation happens.

- *Key details:* The workshop is both a medical facility and an artist's studio. The equipment is a mix of understood technology and things the biomancer has built or modified himself. There are specimens — finished work, failed work, experiments in progress. The PC will see what the biomancer does before the biomancer does it to him.
- *Atmosphere:* Quiet. Controlled. The biomancer works best without interruption. The warlord's people are not welcome in the workspace during procedures.

### The Warlord's Territory — [LOCATION TBD]

The settlement or stronghold the warlord controls. The PC knows this place — he's operated from here for years. After transformation, he'll see it from inside the walls, literally. The infrastructure he'll navigate during deployments starts here, in familiar territory seen from an alien perspective.

### Deployment Targets — [DEVELOPED IN PLAY]

Rival settlements, enemy strongholds, contested territory. The places the warlord sends the PC to gather intelligence or remove problems. Each deployment is a potential session or arc.

## Main Plot Arc

### Prologue: The Prep Room (Campaign Start)

The PC arrives at the biomancer's workshop. He has been "executed" — as far as anyone outside the warlord's inner circle knows, he is dead. He is here because the alternative was being dead for real.

**Key scenes:**
- The PC sees the workspace. The specimens. The tools. The biomancer.
- The biomancer conducts a pre-procedure assessment — physical examination, questions about the PC's history, explanation of what's about to happen. This is the PC's last opportunity to ask questions with a human mouth.
- The warlord (or the handler) may be present briefly to confirm the commission and explain the terms: the drug, the deployment expectations, the chain of command. Then they leave. The biomancer doesn't want them in the room.
- The PC and the biomancer have a conversation. The biomancer is honest about the procedure. He may not be honest about everything.

**Player choices:**
- What questions does the PC ask?
- How does the PC approach this — resigned, angry, terrified, clinical?
- Does the PC try to negotiate any aspect of the transformation?
- What is the last thing the PC does with his old body?

### Act 1: The Reshaping

The transformation itself. Played through selected conscious moments — the PC is not aware for the entire procedure, but the moments of awareness are the campaign's first test of how the player inhabits the character.

**Key beats:**
- First awareness after initial restructuring — the body is wrong, the senses are changing, the biomancer's voice is the only familiar thing
- A conscious assessment phase where the biomancer tests cognitive function — conversation, memory, problem-solving — while the PC is mid-transformation
- The introduction of the drug — the biomancer explains the dependency as a biological fact, administers the first dose, and the PC feels the quiet euphoria for the first time
- Completion — the PC is aware, fully transformed, inhabiting a body that bears no resemblance to what he had. Learning to move. Learning to perceive.

### Act 2: Early Deployment

The PC is operational. The warlord begins using him. First missions — initially in familiar territory (the warlord's own settlement, testing capabilities) then escalating to rival targets.

**Key threads:**
- Learning the form's capabilities and limits through use
- The drug cycle — regular administration, the subtle euphoria, the awareness of dependency
- The handler relationship — who manages the PC between missions
- Intelligence gathering that reveals things about the warlord's operations the PC didn't know before (from inside the walls, you see things differently)
- Encountering other bioservitors, other escaped or discarded forms, signs that the PC is not the only thing living in the infrastructure

### Act 3: The Crack

The control begins to show its weakness. How this happens depends on play, but possible triggers include:

- A mission runs long and the drug supply is delayed — the PC experiences early withdrawal symptoms and survives past when he should have died
- The PC encounters another biomancer or someone with relevant knowledge who questions the lethality claim
- The PC's own growing understanding of his biology contradicts what he was told
- The biomancer resurfaces with information, a request, or a warning

**This is the turning point.** The PC discovers — or begins to suspect — that the leash is not what he was told it is. What he does with that knowledge is the rest of the campaign.

### Act 4+: Open World

Per the Remade Origin Framework's post-origin structure. The PC is loose (or choosing to remain), aware of the flaw (or suspecting it), carrying a body built for a purpose he may or may not have rejected. Campaign hooks from the framework apply:

- The warlord hunts
- Other escaped bioservitors exist in the margins
- The biomancer has his own agenda
- The body continues to reveal new capabilities and new horrors
- Reversal — is it possible? Does the PC want it?
- The system that made the PC is still running — other patrons, other specialists, other subjects

## Session Zero Checklist

Per the Remade Origin Framework, establish before play:

1. **Who were you before?** — Name, anthro subtype, background before the warlord's service. How long in the warlord's employ. What kind of work.
2. **Who bought you?** — The warlord's name, territory, personality. The nature of the working relationship before the arrest. Was there trust? Respect? Pure transaction?
3. **What did they want?** — Established above (arthropod infiltration form). Any additional specifications the player wants to add or the warlord would have requested.
4. **Who did the work?** — The biomancer's name, appearance, personality details beyond "curious craftsman." What does the workshop look like? What does the PC notice?
5. **What's the flaw?** — Known to the GM. The player may choose to know OOC that the withdrawal isn't lethal, or may choose to discover it in play. Discuss preference.
6. **The failed job** — What was the mission that got the PC caught? Who was the target? Why did it fail? Does the PC blame the warlord?
7. **The drug** — What does the warlord call it? What does the first dose feel like? How does the PC relate to the dependency?

## Mechanics & Rules Notes

**Transformation sequence:** Run narratively, not mechanically. The focus is on the experience, not the stats. Mechanical character creation (if applicable) happens after transformation is complete — the PC's capabilities are the form's capabilities.

**Sensory translation:** The PC's rebuilt senses provide equivalent or superior information to his old ones, but the *experience* is alien. For gameplay, the PC can still "see" and "hear" — but the player should be encouraged to describe perception in terms of the new senses (vibration, chemical signatures, substrate contact) rather than defaulting to visual/auditory language.

**The drug cycle:** Track doses. The drug is administered every 48-72 hours. Missed doses should be noted. Early withdrawal symptoms begin at 72+ hours: mild discomfort, sensory instability. The PC (and player) will interpret these as the onset of fatal withdrawal. They are not.

**Infiltration mechanics:** The form's access capability is the primary mechanical feature. The PC can navigate infrastructure that is physically inaccessible to unmodified people. This includes vertical surfaces, confined spaces, and structural interiors. The limitation is *time* — moving through walls is not instant — and *detection* — the PC is quiet but not silent, and vibration-sensitive security (if any exists in the target location) may register movement.
