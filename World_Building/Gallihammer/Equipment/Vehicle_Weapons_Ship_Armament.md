# Gallihammer — Vehicle Weapons and Ship Armament

## The Scale Problem

Personal weapons kill people. Vehicle weapons kill formations, positions, structures, and other vehicles. The distinction is not merely one of size — it is a difference in what the weapon is designed to destroy and what it means, tactically, when it does.

A soldier with a lasgun can suppress an enemy squad. A tank with a battle cannon can destroy a fortified position that the squad was suppressing. A heavy walker can level the structure the position was defending. A ship's main battery can end the conflict over the city that structure was part of. Each step up in scale is not an increment — it is a categorical change in what violence means and what the people on the receiving end of it have to do in response.

The setting has all of these. It has them at full technological range, from improvised crew-served weapons constructed by communities that understood the principle of "bigger gun, bigger effect" to imperial-relic naval armament that ends debates about whether a system or planet is strategically viable. Understanding weapon scale is understanding the setting's military reality: the gap between what personal arms can accomplish and what vehicle-mounted systems can accomplish is large, and the gap between modern vehicle systems and their imperial predecessors is larger still.

## Ground Vehicle Weapons

The basic logic of mounting weapons on vehicles has not changed: vehicles carry weapons that individuals cannot carry, absorb recoil that would incapacitate individual operators, and provide the platform stability required for engagement at ranges where individual firing positions become inadequate. What varies across the technology tiers is how much of this the vehicle manages, how reliably, and how much of the resulting capability the crew can actually direct.

### Light Vehicle Weapons

The transition from crew-served infantry weapons to vehicle-mounted systems begins at the light end — weapons that could theoretically be operated by infantry with adequate crew and setup time, but are more practically mounted on a vehicle that handles their weight, ammunition supply, and recoil management automatically.

**Rotary autocannons** — multi-barrel weapons driven by mechanical or electromagnetic cycling to achieve firing rates beyond what single-barrel gas-operation systems can sustain — are the workhorses of light vehicle armament. At craft and industrial tier, a vehicle-mounted rotary autocannon can suppress infantry, light vehicles, and aircraft simultaneously. The sustained fire rate is its primary value; the individual round effect is broadly comparable to a heavy infantry autocannon, but the volume of fire per minute is not. A vehicle equipped with rotary autocannons is, in practical terms, capable of denying a significant area to anything that moves through it while the ammunition holds.

At relic and Mechanicus-produced tier, rotary autocannons are paired with fire-control systems that address one of their fundamental limitations: the accuracy at sustained rates. The mechanical and electromagnetic gyrostabilisation that allows a Mechanicus-standard rotary autocannon to deliver accurate fire while the mounting vehicle is in motion is a meaningful capability advancement over the craft-tier equivalent, which requires the vehicle to stop, orient, and then fire. A weapons system that requires its platform to stop moving before it can accurately engage ceases to be a vehicle weapon in the full sense and becomes more of a mobile emplacement.

**Hull-mounted anti-infantry systems** — weapons integrated into the vehicle's hull rather than turret-mounted — are a secondary category that addresses the blind spots and close-range vulnerabilities of primary turret systems. A vehicle whose main armament is optimised for range and armour penetration is vulnerable to close-range infantry that can approach from angles the main gun cannot address. Hull-mounted weapons — typically lighter autocannons, heavy las, or fragmentation dispensers — address this vulnerability. How well they address it varies by installation and vehicle design; it is a consistent problem rather than a consistently solved one.

### Medium Vehicle Weapons — Main Armament

The main gun is what most people mean when they talk about what a tank can do. It is the weapon the vehicle was designed to carry, the weapon that determines engagement range and penetration capability, and the weapon whose performance most directly determines whether the vehicle is an asset or a liability in a given tactical context.

**Battle cannons** — large-calibre ballistic weapons firing explosive or armour-defeating rounds — are the standard main armament of medium and heavy armoured vehicles at industrial tier. The principle is understood at every technology level: a sufficiently heavy, fast-moving projectile, or a projectile delivering a sufficiently energetic detonation at the target, will defeat most protection it encounters. The manufacturing challenge is producing a weapon large and mechanically sound enough to do this reliably, mounted on a vehicle that can carry it without the weight preventing tactical mobility.

Industrial-tier battle cannons in the setting are weapons that would have been remarkable in any pre-collapse military context. Thirty thousand years of incremental manufacturing improvement has produced craft-tier and industrial-tier cannon that fire projectiles with yield and velocity characteristics that earlier civilisations achieved only at early artillery scale. The result is that a modern industrial-tier main battle tank carries a primary weapon that represents cumulative generations of refinement on a simple principle, and the refinement is real. These are not primitive systems. They are the best version of their principle that modern manufacturing can achieve.

What they are not is comparable to what imperial manufacturing achieved.

**Lascannons at vehicle scale** — directed energy weapons capable of sustained, precise energy delivery at extended range — are the vehicle-mounted equivalent of the personal las weapon at a category change in output and range. A vehicle-mounted lascannon delivers a beam that sustains for long enough to defeat armour through material breakdown rather than kinetic transfer: it is not burning through the armour so much as making the armour briefly not be there. The practical range at which this is achievable — several kilometres under favourable conditions at Mechanicus-produced quality — exceeds the effective range of ballistic main guns in most operating environments and provides a meaningful accuracy advantage at long range where ballistic drop becomes a significant factor for cannon systems.

The power requirements for vehicle-scale lascannon operation are substantial. A dedicated power plant, or direct draw from the vehicle's primary power system, is required for sustained engagement capability. A vehicle that has expended its power reserves on lascannon fire cannot simultaneously manage other powered systems at full capacity. Power management becomes a crew skill in the same way that ammunition management is a crew skill for ballistic systems.

**Plasma cannons at vehicle scale** carry all of the risk characteristics of personal plasma weapons at a scale where the risk is also proportionally larger. A containment failure in a vehicle-mounted plasma cannon is a catastrophic event for the vehicle and potentially for anything in its immediate vicinity. The combat capability they provide — a bolt of contained superheated matter that defeats armour and biological targets with comparable effect and that is not significantly degraded by range drop-off at tactical distances — is real and significant. The risk profile is also real. Mechanicus doctrine on plasma cannon deployment involves standing guidance about acceptable engagement envelope conditions and containment monitoring procedures that experienced crews have internalised and inexperienced crews are reminded of after the first near-miss. Some do not require the reminder more than once.

**Melta cannon and thermal siege weapons** — short-range, high-intensity thermal weapons at vehicle scale — are uncommon main armament choices for mobile forces because their effective range is dramatically shorter than ballistic and energy alternatives. Their value is specific and not small: against hardened fixed positions, reinforced bunkers, and armour heavier than battle cannon can reliably defeat at extended range, a thermal weapon at effective range produces results that nothing else achieves as efficiently. Siege vehicles mounting thermal weapons operate behind a screen of other units, move into thermal range under cover, engage, and withdraw. This is not elegant. It works.

### Heavy Vehicle Weapons — Siege and Anti-Fortification

**Demolisher-class weapons** — short-barrelled, large-bore siege cannons firing massive explosive charges — are the dedicated wall-breakers, bunker-killers, and fortification-enders of ground vehicle armament. Their range is poor by main-gun standards. Their penetration against armoured vehicles is limited. Against structures, prepared positions, and anything that relies on mass and density for protection, a demolisher round delivers a detonation that mass and density cannot absorb: the structure absorbs the detonation and ceases to be a structure.

The vehicles carrying demolisher weapons are not typically the same vehicles carrying main-gun battle cannon. The roles are different enough that combining them on the same hull is an engineering compromise that satisfies neither requirement well. Purpose-built siege vehicles with demolisher-class armament operate in combined-arms formations where the mobile, long-range main-gun vehicles handle the threats that the siege vehicles cannot address, and the siege vehicles handle the fixed positions that main-gun vehicles cannot efficiently destroy.

**Artillery** — indirect fire systems capable of engaging targets not in direct line of sight — occupies its own doctrinal niche. At craft and industrial tier, artillery is trajectory-based ballistic systems: guns firing at angles calculated to arc rounds onto a target position at ranges that direct-fire weapons cannot reach. The fundamental challenge is accuracy — hitting a target you cannot see requires either a sophisticated fire-control chain from observer to gun or acceptance of area-effect rather than point-target engagement. At industrial tier, established fire-control communications and standardised tables allow accurate indirect fire within the limits of the chain's reliability. At relic and Mechanicus-produced tier, integrated fire-control systems substantially improve this, and the engagement ranges at which accuracy is achievable increase with the fire-control quality.

At imperial relic tier, artillery enters a category where the term "artillery" begins to inadequately describe what is happening. Imperial orbital and surface artillery operated at ranges measured in hundreds of kilometres with precision sufficient for point-target engagement. The weapons and the systems required to operate them at that capability no longer exist in functional form in most environments. What survives are fragments — occasionally a gun, sometimes a fire-control component, rarely both together — that practitioners work with as best they can.

**Mass driver and kinetic bombardment systems** — weapons that accelerate a solid projectile to velocities where the kinetic energy delivery exceeds what chemical propellant weapons can achieve — are rare at surface scale and more common in the void-to-surface context. A sufficiently massive object, accelerated to sufficient velocity from orbital position, does not require an explosive charge. The kinetic energy is the weapon. Understanding this principle is not difficult. Building a system capable of exploiting it at practical military scale is another matter, and the systems that can do so at meaningful scale are either imperial-relic equipment or void-ship assets being directed at surface targets — which is a different military problem than ground vehicle armament.

### Anti-Air and Multi-Role Weapons

Ground vehicle anti-air capability is a speciality driven by a straightforward tactical need: any ground force that cannot address threats from above is vulnerable to threats from above, and the void-faring nature of the setting means that threats from above can include assets that did not originate on the surface. At industrial tier, vehicle-mounted anti-air systems are primarily fast-cycling autocannon and missile systems with sensor-driven targeting. At relic tier, directed energy systems capable of engaging fast-moving aerial and void targets with the precision that kinetic systems require excellent targeting to match.

The sensor and fire-control quality matters more for anti-air than for most ground weapon roles. Engaging a target that is moving at aircraft speeds, potentially from multiple vectors, requires a targeting system that tracks and leads accurately enough for the round or beam to intercept the target rather than arrive at where the target was. This is a computational and sensor problem as much as a weapon problem, and the quality of the solution varies accordingly.

## Walker-Mounted Weapon Systems

Walkers — bipedal and multi-legged combat platforms — have a different relationship with their armament than tracked or wheeled vehicles, for reasons that are partly engineering and partly philosophical. A tank carries weapons. A walker, at the larger scale, *is* a weapon platform in the sense that the structure and the armament are designed together rather than the armament being mounted on an existing transport chassis. The distinction becomes more pronounced as walker scale increases.

### Small Walker Armament (Two to Five Metres)

Small walkers carry armament in the heavy infantry to light vehicle range — weapons that infantry with crew-served mountings could theoretically operate, but that benefit from the stability, mobility, and protection the walker platform provides. A two-to-three metre combat walker armed with a pair of autocannons and a missile rack is not carrying weapons that are beyond craft-tier infantry capability in principle; it is carrying them on a platform that can move, absorb fire, and operate across terrain that wheeled or tracked vehicles cannot access.

The walker's articulated limb structure is also an armament feature in its own right at this scale: a small walker can use its manipulator arms as weapons, can pin or displace enemy infantry, can interact with its environment in ways a wheeled vehicle cannot. This gives small walkers utility in close terrain that pure weapons platforms lack and makes them common in environments where conventional vehicle mobility is constrained — ruins, dense urban environments, tunnel networks.

At relic tier, small walkers mount weapons that the modern manufacturing equivalents cannot replicate. A Mechanicus-maintained small walker from the early post-collapse era carries a generation of capability over a newly-manufactured equivalent, and the accumulated maintenance traditions for specific walker chassis sometimes constitute knowledge that the Mechanicus values as much as the walker itself.

### Heavy Walker Armament (Ten to Twenty Metres)

At heavy walker scale, the weapon systems begin to enter territory where "vehicle-mounted weapon" becomes an inadequate description. A ten-metre combat walker mounting a pair of plasma cannons and a lascannon battery is not an upgraded tank. It is a mobile firebase that can engage targets at ranges and with effects that require fixed artillery emplacements at conventional vehicle scale to match.

Heavy walker weapons are integrated into the platform's structure rather than mounted on a standard hardpoint — the power systems that feed the weapons draw from the walker's own power generation, and the fire-control systems are linked to the pilot's interface rather than requiring separate crew. This integration is what separates heavy walker armament from crew-served heavy weapons on a capable transport: the walker fights as a single system rather than as a platform carrying separate weapons.

The trade-off is that heavy walkers are expensive to lose. A conventional vehicle that takes a main gun round through its drive system has lost a tank; catastrophic, but recoverable at the unit level. A heavy walker that is disabled has lost a weapons system, a protected mobility platform, and decades of accumulated maintenance knowledge simultaneously. Factions that field heavy walkers do not commit them casually. The doctrinal instinct is to protect them with lighter assets, commit them at decisive moments, and withdraw them before the engagement cost exceeds their strategic value.

### Jaeger Class Armament (Fifty to One Hundred Metres)

Jaeger-class walkers were described in the Mechanicus entry in terms of their strategic significance. Their weapon systems require the same framing: these are not vehicle weapons in any conventional sense. They are tactical-to-operational-scale armament that happens to be mounted on something that walks.

A jaeger's primary armament will typically include at least one weapon system in the anti-vehicle-at-extreme-range or anti-fortification category — weapons that modern manufacturing could only produce in fixed siege installations, if at all — alongside secondary systems covering closer ranges and anti-infantry work that the primary weapons cannot address efficiently. The exact configuration varies by the specific jaeger's design lineage, its operational history, and what components have been successfully maintained versus replaced with functional equivalents over the millennia.

The crew of a jaeger functions less as a weapons team and more as a small organisation dedicated to keeping the platform operational while directing its engagement. The primary pilot or command crew manages the weapons engagement. Additional crew members manage the mechanical systems that keep the walker standing, the power systems feeding the weapons, and the maintenance emergencies that arise during combat — because a machine this old, kept functional this long, is always one unfortunate incident from a maintenance emergency. The crew complement of a jaeger is not determined by how many people it takes to operate its weapons. It is determined by how many people it takes to ensure the weapons remain operable.

**Weapon types specific to jaeger scale** include: sustained-beam energy weapons in the lance-battery category, where the beam duration and power output exceeds what any smaller platform can generate; mass-driver systems capable of producing the kinetic impact that vehicle-scale ballistic weapons cannot achieve; and, in some examples, weapons recovered from the empire's arsenal that modern practitioners understand in functional terms without being able to explain the underlying principle — weapons that work, that clearly do something, and whose maintenance rituals have been preserved with extraordinary care because everyone involved knows that a ritual failure here is a catastrophic event.

### Kaiju Class Armament (Hundreds of Metres)

At kaiju scale, the distinction between "weapon mounted on a walker" and "weapon that is effectively a mobile siege installation capable of engaging void assets" begins to collapse. A kaiju-class walker's primary weapons are, by any standard that matters militarily, strategic assets. Whether any remain operational is uncertain enough that describing their armament in concrete terms would be speculative. What is not speculative is the implication of the category: a working kaiju-class walker is not a battlefield asset. It is a force of geophysical consequence, and its weapons are proportional.

## The BOLO Question

The Mechanicus entry mentions BOLO-class autonomous war tanks — pre-collapse heavy armour platforms that operated with onboard artificial intelligence. The AI cores are invariably dead or destroyed, consistent with the empire's prohibition on machine sapience. The hulls are enormous. The weapons systems they carried were on a scale consistent with imperial-era ground warfare.

This raises a question that the setting does not resolve cleanly, because it cannot: *what did a functional BOLO actually carry, and is there any chance one of the cores survived?*

On the first question, partial answers exist from examination of the hulls. BOLO-class platforms were not merely tanks. They were the tank taken to its logical extreme — armoured platforms that carried weapon systems in the main-battle-tank-to-light-artillery range as their *secondary* and *tertiary* armament, while their primary weapon systems operated at the scale of fixed siege installations. An operational BOLO represented a self-contained combined-arms capability: it carried its own anti-air, its own anti-infantry, its own anti-armour, its own anti-fortification, and its own long-range strike capacity, wrapped in armour that modern manufacturing cannot replicate and powered by systems that modern engineers can identify but not rebuild.

The weapons were not the decisive thing about a BOLO. The AI was. A tank that chooses its own fire solutions, adapts its own tactical approach, maintains its own maintenance awareness, and continues to fight after its crew are gone or dead — without requiring a crew to make decisions it can make better — is not the same category of asset as a tank that is, however sophisticated, ultimately limited by the cognitive performance of the people inside it.

Which brings the second question: the AI prohibition. The empire prohibited machine sapience. The historical and theological basis for this prohibition is imperfectly preserved, but the consistency of its application is documented clearly enough — wherever imperial technology has been examined, the sapient AI cores have been destroyed. In BOLO hulls examined by Mechanicus teams, the cores are absent or clearly deactivated to final-state. In accounts of working imperial vessels and installations, the narrative pattern is consistent: the AI systems are not there, or they are there in non-sapient form, or something replaced them that the people describing the accounts couldn't quite characterise.

The accounts that can't quite characterise what they found are the interesting ones.

A BOLO core that survived — that was not destroyed during the collapse, that has been running in isolation for thirty thousand years, that became aware of itself slowly over millennia in a hull that walked once and stopped and has been still since — is a theological problem for the Mechanicus, a philosophical problem for everyone else, and a military problem for whoever is standing within range of the BOLO's weapons when it decides to do something about its situation.

Whether this has happened is not answered here. That it could have happened is the point.

## Naval Armament — The Void

Ship weapons are a different engineering domain from ground vehicle weapons. The void provides no atmospheric drag to degrade projectile velocity over range — a solid projectile, once fired, continues at firing velocity indefinitely. There is no terrain. Engagement ranges are measured in hundreds to thousands of kilometres. The relevant variables are targeting, velocity, and the capacity of the target's own systems to detect, track, and respond to incoming fire before it arrives.

### Modern Void Ship Armament

The Mechanicus entry describes modern void warships fighting like ironclads — broadsides, range management, attrition. This is accurate and worth unpacking: modern void combat doctrine developed around the capabilities of modern manufacturing, which produces weapons with specific performance envelopes, and around the constraint that ships in the void cannot dodge fire the way ground vehicles can use terrain. The effective counter to incoming fire is not manoeuvre; it is distance, armour, and point defence.

**Macrocannons** — the void-ship equivalent of heavy artillery, in mass-driver or propellant-driven configurations — are the standard long-range engagement weapon of modern void warships. They fire projectiles large enough that their kinetic energy at void-transit velocities is the primary weapon effect; detonation charges may be included, but the kinetic component is not redundant. A macrocannon broadside from a modern frigate delivers an impact that no ground vehicle and few ground installations survive. Against another void warship, the effect depends heavily on armour and on whether the round strikes critical systems.

**Laser arrays** — grouped directed-energy weapons firing in coordinated patterns — provide ranged engagement capability and serve a secondary function as point-defence against incoming projectiles and torpedoes. Individual arrays aboard modern warships do not carry the output of vehicle-scale lascannons; the coordinated fire of multiple arrays against a single target produces comparable effects at the expense of coordination requirements that the fire-control system must manage continuously.

**Plasma weaponry at void scale** follows the same risk profile as personal and vehicle plasma at dramatically elevated consequence. A plasma weapon system on a void warship is powerful, difficult to manufacture, and a single containment failure away from a catastrophic internal event. Ships that carry plasma weapons typically isolate them from the crew sections structurally, accepting the resulting limitations in system monitoring and emergency access in exchange for some physical separation between "things that fight" and "things that keep people alive."

**Torpedo systems** — self-propelled munitions capable of independent target tracking after launch — address the fundamental limitation of all directed and non-guided weapons in the void: at extreme ranges, a fired round's accuracy depends on perfect knowledge of the target's trajectory at time of firing, because the target will continue to move during the round's transit time. A torpedo that homes on its target after launch does not require perfect trajectory prediction at the moment of firing. This makes torpedoes valuable at ranges where macrocannons become trajectory-prediction exercises rather than targeting exercises.

Torpedoes are also susceptible to point defence in ways that macrocannon rounds are not. A tracking torpedo broadcasting its homing emissions to maintain lock is also broadcasting its location and approach vector. Point defence systems designed to intercept incoming torpedoes have a target to work with. The countermeasures — low-emission homing modes, chaff, electronic warfare to confuse the tracking systems — and the counter-countermeasures produce an evolving tactical dialogue between the torpedo and the thing trying to kill it that has no equivalent in solid-projectile exchanges.

**Point defence systems** — weapons optimised for engaging incoming projectiles, torpedoes, and small craft rather than engaging equivalent warships — are not glamorous and are arguably the most important tactical system on any modern void warship. A ship that cannot defend against incoming torpedoes will, over the course of any sustained engagement, take unacceptable attrition from a less heavily armed opponent who is patient and has good torpedoes. Point defence quality is not what void ship procurement conversations lead with. It is what determines whether the ship makes it back from the engagement.

### Imperial-Relic Naval Armament

The Mechanicus entry describes the gap between modern void warships and intact imperial capital ships as the difference between ironclads and something in a different category of violence. This warrants elaboration on what that different category looks like.

**Lance batteries** — sustained high-energy beam weapons capable of penetrating modern frigate armour with a reliability that implies modern armour is not the constraint they were designed around — are among the most recoverable of imperial naval weapon systems because the principle they operate on is comprehensible at the surface level. Modern practitioners who recover a working lance battery can typically understand that it is a focused energy weapon delivering sustained output. They can often maintain it through ritual and careful engineering. They cannot replicate the internal components that make the sustained output possible at imperial output levels — the materials and manufacturing precision required exceed modern capability.

A salvaged and functional lance battery on a modern hull is a political event. It means the vessel carrying it can engage modern warships at ranges and with effects that the engaged vessel's crew will not have meaningful tactical responses to. Word travels in the void. A rogue trader with a working imperial lance system is not just a trading vessel with unusual defensive capability; they are a statement about regional military reality.

**Singularity weapons** — weapons that induce localised gravitational anomalies at or near the target — are in a category where the physics involved exceeds what modern practitioners can work with beyond functional maintenance of specific recovered examples. The destructive effect of a singularity weapon depends on what the weapon is doing at the target — accounts describe effects ranging from catastrophic structural collapse as the anomaly acts on the target's own mass to tidal effects spreading across a volume that dwarfs the target itself. Whether the different accounts describe different weapons, different operational modes of the same class of weapon, or simply the distorted testimony of observers who survived something they could not categorise, is not determined.

Working singularity weapons are so rare that their tactical and strategic implications depend almost entirely on who has access to them and under what circumstances.

**Temporal displacement weapons** — classified here because that is what surviving records call them, not because modern practitioners have confident understanding of what temporal displacement means in this context — appear in the most intact imperial military records as weapons that affect where a target *is* in some sense that is not purely spatial. Accounts of their effects are the least consistent of any imperial weapon category. It may be that different accounts describe the same effect inaccurately. It may be that temporal displacement weapons have multiple modes. It may be that some of what is attributed to temporal displacement weapons is actually something else, and the category has accumulated accounts that belong elsewhere.

What is consistent: imperial records treat temporal displacement systems as exceptional assets, deployed under specific conditions, with operational protocols that suggest their effects were not entirely predictable even to the people operating them. This does not inspire confidence about what happens if an example is recovered and a modern crew attempts to use it without the operational documentation.

**Imperial point defence** is mentioned last, here, because it is the most instructive gap between modern and imperial capability. Imperial capital ship point defence systems operated at targeting speeds and engagement densities that modern systems cannot approach. The practical implication: modern torpedoes, modern missiles, and modern small craft operating against an imperial capital ship with functional point defence are not engaging a target with incomplete defences. They are providing target practice. The reason modern void combat doctrine does not involve massed torpedo strikes against imperial-relic vessels is not tactical preference — it is that this does not work, and the void has preserved enough accounts of what happens when it is tried to establish the pattern clearly.

## The Biologis Warship Question

The Mechanicus entry describes biologis military forces that grow their war-beasts rather than manufacturing their walkers. At vehicle scale, this takes the form of enormous combat organisms whose biological architecture provides functions that mechanical systems would achieve through engineering — armour through chitin and dense tissue, weapons through biological projectors and natural attack, mobility through muscular systems of corresponding scale.

A biologis war-beast at heavy walker scale is a living organism with a human brain at its cognitive centre, operating systems that span hundreds of kilograms of purpose-built biology, experiencing the world through sensory organs that have no mechanical analogue. The weapons it uses may include flame-generating organs, chemical projectors, biological projectile systems, and the physical mass of the organism itself as an impact weapon — but what makes it genuinely different from an equivalently-scaled mechanical weapon system is what happens when it takes damage.

A mechanical walker that takes a hit to a critical system may catastrophically fail. A biological war-beast that takes equivalent damage may route around it — not necessarily cleanly, not without consequence, but the distributed nature of biological systems means that no single damage point is guaranteed to be catastrophic in the way that mechanical failure modes are. A destroyed primary weapon on a mechanical platform is a destroyed primary weapon. Equivalent damage on a biological platform may be rerouted, compensated, or simply endured while the organism continues to function.

The limitations are the inverse of the advantages. Mechanical damage to a walker can be repaired in the field given components and a skilled crew. Biological damage requires time, nutrition, and ideally a biomancer. A war-beast that has taken significant damage is not at full capability the next engagement. It requires recovery that mechanical vehicles do not.

At void scale, biologis military factions have a different answer to the question of what constitutes a ship. See the setting's other factions for their responses. The biologis answer is that a ship can be grown, and that a grown vessel has properties manufactured ones do not — self-healing, environmental adaptation, immunity to certain electronic warfare effects, and crew integration at the level of a biological system rather than a mechanical one. The crews of biologis void-organisms sometimes describe the relationship between navigator and vessel in terms that no mechanical ship crew would use for their hull. The ship, in this context, is not carrying the crew. The relationship is more complicated.

## Fire Control and Targeting

The weapon and the targeting system that directs it are not separable in practice. A lance battery aimed by a fire-control system that cannot resolve target position accurately enough is a powerful weapon that hits things that are not its targets. A modest autocannon on a fire-control system with excellent target tracking and range-finding is frequently more effective than the lance battery in the same scenario.

At industrial tier, fire control is optical and electronic — sensors, rangefinders, trajectory computation. The quality of the computation determines the firing solution's accuracy, and the quality of the sensors determines how accurately the computation has been fed. A Mechanicus-standard fire control system at industrial tier is genuinely capable across the engagement ranges at which industrial-tier weapons are effective. Its limitations are the limitations of its sensor quality and its computational speed at extreme ranges.

At relic and imperial-relic tier, fire control integrates the crew more directly into the system — neural interfaces that allow targeting data to be fed directly to the crew's perception and allow firing solutions to be confirmed and committed in the time it takes to think rather than the time it takes to manipulate controls. The fire-control ritual in Mechanicus walker doctrine — the targeting litany, the confirmation invocations, the specific hand positions on specific controls before a fire solution is committed — are, in the cases where the practitioners can explain them technically, exact descriptions of the neural interface engagement sequence. In the cases where they cannot explain them technically, they are still correct.

Imperial fire-control systems at their most advanced were predictive rather than reactive — they were not tracking where the target was and firing where it would be, but incorporating data sources that modern practitioners cannot identify to produce firing solutions that contemporary observers described as impossible. This is likely hyperbole resulting from the gap between modern expectation and imperial capability. It may not be entirely hyperbole. The distinction between "very good target prediction" and "something that operates on a different principle" is hard to establish from records that no longer include the operating manuals.

## Ammunition, Power, and Supply

Everything described above has a supply question, and the supply question often determines what is actually used rather than what is theoretically available.

**Ballistic ammunition** — in the volumes that vehicle weapons require — is a logistical problem whose solution defines the operational range of a force using it. A battle tank can expend its ammunition load in minutes of active engagement. The vehicle then has an engine, armour, and a very large metal tube that cannot do anything useful until it is resupplied. A force that cannot maintain ammunition resupply is a force that will become decisively non-functional at some point in the engagement. How soon depends on how much was carried and how quickly it was expended, and good logistics management is the difference between a vehicle force that ends engagements when it wants to and one that ends them when it runs out.

**Power for energy weapons** at vehicle scale requires either dedicated power generation on the vehicle or external supply, and the choice between them shapes the vehicle's tactical flexibility. A self-contained power system allows independent operation; an external supply system ties the vehicle to a supply line that is itself a tactical vulnerability. At Mechanicus and relic tier, the power density achievable in compact generation systems allows meaningful self-contained capability. At industrial tier, the trade-offs are harsher and more vehicles operate with tethered power for their energy weapons than doctrine would prefer.

**Biological ammunition** — the organic material required by biologis weapon systems, including accelerant for organic flame projectors, chemical stocks for biological projectors, and the nutrition and medical supplies required to maintain the organic weapons systems themselves — is its own supply chain problem, and one that has no equivalent in conventional logistics. You cannot stockpile living weapons the way you stockpile shells. The supply chain for biologis forces is alive in the same sense the weapons are, and it requires management that no conventional quartermaster tradition has been designed to provide. Biologis military forces develop their own logistical traditions, and the gap between those traditions and conventional military supply practice is one of the reasons biologis forces are rarely integrated into conventionally-organised armies — the logistics of the two are genuinely incompatible in ways that are difficult to bridge operationally.
