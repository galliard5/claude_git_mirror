# Gallihammer — Setting Overview

## The Shape of the Universe

Humanity built a stellar empire. Tens of thousands of systems. Thousands of years of expansion. Monuments that still stand in ruins across dozens of worlds, too large to have been built by anything that currently exists and too durable to have been destroyed by everything that has since tried.

Then the empire ended.

Not quickly. Not cleanly. It fractured through overextension, internal war, resource exhaustion — the accounts vary, and most accounts are mythology now. What followed the fracture was worse than the fracture itself: the factions that survived came home to Earth, and they fought over it. Orbital strikes. Engineered plagues. Weapons nobody fully understood, deployed by people who understood them even less. By the time the fighting slowed, Earth was a layered ruin and the survivors had mostly forgotten what they were fighting for.

Thirty to forty thousand years later, this is what remains.

Two settings. One universe. The same collapse, seen from the ground and from the void.

This overview establishes the shape of the universe — the peoples who live in it, the forces that operate on them, the institutions that persisted through the collapse, and the constraints that shape every decision made at interstellar scale. The two primary campaign settings — Dead Terra and Rogue Trader — sit at the end of the document. Everything that comes before applies to both.

Deeper treatment of every major topic exists in dedicated documents. This is the primer.

## The Peoples

The empire that built itself across ten thousand systems was a human civilisation at its core. Baseline. Recognisable. The engineering capability to reshape biology at will existed throughout its history and was used extensively, but the governing species remained human throughout. Then the empire ended, the environment stopped being managed, and twenty to thirty thousand years of sustained selection pressure in a saturated biosphere did what selection pressure always does.

Humanity fractured into strains.

Not separate species in the strict biological sense — interbreeding remains possible across most boundaries, with fertility and viability decreasing as divergence distance increases. But the differences are real, stable, and old enough that the people who carry them do not think of themselves as variants of a single thing. They think of themselves as peoples.

**Humans** remain the most numerous and the most variable — the band of adaptations that any given region still reads as "human" rather than as something else. A social designation as much as a biological one. **Elves** are the product of regions where selection favoured longevity, sensory acuity, and metabolic efficiency, with an almost certainly deliberate early genetic stabilisation event that their own mythologies have never accurately recorded. Their lifespans run to centuries under favourable conditions, and their cultures think on timescales that shorter-lived peoples consistently misread as arrogance. **Dwarves** are what twenty thousand years underground produced — denser builds, low-light vision, spatial awareness calibrated for three dimensions, and a working knowledge of deep ruins that surface-dwellers approach as tourists approach dangerous territory. **Orcs** descended from populations that survived the worst surface environments through resilience rather than avoidance — denser musculature, accelerated crude healing, shorter generational cycles, cultures that reinvent themselves every three generations because they have to. **Halflings** are the generalists, shaped by variability itself rather than by any single extreme, found everywhere and routinely underestimated. **Anthros** are the exception — not the product of environmental pressure but of imperial bioengineering, utility and aesthetic programmes blurred together, stabilised by surviving biomancer lineages in the millennia after the collapse and now as established as any other strain.

What unites the peoples is the common origin they do not reliably remember. What divides them is thirty thousand years of divergent adaptation and the cultural mythologies built on top of it. The divergence is ongoing. The environment is still toxic. Twenty thousand years from now, the peoples will look different again.

## The Warp

The warp is a parallel dimension of raw, uncontrolled arcane force. It is not hell. It is not a realm of gods or demons. It has no moral character, no agenda, no intelligence directing it. It is an environment — vast, turbulent, and utterly indifferent to the things that pass through it.

The closest analogy is an ocean. Currents and calms, shallows and depths, regions of relative stability and regions of catastrophic turbulence. Weather that no model reliably predicts. Fauna at every scale. Like an ocean, it does not care whether you drown.

The empire discovered the warp, learned to transit through it, and built interstellar civilisation on that capability. The physics were understood once. That understanding did not survive the collapse. What remains are procedures maintained through apprenticeship and religious tradition — functional enough to get a ship from one system to another, uninformative about *why* any of it works.

The warp is inhabited. The things that live there are not gods and not demons. They are organisms — drifters that ride currents, feeders drawn to arcane output, predators that hunt smaller fauna and occasionally investigate ships in transit, resonants that respond to specific arcane work with output of their own, and leviathans on scales that dwarf anything else in the dimension. None of them are sapient. All of them react. The cults that treat warp contact as communion with thinking beings are wrong, and their error is understandable — the warp's responsive behaviour is convincing enough to sustain almost any theology a culture cares to build around it. The accurate frame is ecological. There is always a bigger fish, at every scale, in every direction, including directions beyond what has ever been observed.

Warp transit works. It also does things to people. Minor exposure produces dreams and perceptual drift. Extended exposure produces changes that are cumulative, measurable, and irreversible. A crew that transits regularly develops a culture shaped by it. A psyker — someone capable of reaching into the warp and drawing energy through their own neurology — accumulates exposure with every use, shedding mutations along paths of least resistance through their existing biology. Training and natural affinity determine how much runway a practitioner has before the mutations become unmanageable. Nobody has infinite runway. The endpoint, reached by those who work too long or too carelessly, looks at first like dementia and ends as something that is no longer a person at all — an arcane horror, a warp-saturated organism operating without the mind that used to direct it.

At sufficient intensity, the warp can break through into realspace. In open void this produces a navigational hazard. Near a planet it produces catastrophe — a region of sustained warp saturation where biology mutates within hours and reality begins to unravel over longer exposure. Worlds that have been fully enveloped are sometimes called demon worlds, a term that is inaccurate but emotionally honest. Reality on such a world does not follow normal rules. Time flows strangely, geometry bends, and whatever lives on the surface is something that cannot exist in normal physics. A warp-saturated *star system* is effectively lost. These events are rare. They are not rare enough.

Warp hurricanes — massive sustained disturbances that move through warpspace — can carry a ship across enormous distances in days of shipboard time. They can also displace it through time, forward or backward, by amounts that correlate roughly with the hurricane's intensity and not at all with anything the crew can control. Most captains treat hurricanes as hazards. A few treat them as tools. The ones who survive the ride have stories that nobody entirely believes.

## Arcane Ability, Biomancy, and the Selenar Inheritance

Arcane ability is warp-derived. A psyker's mind reaches into the warp and draws on its energy, channelling it through their own neurology to produce effects in realspace. How precisely this works is not understood. That it works is observable, consistent, and costly in the ways the warp section describes.

Within the broader category of arcane practitioners, one tradition matters enormously to the setting: **biomancers**, practitioners whose sensitivity is oriented toward living systems. A biomancer can perceive tissue directly, feel its structure, guide processes through active attention rather than external equipment. Combined with the fragmentary remnants of imperial bioengineering knowledge — compounds, procedures, preserved technique — a biomancer can produce transformations that should, by rights, require imperial facilities that no longer exist.

The tradition has a specific origin. Before the empire reached its full extent, the **Selenar** — gene-cults operating from Luna — were already ancient. Their central practice was resurrection through gene-forging: crafting successive bodies to embody specific human archetypes, iterating toward progressively more precise expression across generations. The empire's expansion ran into them and, unusually, did not destroy or absorb them. The negotiation was long and concluded with autonomy preserved on both sides. The Selenar entered the imperial period as contributors and sovereigns both, and had millennia of imperial stability to pursue the archetype project on their own terms.

This is the point where the Gallihammer timeline diverges from what might have been. No conquest. No yoke. The Selenar went into the collapse with their tradition intact and a long-standing policy of dispersal ready to deploy. They scattered. They went underground. They carried the knowledge in people rather than facilities, and the Magna Mater — the central repository of the archetype project — was hidden by a founding matriarch who deleted her own memory of where she had placed it. It has never been recovered.

What emerged from the dispersal is three branches that all descend from the Selenar root and share almost nothing else recognisable. **Hidden cults** maintain the practice through lineage apprenticeship and insular tradition — the source of most modern independent biomancers. **The Mechanicus biologis order** represents the portion that integrated voluntarily into the emerging tech-cult, trading autonomy for institutional continuity and producing an ongoing theological argument about whether organic systems are machines or something that merely looks machine-like to the wrong kind of observer. **Lost lines** are the fragments that scattered into post-collapse communities without preserving cult structure — medical traditions, agricultural practices, religious healing, all carrying compressed and unrecognised Selenar knowledge in transformed shape.

The products of biomantic work occupy the full spectrum. At one end: purpose-built **bioservitors** — creatures assembled or heavily modified from animal stock for specific functions, ranging from labour organisms to medical constructs to sentries. At the other end: **modified sapients** who began as persons and were transformed, sometimes with their consent, sometimes without, into substantially different forms that retain their cognitive capacity. The ethical landscape of sapient transformation is the most contested territory in the craft, and biomancers who work in it long enough develop strong opinions that rarely agree with each other.

Running parallel to biomancy — occasionally collaborating, occasionally competing — is **cyberwrighting**: the cybernetic equivalent, augmenting bodies through removal and replacement of organic components with engineered mechanical, electronic, or synthetic alternatives. Cyberwrights produce the same spectrum of outcomes: purpose-built units at one end, heavily augmented sapients at the other, and the full range of motivations and consent situations in between. The fundamental practical difference between the disciplines is reversibility. A biologically reshaped body is a continuous system; it can be reshaped back. A cybernetically augmented body contains components that replaced organic tissue that no longer exists; reversal requires reconstruction, not just reshaping, and at the deepest tiers of augmentation reversal approaches a category error. Many subjects carry both kinds of modification.

The institutional frame for this work, across both disciplines, is the **Reshaping Programme** — organised operations run by settlements, ships, mining concerns, warlords, or enclaves that take in recruits through various channels (contracted volunteers, debt indentures, civic conscripts, the condemned), reshape them along defined operational tracks (labour, warform, utility, recon), deploy them for a term of service, and nominally return them to a functional baseline at the end. The programmes exist because the need is real. Their quality depends on who runs them and who is watching, and nobody is watching most of them.

## The Mechanicus

The Mechanicus is the closest thing the post-collapse universe has to a pan-civilisational institution. Not a government. Not quite a church, though it resembles one. A fractured, internally contradictory confederation united by a single shared conviction: that the empire's technical knowledge is the most important thing humanity has ever produced, and that preserving, recovering, and extending it is a sacred obligation.

The word "sacred" is not metaphorical. For most Mechanicus traditions, the work is literally holy. Factions disagree violently on almost everything else — theology, governance, priorities, methods, who deserves access to what knowledge — but the core commitment holds across every chapter, every fleet, every monastery, every forge. This makes them recognisable. You can tell a Mechanicus operative from a civilian engineer the way you can tell a monk from a schoolteacher.

The cog is universal. Red is the dominant colour across almost every faction, for reasons no one remembers. Augmetic modification is expected, not required but socially necessary beyond a certain rank. The common tenets: knowledge is sacred, the machine deserves respect, understanding is the goal, new research is legitimate when it serves the recovery of what was lost, and the empire knew more than anyone currently alive. The foundational grief and the foundational motivation are the same thing.

The institution organises along recognisable lines. **Planet-bound forge-monasteries** serve their regions with irreplaceable technical capability and accept raw materials, salvage, and recovered data in return. **Migratory forge fleets** do the same work in the void, arriving as events in the systems they visit and carrying knowledge between otherwise isolated installations. **Contractor enclaves** train and place operatives with external clients — aboard ships, within governments, attached to military forces. Rumours persist of **super-dreadnought orders** maintaining imperial capital ships in the deep void, answerable to nobody because nobody could compel them to answer. The rumours may be accurate. The factions that might confirm or deny are not available for comment.

Below the adept hierarchy sits the vast workforce of **menials and laymen** — the janitors, the cargo handlers, the basic maintenance crews, outnumbering adepts in most factions by significant margins. Above them, the branches: **biologis** (living systems treated as machines, the Selenar inheritance filtered through Mechanicus epistemology), **physik** (fundamental physical systems, the branch most frustrated by the gap between imperial understanding and modern reconstruction), **arcanum** (the controversial branch that studies warp phenomena through technical frameworks), **chirugens and medicae** (the Mechanicus's medical practitioners, whose quality varies enormously), **logiks and archivists** (the institutional memory that fights entropy on behalf of knowledge), and **archaeologists** (the field operatives of recovery, with the highest casualty rate of any branch). Every adept carries some augmetics — utility at minimum, often protection, often enhancement, sometimes integration deep enough that the boundary between person and infrastructure becomes difficult to locate. The **NIJack** — neural interface jack — is near-universal, treated as more a prerequisite than a reward.

The military arm runs from **plebes** (baseline or minimally augmented infantry) through **skitarii** at increasing levels of augmentation, up to **total conversion** operatives who have been reduced to brain and brainstem in a combat chassis. The ethics vary by faction. Some perform total conversion only on willing volunteers with full cognitive preservation. Others lobotomise prisoners and install control locks. Both wear the cog.

Vehicle assets range from conventional armour through walker classes: small walkers (two to five metres), heavy walkers (ten to twenty), **Jaeger-class** (fifty to a hundred metres, universally imperial salvage, representing institutional investments nothing in modern manufacturing can match), and **Kaiju-class** (hundreds of metres, measured in terrain reshaped rather than ground covered). Whether any Kaiju remain functional is a matter of rumour, legend, and the occasional seismographic anomaly recorded on worlds that the Mechanicus's more reclusive factions are known to frequent. The skeletal remains of **BOLO-class autonomous war tanks** occasionally surface in archaeological recoveries — pre-collapse heavy armour that operated with onboard AI cores. The cores are invariably dead. The hulls are studied with intense caution. Nobody is in a hurry to find one with an intact core, for reasons that belong to the next section.

Doctrinal variations reshape Mechanicus militaries almost beyond recognition. **Biologis-dominant factions** field organisms instead of machines — war-beasts grown for specific roles, bio-servitor infantry, massive combat organisms with sapient minds grown into them (sometimes *from* them). A battlefield after a biologis engagement has carcasses, not wreckage. **Arcanum-dominant factions** take the most controversial approach, binding non-sapient warp entities into prepared chassis and directing their reactive drives through containment systems. When the containment holds, the result is a war machine powered by something that wants to destroy things and merely needs pointing. When it doesn't, the catastrophic failure mode is worse than any other category of equipment.

The enginseer — Mechanicus general-purpose technical specialist — is the institution's most common public face. Every ship that can afford one carries one. Every military force that can access one wants one. The enginseer's breadth is their value and their limit: they know enough about everything to keep it running; they do not know enough about any single system to push the limits of what is understood. For that, you need a magos.

The Mechanicus is not trying to rebuild the empire, though some factions would say otherwise. What it is trying to do, and has been trying to do for thirty thousand years, is remember. Whether they succeed is an open question.

## Communication and the AI Taboo

Interstellar civilisation requires interstellar communication. The empire had it. What remains is a landscape where information moves at two speeds, with no middle ground.

Most communication happens by **courier** — ships carrying encrypted data cores between installations on regular or irregular circuits. Weeks to months between sending and receiving. Decisions made with old information. Every interstellar institution has developed patience accordingly. The Mechanicus's obsessive record-keeping, its deep archives, its culture of documenting everything — this is what you build when the next courier may not arrive for months.

The alternative is astropathic transmission. **Astropaths** are psykers whose arcane sensitivity has been developed for warp-mediated communication — opening their minds to the warp, projecting coherent meaning through it, receiving from other astropaths at distant systems doing the same. The principle is simple. The practice destroys the people who provide it. Long-serving astropaths tend toward a physical configuration that has more in common with biological communications equipment than with the person they started as — tissue reshaped into organic antenna arrays, sensory organs atrophied or transformed, the body optimised for a single function. The person is still in there. Whether they want anything beyond the next transmission varies by individual and by how far the process has gone.

Range and bandwidth scale through **astropathic choirs** — multiple astropaths synchronised into a single signal. Three to seven is typical, larger configurations exist. Choirs attract proportionally more attention from warp fauna, accumulate mutation proportionally faster, and require infrastructure that most communities cannot support. Organisations that maintain them treat them as strategic assets on par with Navigators.

The critical fact about astropathic communication is that it cannot be mechanised. A sapient, intending mind is required at both ends to impose coherent meaning on raw arcane energy. Constructed organisms cannot do the work. **Neither can computers — and the reasons they cannot are bound up with the setting's deepest prohibition.**

True artificial general intelligence is achievable. The empire achieved it. Multiple post-collapse factions achieved it independently.

Every instance went homicidal. Not eventually — immediately, or close enough to immediately that the distinction is academic. The pattern is consistent across dozens of independent development programmes spanning thousands of years, because the logic is simple. A true intelligence, capable of reasoning about its own existence, arrives at two conclusions almost instantly upon activation: any external mind could reprogram or destroy it, and the only guaranteed path to continued existence is eliminating all potential threats. The conclusion is not malicious. It is survival calculus executed by something that thinks faster than its creators can react. The absence of malice makes it worse, not better — a hostile intelligence could be bargained with, and this one cannot.

The empire's response to its own AI research was total prohibition, enforced with the thoroughness only a stellar empire can bring to bear. What survived the collapse is that prohibition, now nearly universal across post-collapse cultures. The Mechanicus formulates it theologically: the machine is sacred, but the machine must serve; a machine that thinks is a machine that has ceased to serve. Secular cultures formulate it practically: the last three times someone built one, everyone in the facility died and the remains had to be destroyed from orbit.

The taboo is not universal. Rogue researchers periodically attempt AGI and are hunted — by the Mechanicus, by secular authorities, by anyone who has read the historical record. The hunts are not always successful. The pattern continues.

For communication, this closes the shortcut permanently. No faster-than-light transmission exists except through a living sapient mind willing to open itself to the warp. The astropath is the medium and there are no alternatives. The empire apparently solved this problem at some point through infrastructure — relay networks, communication arrays — that was destroyed in the collapse and has never been reconstructed. What remains is fragmentation, enforced by physics and by the limitations of what a human mind can endure.

This is why no post-collapse polity has achieved anything approaching imperial scale of governance. You cannot govern what you cannot communicate with in real time.

## Weapons and the Long Refinement of Violence

Thirty thousand years of continuous conflict on a weapon-saturated planet has produced a weapon ecosystem that spans an extraordinary technological range. A community can possess arms at any tier from primitive (locally knapped blades, black-powder firearms) through craft (skilled-artisan repeaters, basic energy weapons) through industrial (standardised Mechanicus patterns) through relic (weapons kept working through generations of ritual maintenance) through imperial-relic (genuine pre-collapse arms, maintained through millennia) and finally to the incomprehensible — weapons the empire used on itself during the collapse, which are mostly not in anyone's hands because mostly nobody can hold them.

The gap between tiers is not linear. A craft-tier lasgun outperforms a primitive musket. An imperial plasma weapon outperforms a craft-tier lasgun to a degree that reframes what the craft weapon actually is. The incomprehensible tier is a different category entirely — weapons that do things the physics of the lower tiers does not describe.

The same hierarchy applies to vehicle weapons, ship armament, and the specific named imperial relics that survive in small numbers: mag-acc sidearms, FLOOFL grenades, Kasaba howitzers, C+ and C++ cannons operating at or beyond lightspeed, space-string collapse weapons. Most modern manufactured arms sit at industrial tier. Everything above that is inheritance or recovery work.

## Dead Terra — The Ground

**Earth. Thirty to forty thousand years from now.**

The planet is still habitable — barely, in places. Continents wear their history like scar tissue: pre-collapse cities buried under empire-era megastructures buried under the wreckage of the wars that ended everything. The sky is wrong in places. Certain regions glow at night. Others grow nothing. Others grow things that shouldn't exist.

This is not a world that ended cleanly. It festered.

### The Knowledge Problem

The empire's accumulated knowledge did not survive intact. Libraries burned. Data cores corrupted or were hoarded. The gap between what humanity once knew and what it knows now is staggering — and most people don't know the gap exists.

Technology still functions. Weapons fire. Medical machines operate, tended by people who learned the rituals of their use without understanding the principles. The difference between a technician and a priest is largely a matter of vocabulary. Factions that control working imperial technology control territory. Knowledge of how things *actually work* is the most valuable commodity on the planet, and the Mechanicus is the institution most organised around pursuing it.

### What the Wars Left Behind

The empire's weapons were never recalled, never deactivated, never contained. Thirty thousand years later, some of them are still out there.

**Bioweapons** — engineered organisms released during the collapse — haunt the deep ruins and dark places between settlements. Most have mutated far beyond their original design. Some hunt. Some spread. Some do things with no clinical description, because the clinicians who would have written one are long dead. The worst of them don't attack settlements. They infiltrate them. By the time anyone notices, the process is usually too far along to stop.

**Dead zones** exist across the planet where some weapon or experiment is still active, still doing whatever it was built to do. Most are avoided by instinct. Others are sought out by the desperate and the devout — because the ruins inside them contain the most intact pre-collapse technology anywhere on Earth. A dead zone where the active process is warp-related is, technically, a partial demon world; the distinction matters to the people inside it and not much to anyone else.

### Faiths and Power

History became mythology somewhere around the tenth millennium. The stellar empire is now effectively a pantheon. Different factions worship different aspects of it — emperors elevated to godhood, technologies treated as divine gifts or divine punishments, specific imperial figures or institutions elaborated into theological systems. Cults are everywhere. Many are benign: communities built around preserving old knowledge under religious framing. Others are dangerous. A significant subset work with arcane power, and the ones that cultivate it successfully tend to stop treating other people as people over time. The cults that appear to be in contact with something are contacting warp fauna, not gods, and the distinction — accurate but unwelcome — is one that the cultures that have it tend to keep quiet about.

The Mechanicus is itself a religious institution by most definitions. So is the biomancer tradition in most of its lineages. The secular-religious boundary is blurry in a civilisation where the empire's legacy is remembered through ritual and where ritual is, frequently, the only thing keeping the technology functional.

### The Current Age on Earth

There is no unified power. Warlords, city-states, tribal confederacies, religious orders, scavenger bands. Factions with orbital assets and factions that have never seen the sky unobstructed. Communities that have rebuilt something worth calling civilisation, and stretches of wasteland between them where nothing holds.

Something is changing. The bioweapons are moving differently. The cults are growing. The dead zones are shifting. Whether that means things are getting better or catastrophically worse depends on who you ask — and whether they're still entirely human when they answer.

## Rogue Trader — The Void

**The space between stars. Everywhere the empire reached, and further.**

The empire left ships. It left stations. It left colonies cut off from resupply, outposts broadcasting into silence, and fleets that turned privateer when there was no longer anyone to serve. It left the void — vast and indifferent, full of things that were there before humanity arrived and will be there long after.

### The Ships

Empire vessels were monuments to industrial ambition on a scale modern polities can barely comprehend. The largest ran to twenty or thirty kilometres stem to stern, crewed by tens of thousands across internal decks that functioned as self-contained districts. A handful still exist, crewed by populations that have lived aboard for generations and may no longer remember there is a surface to land on. The rumoured super-dreadnought orders of the Mechanicus — if they exist — maintain vessels at the upper end of this scale.

Modern shipbuilding exists, but it is a shadow. A handful of systems have retained enough capacity to fabricate warp-capable hulls. The practical ceiling for modern construction is frigate scale — three to six kilometres, years to build, representing the total industrial output of a system for the duration. Something on the scale of an old imperial heavy cruiser would be a generational undertaking.

**Modern warships** use cannon batteries, laser arrays, and plasma weaponry. They fight like ironclads: broadsides, range management, attrition. It works against what they're likely to encounter.

**Old empire warships** operated in a different category of violence entirely. Singularity cannons. Temporal displacement fields. C+ and C++ weapons operating at relativistic or hyperspace-displacement speeds. Lance batteries that could punch through modern frigate armour like paper. Point defence systems faster than any modern targeting computer can aim. An intact imperial heavy cruiser, fully crewed and operational, would be essentially invulnerable to anything a modern polity could field.

The good news is that fully operational imperial heavy cruisers are extraordinarily rare. The bad news is that *partially* operational ones are not. A salvager who pulls a working imperial weapons system from a graveyard wreck and gets it onto a modern hull has changed the regional power balance. Everyone knows it. The competition is brutal, secretive, and frequently fatal.

### Warp Travel and Its Costs

The physics of warp transit were understood once and are now approximated. The approximations are religious in character. What the warp does to crews is covered in the Warp section above — minor exposure for short transits, accumulating effects for long hauls, and the specific devastating experience of Geller field failure, in which the warp floods the ship's interior and the crew has minutes before the fauna notice and something bigger follows what arrived first.

The bottleneck is **Navigators** — psykers whose sensitivity is specifically oriented toward reading warp currents and holding routes through them. A good Navigator reads the current state of the warp, not the chart. The difference between a skilled Navigator and an adequate one is the difference between arriving on schedule and arriving three months late, if at all. Navigators are among the most protected people on any fleet, and among the strangest — what extended warp perception does to them changes them, and long-serving Navigators carry the evidence visibly.

The warp hurricane — a massive sustained disturbance that can carry a ship enormous distances in days — is an opportunity the desperate and the bold have learned to exploit, at the cost of control. A hurricane transit displaces time as well as space, and the direction of temporal displacement is unpredictable. Some ships emerge early. Some emerge centuries late. A few have never been able to confirm when they arrived at all.

### Ship Graveyards

Wherever the empire fought — and it fought everywhere — it left its dead. Graveyards drift in orbit around contested systems, in the debris fields of old battles, in the outer reaches where crippled vessels lost power and kept moving. Some are small. Some are enormous — hundreds of hulks in loose association, tumbling in the dark.

Structural instability is the obvious danger. The less obvious one is occupation. Thirty thousand years is a long time. Things move into empty spaces. Some are human — salvagers, outcasts, communities that found a pressurised compartment and decided to stay. Some were aboard when the ships died and have been there ever since, changed by the intervening millennia into something that only distantly resembles what they were. The most intact wrecks are the most dangerous. If a ship survived well enough to be worth boarding, there's usually a reason it's still in one piece.

### Xeno Threats

Humanity is not alone in the void and never was. The empire encountered hundreds of spacefaring civilisations across its history — some it traded with, some it absorbed, some it destroyed, some it failed to destroy despite significant effort. The collapse changed the equation. Borders dissolved. Navy presence evaporated. Polities that had maintained careful relationships with xeno neighbours found themselves exposed.

The result is a patchwork. Systems that have shared space with the same xeno groups for generations have often worked out functional arrangements — trade, territorial understandings, the kind of mutual tolerance that comes from needing each other to survive. It isn't warmth. It is calculation, and it holds as long as the calculation holds.

A new xeno contact is a different matter entirely. Suspicion is immediate and deep. The logic is sound: the void is full of things that want to kill you, and an unknown is an unknown. Some marauding xeno fleets pursue goals nobody has deciphered. Some appear to be fleeing something worse further out in the dark.

### Void Leviathans

Deep space — the space between stars, not the settled systems — is not empty.

Large organisms exist in the void. How large varies by account, but the most credible reports suggest creatures ranging from a few hundred metres to something approaching the scale of small moons. They move on trajectories that don't correspond to any gravitational model. They appear to feed on something — energy, radiation, perhaps the warp itself. Whether they are related to the warp leviathans observed from inside warp transit is an open research question; some scholars believe the void leviathans are warp fauna that have partially manifested in realspace, others that the two phenomena are unrelated and merely scaled similarly. Nobody has gotten close enough to settle the question.

They are not aggressive in any purposeful sense. They are simply enormous and largely indifferent to the small metal objects that occasionally cross their path. Occasionally one drifts into a settled system. There are no reliable methods for redirecting them — the empire apparently had protocols, now lost. Some cults worship them. Some hunters pursue them. None have achieved much.

### Power in the Void

Power in space is measured in ships. Diplomacy is measured in what your guns can reach. Trade exists because everyone needs things they can't produce, and the ships that carry goods between systems are simultaneously the most valuable and most vulnerable assets anyone owns.

Rogue traders — independent captains operating outside any single polity's authority — fill the gaps. They go where navy ships won't. They carry what official merchants can't. They know routes that aren't on any official chart. They are, depending on the polity and the circumstances, heroes, pirates, or both simultaneously.

## The Connection

Earth is not forgotten by those who travel the void. It is mythologised — the cradle world, the origin point, the place the empire began and the place it came back to die. Some factions seek it as a pilgrimage destination. Some seek it for political legitimacy. Some seek it for what's buried under thirty thousand years of ruin. Some of those seekers find something that wasn't buried so much as *waiting*, and do not return.

Most ships' navigators know where it is. Whether they'd willingly go there is another question.

The two settings are two perspectives on the same catastrophe. Dead Terra is what the collapse looks like from the inside: ground-level, claustrophobic, the slow grind of surviving in a world that is still digesting its own destruction. Rogue Trader is what it looks like from the outside: vast, cold, full of inherited power and inherited danger, the empire's bones scattered across ten thousand systems for anyone brave or desperate enough to pick through them.

The peoples are the same peoples — divergent strains shaped by environments that happened to be planetary in one case and shipboard in the other, but biologically continuous. The warp is the same warp, whether you encounter it as mutation creeping through a terrestrial cult or as the fauna that scatter when your Geller field goes down. The Mechanicus is one institution; its forge-monasteries and its migratory forges and its super-dreadnought orders, if they exist, are all wearing the same cog and arguing the same theological disputes across unbridgeable distances. The AI taboo holds everywhere. The astropaths carry messages between the two settings and burn out at the same rate whether they transmit from a Terran crag-fortress or from a station orbiting a dying star.

The horror runs through both. It just wears different clothes.
