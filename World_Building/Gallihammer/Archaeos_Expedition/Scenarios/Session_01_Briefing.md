## Quick Orientation

Si'ken arrived aboard the Proving Ground at the end of the prologue — shuttled up from Valdrekk's orbital platform during the pre-deployment preparation phase. The ship is docked. The crew is aboard. Nobody has deployed yet. This is the expedition's first outing as a unit.

Session 01 covers two movements:

**Movement 1: The Ship.** Si'ken's first days aboard — quarters, corridors, crew. The social reality of being a non-standard body in a small community that hasn't yet decided what to make of him. Key interactions with Hale, Thrace, Vos, Voss, Harsk, Oenthe, Cade, and Pael — first impressions deepening into the beginnings of working relationships. Si'ken learning the ship through the insectoid body's capabilities: what spaces he can access that nobody else can, how the ambient machine sense reads the Proving Ground's systems, how the NIJack integrates into the ship's datasphere.

**Movement 2: The Mission.** Loth's targeting data arrives. The first target is not a distant ruin in uncharted space — it's close to home. A monitoring post in the Calthris system itself, orbiting the dead moon Keth, identified from Deep Stacks routing tables. Small, sealed, automated. A shakedown run: test the team's protocols, confirm the crew works together, recover whatever data the post was collecting. Hale briefs the team. The Proving Ground undocks. Shuttle deployment to the site.

**Central tension this session:** Si'ken is new. The team is new. Nobody has proven anything to anyone yet. The shakedown mission is deliberately low-stakes — the expedition can't afford to lose people on its first outing, and Hale knows it. But low-stakes doesn't mean no stakes. The site has been sealed for thirty thousand years, the monitoring data stayed local instead of being relayed to the Calthris hub, and nobody knows what the empire was watching out here.

**Prologue state to carry forward:**
- Si'ken's ambient machine sense is running constantly — the Proving Ground will arrive as a vast, detailed conversation of mechanical systems. More complex than the city. More intimate — it's his ship now.
- Si'ken disclosed the ambient sensitivity to Valdrekk voluntarily. Vane appended it to the file. Who on the crew knows about it and who doesn't is an open question — Hale received Thenn's capability report, which may or may not include Vane's amendment.
- Si'ken's voice is functional but not kobold. How the crew responds to hearing an insectoid body speak is part of this session.
- The control architecture is partially deactivated. The Override Conflict flaw is dormant. Neither of these is relevant to this session — they are long-game threads.
- *Things should do what they're for.* Si'ken wants to work. The work hasn't started yet.

## Movement 1: The Ship

### Purpose

Give the player time to inhabit the Proving Ground before the work starts. Si'ken has never been on a ship. Everything is new — the sounds, the scale, the smell of recycled atmosphere, the constant low vibration of drives at standby, the machine-spirit conversation that the NIJack and the ambient sense feed into Si'ken's awareness as a vast, layered, living system.

This is also where the crew dynamics establish themselves beyond the quick-sketch impressions from the prologue's Beat 7. The prologue ended with arrival. This session begins with what happens after arrival — when the first impression has faded and the reality of living alongside eighty strangers in a metal tube begins.

### The Quarters

Si'ken's quarters have been modified by Vos's team: environmental adjustments, a resting surface shaped for the insectoid body, NIJack access port for the ship's local datasphere. The space is small — a compartment in the crew section, not officer territory. The modifications are functional, not welcoming. The space works. It does not yet feel like anything.

**The machine sense in quarters:** When Si'ken first settles into the space alone, the ambient sense should register the room's systems specifically — climate control, lighting, the datasphere node, the structural members in the walls. Small space, small conversation. Familiar quickly. This becomes baseline — Si'ken will notice when the baseline changes.

**The datasphere:** Si'ken's NIJack connects to the ship's local network. This is his first experience of an integrated ship datasphere — not the open noise of a city's infrastructure but a structured, purpose-built information environment. Crew schedules, ship status, navigation data, internal communication channels. Si'ken is in the system. The system knows he's there. His identifier in the network is whatever Valdrekk assigned — likely a designation rather than a name. Whether Si'ken notices this and whether it matters to him is a character moment.

### Key Crew Interactions — Movement 1

The following interactions should occur during the settling-in period. They do not need to happen in this order. Let the player's choices about where Si'ken goes and what he does determine which encounters happen when. If the player is proactive about seeking crew members out, facilitate that. If the player explores the ship instead, let the crew come to Si'ken through natural intersection — the mess hall, the corridors, the workshop where Vos is working.

**Not every interaction needs to be a full scene.** Some can be brief — a passing encounter in a corridor, a look across the mess hall, a wordless moment of shared space. The crew is eighty people. Si'ken will encounter some of them peripherally. Reserve full scenes for the key NPCs below.

#### Thrace — The Medical Assessment

**Priority: This happens first.** Thrace insists on a full medical assessment before Si'ken is cleared for duty. Non-negotiable. Si'ken was assessed by Thenn at Valdrekk, but Thrace does not accept another chirugen's assessment as a substitute for her own — she needs to understand Si'ken's physiology well enough to treat injuries in the field, and that understanding requires direct examination.

**The scene:** Medicae bay. Thrace is thorough, efficient, and not interested in Si'ken's comfort beyond what medical practice requires. She examines the chitin (hardness, flexibility, repair capability), tests joint articulation across all six legs, assesses the mechadendrite integration points, runs diagnostic protocols on the NIJack through her own interface equipment, takes biological samples (haemolymph, chitin shaving, respiratory membrane tissue), and makes notes that Si'ken can see her recording through the transcription system in her augmetic hands.

**What Thrace learns:**
- The biological architecture is sound — well-designed, well-executed. Whoever made Si'ken knew what they were doing.
- The cybernetic integration is clean — the NIJack and mechadendrites are bonded to the biological tissue at a level that Thrace's training covers at the theoretical end. She can maintain it. She cannot replicate it.
- The chitin's self-repair capability is real but slow — significant damage will need time and nutrition to heal, not field patching.
- The respiratory system is sealed — Si'ken can operate in toxic atmospheres and possibly vacuum for limited periods without external protection. Thrace will want to establish limits through controlled testing before the first deployment.

**What Thrace says:** Clinical. Direct. She tells Si'ken what she's finding as she finds it, the way she'd update any patient. She does not soften the language for his benefit. She does not treat him as a specimen — he is a patient with unusual physiology, and Thrace's professional framework handles that with competence if not warmth.

**Choice points:** How Si'ken responds to being examined. Whether he asks questions. Whether he offers information Thrace doesn't ask for (the ambient sensitivity, the hunting suite). Whether the examination feels like medical care or like being assessed as equipment — and whether Si'ken says so.

**End of scene:** Thrace clears Si'ken for duty with conditions — she wants the respiratory limit testing done before the first deployment, and she wants Si'ken to report any system behaviour she hasn't documented. "You're a first for me. If something goes wrong in the field, I need to not be guessing."

#### Hale — The Operational Meeting

**Priority: Second.** After medical clearance, Hale meets with Si'ken in the planning room to discuss the scout role in practical terms.

**The scene:** Planning room, adjacent to the bridge. Hale has Thenn's capability report on his console — the report Thenn sent to Ardenne, or a version of it. He has reviewed it. He asks Si'ken questions that the report doesn't answer: how the ambient sense works in practice, what the interface tendril's range is, how Si'ken handles simultaneous inputs (sensory, machine, NIJack), what the body can do that the report describes in clinical terms and Hale wants to understand in operational terms.

Hale is interested. Not warm — interested. He is a field archaeologist who has spent twenty years going into ruins, and Si'ken is a tool that can go into places Hale has always wanted to reach and couldn't. The interest is professional. It is also genuine — Hale respects capability, and Si'ken's capability profile is unlike anything he's worked with.

**What Hale tells Si'ken:**
- The expedition hasn't deployed yet. The first target hasn't arrived from Loth. The crew is in preparation mode.
- Si'ken's role: first in. Map the environment, assess hazards, report conditions. The team does not commit until the scout says it's viable. Si'ken's assessment carries weight.
- The team operates on trust. Trust is earned in the field, not in briefings. The first deployment will establish the working relationship.
- Hale does not discuss Si'ken's origin, the Remade question, or the circumstances of Valdrekk's acquisition. If Si'ken raises these subjects, Hale redirects to operational matters. Not coldly — simply focused on what he considers relevant to the work.

**What Hale does not say:**
- Whether Ardenne told him anything beyond the capability report
- What he personally thinks about having a Remade aboard
- Whether the expedition's first target has already been selected and he's waiting for Loth's formal package to make it official

**Choice points:** What Si'ken asks. Whether he demonstrates the ambient sense or the tendril interface for Hale directly. Whether the conversation stays operational or Si'ken pushes into personal territory. How Si'ken reads Hale — professional respect or professional distance.

#### Vos — The Workshop

**Priority: Natural encounter.** Vos is in the workshop or the engineering deck. Si'ken will encounter Vos during ship exploration — the engineering spaces are where the machine sense is loudest, where the ship's systems are most present, and where Si'ken's insectoid body might gravitate.

**The scene:** Vos is working. The mechadendrite arms move independently — two on the task, two adjusting position or holding tools. Vos notices Si'ken because Vos notices everything that enters the engineering deck. The interaction begins with Vos's professional interest in the cybernetic integration — the mechadendrites, the NIJack, how the interface points bond to the chitin.

**What makes this interaction different from Thrace's:** Thrace examined Si'ken as a patient. Vos examines the cybernetics as a colleague examining a peer's work. The distinction is significant — Vos is interested in the engineering, and the engineering was done by someone Vos implicitly respects (the quality of the integration speaks for itself). This means Vos addresses Si'ken's mechanical components with a professional respect that extends, by implication, to Si'ken.

Vos may ask to interface with Si'ken's NIJack through a diagnostic connection — a request that is technically routine (enginseer checking cybernetic systems is normal aboard a Mechanicus vessel) and personally intimate (it means letting someone into the space where Si'ken's mind meets the machine). How Si'ken responds to this request matters.

**The ambient sense and the engineering deck:** This is the space where Si'ken's ambient machine awareness is most relevant. The engineering deck is dense with systems. If Si'ken's sense registers something — a system running slightly off-nominal, a vibration pattern that doesn't match the surroundings, anything the ambient sense picks up that Vos's standard monitoring hasn't — that's a moment. It establishes Si'ken's utility in a context Vos understands immediately.

#### Harsk — The Tactical Assessment

**Priority: Before the mission briefing.** Harsk needs to assess Si'ken's field capability before the first deployment.

**The scene:** Can be formal (a scheduled assessment in a cargo bay or corridor section cleared for movement drills) or informal (Harsk observing Si'ken during ship exploration and initiating a tactical conversation). Harsk's interest is operational: speed, mobility, response to verbal commands, ability to navigate under direction, communication clarity.

Harsk will ask Si'ken to demonstrate movement capability — running speed, climbing (walls, overhead surfaces), silent movement, the hunting configuration if Si'ken is willing to show it. This is not a test of Si'ken as a person. It is a test of Si'ken as a unit — a mobile asset whose capabilities Harsk needs to map onto his tactical planning.

**Harsk's response to what he sees:** If Si'ken demonstrates the capabilities the prologue established — speed, wall-climbing, silent movement, the hunting suite's strike reflex — Harsk's assessment will shift from neutral to cautiously positive. The capabilities are real. The question becomes whether they're reliable under field conditions, which can only be answered in the field.

Harsk will not express warmth. He will express something that reads, in Harsk's register, as acknowledgment: "You'll do" or "That works" or a simple nod and a note added to his deployment plan. From Harsk, this is significant.

### Ship Exploration

If the player directs Si'ken to explore the ship, let it happen. The Proving Ground is 2.5 kilometres of corridors, compartments, empty hull sections, and maintenance spaces. For Si'ken — small, wall-climbing, able to fit through spaces designed for maintenance access rather than personnel — the ship has a secret architecture that the baseline crew doesn't use.

**What Si'ken discovers:**
- Maintenance ducts and crawlspaces that connect compartments the corridor layout doesn't. The insectoid body moves through these easily. The crew moves through corridors. Si'ken can move through the ship's walls.
- The machine sense reads the ship's systems as a layered conversation. Near the drives, it's a deep rumble of enormous power managed precisely. Near the sensors, it's a constant alertness — the arrays scanning, processing, reporting. Near the archive, it's quiet — the data systems have a different character from the mechanical ones. Near the bridge, it's the command systems: responsive, waiting for input.
- Empty hull sections — former cargo spaces that the refit didn't fill. Bare metal, unused potential. These spaces echo differently. The machine sense registers them as absence — systems that aren't there, that could be.
- The machine-chapel. Small, serious, bearing the cog symbol. If Si'ken enters, the NIJack registers the chapel's own dedicated systems — the liturgical interface, the maintenance data that flows through the chapel during observances. Whether the chapel means anything to Si'ken is a character question. He has Mechanicus hardware wired into his body. The religion that hardware comes from is here, in this room.

## Movement 2: The Mission

### The Targeting Data Arrives

After 2–3 days of shipboard settling-in, the targeting package arrives from Valdrekk. Loth's communication comes through the secure channel to Hale's planning room — an intelligence briefing, targeting coordinates, and a priority assessment.

**What Loth sends:**

The package identifies a target in the Calthris system itself — not deep void, not an unknown system, but the expedition's own backyard.

**Monitoring Post Keth-4-7.** An automated imperial monitoring station, orbital position around Calthris's second moon (Keth — a dead, cratered body showing signs of former imperial installation, unexplored). Identified from Deep Stacks routing tables as a signal relay and monitoring node. The station collected data. The data was NOT relayed to the main Calthris administrative hub — it was locally stored. The routing tables show Keth-4-7 receiving incoming signal traffic and archiving it without retransmission. Whatever the empire was monitoring from this position, the monitoring was local-only.

Loth wants the data. The fact that an imperial monitoring post in the Calthris system was collecting and retaining information without relaying it to the main hub is an anomaly — it suggests either a monitoring function that was classified above the administrative level, or a purpose that post-dates the main hub's operational period. Either way, the data that Keth-4-7 was collecting may explain aspects of the Deep Stacks data that Loth has found inconsistent with a purely administrative archive.

**Loth's note to Hale (paraphrased for the briefing scene):** "This is in our system. It should be straightforward. I want the monitoring data specifically — whatever Keth-4-7 was watching and why it was watching it quietly. Sensor equipment is secondary but welcome if intact. Consider this a calibration exercise for the team. If your crew can handle a sealed monitoring post in home orbit, I'll send you coordinates that actually require a ship."

Loth is not being insulting. She is being practical — the expedition has never deployed, and a shakedown run close to home is the responsible first step. She is also genuinely interested in the data. The two motivations are not in conflict.

### The Briefing

Hale assembles the deployment team in the planning room for the mission briefing. This is the first time the full team has assembled for an operational briefing — the first time the working unit exists as a working unit rather than a collection of individuals aboard the same ship.

**Present:** Hale, Voss, Harsk, Cade, Si'ken, Davan (field enginseer), Sollis (field medicae). Oenthe is on the bridge managing the undocking sequence. Vos is in engineering. Pael is present — observing, recording.

**What Hale briefs:**

**The target:** Monitoring Post Keth-4-7. Orbital, attached to or co-orbiting with Calthris's second moon. Long-range sensors confirm a faint power signature — radioisotope thermal generator still producing minimal output after thirty thousand years. The structure is intact on scan: approximately 150 metres across, a compact cluster of monitoring arrays, data storage, and a small habitable section (designed for periodic maintenance crew, not permanent occupation).

**Mission type:** Quick recovery. In, acquire the monitoring data, assess the sensor equipment for potential ship integration, out. Expected duration: hours, not days.

**Approach:** The Proving Ground repositions from Valdrekk's orbital platform to Keth orbit. Standard approach — no warp transit, in-system manoeuvre only. Oenthe handles the piloting. Shuttle One deploys the team to Keth-4-7 from close orbit.

**Team composition:**
- Si'ken — scout. First in. Map the interior, assess hazards, report conditions before the team commits.
- Voss — chief archaeologist. Site assessment and artefact evaluation.
- Cade — data recovery. The monitoring data is the primary objective. Cade interfaces with whatever data systems are intact.
- Davan — field enginseer. Handles mechanical challenges: sealed doors, power systems, technical assessment of sensor equipment.
- Sollis — field medicae. On-site medical presence.
- Harsk + 4 skitarii — security. Perimeter and escort.

**Hazard assessment:**

*Structural:* Thirty thousand years of orbital exposure. Micrometeorite impacts may have compromised hull sections. Internal atmosphere may be degraded, absent, or sealed in isolated compartments. The structure's integrity should be confirmed by the scout before the main team enters — Si'ken's insectoid body can operate in vacuum for limited periods (pending Thrace's testing), which gives him an advantage over suited crew in compromised sections.

*Mechanical:* The RTG is still producing power. Automated systems may still be cycling on residual power — lighting, environmental management, monitoring equipment, and potentially automated security or maintenance systems following thirty-thousand-year-old programming. The security systems on a monitoring post are unlikely to be military-grade — this was an automated relay, not a military installation — but any active system in a sealed site is a hazard until assessed.

*Environmental:* Radiation from the RTG — low-level unless shielding has degraded. Vacuum in breached sections. Cold throughout — the RTG's thermal output is minimal and the station's climate systems are likely offline or running at survival minimum.

*Biological:* Unlikely. An automated orbital installation with no permanent crew and no biological hazard vector should be clean. This assessment is not a guarantee. First deployment caution applies.

*Data:* BlackICE on the monitoring data is possible but not expected — administrative and monitoring data is typically secured at lower levels than military or classified archives. Cade's assessment of the data environment before attempting interface is standard protocol.

**Rules of engagement:** Standard first-deployment protocol. Scout first. Team follows only on scout's all-clear or partial-clear with identified hazards. Extraction on Harsk's call if the security situation deteriorates. No heroics. Nobody dies on a shakedown run.

**Hale's tone during the briefing:** Professional, measured, the quieter register he uses for things that matter. He is aware this is the team's first outing. He is aware that everyone in the room knows it. He does not give a speech about teamwork or trust. He gives a clear briefing and trusts the team to be professional. The closest he comes to acknowledging the significance is at the end: "Questions. Ask them now. Once the shuttle's loaded, we're operating."

### Deployment

After the briefing, the session moves to deployment preparation and the shuttle ride to Keth-4-7.

**Preparation:** Equipment check in the shuttle bay. Si'ken's loadout is light — the scout's advantage is mobility and sensing, not gear. Communication rig (NIJack-integrated), personal auspex (handheld scanner supplementing the ambient sense), marker beacons for mapping (small devices left at route points for the team to follow), and a sidearm if Si'ken carries one (to be established — Si'ken's combat capability is the hunting suite and the chitin, not firearms).

The other team members load standard field equipment. Cade's interface kit. Davan's tool harness. Harsk's security detail with Mechanicus-standard autorepeaters and void-sealed flak. Sollis's medical satchel. Voss's field kit including the augmetic sampling array.

**The shuttle ride:** Si'ken in Shuttle One with the deployment team. Short transit — Keth is in-system. The shuttle's interior is designed for baseline crew. Si'ken's body doesn't fit the standard seating. He adapts — wall, ceiling, the spaces between seats that the insectoid form can occupy. The ambient sense reads the shuttle's systems: small, focused, a simple conversation compared to the Proving Ground's orchestra.

The team is quiet. Not tense — focused. First deployment energy. Everyone present has trained for this. Nobody has done it together.

**Approach:** The shuttle approaches Keth-4-7 from the survey bay's recommended vector — the angle that gives the best sensor return on the station's structural condition. Through the shuttle's viewports (or through Shuttle Three's reconnaissance pass if Hale orders a preliminary flyby), the team gets their first visual of the site.

**What they see:** A compact structure, clearly imperial in origin — the architecture has the angular, purposeful character that imperial construction maintained across its entire range. The monitoring arrays extend from the main body like antennae — long sensor booms radiating from the central cluster. Several show visible micrometeorite damage — pitting, bent elements, one array that has been sheared off entirely and is drifting nearby, still tethered by a cable. The main body appears intact. The hull shows pitting and scarring consistent with thirty thousand years of exposure but no catastrophic breaches visible from the approach angle. A faint thermal signature from the RTG section confirms power.

The station is dark. No running lights. No active emissions beyond the thermal signature. Whatever systems are still operating are doing so quietly, drawing minimal power, performing functions that don't require external indication.

**Docking or EVA entry:** The station may have a docking port compatible with the shuttle, or entry may require EVA to an airlock. Either works for the scenario. If EVA: Si'ken goes first, crosses to the hull, and assesses the airlock. If docking: the shuttle connects, and Si'ken enters first through the connecting passage.

**Session endpoint options:**
- End at the threshold — Si'ken at the airlock, about to enter. The team behind him. The station dark and waiting. *Day one. The ruins are waiting.*
- End partway in — Si'ken has entered, completed initial scouting of the first compartment, and reported back. The team is preparing to follow. What Si'ken found is the cliffhanger.

Either works. Let pacing determine which feels right.

## The Site: Monitoring Post Keth-4-7

### Physical Layout

The station is approximately 150 metres across its widest span (including sensor booms) and approximately 40 metres across the central habitable/equipment cluster. It was designed for automated operation with periodic crewed maintenance visits — not permanent habitation.

**Sections (from entry inward):**

**Airlock and access corridor:** Standard imperial automated-facility access. A cycling airlock designed for maintenance crew in void suits. Interior corridor leading to the main compartments. The corridor may retain atmosphere (sealed, the RTG's minimal thermal output keeping things above hard vacuum) or may be vacuum (seal failure over millennia).

**Monitoring control room:** The station's primary workspace — consoles, displays (all dark), the monitoring systems' interface stations. This is where a maintenance crew would have reviewed the station's output and performed system checks. The consoles connect to the data storage section. This is where Cade works.

**Data storage:** The station's archive — the locally retained monitoring data that Loth wants. Physical data cores in sealed racks, connected to the monitoring arrays through the station's internal architecture. The RTG's power feed keeps the storage systems in a minimal-power preservation state. The data should be intact if the power has remained consistent — thirty thousand years of radioisotope power maintaining a steady, low-draw preservation state.

**Sensor array control:** The junction between the monitoring arrays and the data systems. This section manages the arrays — which ones are active, what they're pointed at, what data filtering the station is applying to the incoming signal traffic. The arrays are the station's purpose-built equipment. If any are intact enough to recover, this is where the integration assessment happens (Davan and Vos).

**RTG compartment:** The radioisotope thermal generator — the station's power source. Sealed, shielded, still producing minimal output. The shielding should be intact (imperial construction, purpose-designed for millennia of operation), but the shielding's integrity must be confirmed before the team moves through adjacent sections. Si'ken's chitin provides moderate radiation resistance. The baseline crew in standard void-sealed flak does not have equivalent protection.

**Maintenance crew quarters:** A small habitable section designed for 2–4 maintenance crew during serviced periods. Bunks, basic life support, a food storage unit, personal effects of whoever was aboard when the station was last serviced. This section has the most human-scale detail — the closest thing to a lived space in an otherwise automated facility.

### What Si'ken Finds (Scout Phase)

When Si'ken enters the station, the ambient machine sense should register the station's systems immediately — differently from the Proving Ground. The ship is modern, busy, alive with purpose. The station is old, quiet, running on the minimum power required to maintain its core function. The machine conversation here is not a conversation. It is a vigil — systems that have been doing the same thing, alone, for thirty thousand years.

**Atmosphere:** Variable by section. The airlock and access corridor may be vacuum. The inner sections — control room, data storage, maintenance quarters — may retain a thin, cold atmosphere if the seals held and the life support system has been cycling at its lowest possible output. The air is not breathable by baseline crew standards without filtration. Si'ken's sealed respiratory system can handle it.

**Lighting:** Dark except where the RTG's power feed reaches active emergency lighting — a dim, amber glow from panels that have been running for millennia, some flickering, some dead, some steady. The insectoid visual system handles low light well. The baseline crew will need suit lights.

**Temperature:** Cold. Not hard vacuum cold in the sealed sections, but well below human comfort. Si'ken's chitin insulates adequately. The baseline crew's void-sealed gear handles it.

**Active systems:** The data storage preservation systems are running — a low hum that the machine sense registers as the station's heartbeat. The RTG is producing. Some monitoring control room systems may be in standby — not active, not off, waiting for input they haven't received in thirty thousand years. These systems are not hostile. They are patient.

**The data:** The monitoring data cores are intact. Cade can interface. The data is what the empire was watching — and the first indication of what that was comes from the monitoring control room's configuration: what the arrays were pointed at, what filtering the station was applying, what the station was told to look for.

### What the Station Was Watching (GM Information)

**The debris belt.** Keth-4-7 was monitoring the Calthris system's debris belt — specifically, tracking objects within the belt that exhibited non-random motion or energy signatures inconsistent with inert debris. The station's monitoring filter was set to flag movement patterns that suggested powered objects, energy emissions above background, and gravitational anomalies that didn't correspond to the belt's expected mass distribution.

**Why locally stored:** The monitoring was classified above the Calthris administrative hub's access level. The station received its instructions from a source that bypassed the hub's administrative chain. The data stayed local because the hub was not authorised to see it. This is why Loth's routing tables show the anomaly — data flowing into a node that never relayed outward.

**What the data contains:**
- Thousands of years of monitoring records, most routine (the belt was debris; the station watched debris)
- Periodic flagged events — energy spikes, motion anomalies, gravitational readings that the station's filters caught and archived for review by maintenance crews who may or may not have ever reviewed them
- A pattern in the flagged events that becomes apparent only in aggregate: something in the debris belt has been intermittently active over a very long period. Not continuously — the events are separated by years, sometimes decades, sometimes centuries. But they cluster geographically around a specific region of the belt.

**What this means for the campaign:** The debris belt contains something. The monitoring post was watching it. The data tells the expedition where to look. This is the hook for the next mission — a real deployment into the belt to investigate what the empire was tracking.

**How much of this to reveal in Session 01:** The monitoring data is the session's discovery. Si'ken doesn't need to understand it — Cade and Voss interpret it.
