# Archaeos Expedition — Campaign Framework

**Setting:** Gallihammer (Void / Planetary)
**Campaign Style:** Episodic exploration with persistent base development
**Format:** Variable-length missions (1-2 sessions for small recoveries, multi-session for major explorations) with interludes aboard ship
**Tone:** Exploration-horror, institutional tension, the sacred obligation of knowledge recovery vs. the reality of what lives in the ruins

---

## Core Concept

The PC is a member of a Mechanicus archaeological expedition — a dedicated team aboard an explorer-class vessel, tasked with surveying, entering, and recovering knowledge and technology from pre-collapse sites. The ship operates under the authority of a parent forge-monastery, receiving target coordinates, intelligence packets, and resupply in exchange for whatever it recovers.

The work is holy. It is also extraordinarily dangerous. The sites that contain the most valuable knowledge are the ones that have been sealed longest, guarded best, and changed most in the intervening millennia. The team goes in, documents what it finds, recovers what it can, and gets out — ideally before whatever occupies the site objects to the intrusion.

Between missions, the ship is home. The crew maintains equipment, processes findings, argues about theology, and waits for the next set of coordinates. The ship itself is a resource that develops over time — salvaged technology integrated into its systems, workshops expanded, capabilities improved. What the team brings back from the field doesn't just feed the monastery's archives. Some of it stays aboard, and the ship becomes more capable with each successful recovery.

## The Ship

### Classification

A dedicated Mechanicus expeditionary vessel — purpose-built or purpose-converted for archaeological recovery operations. Smaller than a full warship, larger than a scout: approximately 2-3 kilometres, carrying enough crew to sustain extended independent operations while remaining manoeuvrable enough to navigate debris fields, orbital ruins, and close planetary approaches that larger vessels would avoid.

The ship is not a combat vessel. It has defensive armament — point defence systems, a modest broadside battery — sufficient to discourage opportunistic raiders and survive long enough to break contact with a serious threat. It is not designed to win stand-up fights. Its survival strategy is avoidance, speed, and the hope that whatever it encounters has better things to shoot at.

What it does carry is workshop space, archive capacity, a well-equipped medicae bay, shuttle and lander bays for surface deployment, and the sensor arrays needed to survey sites from orbit before committing a ground team. The ship is a mobile laboratory and staging platform — everything the team needs to reach a site, deploy into it, and process what they recover before transit to the next target or return to the monastery.

### The XCOM Principle — Ship Development

The ship starts functional but limited. Workshop capacity is adequate for maintenance, not fabrication. Archive systems can store findings but not fully analyse them. The sensor arrays work but miss things a better system would catch. The shuttle bay holds what it holds.

Every successful mission brings back salvage — and salvage is potential. An intact imperial sensor module recovered from a dead station can be integrated into the ship's own arrays, extending detection range or adding capabilities the original fit-out lacked. A recovered fabrication template means the workshop can produce something it couldn't before. A pre-collapse medical system gives the medicae bay capabilities that save lives on the next mission.

This is the progression loop: the team deploys, recovers, returns. Some of what they recover goes to the monastery (obligation, politics, the terms of the charter). Some stays aboard (operational necessity, crew initiative, creative accounting). The ship improves. The next mission becomes possible that the last one wasn't.

**Development categories:**

**Sensors and Survey** — Better arrays mean better pre-deployment intelligence. Knowing what's in a ruin before you enter it is the difference between preparation and surprise. Upgrades here reduce unknowns.

**Workshop and Fabrication** — The ship's ability to build, repair, and modify equipment. Early-game, the workshop handles maintenance and basic repairs. Late-game, with enough recovered templates and tooling, it can fabricate specialist equipment for specific missions — breaching tools for a sealed facility, environmental gear for a toxic site, hardened data-recovery rigs for a BlackICE-defended archive.

**Archive and Analysis** — Processing capacity for recovered data, artefacts, and specimens. Better archive systems mean faster analysis, which means the team understands what they've found sooner — sometimes soon enough to matter during a multi-session exploration.

**Medical and Life Support** — The medicae bay and the ship's environmental systems. Better medical capability means the team survives injuries that would otherwise end careers. Better life support means the ship can sustain longer independent operations and support crew with non-standard biological requirements — relevant if a Remade PC is aboard.

**Shuttle Bay and Deployment** — The team's ability to reach sites. More shuttles, better landers, specialist deployment vehicles (submersibles for underwater sites, shielded crawlers for irradiated zones, void-capable pods for orbital wreck penetration). Upgrades here expand the range of sites the team can physically access.

**Armament and Defence** — Not the priority, but not ignorable. Better point defence keeps the ship alive when something hostile takes an interest. A recovered imperial weapons system changes the threat calculus entirely — but also draws attention from anyone who learns the ship is carrying it.

**Crew Capacity and Quarters** — The ship can only support so many people. Expanding habitation space, improving life support efficiency, or adding specialist facilities (a proper biologis lab, an arcanum containment chamber) means the team can grow, diversify, and take on missions that require expertise it didn't have at campaign start.

### The Monastery's Cut

The parent forge-monastery funded this expedition. It expects returns. The charter specifies what proportion of recovered material goes back to the monastery and what the ship retains for operational use. In practice, this is negotiable — a ship that consistently delivers high-value recoveries earns latitude. A ship that hoards everything and delivers scraps earns a recall order.

The tension between what the team wants to keep (everything useful) and what the monastery demands (everything valuable) is a persistent background pressure. The expedition commander navigates this relationship, and how well they do it determines how much freedom the ship has.

Monastery representatives may be aboard — an archivist assigned to catalogue recoveries, a magos observing the expedition's methods, a political appointee whose function is unclear and whose loyalty is to the forge-master rather than the ship's commander. These individuals are crew but also auditors, and the team's relationship with them shapes what happens to the good finds.

## The Team

### Command Structure

**Expedition Commander** — The senior authority aboard. Usually a magos or senior adept with both archaeological expertise and enough political standing to have been entrusted with a ship. Responsible for mission selection (within the monastery's targeting directives), deployment decisions, and managing the relationship with the parent forge. Not necessarily the best archaeologist on board — sometimes the best politician. Sometimes both.

**Ship Master** — Responsible for the vessel itself: navigation, transit, engineering, defence. The ship master keeps the ship alive and moving. Their authority is absolute in matters of ship safety and can override the expedition commander if the ship is threatened. This creates a productive tension — the commander wants to stay at a site longer; the ship master wants to leave before the local threat escalates.

**Chief Archaeologist** — The senior field operative. Plans deployments, leads major expeditions, makes the call on what's worth the risk inside a site. Reports to the expedition commander but has operational autonomy once the team is deployed — the commander can order the team in or out, but the chief archaeologist decides how they move once they're inside.

### Field Team Roles

The deployment team — the people who actually enter the sites — is structured around complementary capabilities:

**Scouts** — First in. Scouts assess the environment before the main team commits: structural stability, atmospheric conditions, biological or mechanical hazards, signs of occupation. A good scout returns with a map of what the team is walking into. A great scout returns with a map that includes the things that don't want to be found.

Scouts operate ahead of the team, often alone or in pairs, in environments where the margin between discovery and death is measured in seconds of reaction time. They need speed, awareness, and the kind of survival instinct that keeps them moving when the sensible response is to freeze. Their equipment prioritises mobility and sensing over protection — light armour, advanced auspex arrays, environmental samplers, and whatever personal modifications let them operate in places baseline humans cannot.

This is where a Remade PC fits naturally. A scout whose body has been reshaped — whether biologically or cybernetically — into something that can survive environments that would kill unmodified crew is not a liability. They are an asset. An insectoid form that can navigate collapsed structures too unstable for a person in armour. A chassis with integrated sensors that see in spectra human eyes cannot. A body that doesn't need to breathe standard atmosphere, or that can squeeze through gaps no baseline skeleton could manage. The team knows what the scout is. They may not be comfortable with it. But they deploy them first because the scout comes back with information that keeps everyone else alive.

**Archaeologists** — The core specialists. They read the ruins — structural analysis, artefact identification, site chronology, hazard assessment based on what the empire built here and what it's become since. An archaeologist in the field is part historian, part engineer, and part detective, reconstructing what happened in a facility thirty thousand years ago from whatever evidence survived the intervening millennia of decay, occupation, and active hostility.

**Logiks / Data Recovery Specialists** — The people who interface with whatever information systems the site contains. Data cores, cogitation arrays, archival terminals, communication logs. Their work is the most valuable thing the expedition does — recovered data is worth more than any physical artefact — and also the most dangerous. BlackICE doesn't care how carefully you approach it. Logiks work in pairs or with dedicated security precisely because the moment they jack in is the moment they're most vulnerable.

**Enginseers** — Field maintenance and technical problem-solving. The enginseer keeps equipment running in hostile conditions, assesses recovered technology for safety and function, and handles the mechanical challenges the site presents — sealed doors, active defence systems, power systems that need to be coaxed back to life or safely shut down. An enginseer in the field is the person who figures out how to open things that don't want to be opened.

**Skitarii Security** — Armed escort. Sites are dangerous not just environmentally but occupationally — things live in the ruins, and some of them are territorial, intelligent, or both. The skitarii detachment provides security during deployment: perimeter watch, threat response, fighting retreat when the situation deteriorates beyond the team's ability to manage. A standard deployment includes enough skitarii to hold a defensive position while the specialists work and to cover the extraction when it's time to leave.

**Medicae / Chirugen** — Field medicine. Injuries in the field range from environmental exposure to structural collapse to things that defy medical categorisation. The field medicae keeps the team functional long enough to extract. Serious cases go back to the ship's medicae bay. The worst cases don't make it back at all, and the medicae carries that weight into the next deployment.

**Biologis** — Present when the site involves biological hazards, specimens, or biological technology. A biologis assesses organic threats, identifies engineered organisms, and handles biological material that the rest of the team knows not to touch. Not always deployed — some sites are purely mechanical. But when a site involves the empire's bioweapons, the biologis is the person who keeps the team from bringing something home that shouldn't leave containment.

### Shipboard Roles

Between deployments, the ship sustains its own ecosystem:

**Archivists** — Processing and cataloguing what the field team recovered. Analysis, cross-referencing with existing records, assessment of value and significance. This is where raw salvage becomes knowledge.

**Workshop crew** — Enginseers and fabricators maintaining the ship and its equipment, and integrating recovered technology into the ship's systems. The XCOM progression happens here — the workshop crew are the ones who take a recovered sensor module and make it part of the ship.

**Navigation and transit crew** — Getting the ship where it needs to go. Warp transit is handled by the navigator (if the ship has one) or by careful use of established routes. The transit crew manages everything else: in-system manoeuvring, orbital insertion, approach vectors to sites.

**Support staff** — Menials, lay crew, the people who keep the ship fed, clean, and liveable. They outnumber the adepts and the work matters — a ship that can't feed its crew doesn't get to the next site.

## The Remade Scout

The Remade Origin Framework slots directly into this campaign. A PC who has been reshaped — biologically, cybernetically, or both — and subsequently escaped or been recovered by the expedition operates as a scout with capabilities no baseline crew member can match.

### How They Got Here

**Recovered from a site** — The team entered a ruin and found the PC. A patron's discarded experiment, a control-locked servitor whose obedience firmware degraded, a biological reshaping left in a stasis chamber when the facility was abandoned. The expedition commander made the call to bring them aboard rather than leave them. Whether this was compassion, pragmatism, or curiosity depends on the commander.

**Assigned by the monastery** — The forge-monastery acquired the PC through channels that were not fully explained and attached them to the expedition as a specialist asset. The team was told to use them. The team was not told where they came from. Questions about the PC's origin are met with the institutional equivalent of "that's classified."

**Volunteered** — The PC escaped their patron independently and sought out the Mechanicus as the only institution that might value them for what they can do rather than what they look like. The expedition needed a scout who could survive environments that kill baseline crew. The PC needed a purpose and a place. The arrangement is transactional and may become something more.

**Created for purpose** — The monastery's own biologis or cyberwright division produced the PC specifically for expeditionary scouting. This is the most institutionally comfortable option and the most ethically uncomfortable one — the PC was made, not recruited, and the question of whether they consented to being made is a question the monastery would prefer not to answer clearly.

### The Scout's Position

Within the team, the Remade scout occupies an ambiguous space. They are valued — undeniably, practically valued. They go where no one else can go and come back with information that saves lives. The chief archaeologist relies on them. The skitarii respect their capabilities even if they don't understand their nature.

But they are not normal crew. They don't look like crew. They may not eat like crew, sleep like crew, or fit in the same spaces as crew. Their quarters may need to be modified. Their medical needs may be outside the field medicae's training. Their psychological needs — the weight of inhabiting a body that was forced on them, the question of whether they are a person or an asset — are not things the team is equipped to address.

The ship is small enough that everyone knows everyone. The Remade scout is known. How they are treated — with respect, with wariness, with pragmatic acceptance, with something approaching friendship — depends on the crew, and on what the PC does with the space they're given.

## Mission Types

### Quick Recovery (1-2 Sessions)

A small site with a specific target. A wrecked shuttle with a data core. A surface ruin with accessible lower levels. A derelict satellite with intact sensor logs. The team deploys, the scout goes in first, the specialists follow, they grab what they came for and extract. Complications arise from the site's condition, its occupants, or the discovery that the target is more than expected — more valuable, more dangerous, or both.

Quick recoveries are the bread and butter of the expedition. Most missions are this scale. They test the team's competence without committing to a multi-session investment, and a string of successful quick recoveries is what funds the equipment and upgrades that make larger operations possible.

### Standard Exploration (3-5 Sessions)

A significant site requiring sustained deployment. A sealed pre-collapse facility. A partially intact orbital station. A ship graveyard with multiple hulks worth surveying. The team deploys with full equipment, establishes a forward position, and works the site methodically — the scout mapping ahead, the archaeologists assessing, the logiks probing data systems, the skitarii holding the perimeter as the team pushes deeper.

Standard explorations are where the real discoveries happen — and where the real casualties occur. The deeper the team goes, the further they are from extraction. The longer they stay, the more the site's occupants notice them. Pacing is critical: push too fast and miss something important or walk into something unprepared; push too slow and the window closes — structurally, environmentally, or because something wakes up.

### Major Expedition (6+ Sessions)

A site large enough or complex enough to require basecamp operations. A pre-collapse underground city. An intact imperial research facility spanning multiple levels. A planetary surface site covering square kilometres of ruins. The team establishes a basecamp with supplies, communications, and defensive positions, then conducts operations from the camp over days or weeks of in-game time.

Major expeditions are campaign centrepieces. They involve the full team, consume significant resources, and produce findings that can reshape the expedition's capability — or its understanding of what the empire was doing in this particular corner of the void. They also involve the highest stakes: a basecamp that gets overrun, a team that gets cut off from the ship, a discovery that is too dangerous to recover but too important to leave.

Basecamp management becomes a factor: supply lines, rotation of personnel, defence in depth, the gradual mapping of a site that reveals its structure session by session. The XCOM element is strongest here — what the team found on Level 3 determines whether they can access Level 5, and what they recover from the data core on Level 5 might change how they understand everything they've seen so far.

### Shipboard Interludes

Between missions, play shifts to the ship. These sessions handle:

**Processing** — The archivists and logiks analyse what the team recovered. Findings are documented, assessed, and categorised. Some require further analysis. Some raise more questions than they answer. Some are immediately useful — a recovered schematic that the workshop can act on now.

**Integration** — The workshop crew installs recovered technology into the ship. This is where the XCOM progression is felt most directly: the player sees the ship change, sees capabilities expand, sees the next mission become possible because of what the last one provided.

**Crew dynamics** — Interpersonal scenes. The team decompresses after a hard deployment. Relationships develop, tensions surface, arguments about theology and methodology play out in the corridors and common spaces. The Remade scout navigates their place in this community — welcomed by some, tolerated by others, observed by the monastery's representative.

**Communication with the monastery** — Reports, requisitions, new targeting directives. The monastery sends coordinates for the next site. The expedition commander negotiates what they need to attempt it. The monastery's representative aboard weighs in on what was recovered and what should be sent back. Politics.

**Maintenance and preparation** — The ship needs upkeep. Equipment needs servicing. The team needs to prepare for the next deployment — studying whatever intelligence is available about the target site, planning their approach, loading supplies.

## Site Hazard Categories

What the team encounters in the field falls into broad categories, though any given site may combine several:

**Structural** — The ruins are old. They collapse, they flood, they vent atmosphere through breaches that weren't there yesterday. Gravity may be compromised in orbital sites. Environmental seals may fail without warning. The scout's primary job is assessing structural stability before the team commits — and recognising when the structure is telling them to leave.

**Environmental** — Radiation, toxic atmosphere, extreme temperature, biological contamination. The empire's facilities were often built in or for conditions that baseline humans cannot survive without protection. Thirty thousand years of degradation may have made conditions worse. The Remade scout may be able to operate in environments that would require the rest of the team to suit up — or that would kill them regardless.

**Biological** — Things live in the ruins. Some are natural colonists that moved in after the site was abandoned. Some are engineered organisms — bioweapons, bio-security systems, experimental subjects — that have had thirty millennia to evolve, multiply, and become territorial. The most dangerous biological hazards are the ones that were designed to be dangerous and have only gotten better at it.

**Mechanical** — Active defence systems, automated security, maintenance drones still following routines for a facility that no longer exists in any functional sense. Mechanical hazards are predictable once understood — they follow programming — but the programming may have drifted, corrupted, or been deliberately modified by previous occupants.

**BlackICE and Data Hazards** — The empire's information security did not expire when the empire did. Data cores protected by intrusion countermeasures will attack anyone who attempts unauthorised access through whatever interface they're using — and for augmented Mechanicus operatives, that interface is wired into their nervous system. A BlackICE strike can disable augmetics, corrupt neural implants, or command cybernetic components to harm their owner. Logiks trained for hostile data recovery are the most cautious people on the team for good reason.

**Occupants** — Human or otherwise. Salvager crews who got there first and don't want to share. Feral communities that have lived in the site for generations. Xeno groups using the facility for their own purposes. Things that were human once and aren't anymore. The team's rules of engagement vary by situation — some occupants can be negotiated with, some must be avoided, some must be fought. The chief archaeologist and the skitarii detachment leader make these calls together, and they don't always agree.

**The Unknowable** — Certain sites contain things that don't fit any category. Phenomena that the team's training and equipment cannot explain or counter. Arcane residue, warp contamination, temporal anomalies, spatial geometry that doesn't conform to the architecture. These are the sites that produce the most valuable findings and the most disturbing debriefings. The reports that go back to the monastery from these deployments are read carefully and shared reluctantly.

## PC Roles

Any of the field team positions work as a PC role. The Remade scout is the flagship option, but the campaign supports:

**Scout (Baseline or Remade)** — First in, mapping, hazard assessment. High autonomy, high risk. The player sees the site before anyone else and makes the first calls about what's safe and what isn't.

**Archaeologist** — The knowledge specialist. Reading the ruins, identifying artefacts, reconstructing site history. The player pieces together what happened in the facility and why it matters now.

**Logik / Data Recovery** — The interface specialist. Jacking into ancient systems, navigating data architectures, duelling BlackICE. High-stakes technical play — the data is the mission's most valuable product, and getting it out is the most dangerous part.

**Enginseer** — The problem-solver. Opening doors, bypassing defences, keeping equipment running, assessing recovered tech. The player is the one who figures out how things work — and how to make them work for the team.

**Skitarii Squad Leader** — The security specialist. Protecting the team, managing threats, making the call on when to fight and when to run. The player controls the tactical situation and carries the weight of every decision about force.

**Medicae** — The field medic. Keeping the team alive in conditions that human bodies were not designed for. The player faces the consequences of every site hazard directly — they're the one who sees the injuries, treats the exposures, and makes the calls about who can keep going and who needs to extract.

**Biologis** — The specialist for living hazards. Present when the site involves bio-threats, specimens, or biological technology. The player is the expert on the most unpredictable category of danger — things that adapt, that mutate, that don't behave the same way twice.

Multiple PCs from the same team work naturally — a scout and a logik operating together in the deep levels while the archaeologist and enginseer work a different section is exactly how a real deployment would run.

## Campaign Progression

### Early Campaign

The ship is functional but basic. The team is competent but unproven. Missions are quick recoveries — small sites, known quantities, manageable risk. The expedition commander is cautious. The monastery's directives are conservative. The team is building its record and its capability simultaneously.

The Remade scout (if present) is new to the team. Trust is being established. Crew reactions range from curiosity to discomfort. The scout's value is theoretical until the first deployment proves it practical.

### Mid Campaign

Successful recoveries have improved the ship. The workshop can fabricate specialist equipment. The sensor arrays see further. The archive contains enough data that the archivists can provide real intelligence before deployment rather than educated guesses. The team has survived enough to know each other — strengths, weaknesses, the person you want beside you when the site goes bad.

Missions scale up: standard explorations, sites that require planning and sustained deployment. The monastery's directives become more ambitious as the expedition proves its capability. The political relationship with the monastery develops — the expedition has earned latitude, but the monastery wants bigger returns to justify that latitude.

The Remade scout is established. Their place on the team is no longer provisional. What that place looks like — trusted colleague, valued tool, something in between — has been shaped by play.

### Late Campaign

The ship is significantly upgraded — recovered imperial technology integrated into multiple systems. The team is experienced and capable. The monastery trusts the expedition with its highest-priority targets: the sites that previous expeditions failed to crack, the coordinates that come from sources the monastery won't explain, the ruins that other factions are also hunting.

Major expeditions dominate: basecamp operations, multi-session explorations, discoveries that have implications beyond the immediate mission. The team faces threats at the upper end of the hazard scale — intact defence systems, intelligent occupants with their own agendas, phenomena that push the boundaries of what the team's training prepared them for.

The ship may be approaching the point where its capability exceeds what the monastery is comfortable with. A vessel that has integrated enough imperial technology becomes a strategic asset, and strategic assets attract attention — from the monastery's leadership, from rival factions, from parties who want what the ship is carrying.

## Skill Tree Integration

This campaign uses the Emergent Skill Tree System (Core_Rules/Skill_Trees.md). The expedition format — repeated high-stakes deployments across varied environments — is a strong driver for all six foundation archetypes and a natural generator of discovered trees.

### Foundation Tree Development

**Body** — Field deployments push Combat Form, Conditioning, and Endurance hard. Sites involve physical hazards, hostile occupants, and conditions that test the body's limits every session. A scout PC will develop Body faster than most roles simply because they are first into every environment.

**Mind** — Perception is the scout's primary tool. Knowledge develops through accumulated site experience — recognising imperial architectural patterns, identifying hazard signatures, reading the history written in decay. Focus matters when the environment is actively hostile to concentration: radiation, atmospheric contamination, the disorienting geometry of compromised orbital structures.

**Social** — Slower to develop than Body or Mind unless the PC actively engages with crew dynamics during shipboard interludes. Presence and Read develop through interactions with the team — earning trust, navigating the Remade's ambiguous social position, dealing with the monastery's representative. Connection develops through sustained relationships aboard the ship.

**Craft** — Making and Systems develop through field problem-solving: jury-rigging equipment in hostile conditions, understanding how imperial facilities were built and how they've degraded. Formulation is relevant for biologis-adjacent PCs or any character who works with recovered compounds or environmental hazards.

**Edge** — The expedition's bread and butter. Survival is tested every deployment. Cunning is what separates a scout who maps a route from a scout who maps the route that avoids what's hunting them. Stealth is core to scouting operations — moving through occupied ruins without triggering whatever lives there.

### Essence

For a Remade PC, Essence initializes at the origin event — the reshaping, whether biological, cybernetic, or hybrid. The tree is named at that moment, specific to the character's experience.

Potential Essence branches for an Archaeos Expedition Remade scout:

**Form Integration** — Adapting to the reshaped body. Moving naturally in a form that was designed for a purpose the PC has rejected. Learning what the body can do when directed by a mind that chooses its own objectives. Early nodes are basic motor control and spatial awareness in the new form. Later nodes represent the point where the body stops feeling alien and starts feeling like *theirs*.

**Sensory Translation** — Using non-human senses effectively. A Remade scout may perceive through echolocation, chemoreception, vibration sensing, electromagnetic awareness, or spectra that human eyes cannot process. Early nodes are raw perception — the scout detects things but struggles to interpret them. Later nodes represent fluency: the scout reads the environment through their new senses as naturally as a baseline human reads a room with their eyes.

**Chassis Interface** (cybernetic Remade) — Communicating through and with the mechanical body. Reading diagnostic data, interfacing with external systems through the chassis's ports, understanding the machine that houses them. This branch can cross-pollinate with Craft/Systems when the PC begins applying their intimate knowledge of their own chassis to understanding other mechanical systems.

**Instinct Navigation** (biological Remade) — Managing the drives and reflexes that came with the new form. The body was built for a purpose — combat, labour, environmental survival — and it has instincts that serve that purpose. The PC must decide which instincts to use, which to suppress, and which to redirect. Later nodes represent the integration point where the PC's will and the body's drives are no longer in conflict but in dialogue.

### Discovered Trees

The expedition's episodic format is a natural generator of discovered trees. Each new site can expose the PC to something they've never encountered before:

**Imperial systems** — A PC who interfaces with a specific class of imperial technology (a navigation array, a biological containment system, an arcanum-adjacent power source) may develop a discovered tree around that encounter if engagement is sustained and meaningful.

**Arcane phenomena** — Sites contaminated by warp energy or containing arcanum-adjacent systems can produce discovered trees in PCs who are sensitive to or affected by these phenomena. The Remade may be particularly susceptible depending on the nature of their reshaping.

**Xeno contact** — Sustained interaction with a xeno species or xeno technology could generate a discovered tree — a framework for understanding something genuinely alien that the PC's existing knowledge cannot accommodate.

**BlackICE survival** — A logik or data-recovery PC who survives a serious BlackICE encounter may develop a discovered tree around hostile data navigation — not a Craft/Systems branch but something more personal, shaped by the specific trauma and insight of having a hostile intelligence reach into their augmetics.

### Tracking

Skill tree tracking follows the standard format from Skill_Tree_Block.md. Given the episodic structure, natural review points for mark assessment are:

- End of each deployment (field performance)
- End of each shipboard interlude (crew dynamics, processing, integration work)
- After major expeditions (concentrated development across multiple sessions)

The GM tracks marks silently per standard rules. Gates emerge from the fiction — a deployment that goes wrong in a specific way, a discovery that changes how the PC understands their capabilities, a moment with a crew member that reshapes the PC's social position aboard the ship.

## Connections

This framework connects to existing Gallihammer content:

**The Mechanicus** — The expedition operates within the institutional structure described in The_Mechanicus.md. All branches, ranks, and cultural elements apply.

**Rogue Trader** — The ship may encounter rogue trader vessels, interact with void-based communities, or visit sites in systems described in the Rogue Trader setting material. An expedition vessel and a rogue trader ship occupying the same system have immediate reasons to cooperate and compete.

**Dead Terra** — If the expedition reaches Earth, the Dead Terra setting provides the ground-level environment. Archaeological sites on Earth are the most mythologised targets in the Mechanicus's institutional imagination — and the most dangerous.

**Remade Origin Framework** — The Remade scout option draws directly from this framework. The origin sequence (capture, commission, reshaping, flaw, escape) can be played as a prologue or established in session zero.

**Bioservitor / Cyberservitor** — Setting material for the biological and cybernetic reshaping methodologies that may have produced the Remade PC.
