# Rogue Trader — Setting Overview

## The Space Between

The stellar empire that scarred Earth stretched across tens of thousands of systems and lasted thousands of years. What it left behind, when it died, was not nothing. It left ships. It left stations. It left colonies cut off from resupply, outposts broadcasting into silence, and fleets that turned privateer when there was no longer anyone to serve.

It left the void, vast and indifferent, full of things that were there before humanity arrived and will be there long after.

---

## The Ships

Empire vessels were monuments to industrial ambition on a scale that modern polities can barely comprehend, let alone replicate. The largest — fleet carriers, world-barges, mobile dockyards — ran to twenty or thirty kilometres stem to stern, crewed by tens of thousands across multiple internal decks that functioned as self-contained districts. Some were effectively cities that happened to travel between stars. A handful still exist, crewed by populations that have lived aboard for generations and may no longer remember — or care — that there is a surface to land on.

The smallest warp-capable vessels run two to three kilometres. Anything smaller cannot survive the transit.

Across tens of thousands of systems, the empire fielded fleets numbering in the thousands of hulls. Nobody does that anymore. The math of rebuilding that kind of capacity from scratch — system by system, decade by decade — is not math anyone currently alive will see completed.

**Modern shipbuilding** exists, but it is a shadow of what came before. A handful of systems have retained or reconstructed enough industrial capacity to fabricate warp-capable hulls — a genuine achievement that most cannot match. The practical ceiling for modern construction is frigate and destroyer scale: vessels in the three to six kilometre range, taking years to build, representing the total industrial output of a system for the duration. Something on the scale of an old heavy cruiser — ten kilometres, heavy armour, full weapon decks — would be considered a generational undertaking, the kind of project that defines a polity's entire political agenda for decades.

And even then, what rolls out of a modern shipyard is not what the empire built.

**Modern warships** rely on cannon batteries, laser arrays, and plasma weaponry — effective, understood, maintainable. They fight the way wet-navy ironclads fought: broadsides, range management, attrition. It works, within the limits of what they're likely to encounter.

**Old empire warships** operated in a different category of violence entirely.

The weapons systems on imperial heavy combatants were not extensions of conventional physics — they were exploitations of it. Singularity cannons collapsed localised gravity wells in the path of enemy vessels, tearing hulls apart at the molecular level or swallowing them whole. Temporal displacement fields could phase a ship fractionally out of the present moment, causing incoming fire to pass through space the vessel had technically already vacated. Lance batteries fired coherent beams of energy that could punch through the armour of a modern frigate like paper and keep going for another hundred kilometres. Point defence systems could track and intercept incoming fire faster than any modern targeting computer can aim.

An intact imperial heavy cruiser, fully crewed and operational, would be essentially invulnerable to anything a modern polity could field. The good news is that fully operational imperial heavy cruisers are extraordinarily rare. The bad news is that *partially* operational ones are not, and even a vessel running at a fraction of its designed capacity is a nightmare to fight.

This creates an entire sub-economy around old imperial weapons technology. A salvager who pulls a working singularity cannon from a graveyard wreck and gets it onto a modern hull has changed the regional power balance. Everyone knows it. The competition for intact imperial weapons systems is brutal, secretive, and frequently fatal.

The deeper problem with imperial weapons technology — and imperial systems generally — is that maintaining them is nearly as impossible as building them. The engineering principles behind a singularity cannon or a temporal displacement field are not fully understood by anyone alive. The manuals, where they exist at all, describe procedures without explaining the underlying physics. Technicians can follow the steps. They cannot diagnose what's wrong when the steps stop working.

The result is that captured or salvaged imperial systems have a lifespan. Sometimes a long one — certain systems have been kept functional for centuries through obsessive ritual maintenance and a great deal of luck. But every firing stresses components that cannot be replaced. Every transit cycle degrades systems that cannot be recalibrated from first principles. The clock runs in one direction only. A ship running imperial weapons is a ship slowly spending down a fortune it cannot replenish, and everyone aboard knows it.

This makes the engineers and tech-priests who *do* understand fragments of imperial technology some of the most valuable — and most dangerous — people in the void. Dangerous because the knowledge they carry is leverage, and leverage attracts violence. Guilds, cults, and polities hoard imperial technical knowledge the same way they hoard working hardware. Sometimes more fiercely. A cannon can be destroyed. Knowledge, if it escapes, cannot be unlearned.

## Warp Travel

The empire developed faster-than-light travel through what is now called warp transit — punching through a layer of reality that doesn't behave like normal space and emerging somewhere else. The physics of it were understood once. Now they are approximated, and the approximations are religious in character.

Warp transit works. It also does things to people. Extended transit — weeks, months, the kind of journey required to cross significant interstellar distances — leaves marks. Minor: strange dreams, mild perceptual drift, time perception anomalies. Major: depending on the ship, the route, the experience of the navigator, and factors nobody has ever successfully quantified. Crews that transit regularly develop cultures shaped by it. Some develop other things.

Navigators — individuals capable of guiding a ship through warp — are among the most protected people in any fleet. They are also, frequently, among the strangest.

## Ship Graveyards

Wherever the empire fought its wars — and it fought them everywhere — it left its dead. Ship graveyards drift in orbit around contested systems, in the debris fields of old battles, in the outer reaches where crippled vessels lost power and just kept moving. Some graveyards are small — a handful of hulks. Some are enormous — hundreds of vessels in loose association, tumbling slowly in the dark.

They are dangerous for multiple reasons.

The obvious one: structural instability. Thirty thousand year old hulls breach without warning. Atmosphere vents. Power cores fail in spectacular ways.

The less obvious one: occupation. Thirty thousand years is a long time. Things move into empty spaces. Some of them are human — salvagers, outcasts, communities that found a pressurised compartment and decided to stay. Some of them arrived after the ships did. Some of them were aboard when the ships died and have been there ever since, changed by the intervening millennia into something that only distantly resembles what they were.

The most intact wrecks are the most dangerous. If a ship survived well enough to be worth boarding, there's usually a reason it's still in one piece.

## Xeno Threats

Humanity is not alone in the void and never was. The empire encountered hundreds of spacefaring civilisations across its history and tens of thousands of systems — some it traded with, some it absorbed, some it destroyed, some it failed to destroy despite significant effort. It was never friendly in the way idealists imagine, but it was pragmatic. Coexistence happened when it was cheaper than war.

The collapse changed the equation. Borders dissolved. Navy presence evaporated. Polities that had maintained careful, grudging relationships with xeno neighbours suddenly found themselves exposed, and the xeno neighbours found themselves unconstrained. Some kept the peace. Some didn't. The distinction matters enormously to the humans who survived the difference.

The result is a patchwork. Systems and polities that have shared space with the same xeno groups for generations have often worked out functional arrangements — trade relationships, territorial understandings, the kind of mutual tolerance that comes from needing each other to survive. It isn't warmth. It is calculation, and it holds as long as the calculation holds.

A new xeno contact — a species not previously encountered, or one arriving from outside the local volume — is a different matter entirely. Suspicion is immediate and deep, and it doesn't fade quickly. The logic is sound: the void is full of things that want to kill you, and an unknown is an unknown. New contacts are watched, tested, kept at distance. Trade may eventually happen. Something approaching trust may eventually develop. But *eventually* is measured in decades at minimum, and some polities never get there at all.

Marauding xeno fleets remain a genuine threat — some opportunistic, some pursuing goals nobody has deciphered, some apparently fleeing something worse further out in the dark. Contact protocols vary by polity. A few maintain standing diplomatic channels with known groups. Most arm first and negotiate from a position of demonstrated force. The ones that don't tend not to last long enough to revise their approach.

## The Leviathans

Deep space — the space between stars, not the settled systems — is not empty.

Large organisms exist in the void. How large varies by account, and accounts are not always reliable, but the most credible reports suggest creatures ranging from a few hundred metres to something approaching the scale of small moons. They move through deep space on trajectories that don't correspond to any gravitational model. They appear to feed on something — energy, radiation, perhaps the warp itself. They are not aggressive in any purposeful sense. They are simply enormous and largely indifferent to the small metal objects that occasionally cross their path.

Occasionally one drifts into a settled system. This is an event. Sometimes they pass through without incident. Sometimes they don't. There are no reliable methods for redirecting them — the empire apparently had protocols, now lost — and so most polities simply watch and hope.

Some cults worship them. Some hunters pursue them. A very small number of researchers are trying to understand them. None of these groups have thus far achieved much.

## Polities and Power

The interstellar political map is fragmented almost beyond description. Surviving empire successor states, rebuilt colony worlds, pirate confederacies, generation ship communities, xeno-adjacent hybrid cultures — all of them operating with partial information about a volume of space too large for any of them to fully survey.

Power in space is measured in ships. Diplomacy is measured in what your guns can reach. Trade exists because everyone needs things they can't produce, and the ships that carry goods between systems are simultaneously the most valuable and most vulnerable assets anyone owns.

Rogue traders — independent captains operating outside any single polity's authority, licensed or unlicensed — fill the gaps. They go where navy ships won't. They carry what official merchants can't. They know routes that aren't on any official chart. They are, depending on the polity and the circumstances, heroes, pirates, or both simultaneously.

## The Connection to Earth

Earth is not forgotten by those who travel the void. It is mythologised, like everything else about the empire — the cradle world, the origin point, the place the empire began and the place it came back to die. Some factions seek it as a pilgrimage destination. Some seek it for political legitimacy — whoever controls Earth controls a symbol, and symbols matter when you're trying to hold a fragile polity together. Some seek it for what's buried under thirty thousand years of ruin.

Most ships' navigators know where it is. Whether they'd willingly go there is another question.
