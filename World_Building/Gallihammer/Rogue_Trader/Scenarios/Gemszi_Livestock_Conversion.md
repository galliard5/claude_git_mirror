# Gemszi — Livestock Conversion

**Setting:** Rogue Trader — aboard a black-market trader vessel, then frontier agri-world Erdfall
**PC:** Gemszi (kobold male, ship crew, pre-conversion)
**Tone:** Slow horror, isolation, adaptation. Character-driven.
**Estimated Duration:** Open-ended (conversion arc + farm life arc)

---

## Overview

Gemszi is a kobold crewman aboard the *Geld Maw*, a mid-tonnage trader with a reputation for black-market commerce and a captain who treats crew as inventory — assets with variable liquidation value. Caught stealing extra rations from the galley stores, Gemszi is sentenced not to brig time or flogging but to conversion: the ship's biomancer remakes him into draught livestock for sale at the next supply stop.

The conversion is total. Gemszi's kobold body is rebuilt from the ground up into a heavy-framed quadruped — bovine-adjacent, built for sustained draught work, with a secondary dairy installation and subdermal armour plating suited to frontier conditions. His genitals are removed as unnecessary to the product specification. His vocal apparatus is restructured for the new form — he can low, grunt, breathe hard under load, but cannot produce speech. No vox unit is installed. A talking draught animal raises questions.

His mind is untouched. Memory, personality, cognition — all intact, all present, all trapped inside a body that cannot communicate what it contains.

He is sold at Erdfall, a frontier agri-world, to a farmer who has no reason to suspect he is purchasing anything other than unusually well-built stock. The ship leaves. Gemszi is alone on a world that has no concept of what he is.

**Core Conflict:** Identity without expression. Gemszi is a person inside a form that everyone around him reads as an animal. The scenario explores what he holds onto, what slips, and whether the gap between what he is and what he appears to be can ever be bridged.

## Setup & Background

### The Geld Maw

A vessel operating at the margins of legitimate trade. The *Geld Maw* runs supplies, salvage, and contraband between frontier worlds and mid-tier systems, taking contracts that reputable traders won't touch. The captain maintains enough surface legitimacy to dock at regulated ports but the crew knows the real money comes from the black-market work — stolen cargo, unlicensed biomantic products, and occasionally, converted prisoners sold as livestock or labour organisms to frontier buyers who don't inspect provenance.

The ship's internal culture is transactional. Crew are useful until they aren't. Discipline is handled economically — infractions cost the offender something, and the ship profits from the cost. Minor offences draw pay docking or extra shifts. Serious offences — or offences committed by crew whose remaining value is low — draw conversion. The biomancer's workshop is below the medical bay. Everyone aboard knows what happens there.

Kobolds occupy the lowest tier of the ship's social hierarchy. Gemszi was galley support — cleaning, hauling, the work nobody else wanted. His rations were already short. Stealing extra food was survival arithmetic, not greed. The ship doesn't care about the distinction.

### The Biomancer — Vossk

Vossk is the *Geld Maw's* resident biomancer — competent, experienced, and professionally indifferent to the consent question. He views conversion as manufacturing: raw material in, finished product out. Prisoners are raw material. The ethical framework described in biomancer knowledge traditions — the debates about consent, the obligations to transformed subjects — exists in Vossk's training but not in his practice. He learned it. He set it aside. The ship pays him for results, not reflection.

Vossk is genuinely skilled, which makes him more dangerous, not less. A poor biomancer attempting the kind of radical transformation Gemszi undergoes would produce a non-viable result. Vossk produces a functional, durable, commercially attractive product. He takes professional pride in the quality of his work. He does not extend that pride to the experience of the subject.

### Erdfall

A frontier agri-world in the early stages of colonial development — two generations established, maybe three. The population is small, spread across scattered farmsteads connected by rough tracks. The soil is productive but the work is hard — large-scale mechanisation hasn't arrived and may never. Draught animals are the primary source of agricultural power. A well-built draught beast is a significant capital investment for a frontier farmer.

Erdfall receives supply ships irregularly. When one docks, the farming communities send buyers to the landing field to trade for equipment, seed stock, medical supplies, and livestock. Provenance is not examined closely. If the animal looks healthy, pulls hard, and the price is fair, the sale closes.

### The Farmer — Oskar Haldren

Oskar farms a mid-sized holding on Erdfall's northern plain — mixed crops, some pasture, a small woodlot. He works the land with his wife Maren and their teenage son Edric. They had a draught beast that died over the winter — age and a respiratory infection — and Oskar has been ploughing by hand and borrowed neighbour stock since. He needs a replacement badly and cannot afford to be choosy about where it comes from.

Oskar is not cruel. He is a practical man who treats his animals well because well-treated animals work better and last longer. He feeds properly, shelters properly, maintains harness and yoke in good condition. His relationship with his stock is the rough, genuine affection of a working farmer — the animal is not a pet, but it is valued and cared for within the framework of what it is understood to be.

He has no framework for what Gemszi actually is.

## Key NPCs

**Vossk** — Ship's Biomancer, *Geld Maw*
- *Motivation:* Professional. Produces what the ship needs, collects his fee, maintains his workshop.
- *Relationship to Plot:* The architect of Gemszi's conversion. Present for the procedure; absent afterward.
- *Voice:* Clinical, efficient, occasionally technical. Does not explain himself to subjects.

**Captain Dresk** — Captain, *Geld Maw*
- *Motivation:* Profit. Crew discipline that generates saleable product is ideal discipline.
- *Relationship to Plot:* Authorises the conversion, oversees the sale. Makes the economic calculation.
- *Voice:* Flat, transactional. Doesn't enjoy cruelty — simply doesn't weight it.

**Oskar Haldren** — Farmer, Erdfall
- *Motivation:* Needs a draught animal to keep his farm running. Wants to provide for his family.
- *Relationship to Plot:* Gemszi's owner. The person whose perception determines Gemszi's daily reality.
- *Voice:* Quiet, practical, observant. Talks to his animals the way farmers do — running monologue, half to himself.

**Maren Haldren** — Farmer's wife, Erdfall
- *Motivation:* Family stability. Cautious about the new animal — it cost them most of their trade credit.
- *Relationship to Plot:* Secondary observer. May notice behavioural anomalies from a different angle than Oskar.
- *Voice:* Direct, warm with family, reserved with strangers and new things.

**Edric Haldren** — Farmer's son (15), Erdfall
- *Motivation:* Curiosity. Adolescent restlessness on a frontier world with limited stimulation.
- *Relationship to Plot:* Potentially the first to treat Gemszi as something other than stock — teenagers test boundaries, talk to animals differently, pay attention to things adults filter out.
- *Voice:* Earnest, talkative, asks questions.

## The Conversion

### The Charge

Gemszi is caught taking extra rations from the galley stores — a partial loaf and some dried protein strips. On another ship, this draws a reprimand or pay docking. On the *Geld Maw*, the calculation is different. Gemszi is galley support — replaceable. His labour value is low. His conversion value — a finished livestock product saleable at the next frontier stop — is higher. The decision is economic.

He is taken from the galley to the brig. He is told what will happen to him. Whether he understands the full scope of what "conversion" means before it begins depends on how much he has seen of Vossk's other work and how much the crew talks.

### The Procedure

Vossk works from the *Geld Maw's* conversion bay — a space that functions as both medical suite and manufacturing floor. The procedure takes days. Gemszi is conscious for parts of it and sedated for others, depending on what Vossk needs from the subject's nervous system at each stage.

**Structural rebuild.** The kobold frame is too small to serve as scaffold for the target form. Vossk uses biomantic compounds to trigger massive tissue growth — bone elongation, muscle layering, organ scaling — guided by active arcane manipulation. The target is a heavy quadruped frame in the 400–500 kilo range, built from a subject that started at perhaps 18 kilos. This is construction, not modification. The kobold's original skeletal and muscular architecture is consumed in the process, replaced by the new form as it develops. Bovine-adjacent body plan: deep chest, heavy shoulders, broad back suited to harness and yoke.

**Limb and extremity conversion.** Hands become forelimb terminations — broad, weight-bearing, hoofed or semi-hoofed. This is the specific loss for a kobold. Kobolds are tool-users; their hands are central to how they interact with the world. The dexterity is gone. The digits are gone. What remains are structures built for ground contact and load-bearing. Hind limbs are rebuilt similarly — powerful, driving, configured for sustained pulling effort. The tail is retained but thickened, rebalanced for the new body's proportions.

**Sensory and vocal modification.** Eyes are adjusted for the larger skull but retain functional acuity — a draught animal that can't see obstacles is a poor product. Hearing remains acute. The vocal apparatus is rebuilt to match the new form's respiratory system — Gemszi can vocalise in registers appropriate to the body (lowing, grunting, distress calls) but the fine motor control required for speech is gone. The tongue, larynx, and oral cavity are configured for feeding and breathing in the new form, not for language.

**Dairy installation.** Gemszi's original reproductive and endocrine systems are removed entirely — gonads, associated ducting, external genitalia. In their place, Vossk builds a mammary-glandular complex wired into a new hormonal regulation cycle. This is technically demanding work — installing functional lactation architecture in a subject with no developmental basis for it, then linking it to an endocrine cycle that will sustain production under the metabolic demands of draught work. The system is designed to respond to regular milking stimulus, establishing and maintaining output as long as the routine continues.

**Frontier hardening.** Subdermal plating across the shoulders, flanks, and spine — not heavy armour but a layer of toughened, semi-rigid tissue beneath the hide that provides meaningful protection against impacts, predator bites, and rough terrain. Bone density is reinforced throughout. Hide is thickened — not to the point of inflexibility but enough to resist cuts, abrasion, and temperature extremes. The product needs to survive hard use on an undeveloped world without veterinary infrastructure.

**Cognitive preservation.** The brain is untouched. Vossk does not damage, suppress, or modify Gemszi's cognitive function. This is not mercy — it is not a consideration at all. The brain is simply not part of the product specification. Vossk is manufacturing a draught animal; the brain's continued operation does not interfere with that function and destroying it would add procedural risk for no commercial benefit. The fact that the finished product contains a fully sapient, fully aware mind with complete memory of who it was and what was done to it is, from Vossk's perspective, irrelevant to the product's performance.

### Post-Conversion

Gemszi spends several days in the conversion bay recovering and adapting to basic motor function in the new body. He must learn to stand, walk, and move on four legs — the motor cortex adapts, but not instantly. Vossk monitors viability during this period with professional attention. Once Gemszi can stand, walk, and feed in the new form, he is moved to the cargo hold to await sale.

In the hold, he is livestock. Fed from a trough. Watered from a basin. Confined by a simple stall partition. The crew who pass through the hold see an animal. If any of them know what he was — and some of them do — it does not change how they interact with what he is now.

## The Sale

The *Geld Maw* puts in at Erdfall's single landing field for routine supply exchange. Among the goods available for trade: one heavy draught animal, frontier-hardened, in good condition.

Oskar Haldren arrives at the field with trade credit and a desperate need for pulling power before spring planting advances past the point of recovery. He examines the animal the way a farmer examines stock — teeth, joints, feet, general condition, temperament. The animal is calm. Well-built. Obviously quality work, wherever it came from. The price is steep but within his means if he cuts other purchases.

The sale closes. Oskar leads his new draught beast back to the farm on a rope halter. The *Geld Maw* finishes its supply exchange and leaves orbit. The transaction, from the ship's perspective, is complete.

Gemszi walks behind a stranger down a dirt track on a world he has never seen, wearing a halter, unable to speak, heading toward a life that has no category for what he is.

## Main Scenario Arc

### Act 1: The Farm — First Days

Gemszi arrives at the Haldren farmstead and is installed in the barn — a stall with clean straw, a water trough, a hay rack. Oskar is attentive in the way of a man who has just spent most of his trade credit: checking the animal's condition, adjusting the stall, introducing it to the existing farm routine.

The harness fitting comes within the first day or two. Oskar needs this animal working. The yoke and traces are adjusted for Gemszi's frame — the deep chest and heavy shoulders Vossk built are perfectly suited to draught work, which is the point. The first time Gemszi is hitched to the plough and directed to pull, the physical reality of what he now is lands with full weight.

**Key beats:**
- The harness. The yoke settling across shoulders that used to have hands at the end of arms.
- The first full day of ploughing. Physical exhaustion in a body he is still learning. The work is relentless and the body is built for it, which is its own kind of horror — he can do this. He will be doing this.
- The milking. Oskar or Maren establishing the routine — the dairy installation responds to stimulus, and the sensation is entirely alien. A process happening to his body that his mind has no framework for.
- The barn at night. Alone, in straw, in the dark. The mind that remembers being Gemszi, the kobold who worked the galley, who had hands, who stole bread because he was hungry.

### Act 2: The Routine — Weeks and Months

Life on the farm establishes its rhythm. Gemszi learns the work — not willingly, but because the body responds to the harness and the commands and there is no alternative. He ploughs. He hauls. He is milked. He is fed, watered, sheltered, and maintained as a working asset.

Oskar talks to him the way farmers talk to stock — running commentary, half-conversation, the ambient narration of a man who works alone most of the day. "We'll do the east field tomorrow." "Rain's coming, can smell it." "You're pulling well today." It is not addressed to a person. It is addressed to a presence. Gemszi hears every word and understands all of it.

**Key beats:**
- Gemszi's internal landscape. What does he think about during the hours of ploughing? What memories does he return to? What fades? Does the body's rhythm — the repetitive physical work, the feeding cycle, the sleep — begin to impose its own structure on his thinking?
- The dairy routine becoming normalised. The first time it happens is violation. The twentieth time it happens is routine. The hundredth time, it is simply part of the day. What does that normalisation cost?
- Small moments of recognition that go unnoticed. Gemszi responding to his old name when Edric happens to say a word that sounds like it. Gemszi refusing to step on a piece of dropped equipment. Gemszi watching Maren read aloud to Edric in the evening through the barn's open door.
- The isolation. No other sapient being on this world knows he is a person. The loneliness of that is not dramatic — it is constant, ambient, total.

### Act 3: Recognition — The Slow Crack

The farmer begins to notice things that don't fit the framework of "animal."

This is not a single revelation. It is accumulation. Oskar is observant — he watches his stock because good husbandry requires it — and the draught beast behaves in ways that register as wrong without immediately resolving into an explanation.

**Escalating signs:**
- Gemszi pulls the plough around obstacles before Oskar directs him to. Not avoidance — *navigation*. He reads the field and adjusts his line.
- Gemszi responds to complex verbal instructions on the first attempt. Not trained commands — conversational sentences. "Take us along the fence line to the corner post" produces an immediate, correct response.
- Gemszi reacts to emotional tone in ways stock does not. When Oskar argues with Maren within earshot, the animal becomes visibly tense — not startled by volume but responding to the content.
- Gemszi refuses a command for the first time — Oskar directs him toward a section of soft ground that Gemszi has identified as unsafe. The refusal is deliberate and specific. When Oskar investigates, he finds the ground is indeed waterlogged and would have bogged the plough.
- Edric, talking to the animal the way a lonely teenager talks to animals, says something personal — a worry, a hope, something he wouldn't say to his parents. Gemszi's response — a shift in posture, a sustained look, something in the eyes — is wrong for an animal. Edric notices.

**The crossing point:** Oskar begins testing deliberately. He gives instructions of increasing complexity, watches the responses, and arrives at a conclusion he does not want: this animal understands language. Not commands. Language. The implications of that — and the question of what, exactly, he bought — become the scenario's central tension.

**Possible directions:**
- Oskar seeks information about the animal's origin and finds nothing — the *Geld Maw* is long gone, the sale was cash, no records.
- Oskar consults someone — a neighbour, a healer, anyone with knowledge of biomantic work — and gets a partial answer that raises worse questions.
- Oskar attempts to communicate with Gemszi directly and confronts the reality that the animal cannot respond in kind. It understands everything. It can say nothing.
- The family's relationship to Gemszi shifts — from stock to something they don't have a word for. The work doesn't stop. The farm still needs a draught animal. But the milking becomes uncomfortable. The harness becomes a question. The stall becomes a problem.

## Threads and Hooks

**The communication problem.** Gemszi's vocal apparatus cannot produce speech. If the Haldrens accept that there is a person inside the animal, how do they communicate? Gemszi can nod, shake his head, respond to yes/no questions. Anything more complex requires tools he cannot manipulate — he has no hands. Can someone on Erdfall fabricate a vox unit? Is there a biomancer within reach who could modify the vocal system? Each potential solution raises its own problems.

**The legal question.** If Gemszi is a person, then Oskar purchased a person. That has legal implications on most worlds, even frontier ones. Oskar is now in possession of what may be a kidnapping victim who was sold to him as property. His legal exposure, and Gemszi's legal status, depend entirely on what framework Erdfall has — if any — for transformed sapients.

**The practical question.** The farm needs a draught animal. If Gemszi is recognised as a person, the ethical framework for keeping him in harness collapses — but the economic framework that requires his labour hasn't changed. Oskar cannot plough without him. The family's survival is tied to Gemszi's continued work. The scenario does not offer a clean resolution to this.

**The biomancer question.** Is there anyone on Erdfall, or reachable from Erdfall, with the knowledge to reverse or modify the conversion? The Bioservitor framework suggests reversal is extraordinarily difficult or impossible — the original body was consumed in the process, not stored. But modification — restoring speech, adjusting the form — might be within reach if the right practitioner exists. Finding one on a frontier world is its own quest.

**The Geld Maw.** The ship that did this to him is out there. Other converted prisoners may have been sold to other worlds. Whether Gemszi's situation eventually connects to a larger pattern — and whether anyone is positioned to do anything about it — is a long-term thread.

## GM Notes

**Pacing.** This scenario breathes. The conversion should be visceral but not prolonged — the horror is in the completeness, not the detail of each cut. The farm arc is where the scenario lives, and it should be given time. Daily routines, seasonal changes, the slow accumulation of small moments. Don't rush the recognition — let the farmer's suspicion build through specific incidents rather than a single dramatic reveal.

**Gemszi's interiority.** The scenario's weight depends on access to Gemszi's internal experience — what he thinks, what he remembers, what he feels during the milking, the ploughing, the long nights in the barn. This is first-person horror: a mind fully present in circumstances it cannot change and cannot communicate about.

**The Haldrens as people.** Oskar is not a villain. He is a farmer who bought an animal and is slowly discovering that what he bought is not what he thought. His response — confusion, guilt, practical paralysis, genuine concern that has no good outlet — should be played honestly. He is in an impossible situation too, and he didn't choose it.

**The dairy.** This is the detail that cuts deepest and should be handled with weight rather than spectacle. The routine of being milked — by people who see it as animal husbandry — while possessing full awareness of what is happening and what it means. The normalisation of it over time. The moment when the Haldrens realise what they've been doing and have to sit with that.
