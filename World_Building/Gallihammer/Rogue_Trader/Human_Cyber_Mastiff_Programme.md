# Human Cyber-Mastiff Conversion Programme
### Biological Research Branch — Tier 2 Advanced Production (formerly Tier 3 Experimental)

## Origin

The standard cyber-mastiff conversion uses a canine subject as the base organism. The process is well-documented if ugly — systematic augmentation and replacement of biological systems, the animal's existing quadruped anatomy providing a natural scaffold for the chassis architecture. Joints map. Weight distribution maps. The motor cortex already knows how to be a quadruped. The engineering is largely a matter of reinforcement, replacement, and integration.

The human body does not cooperate.

Human skeletal geometry, musculature, organ placement, and neurological architecture are built for bipedal operation. Attempts to force human biology into a quadruped chassis encounter problems at every level — proprioceptive rejection, structural incompatibility, organ systems packed for the wrong frame, and a motor cortex that fights the remapped body with everything it has. Standard augmentation and replacement techniques, effective on canine subjects, produce non-viable results on human ones.

Damien's goal was not to produce another servitor. Servitor conversion is straightforward precisely because higher cognitive function is destroyed first — the remaining brain stem and motor centres don't care what body they're wired into. Damien wanted the opposite: full cognitive preservation. The person — memory, personality, reasoning, judgement — intact and operational inside a quadruped combat chassis. A human mind in a mastiff's body, fully aware, fully capable, and fully themselves.

This required solving a problem that standard conversion techniques were never designed to address.

## Development History

### Phase 1 — Extensive Restructuring

**Approach:** Reshape the human musculoskeletal system to fit the quadruped chassis architecture. Extensive surgical modification of spine, joints, and limb geometry, followed by augmetic reinforcement and chassis integration.

**Subjects:** Prisoners and mortally wounded crew, with full documentation.

**Results:** Subjects survived initial surgery. Motor cortex rejected the remapped body within days. The brain continued sending bipedal movement signals into a quadruped architecture, producing cascading proprioceptive conflicts — seizures, phantom limb storms, progressive cortical burnout. No subject achieved stable locomotion. All subjects experienced the failure consciously, able to articulate what was happening to them throughout.

**Duration before termination:** Weeks. Subjects deteriorated rapidly once the rejection cascade began.

**Conclusion:** The human motor cortex cannot be retrained to accept a fundamentally restructured body while the original body's architecture is still partially present. The biological frame fights the chassis. The brain fights the biological frame. The system cannot stabilise.

### Phase 2 — Progressive Replacement

**Approach:** Incremental conversion — replacing biological systems in stages, allowing the brain to adapt between surgeries. Each stage removed more of the original body and replaced it with chassis-compatible augmetics, with recovery and neurological assessment periods between operations.

**Subjects:** Prisoners and mortally wounded crew.

**Results:** Significant improvement over Phase 1. Some subjects achieved basic quadruped locomotion after extended adaptation periods. Motor cortex showed partial remapping when given sufficient time between conversion stages. However, the remaining organic core systems — circulatory, respiratory, digestive — were still configured for a bipedal torso and failed under the mechanical stresses of quadruped movement. Organ failure rates were unacceptable. Subjects who achieved locomotion subsequently deteriorated as internal systems could not sustain the chassis demands.

**Duration before failure:** Months. Subjects lived through the full progressive conversion process, cognisant throughout, before organ failure overtook them.

**Conclusion:** Progressive adaptation can partially resolve the motor cortex problem, but the human organic core cannot be reconfigured to operate reliably inside a quadruped chassis regardless of adaptation time. The body is not the asset — it is the obstacle.

### Phase 3 — Radical Reduction

**Approach:** Strip everything. The human body is not a system to be preserved — it is an obstacle wrapped around the only component that matters. Reduce the subject to brain and brainstem with the absolute minimum of biological support tissue, encase the result in a hardened life-support capsule, and install the capsule in the chassis as a self-contained unit.

**Development:** This was not a single breakthrough but an iterative refinement — determining exactly how much biological tissue could be removed and how much had to remain, developing the life-support systems to sustain the reduced biological core, engineering the interface between brainstem and chassis nervous system, and solving the sensory integration problem so the brain would accept the chassis as its body rather than experiencing sensory deprivation.

**Subjects:** Final development iterations on prisoners and mortally wounded. First full-protocol conversion on a volunteer — the individual now known as the Psihound.

**Results:** Viable. With no residual body sending conflicting signals, the motor cortex remaps onto the quadruped locomotion suite cleanly. The brain accepts the chassis as its body. Full cognitive function preserved — memory, personality, reasoning, speech (via integrated vox), sensory experience through the chassis augments. The subject is the person they were, operating a new body.

**Status:** Proven technique. The Psihound remains stable, functional, and cognitively intact. Programme approved by Sebastian for volunteer expansion.

## The Conversion Process

### Reduction

The subject is surgically reduced to brain, brainstem, and a minimal envelope of biological support tissue. This support cluster includes enough vascular tissue to maintain direct nutrient delivery to the neural mass, a stripped-down hormonal regulation node to sustain neurochemical balance, and the minimum connective tissue required to keep the biological components structurally stable. Everything else — skeleton, musculature, organs, skin — is removed.

This is not a fast procedure. It is performed in stages over several days, with continuous monitoring of neural function throughout. The brain must remain viable at every step. There is no recovery from a mistake at this stage.

### Encapsulation

The reduced biological core is sealed into a hardened cranial capsule — armoured, shock-mounted, environmentally controlled. The capsule contains the dedicated life-support module: nutrient circulation, oxygenation, waste filtration, thermal regulation, and chemical buffering to maintain the neurochemical environment the brain requires. This capsule is the most critical component of the entire system. If it fails, the subject dies. It is engineered accordingly — redundant systems, multiple fail-safes, armoured against impacts that would destroy the chassis around it.

### Integration

The capsule is installed in the core of the cyber-mastiff chassis — the most protected position in the frame, surrounded by armour plating and structural reinforcement. The brainstem interfaces with the chassis through a spinal-tap MIU (Mind Impulse Unit) link, connecting the brain to the chassis nervous system. Motor output, sensory input, proprioception, and communication systems all route through this link.

The MIU connection is not merely functional — it must support the full bandwidth of conscious experience. The subject needs to feel the chassis as their body. Proprioception, spatial awareness, pressure, temperature, and a functional analogue of touch are all fed back through the interface. Without this, the subject would experience permanent sensory deprivation — technically alive, functionally isolated, and cognitively deteriorating.

### Conditioning

Once integration is complete, the subject undergoes a conditioning period — learning to operate the chassis, remapping motor skills, calibrating sensory input. Damien supplements this with flash-conditioning protocols that accelerate quadruped movement pattern acquisition. The brain adapts. With no residual body providing contradictory signals, the adaptation is far faster and more complete than Phase 2 achieved.

Subjects report that the chassis begins feeling natural within weeks. The old body map fades. The new one takes its place.

## Operational Deployment

### Shipboard Security Units

Human-cognition mastiffs assigned to the Fates Cast's internal security. Roles include:

**Independent patrol** — the Fates Cast is vast, old, and contains sections that even the current crew does not fully understand. Security mastiffs patrol autonomously, making judgement calls that no servitor or conditioned animal could manage. They can assess whether a situation requires intervention or observation, distinguish between a lost crew member and an intruder, and report coherently to the security chain.

**Lower deck operations** — the deeper sections of the Fates Cast, where the warp-adjacent atmosphere is thickest and the old systems occasionally activate without explanation, require security assets that can think, assess, and withdraw intelligently rather than simply attacking whatever they encounter or standing post until something kills them.

**Communication and coordination** — integrated vox systems allow full verbal communication with security teams, command, and crew. Security mastiffs participate in briefings, report in plain language, and coordinate with human personnel as equals in assessment if not in form.

**Crisis response** — armoured, fast, low-profile, and capable of operating in spaces too tight or too dangerous for human security personnel in full kit.

### Ground Combat Units

Human-cognition mastiffs deployed as specialist attachments to ground teams under Balphereon's tactical command. Roles include:

**Reconnaissance in force** — forward deployment into hostile territory, combining the chassis's speed, low profile, and sensory capability with human-level tactical assessment. A ground combat mastiff doesn't just detect threats — it evaluates them, decides whether to engage or report, and communicates detailed tactical intelligence to the team.

**Boarding actions and confined space operations** — the chassis form factor excels in the tight corridors, ducts, and crawlspaces of hulk recovery, station boarding, and urban operations. Mastiff-form operators can access spaces that human infantry in carapace armour cannot, while maintaining combat capability throughout.

**Independent objectives** — unlike standard cyber-mastiffs or servitors, human-cognition units can be assigned complex multi-stage objectives and trusted to exercise judgement in execution. Infiltrate a section, assess enemy strength, locate a specific target, decide on engagement — these are tasks that require a mind, not a targeting algorithm.

**Combined arms integration** — full communication capability means ground combat mastiffs operate as squad members, not attached assets. They participate in fire-and-manoeuvre, call adjustments, warn of flanking threats, and coordinate with human fireteams in real time.

## The Volunteer Programme

### Recruitment

The programme accepts volunteers only. Damien provides full technical briefings on the conversion process, expected outcomes, and known risks. The Psihound's continued stable operation serves as the primary existence proof that the process works and that cognitive preservation is achievable.

### Volunteer Profiles

Candidates typically fall into several categories:

**Mortally wounded personnel** — individuals who would otherwise die or be permanently invalided from injuries sustained in operations. For these candidates, the conversion offers continued service and capability where the alternative is death or permanent incapacity. The Fates Cast's limited veteran roster (15 Tier 2 individuals among over a thousand basic soldiers) means that preserving experienced personnel in any functional form represents a significant return on investment.

**Voluntary candidates from the general complement** — crew members who choose conversion for personal reasons: a desire for greater capability, ideological commitment to the Fates Cast, or individual motivations that are their own business. On a ship where the chief engineer is a mouse who gene-edited himself and the security complement includes a psyker in a mastiff chassis, radical self-modification is within the cultural range of the acceptable.

**Individuals with specific operational aptitudes** — candidates whose existing skills (reconnaissance, security assessment, tactical analysis) would be enhanced rather than limited by the chassis form factor.

### Informed Consent

Damien's briefings are thorough, technically precise, and delivered with his characteristic assumption that sufficient information constitutes sufficient preparation. The development history — including the Phase 1 and Phase 2 failures — is available to any candidate who asks for it.

What Damien's briefings may not fully convey, given his documented blind spots regarding consent, is the weight of the decision. The conversion is permanent. The original body is destroyed in the process. The subject will never hold a tool with human hands, sit at a table, or be seen as baseline human again. The Psihound can speak to this reality from the inside, and whether Damien includes her perspective in the briefing process — or whether candidates seek her out independently — varies.

Ship culture manages the ethics informally. The crew watches. If something feels wrong about a volunteer's circumstances — coercion, desperation mistaken for willingness, insufficient understanding of the permanence — it is more likely to be flagged by the crew around Damien than by Damien himself. Sebastian's position is permissive — he approved the programme's expansion and does not interfere with Damien's recruitment process — but he is aware of the consent dynamics in ways that Damien may not be, and the older hands aboard expect the captain to intervene if the line is crossed.

### The Psihound's Position

The Psihound is the programme's proof of concept, its most visible success, and the person most qualified to speak honestly about what the conversion means from the inside. Her role in the volunteer programme is complicated by her own unresolved relationship with her status — she wants to be understood as what she is rather than what she looks like, and the arrival of others who share her condition offers the possibility of peers who understand her experience without explanation.

It also means she is no longer unique. Whether this is relief or loss — or both — is something she is working through.

Volunteers who seek her out before committing receive the most honest assessment available. What she tells them, and how she frames it, says more about where she has arrived in her own adaptation than any of Damien's technical briefings.

## Programme Status

**Research tier:** Tier 2 — Advanced Production. The technique is proven and reproducible. It is no longer experimental.

**Production capacity:** Limited by surgical complexity, life-support capsule fabrication, and Damien's personal involvement in each conversion. This is not an assembly-line process. Each conversion requires days of surgery and weeks of conditioning, and Damien is personally involved in every critical stage. Concurrent conversions are possible but limited by his attention and the Fates Cast's medical bay capacity.

**Current strength:** One operational unit (the Psihound). Volunteer programme approved by Sebastian for expansion to unit scale — target strength sufficient for dedicated shipboard security and ground combat detachments.

**Dependencies:** Raw materials for chassis and life-support capsule construction. Volunteer candidates. Damien's time and focus, which competes with every other demand on the Fates Cast's chief engineer, medicae, and lead researcher.

## Cultural Impact

The existence of fully cognisant humans in cyber-mastiff chassis changes the social landscape of the Fates Cast in ways that become more significant as numbers grow. A single Psihound is an anomaly — Damien's interesting experiment, a curiosity. A unit of them is a community.

They speak. They have opinions. They remember being human and they are not human now. They serve alongside baseline crew who must decide, individually and collectively, whether to treat them as people or equipment. Both responses will exist, and the friction between them will be ongoing.

The converted personnel will almost certainly develop their own unit identity — shared experience creates bonds that outsiders cannot fully access. How that identity relates to the broader ship culture, the crew hierarchy, and the chain of command is an open question that will be answered by how the first cohort of volunteers integrates.

What is clear is that they are not servitors. They did not have their minds scraped out. They chose this. They think, they judge, they have preferences and grievances and loyalty. Treating them as anything less than crew with standing is a mistake that the Fates Cast's culture will have to navigate, and one that says more about the people doing the treating than about the converted. Sebastian's treatment of the Psihound — as crew, by name, without qualification — sets the tone from command. Whether the rest of the ship follows his lead or whether the converted have to earn that recognition individually is the test.

## Damien's Notes

*The human conversion fails for exactly the inverse reason. The base organism is wrong for the chassis in every dimension that matters. I spent months trying to make the body fit the machine before I understood that the body was never the point. The body was noise. The signal is the brain.*

*Once you accept that, the engineering becomes clean. Reduce to signal. Encase. Integrate. The brain does the rest, given time and a proper interface. It wants to have a body. Give it one that works and it will adapt.*

*The ethics of this are not as complicated as people make them. I am not destroying a person. I am moving one. The volunteers understand this. Most of the crew understands this. The ones who don't are generally uncomfortable with the conclusion rather than the logic.*
