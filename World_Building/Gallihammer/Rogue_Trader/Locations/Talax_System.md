# Talax System — The War Moons of Talax

## System Overview

The Talax system is known colloquially as the War Moons of Talax — a name that reflects both its martial culture and the significant orbital infrastructure that rings its capital world. The system sits within the broader post-collapse political landscape as an independent power, sustained by trade, warrior reputation, and the implicit threat of a population that treats violence as a social institution rather than an aberration.

The system contains four notable worlds: Ju'ukto (capital hive world), Cho'unda (forge world), and Ni'iktu (death world), plus the wasteland ruins of the Shattered Spires. Power flows through a clan structure — great families operating in competition and occasional alliance, with three dominant clans shaping system-wide politics.

---

## Worlds

### Ju'ukto — Capital Hive World

**World Type:** Hive
**Rulership:** Oligarchy — numerous noble clans, predominantly led by three Great Houses
**Tech Level:** Standard
**Religion:** Chaos (Local Variant — Gogreth)

The capital and seat of system power. Ju'ukto is covered almost entirely by cities — the bulk of the system's population concentrated into hive megastructures with wasteland between them. The cities are heavily built up, layered across centuries of construction, each a vertical civilisation in miniature.

The planet is ringed by several dozen orbital structures serving dual purposes: trade facilitation and planetary defence. The orbitals handle the significant volume of traders who come to Ju'ukto for commerce, keeping much of that traffic off the surface while maintaining visibility and leverage over it. They are also hardpoints — weapons platforms and garrison stations that make direct orbital assault a costly proposition.

---

### Cho'unda — Forge World

**World Type:** Forge / Industrial Hive
**Population:** Densely populated
**Character:** The system's industrial heart. The bulk of the population is concentrated into hive-style cities built around key resources and production facilities. Everything outside those cities is ruin and industrial waste — the residue of centuries of extraction and manufacture. Cho'unda produces the weapons, ships, and materiel that sustain the system's martial culture and its mercenary export economy.

---

### Ni'iktu — Death World

**World Type:** Death World (jungle)
**Character:** Covered primarily in lush, dense jungle populated by numerous dangerous species. Ni'iktu serves two cultural functions: as a popular hunting destination for those wealthy or capable enough to survive it, and as a proving ground — a place where worth is demonstrated through survival and kill. The single settlement on the planet is **Moment's Rest**, the only permanent habitation, serving as outfitter, waystation, and morgue for those who underestimate what they came to hunt.

---

### The Shattered Spires

A massive ruin complex within the wasteland territories — the remains of something significant, age and origin unclear. The Shattered Spires are a source of salvage, exploration contracts, and danger in roughly equal measure. What they were before they became ruins is a question with several competing answers, none confirmed.

## Culture

The Talax system is civilised in the sense that it has infrastructure, trade, legal frameworks, and social institutions. It is not civilised in the sense that most outsiders might hope. The influence of Gogreth is omnipresent and structural — not a fringe belief but the organising principle of social aspiration.

Violence is common and partially decriminalised. Crimes can be settled through blood payment — formal arrangements where the debt is paid in injury or life rather than currency, witnessed and recorded. Feuds between families are routinely ignored by civil authority unless they become disruptive to trade. Duelling is normalised and widely practised. Arena combat is popular entertainment. Slaves who are caught can earn their freedom through combat — fight and win, walk free.

Warrior traits are the dominant social ideal — which specific traits are admired varies by location and house affiliation, but the underlying value is consistent. Physical capability, combat skill, tactical intelligence, personal courage, demonstrated ferocity — these are the currencies of social advancement. Wealth matters. Lineage matters. But a lineage that cannot fight for what it has does not hold it for long.

The system has a notable hostility toward Astartes — particularly chaos-aligned ones. The reasons are historical and not fully documented, but the attitude is consistent enough to be a practical consideration for anyone travelling with enhanced soldiers.

---

## Religion — Gogreth

The local deity bears strong resemblance to the warp entity known elsewhere as Khorne — possibly Khorne himself, possibly a powerful servant operating under a local name. The theology has developed its own character over generations: Gogreth is a god of worthy combat, blood spilled in genuine contest, and strength demonstrated rather than claimed. The emphasis is on quality and authenticity of violence rather than quantity, though quantity is not discouraged.

No other religion is officially sanctioned across the system. Alternatives that emerge do not last long — the population handles most suppression independently without requiring government involvement.

Sectarian differences exist within the Gogreth faith, primarily around questions of what constitutes worthy combat and who counts as a worthy enemy. The Cruor clan controls most of the larger shrines and temples, which gives them significant religious authority and an ongoing source of tension with the other great houses.

## Notable Clans

The system's power structure is organised around clans — great families operating in constant competition, forging temporary alliances, pursuing blood feuds, and competing for trade and influence simultaneously. The three dominant clans shape system-wide politics.

### Cruor
The most devout of the great houses. The Cruor make up a large portion of Gogreth's formal priesthood and control the majority of the system's significant shrines and temples. Their religious authority translates directly into political leverage. They are also widely rumoured — and in some circles openly acknowledged — to perform murders for hire for the right price or the right patron. Whether this is sanctioned religious practice, an unofficial clan revenue stream, or both is a question most people in the system know better than to ask formally.

### Occisor
A well-established and powerful house with strong ties to the system's mining operations and shipyards. The Occisor are involved in mercenary work, planetary defence contracting, and military training — they produce soldiers and sell them, equip fleets and maintain them, and have relationships with the ships and mining operations that form the system's economic backbone. A practical house by Talax standards, more interested in capability and contract than theology.

### Verberos
The trade house. Verberos is heavily involved in commerce and specialises in dealing with offworlders — they have the contacts, the translators, the understanding of outside customs, and the patience for negotiation that the other houses frequently lack. Offworld visitors who want to conduct business in the Talax system will almost certainly pass through Verberos channels at some point. They are not less dangerous than the other great houses. They are simply more willing to be pleasant about it first.

## Practical Notes for Visitors

The Talax system is a viable port of call for trade, mercenary contract acquisition, and resupply. A crew that can demonstrate combat credibility will be respected. A crew that cannot is assessed as a potential resource.

Verberos is the recommended first contact for any offworld business. Cruor should be approached with care and only when their specific services or religious authority are relevant. Occisor are reliable contractors but expect demonstrated competence before they commit to anything.

The arena culture means that disputes between crew members and locals may be resolved through formal combat rather than legal process — this is not always a disadvantage depending on the crew.

## Connection to the Campaign

Ju'ukto is the homeworld of Kerberos, pre-dating the current religious framework by millennia. The culture he was recruited from would not have been identical to what Talax is now, but the underlying values — warrior excellence, combat as social currency, personal strength as the measure of worth — would have been recognisable to him. Whether he considers it home in any meaningful sense after so long is an open question he does not address directly. The system's hostility toward enhanced soldiers is something he navigates carefully.
