# The Mechanicus

## What They Are

The Mechanicus is the closest thing the post-collapse universe has to a pan-civilisational institution. It is not a government. It is not a church, though it resembles one. It is not an army, though some of its chapters could field one. It is a loose, fractured, internally contradictory confederation of groups united by a single shared conviction: that the empire's technical knowledge is the most important thing humanity has ever produced, and that preserving, recovering, and extending it is a sacred obligation.

The word "sacred" is not metaphorical. For most Mechanicus traditions, the work is literally holy — a religious commitment to the machine, to the principle of engineering, to the idea that understanding how things work is the highest form of human endeavour. They disagree violently on nearly everything else: theology, governance, priorities, methods, who deserves access to what knowledge, and what should be done with it once recovered. But the core commitment — the conviction that the machine is worth serving — holds across every chapter, every fleet, every monastery, every forge.

This makes them recognisable. You can tell a Mechanicus operative from a civilian engineer the way you can tell a monk from a schoolteacher. The knowledge is similar. The relationship to it is completely different.

## The Shared Identity

Across every Mechanicus faction — planet-bound, fleet-based, isolationist, evangelical, militant, scholarly — certain things are constant. These are the markers that make the institution legible as a single phenomenon despite its internal fragmentation.

### Symbology

The cog is universal. Every Mechanicus faction uses the gear or cog motif as its foundational symbol, though the specific rendering varies enormously by tradition. Some use a simple gear. Some use interlocking cog systems of increasing complexity. Some incorporate the cog into broader emblematic structures — skulls, stars, hammers, circuit patterns, biological diagrams. The variations are as distinctive as heraldry and serve the same function: a Mechanicus operative can look at another's iconography and know immediately that they are dealing with a fellow adherent, even if the specific faction is unfamiliar.

Beyond the cog, common visual elements include: the use of red as a primary colour (the origin of this convention is lost, but it is old enough that it feels essential), augmetic modification displayed openly rather than concealed, tool-bearing as a form of personal expression (a Mechanicus adept's tools are worn the way a soldier wears weapons — visible, accessible, part of their identity), and a general aesthetic that privileges function over beauty in ways that become, over time, a form of beauty in themselves.

An outsider encountering two Mechanicus adepts from completely different factions — one a planet-bound forge-monk, the other a fleet-based salvager — would recognise them both as Mechanicus instantly. The cog, the red, the augmetics, the tools, the way they look at machines. The shared language is visual before it is verbal.

### The Tenets

No single catechism is accepted by all Mechanicus factions, but certain principles recur with enough consistency to be considered foundational:

**Knowledge is sacred.** Not metaphorically. The accumulation, preservation, and transmission of technical knowledge is a religious act. Destroying knowledge — any knowledge, of any kind — is the closest thing the Mechanicus has to a universal sin.

**The machine deserves respect.** A machine is not merely a tool. It is a product of knowledge applied to matter — the physical expression of understanding. Treating a machine carelessly is not just wasteful; it is a failure of reverence. This principle produces the Mechanicus's distinctive relationship with technology: the rituals, the prayers, the spoken acknowledgments that look like superstition from the outside and feel like communion from the inside.

**Understanding is the goal.** Not power, not wealth, not territory — understanding. The Mechanicus exists to know how things work. Everything else — the forges, the fleets, the monasteries, the trade networks — exists to support that mission. In practice, of course, power and wealth and territory become goals in themselves, and factions that have accumulated enough of each tend to forget the original point. But the aspiration persists, and the factions that hold closest to it tend to produce the best work.

**The empire knew more.** This is the Mechanicus's foundational grief and its foundational motivation. The empire understood things that nobody alive understands. Every fragment recovered, every principle reconstructed, every system restored to function is a step back toward what was lost. The work will never be finished. It is done anyway.

## How They Organise

### Planet-Based: The Forge-Monasteries

The most common Mechanicus presence is the forge-monastery — a fixed installation on a habitable world, combining workshop, archive, training facility, and religious community. Forge-monasteries range in scale from small compounds housing a few dozen adepts to sprawling industrial complexes that function as the primary economic engine of their region.

A forge-monastery's relationship with its surrounding community is transactional and usually stable. The monastery provides technical services that nobody else can: repair and maintenance of complex systems, fabrication of equipment beyond local capability, medical services using preserved imperial techniques, and — most valuably — training. An apprentice accepted into a forge-monastery emerges years later with skills that are genuinely rare and immediately employable. Communities compete for monastery placement and tolerate the Mechanicus's eccentricities because the alternative is doing without.

In return, the monastery requires resources — raw materials, food, power, and access to anything that might contain pre-collapse technical information. Ruins, salvage, recovered data cores, old equipment — all of it flows toward the monastery, assessed, catalogued, and either integrated into the archive or traded to other Mechanicus factions for material the local forge cannot source.

Some forge-monasteries are purely local operations, serving their region and maintaining their archives without ambition beyond their walls. Others are hubs — nodes in a broader Mechanicus network, sending out expeditionary teams, training adepts for deployment elsewhere, and maintaining communication with other factions through whatever means are available. A few have grown powerful enough to be political actors in their own right — controlling enough industrial capacity and enough irreplaceable knowledge to negotiate with planetary governments as equals or better.

### Planet-Based: The Expeditionary Orders

Some forge-monasteries maintain a secondary function: launching expeditions. These are organised efforts to recover pre-collapse knowledge from ruins, dead zones, ship graveyards, and other sites where imperial technology may have survived. An expeditionary team typically combines engineering specialists, security personnel, archivists, and enough general crew to operate whatever transport the monastery can provide.

Expeditions are expensive, dangerous, and potentially transformative. A team that returns with an intact data core, a working imperial system, or even a reliable fragment of technical documentation has justified years of institutional investment. A team that doesn't return has cost the monastery personnel it cannot easily replace. The decision to launch an expedition is never taken lightly, and the politics of who goes, what they're authorised to recover, and who gets access to what they bring back can consume a monastery's leadership for months.

The most ambitious forge-monasteries maintain standing expeditionary fleets — small groups of ships dedicated to recovery operations, operating independently for months or years at a stretch and returning periodically with whatever they've found. These fleets are the monastery's most visible external presence and its most significant investment.

### Fleet-Based: The Migratory Forges

Not all Mechanicus factions have a home world. Some exist entirely in the void — fleets of varying size that move between systems, trading technical services for the resources they need to keep moving. A migratory forge fleet is a self-contained Mechanicus community: workshops, archives, training, and religious observance all conducted aboard ship, with planetfall made for trade, resupply, and the occasional recovery operation.

Migratory forges occupy a distinctive niche. They go where planet-bound monasteries cannot — reaching systems that have no local Mechanicus presence, serving communities too small or too remote to attract a permanent installation, and accessing sites that require a ship to reach. Their arrival in a system is an event. They bring capabilities that the local population may not have seen in years or decades: precision fabrication, advanced medical intervention, imperial system maintenance. They also bring demand — for materials, for salvage, for information about what's in the local ruins. The relationship is intense, brief, and mutually exploitative in the way that all trade is.

Fleet-based factions tend to be more pragmatic and less doctrinaire than their planet-bound counterparts. Life in the void strips away institutional inertia. A migratory forge that cannot adapt to what it encounters does not survive long enough to develop traditions about it. The fleet factions are often the ones most willing to work with xeno technology, to incorporate non-standard techniques, to bend the tenets when the tenets get in the way of keeping the fleet running.

They are also, by virtue of their mobility, the primary vectors for knowledge transfer between otherwise isolated Mechanicus communities. A migratory forge that visits a dozen systems in a decade carries information between them — not always intentionally, not always with permission, but inevitably. The planet-bound monasteries that trade with migratory forges know this and factor it into every interaction. Some welcome it. Some resent it. None can prevent it.

### The Rumoured Leviathans

Among the fleet-based factions, certain names circulate that are never quite confirmed and never quite dismissed. Orders that are said to maintain imperial super-dreadnoughts — vessels on the scale of twenty kilometres or more, pre-collapse construction, kept operational through millennia of continuous maintenance by crews that have made the ship itself the centre of their entire existence.

If these vessels exist — and the evidence is suggestive rather than conclusive — they represent something extraordinary. A working imperial super-dreadnought is not merely a ship. It is a mobile forge, a mobile archive, a mobile monastery, and a mobile fortress simultaneously. Its internal manufacturing capability would exceed most planet-based installations. Its archives, if maintained, would contain technical documentation that the wider Mechanicus has spent millennia trying to reconstruct from fragments. Its weapons systems, even partially functional, would make it effectively sovereign — answerable to nobody because nobody could compel it to answer.

The implications are significant enough that most Mechanicus factions prefer not to discuss them directly. A super-dreadnought order that decided to impose its interpretation of the tenets on other factions would have the means to do so. That none have — if they exist at all — is either reassuring or simply means they haven't found a reason to yet.

What is known, from occasional second-hand accounts and the very rare direct sighting, is that if these vessels are out there, they are not hiding. They are simply elsewhere — in the deep void, in systems that nobody else visits, following agendas that have had millennia to diverge from anything the broader Mechanicus would recognise. What they are doing out there, and what they have become in the process, is a question that the Mechanicus's own mythology has not yet settled.

## The Branches

Every Mechanicus faction, regardless of size, location, or theological orientation, requires the same broad categories of expertise to function. The specific titles vary — naming conventions have diverged over millennia — but the roles are recognisable across factions, and an adept trained in one tradition can usually identify their counterpart in another. This shared professional vocabulary is one of the things that holds the Mechanicus together as an institution even when everything else divides it.

### Menials and Laymen

Beneath the adepts — beneath the specialists, the researchers, the military, the initiates — is the vast workforce that keeps a Mechanicus faction physically functional. Menials are the janitors, the cargo handlers, the basic maintenance crews, the cooks, the sanitation workers, the people who sweep the forge floor and empty the waste processors and ensure that the air recyclers have clean filters. They are not initiates. They have not taken vows. They are employees, servants, or in some factions something closer to serfs — people who work for the Mechanicus because the Mechanicus needs labour and they need employment.

Menials outnumber adepts in most factions by a significant margin. A forge-monastery that maintains fifty adepts might employ three hundred menials. A migratory forge fleet's crew is majority menial. The work is unglamorous and essential, and the Mechanicus's relationship with its menial workforce ranges from paternalistic care to barely-acknowledged exploitation, depending on the faction.

Laymen occupy a slightly different category — individuals with some technical training but who have not been accepted into the full adept hierarchy. A layman might maintain basic equipment, perform routine calibrations, assist in the forge under supervision. They know enough to be useful and not enough to be trusted with anything sensitive. Some are apprentices who will eventually advance. Others have reached the limit of what the faction will teach them and will remain laymen permanently.

### The Contractor Enclaves

Several Mechanicus factions have built their entire operational model around hiring out trained personnel to external clients. These contractor enclaves train enginseers, medicae, chirugens, and technical specialists to a standardised competency and then place them — aboard ships, within secular governments, attached to military forces, embedded in communities that need technical capability but cannot support a full Mechanicus presence.

The arrangement is mutually beneficial. The client gets access to Mechanicus-trained expertise without the political complication of hosting an entire faction. The enclave gets revenue, intelligence on what's happening outside its walls, and — critically — access to whatever its placed operatives encounter in the field. An enginseer hired aboard a rogue trader who stumbles across a pre-collapse facility is expected to report what they find back to the enclave. Whether the rogue trader knows about this expectation varies.

Contractor enclaves are among the most politically neutral Mechanicus factions, because their business model depends on being acceptable to a wide range of clients. They tend to be moderate in theology, restrained in augmentation practices, and careful about their public image. They also tend to be very well informed, because they have people everywhere.

### The Sciences

The Mechanicus organises its technical knowledge into disciplinary branches, each with its own training traditions, its own internal hierarchies, and its own views on what constitutes the most important work.

**Biologis** — the study and manipulation of living systems. Biologis adepts work with genetics, bioengineering, pharmacology, and the full range of techniques the empire developed for modifying organisms. The overlap with biomancers is real but contentious — a biologis works through technical procedure and preserved knowledge, not arcane sensitivity, and most biologis traditions draw a sharp line between their craft and anything that involves reaching into the warp. Damien's background as a mechanicus biologis, and his subsequent drift into territory that his order found unacceptable, is a specific example of what happens when that line blurs.

**Physik** — the study of fundamental physical systems: energy, matter, force, and the engineering principles that govern how they interact. Physik adepts are the ones most likely to be working on understanding the actual science behind imperial technology rather than simply maintaining it through ritual. They are also the ones most likely to be frustrated, because the gap between what the empire understood and what they can reconstruct is largest in exactly the domains that physik covers. A physik adept who has spent a career trying to reverse-engineer the principles behind a singularity cannon has usually arrived at a very specific relationship with humility.

**Arcanum** — the study of arcane phenomena through technical frameworks. This is the most controversial branch within the Mechanicus. Some factions maintain robust arcanum traditions, treating warp energy as another physical phenomenon to be measured, documented, and eventually understood. Others reject the branch entirely, viewing any engagement with the warp as incompatible with the tenets. The factions that maintain arcanum specialists tend to produce the most interesting research and the most spectacular failures in roughly equal proportion.

**Chirugens and Medicae** — the Mechanicus's medical practitioners. Every faction needs them, regardless of whether the faction's primary focus is military, scholarly, or industrial. Chirugens are the surgeons — the ones who cut, who implant, who perform the augmetic installations that are central to Mechanicus identity. Medicae are the broader medical practitioners — diagnostics, pharmacology, ongoing care. In practice the distinction blurs, particularly in smaller factions where the same person does both. The chirugen who installs an augmetic arm in the morning and treats a fever in the afternoon is the norm, not the exception.

The quality of Mechanicus medical practice varies enormously. The best chirugens work with techniques preserved from the empire's medical tradition and produce results that no civilian surgeon could match — precision augmetic integration, gene-therapy interventions, procedures that verge on miraculous by the standards of the communities they serve. The worst are butchers with ritual training, following steps they don't understand and producing outcomes they can't explain. Most are somewhere between, competent within the limits of what they've been taught and aware that those limits exist.

### Data and Knowledge

**Logiks** — specialists in computation, data analysis, and the maintenance of whatever cogitation systems the faction operates. In an institution that regards knowledge as sacred, the people who process and organise that knowledge occupy a quietly essential role. Logiks maintain the archives' indexing systems, run the calculations that other branches need, and keep the data infrastructure functional. They are rarely glamorous. They are always necessary.

**Archivists** — the keepers of the records. Where logiks process data, archivists preserve it — physically maintaining the documents, data cores, and storage media that contain the Mechanicus's accumulated knowledge. An archivist's work is conservation as much as scholarship. Pre-collapse data cores degrade. Documents decay. Storage formats become unreadable as the systems that created them are lost. An archivist fights entropy on behalf of knowledge, and entropy is winning slowly enough that the fight feels meaningful.

Archivists and logiks together form the institutional memory of any Mechanicus faction. A forge-monastery that loses its archivists loses more than personnel — it loses the ability to access its own past. This makes them protected in ways that other specialists are not, and gives them quiet leverage that more visible branches sometimes underestimate.

### Exploration and Recovery

**Archaeologists** — the field operatives of knowledge recovery. Archaeologists go where the knowledge is — into ruins, dead zones, collapsed facilities, ship graveyards. They combine technical expertise with the practical skills of survival in environments that are actively hostile to human life. A Mechanicus archaeologist is part scholar, part engineer, and part combat-ready explorer, because the sites that contain the most valuable knowledge are invariably the ones that are most dangerous to enter.

Archaeology is the branch that most directly serves the Mechanicus's foundational mission — recovering what was lost. It is also the branch with the highest casualty rate. The deep ruins do not welcome visitors. What survives in them has had thirty thousand years to become territorial. An archaeological team that enters a pre-collapse facility is gambling that what they find inside is worth what the facility will cost them to navigate. The ones who come back with intact data cores or functional systems are heroes. The ones who don't come back are cautionary tales. Both are discussed with equal reverence in the traditions that train them.

### The Military

The Mechanicus is not primarily a military institution, but every faction maintains armed forces. The forges must be defended, the expeditions must be protected, and the knowledge must be kept from those who would take it by force. The Mechanicus's military capability ranges from modest garrison forces to armies that could challenge planetary governments, depending on the faction's size, resources, and disposition.

#### Plebes

The baseline military force — unaugmented or minimally augmented personnel who serve as general infantry, labour, garrison troops, and support staff. Plebes are the most numerous military asset any Mechanicus faction fields. Their training varies by faction from adequate to professional, their equipment from scavenged to purpose-built. What distinguishes Mechanicus plebes from a civilian militia is institutional support: they are equipped, supplied, and commanded by an organisation that takes logistics seriously as a matter of religious principle. A Mechanicus plebe may not be individually exceptional, but they are reliably equipped and reliably fed, which in the post-collapse context puts them ahead of most.

#### Skitarii

The professional military arm of the Mechanicus — the soldiers who fight as their primary function, trained and equipped to a standard that reflects the institution's technical capability. Skitarii exist on a spectrum of augmentation that runs from baseline-trained professionals to something that is no longer recognisably human.

**Standard skitarii** are trained baseline soldiers equipped with Mechanicus-manufactured weapons and armour. They are professional, disciplined, and better-equipped than most equivalents outside the institution. A standard skitarii regiment is a competent military force by any measure — reliably armed, reliably led, and backed by logistics and engineering support that most armies cannot match.

**Augmented skitarii** have received combat augmetics — enhanced senses, reinforced skeletal structures, integrated weapon systems, replacement limbs optimised for combat applications, neural uplinks that improve reaction time and tactical coordination. The degree of augmentation varies by faction, by role, and by the individual's willingness and suitability. A heavily augmented skitarii is significantly faster, stronger, and more durable than a baseline human, operating with capabilities that bridge the gap between infantry and light vehicle.

**Total conversion skitarii** represent the far end of the augmentation spectrum. The principle is the same as Damien's cyber-mastiff programme — reduce the operative to brain and brainstem, encase in a life-support capsule, and install in a combat chassis — but the Mechanicus is not limited to a single body plan. Total conversion chassis range from humanoid combat frames to purpose-built configurations optimised for specific roles: assault, reconnaissance, heavy weapons, engineering, void operations. Some retain speech and full cognitive function. Some have had cognitive function selectively reduced — memory pruned, personality dampened, obedience structures installed — to produce operatives who execute without hesitation or question. Some have been lobotomised entirely, reduced to biological processing cores running combat algorithms in an armoured shell.

The ethics of this vary by faction, predictably. Factions that perform total conversion only on willing volunteers with full cognitive preservation consider themselves civilised. Factions that lobotomise prisoners and install control locks consider themselves practical. Both wear the cog. The spectrum between them is wide, and where a given faction falls on it says more about their values than any amount of theological discussion.

Whether a total conversion operative is a person or a weapon depends entirely on what was done to the brain before it was installed. The ones who can still think know the answer. The ones who can't don't care. The ones who can think but have control locks installed — who are aware, who remember, who cannot disobey — are the ones that keep ethicists and chirugens awake at night, in the factions that still employ ethicists.

#### Vehicles and Walkers

The Mechanicus fields armoured vehicles across the full range of what post-collapse manufacturing can produce and what imperial salvage can supplement.

**Conventional armour** — tanks, transports, self-propelled guns — forms the backbone of any Mechanicus ground force large enough to require one. These are manufactured to Mechanicus standards, which typically means they are better-engineered and better-maintained than civilian or secular military equivalents, if not always more numerous.

**Small walkers** — bipedal or multi-legged combat platforms in the two to five metre range, crewed or operated by a single pilot. Small walkers fill the gap between infantry and conventional armour, providing mobile firepower in terrain that tanks cannot navigate. They are common enough that most Mechanicus factions of any size maintain at least a few, and the designs vary enormously — some are modern constructions, others are imperial salvage maintained through millennia of continuous service.

**Heavy walkers** — larger platforms in the ten to twenty metre range, requiring small crews and carrying weapons that would otherwise require a fixed emplacement or a naval vessel. Heavy walkers are rare and represent significant industrial investment. A faction that fields one is making a statement about its capability and its willingness to use it.

**Jaeger class** — the designation used for war walkers in the fifty to one hundred metre range. These are not modern constructions. Nothing in the post-collapse industrial base can produce a walker at this scale from scratch. Every jaeger-class walker in operation is imperial salvage — a pre-collapse war machine that has been maintained, repaired, rebuilt, and kept moving through millennia of dedicated effort by the faction that controls it. They are vanishingly rare. A Mechanicus faction that possesses a functional jaeger-class walker possesses a weapon that can reshape the outcome of a planetary conflict. The crew requirements are substantial — dozens to hundreds of personnel for operation, maintenance, and the continuous engineering attention that keeps a thirty-thousand-year-old war machine from catastrophic failure.

Jaeger-class walkers are not deployed casually. They are strategic assets, committed only when the stakes justify the risk of damage to something that cannot be replaced. A faction that loses its jaeger has lost something irreplaceable — not just the machine itself, but the institutional knowledge required to maintain it, which often dies with the maintenance crew if the walker is destroyed in action.

**Kaiju class** — the designation for the largest war walkers ever fielded, operating at scales that defy conventional military classification. A kaiju-class walker is measured in hundreds of metres. It does not walk through terrain — it reshapes terrain by walking through it. Buildings are not obstacles; they are debris. Rivers are not barriers; they are puddles. The ground shakes for kilometres in every direction. A kaiju-class walker is not a weapon in any tactical sense — it is a strategic instrument, a thing that ends wars by existing on the battlefield.

Whether any remain functional is a matter of rumour, legend, and the occasional seismographic anomaly recorded on worlds that the Mechanicus's more reclusive factions are known to frequent. If the super-dreadnought orders exist — the ones maintaining imperial capital ships in the deep void — it is not unreasonable to speculate that some of them also maintain the ground assets that those ships were originally designed to deploy. A twenty-kilometre warship that carried jaeger and kaiju-class walkers in its deployment bays would have been a complete planetary assault package. Thirty thousand years later, if the ship still works, there is no reason to assume the walkers don't.

Nobody has confirmed this. Nobody has disproved it either. The Mechanicus's own records, in the factions willing to discuss the subject at all, contain references that could be interpreted as evidence or as mythology, depending on how badly you want to sleep tonight.

#### Doctrinal Variations

The military classifications above describe the conventional, mechanically-oriented approach to warfare — metal chassis, augmetic enhancement, engineered walkers. This is the most common model, but it is not the only one. A faction's dominant branch of expertise shapes its military doctrine as thoroughly as it shapes everything else, and the results can make two Mechanicus armies almost unrecognisable to each other.

**Biologis-dominant factions** may field forces that contain very little metal at all. Where a conventional faction installs a brain in a combat chassis, a biologis faction grows one. Bio-servitors — purpose-built organisms produced through the same techniques described in the Bioservitor entry but scaled for military application — can fill every role from infantry to heavy armour. A biologis war-beast bred for the assault role is not a machine wearing organic armour; it is an organism, engineered from the cellular level for speed, resilience, and lethality, controlled through implanted command structures or — in the case of sapient bio-servitors — through the same mixture of loyalty, training, and complicated consent that governs any military relationship.

The biologis equivalent of a dreadnought is not a walker with a brain capsule installed. It is a massive combat organism with a brain capsule grown into its central nervous system — the biological architecture shaped around the human mind rather than bolted onto a mechanical frame. The organism's own distributed nervous system handles locomotion, reflexes, and autonomic function; the human brain provides direction, tactical judgment, and the capacity for initiative that no amount of conditioning can replicate in a non-sapient creature. The result is something that fights like an animal and thinks like a person, and the people inside them describe the experience in terms that range from exhilarating to profoundly unsettling.

Biologis forces look different, smell different, sound different, and die different from conventional Mechanicus military. A battlefield after a biologis engagement doesn't have wreckage — it has carcasses. The psychological impact on opponents is considerable, and some biologis factions have leaned into this deliberately, cultivating an aesthetic of organic horror that serves as its own force multiplier.

**Arcanum-dominant factions** take the most radical approach, and the one that most other Mechanicus traditions find the most disturbing. Where a conventional faction builds a machine and installs a pilot, and a biologis faction grows an organism and installs a brain, an arcanum faction builds a chassis and binds a warp entity into it.

The principle is containment and direction. A warp predator — one of the non-sapient entities described in the Warp entry — is drawn through a controlled breach, channelled into a prepared chassis, and held there through a combination of arcane binding techniques and engineered containment systems. The entity's own reactive drives — its aggression, its territorial instinct, its response to stimuli — are directed through the chassis's systems, producing a war machine that is powered by something that wants to destroy things and merely needs to be pointed.

This is exactly as dangerous as it sounds. The binding must hold. The containment systems must function. The entity must remain directed rather than simply unleashed. An arcanum walker that loses containment is not a broken machine — it is a warp predator manifesting in realspace inside an armoured shell, doing whatever its nature compels with no human direction whatsoever. The catastrophic failure mode of an arcanum war machine is significantly worse than the catastrophic failure mode of any other type, and the factions that field them maintain dedicated containment teams whose sole function is to destroy their own assets if the binding breaks.

The advantage, when it works, is that an arcanum war machine does not tire, does not hesitate, and does not stop. The entity bound into the chassis operates on drives that have no human analogue for fatigue or morale. It will fight until the chassis is physically destroyed or the binding fails, whichever comes first. It also operates with a reactivity that no human pilot or biological brain can match — the entity perceives and responds to threats at a speed determined by its own nature rather than by human neurology.

Arcanum war machines are rare even within factions that embrace the practice. The expertise required to perform a successful binding is scarce, the risk of failure is high, and the theological objections from the rest of the Mechanicus are vocal and persistent. Factions that field bound-entity walkers tend to do so quietly, deploying them in situations where witnesses are unlikely to survive to report what they saw. When witnesses do survive, the reports tend to be dismissed as combat stress or propaganda.

They are not dismissed by the people who were there.

## Rank and Advancement

The Mechanicus is, at its core, a competence-based institution. The work is too technical, too dangerous, and too important for rank to be purely political — a forge-master who cannot actually run a forge will, eventually, produce a catastrophe that no amount of political manoeuvring can survive. This creates a baseline of meritocracy that holds across most factions, even as politics inevitably complicate it at the upper levels.

### The Common Ladder

Specific titles vary between factions, but the broad progression is recognisable across the institution:

**Initiate** — a newly accepted member of the adept hierarchy, undergoing foundational training. Initiates learn the tenets, the rituals, the basic technical competencies that every Mechanicus adept is expected to master regardless of eventual specialisation. The initiate period varies by faction from months to years.

**Adept** — a fully trained practitioner qualified to work independently within their branch. An adept biologis can perform standard procedures without supervision. An adept logik can maintain archive systems. An adept skitarii officer can command a unit. This is where most Mechanicus personnel spend their careers — competent, productive, and unlikely to advance further without either exceptional ability or exceptional circumstances.

**Magos** — a senior specialist, acknowledged as an authority within their branch. A magos biologis, a magos physik, a magos arcanum — the title indicates that the individual has mastered their discipline to a degree that other practitioners defer to their judgment. Magos rank is where competence begins to intersect with institutional authority. A magos typically directs research programmes, trains adepts, advises faction leadership, and carries the accumulated weight of decades of work.

**Archmagos** — the highest technical rank, held by individuals whose expertise defines their faction's capability in their branch. An archmagos is not merely skilled — they are often the foremost living authority on their specialty within their faction. The rank carries significant institutional authority and significant institutional expectation. An archmagos who stops producing results that justify their position will eventually hear about it.

**Fabricator-General** — or whatever title the faction uses for its overall leader. The most senior authority in a Mechanicus faction, responsible for strategic direction, resource allocation, and inter-faction relations. In some factions this is the most technically accomplished member. In others it is the most politically accomplished. In a few it is both, and those factions tend to be the most effective.

The titles above are the most common variants. Individual factions may use different terms — a forge-monastery might use "forge-master" where a fleet faction uses "prime artificer" — but the structure maps consistently: initiate, practitioner, senior specialist, master, leader.

### Competence and Politics

The meritocratic ideal holds strongest in branches where incompetence kills quickly. A chirugen who cannot perform surgery produces dead patients. An enginseer who cannot maintain a reactor produces a dead ship. An archaeologist who cannot read a ruin produces a dead team. In these branches, failing upward is self-correcting — the correction is simply unpleasant.

In branches where the consequences of incompetence are slower, politics has more room to operate. Logiks and archivists can sustain a mediocre performer for years before the damage becomes visible — indexing errors accumulate gradually, data integrity degrades over time, and by the time someone notices that the archive has been mismanaged, the person responsible may have already advanced to a position where they manage other logiks rather than doing the work themselves. These are the branches where institutional politics most closely resembles civilian bureaucracy, with all the attendant frustrations.

At the upper levels — archmagos and above — politics is inescapable regardless of branch. Faction leadership requires political skill whether or not the leader arrived there through technical merit, and the interaction between technical authority and political authority produces the full range of outcomes: brilliant leaders who are terrible politicians, effective politicians who are mediocre practitioners, and the rare individual who is genuinely good at both.

The factions that function best tend to be the ones where technical authority and political authority remain at least loosely coupled — where the person making decisions about research priorities actually understands the research, and where the person allocating resources has enough technical knowledge to assess whether those resources are being used effectively. The factions that function worst are the ones where the coupling has broken entirely — where leadership has become purely political and the technical branches have retreated into their own internal hierarchies, answering to leaders they don't respect with results they don't explain.

## Augmentation

Augmetic modification is central to Mechanicus identity in a way that goes beyond practical utility. It is expected. It is not, in most factions, strictly required — but an adept who declines augmentation entirely will find that the absence is noted, discussed, and held against them in ways that range from subtle social pressure to explicit professional disadvantage.

The logic is partly practical. Mechanicus work frequently involves environments that baseline human biology cannot survive — radiation exposure, vacuum, extreme temperatures, toxic atmospheres, the interior of active machinery. An enginseer whose augmetics include rad-hardened internals, sealed respiratory systems, and reinforced skeletal structure can work in conditions that would kill an unaugmented colleague in hours. Declining augmentation is, from this perspective, declining to be fully qualified for the work.

But the logic is also theological. The machine is sacred. To integrate the machine into oneself — to replace fallible flesh with engineered precision — is an act of devotion. The degree of augmentation an adept carries is read, consciously or not, as a signal of commitment. More augmetics generally indicates higher rank, greater dedication, deeper integration into the Mechanicus's values. This is a rough correlation, not a rule — there are lightly augmented masters and heavily augmented initiates — but the pattern is strong enough to shape expectations.

Damien is a notable exception. For a master enginseer, his augmentation is remarkably light — the mechadendrite utility rig and whatever internal modifications his biologis self-experimentation has produced. By conventional Mechanicus standards, he should be carrying significantly more metal. That he isn't reflects both his biologis orientation (why install a mechanical arm when you can grow a better one?) and his general disregard for institutional expectations. It is one of many reasons his former order found him difficult.

### The Augmentation Spectrum

Augmentation follows a loose progression that most adepts traverse over the course of their career:

**Utility augmetics** come first — tools of the trade. A mechadendrite rig, enhanced optics for precision work, a data-interface port, respiratory filters. These are functional and relatively minor. Most adepts receive their first utility augmetics during initiate training or shortly after advancement to adept rank.

**Protective augmetics** follow as the adept's work takes them into more demanding environments. Rad-hardening, vacuum seals, reinforced skeletal structures, thermal regulation systems. An enginseer being posted to a ship or an expeditionary team will typically receive protective augmetics suited to their expected operating conditions. This is where the practical logic is strongest — these augmetics are not devotional, they are survival equipment.

**Enhancement augmetics** begin to cross the line from practical to aspirational. Cognitive accelerators, reaction-time boosters, expanded sensory arrays, neural interfaces that allow direct communion with machine systems. Enhancement augmetics improve the adept's capability beyond baseline human limits, and the decision to install them is as much a statement about the adept's relationship with the machine as it is a practical upgrade.

**Integration augmetics** represent the deep end — modifications that blur or erase the distinction between the person and their equipment. An adept who has replaced enough of their body with augmetics that the remaining organic tissue is a minority component. An adept whose neural architecture has been so extensively modified that their cognition operates differently from a baseline human's. The logik-general of a heavily data-oriented enclave who has been reduced to a brain — or the functional remnant of a brain — connected to an array of cogitation units, processing data at speeds and volumes that no unaugmented mind could manage, still directing the faction's information architecture, still making decisions, still recognised as a person by the people who work under them.

Whether this is devotion or self-destruction depends on who you ask. The adept who has crossed deeply into integration typically considers the question irrelevant — they are what the work required them to become, and the work is what matters. The colleagues around them may see it differently, but if the augmented individual is still performing — still thinking, still contributing, still recognisably *someone* — then Mechanicus culture generally accepts the outcome. Personhood in the Mechanicus is defined by function and identity, not by the percentage of original tissue remaining.

The point where acceptance wavers is the point where the individual stops being recognisably themselves — where the augmentation has changed not just the body but the mind, the personality, the capacity for independent judgment. A logik-general who is a brain in a box but still argues with their subordinates and makes decisions they can explain is a person. A logik-general who has been so extensively modified that they no longer communicate in ways their subordinates can parse, who produces outputs without explanation, who may or may not still contain the individual who originally held the position — that is something else, and the people around them know it even if they cannot articulate exactly when the transition happened.

## The Enginseer

The enginseer is the Mechanicus operative that most outsiders actually encounter. Every ship that can afford one carries one. Every military force that can negotiate access to one wants one. Every community that hosts one becomes dependent on their capabilities within months. The enginseer is the Mechanicus's public face, its most common export, and — through the contractor enclaves — its most effective intelligence network.

### The Role

An enginseer is a general-purpose technical specialist — trained to maintain, repair, diagnose, and where necessary improvise solutions for the full range of systems they are likely to encounter in the field. They are not narrow specialists. A forge-monastery biologis might spend decades studying a single genus of engineered organism. An enginseer needs to keep a ship's reactor running on Tuesday and fix the atmospheric recycler on Wednesday and assess a piece of salvaged pre-collapse equipment on Thursday.

This breadth is the enginseer's primary value and primary limitation. They know enough about a wide range of systems to keep them functional. They do not know enough about any single system to push the boundaries of what is understood. An enginseer can follow the maintenance rituals for an imperial weapons system. They probably cannot explain why the rituals work or diagnose a failure that the rituals don't cover. For that, you need a magos. Enginseers know this and are generally honest about it — the good ones, at least. The bad ones improvise past their competence and produce outcomes that range from embarrassing to catastrophic.

### The Spectrum

Enginseers range from neophytes on their first posting to masters who have maintained the same ship for decades and know its systems better than the people who built them.

**Neophyte enginseers** are freshly trained, recently posted, and discovering the gap between what they learned in the forge-monastery and what the field actually demands. They are competent on paper and uncertain in practice. A neophyte enginseer on a ship will spend their first year asking questions they should already know the answers to and learning things that aren't in any manual. The crew around them will be patient or not, depending on the crew.

**Journeyman enginseers** have a few years of field experience and have begun to develop the practical intuition that distinguishes a functional enginseer from a good one. They can diagnose problems the manuals don't cover. They have seen enough failures to recognise the early signs. They have established relationships with their equipment that border on the personal — a journeyman enginseer knows the sounds their reactor makes when it's happy and the sounds it makes when it isn't.

**Master enginseers** are senior practitioners with decades of experience, deep expertise, and the institutional authority that comes with being the person who has kept everything running for longer than most crew members have been alive. A master enginseer is not merely a technician — they are an authority, consulted on decisions that go well beyond their technical brief because their judgment has been proven reliable across enough crises that the captain trusts it. Damien holds this rank, and his capabilities justify it — even if his augmentation level and his unorthodox methods would make a conventional Mechanicus assessor deeply uncomfortable.

### Augmentation Profile

Enginseers are typically physik-based — their augmetics oriented toward the physical demands of their work: rad-hardening, vacuum tolerance, thermal protection, integrated diagnostic tools, mechadendrite rigs. A working enginseer looks like someone who expects to spend time inside machinery that is trying to kill them, because they do.

Biologis enginseers exist but are rare — specialists in maintaining biological systems, life support, medical equipment, and the organic components of hybrid bio-mechanical technology. A biologis enginseer on a ship that runs biologis-derived systems is invaluable. On a conventional ship, their speciality is narrower, covering medical and life-support maintenance where their biological expertise outperforms a standard enginseer's general training.

Arcanum enginseers are rarer still, and their role is specific: maintaining warp-adjacent systems. The Geller field generators, the Navigator's instruments, the drive systems that interface with warpspace during transit. These systems operate on principles that conventional physik training does not cover, and an arcanum enginseer's value lies in their ability to work with equipment that behaves in ways that standard engineering cannot explain. They are strange people, as a rule. The work requires it.

## Communication

The Mechanicus's internal communication infrastructure reflects the same fragmentation as everything else about the institution. Routine inter-faction traffic moves by fast courier — ships carrying encrypted data cores on regular circuits between allied installations. Urgent traffic moves by astropath, in the factions that have access to one. Everything else waits.

See the standalone Communication entry for a full treatment of couriers, astropaths, astropathic choirs, and the constraints — including the sapient mind requirement and the AI prohibition — that shape interstellar communication across the setting.

The Mechanicus's internal culture — its patience, its institutional memory, its obsessive record-keeping, its emphasis on archivists and logiks — is built around these constraints. When the next courier may not arrive for months, you write everything down. When astropathic transmission costs the transmitter a piece of their humanity, you do not send trivial messages. The institution has operated under these limitations for its entire existence, and they have shaped it as thoroughly as the tenets.

## Internal Politics

### The Faction Problem

The Mechanicus is not one institution. It is dozens — possibly hundreds — of institutions wearing the same cog and claiming the same heritage. The degree of coordination between factions ranges from active collaboration to mutual ignorance to open hostility.

**Knowledge disputes** are the most common source of inter-faction conflict. A monastery that has spent centuries reconstructing an imperial manufacturing process does not willingly share that process with a rival faction. Knowledge is power in the most literal sense — the faction that knows how to maintain a specific imperial system controls access to that system. Trade in knowledge happens, but it is guarded, conditional, and frequently deceptive. Factions trade partial schematics, withhold critical steps, and occasionally provide deliberately flawed documentation to rivals. The tenets say knowledge is sacred. They do not say it should be free.

**Theological disputes** run deeper. Factions disagree on fundamental questions: Is the machine alive? Does it have a spirit that can be communed with, or is the communion metaphorical? Is augmetic self-modification a form of worship or a form of hubris? Should the goal be to restore imperial technology exactly as it was, or to understand its principles and build something new? These questions have no consensus answers, and factions that have built their entire institutional identity around specific positions do not yield them gracefully.

**Resource disputes** are the most pragmatic and the most violent. Two factions that both want access to the same ruin, the same salvage field, the same pre-collapse facility will negotiate if they can and fight if they must. Mechanicus-on-Mechanicus violence is not common — the shared identity creates real if imperfect solidarity — but it happens, particularly between factions that have diverged far enough that they barely recognise each other as kin.

### Heretek

Every tradition has its apostates. Within the Mechanicus, the term is "heretek" — a practitioner who has violated the tenets severely enough that other factions consider them outside the institution entirely. What constitutes heresy varies by faction, but certain violations are broadly agreed upon: deliberately destroying unique knowledge, weaponising technical capability against other Mechanicus factions without provocation, and selling imperial military technology to parties who are actively hostile to the Mechanicus as an institution.

The enforcement mechanism is informal and inconsistent. There is no central authority to declare someone heretek, no tribunal, no process. A faction declares a practitioner or group heretek, and other factions either agree or don't. In practice, the designation functions as a reputational marker — a heretek who arrives at a forge-monastery will find doors closed, trade refused, and occasionally weapons pointed. Whether they are actually guilty of whatever they've been accused of is a question that the accusing faction answers for itself.

Some hereteks are genuinely dangerous — practitioners who have taken imperial knowledge and applied it without any of the restraints that the tenets are designed to provide. Others are simply members of factions that lost a political fight. The distinction matters. It is not always easy to make.

## Relationship to Other Powers

### Secular Polities

Most planetary and system-level governments have a working relationship with the Mechanicus — they need what the Mechanicus provides, and the Mechanicus needs what they control. The relationship is rarely warm. Secular leaders tend to view the Mechanicus as useful, necessary, and uncomfortably independent. Mechanicus factions tend to view secular leaders as temporarily relevant obstacles to the actual work.

The leverage runs both directions. A polity that alienates its local Mechanicus presence loses access to technical capability it cannot replace. A Mechanicus faction that alienates its host polity loses access to resources, security, and the political stability that allows long-term work to continue. The result is a mutual dependency that neither side enjoys and neither side can afford to break.

### Religious Factions

The Mechanicus's relationship with the setting's various religious traditions is complex. The Mechanicus is itself a religious institution — its reverence for the machine and for technical knowledge is genuinely theological, not merely professional. This puts it in an awkward position relative to other faiths that claim authority over the empire's spiritual legacy.

Some religious factions view the Mechanicus as a legitimate expression of empire-worship — the machine-cult as one denomination among many, disagreeable in specifics but recognisable in kind. Others view it as heretical — the worship of created things rather than the creators. The Mechanicus, for its part, tends to view other religious traditions with the polite condescension of people who believe they have the only faith that actually works.

### Rogue Traders and Independents

For independent operators — rogue traders, mercenary companies, salvage crews — the Mechanicus is a critical resource and a potential threat. A rogue trader with a Mechanicus-trained enginseer aboard has access to maintenance and repair capability that would otherwise require a port visit. A rogue trader who finds something in a ruin that the Mechanicus wants has leverage. A rogue trader who finds something the Mechanicus doesn't want anyone else to have, has a problem.

Damien's presence aboard the Fates Cast is a specific expression of this relationship. His mechanicus biologis background gives him capabilities the ship could not replace, and his continued association with the Fates Cast — however informal, however far from any forge-monastery — is still, in some sense, a Mechanicus presence aboard an independent vessel. Whether Damien considers himself Mechanicus anymore is a question he would probably answer differently depending on who was asking.

## What They Are Not

The Mechanicus is not the empire. It does not govern. It does not field navies. It does not claim sovereignty over territory, with rare exceptions. It is not trying to rebuild the empire, though some factions would say otherwise.

What it is trying to do — what it has been trying to do for thirty thousand years — is remember. The empire knew things. Those things are being lost, generation by generation, as the people who understood fragments die and the fragments die with them. The Mechanicus exists to slow that loss, to reverse it where possible, and to ensure that whatever comes next has access to at least some of what came before.

Whether they succeed is an open question. Whether they are the right institution for the job — with their secrecy, their factionalism, their hoarding, their tendency to treat knowledge as property rather than heritage — is a question that the Mechanicus itself is not well positioned to answer honestly.

The cog turns. The work continues. It is not enough. It is what there is.
