# The Selenar — Origins of the Biomancer Tradition

## The Root

Before the empire reached its full extent, before it named itself an empire at all, there were the Selenar.

They were not called Selenar continuously. The name is one of many the tradition accumulated across its history — Eleusinians, Oesirica, Damia, Immacolata, and a dozen others in the languages of the cultures they moved through and occasionally absorbed. The names changed. The core conviction did not: that human nature is fractal, fractured, and transcendent. That each living person is an imperfect echo of deeper archetypes that persist through all of human existence. That the work of a serious practitioner is to understand those archetypes through gene-forging — to read the pattern of what humanity is in what humanity's bodies can be made to become.

They operated from Luna. Subterranean complexes, deliberately inaccessible, maintained by a culture that had been resisting external appropriation of its craft for longer than most of the civilisations around them had existed. They were practitioners first and a political entity second, and their politics were entirely organised around protecting the practice. Every offer from every Terran warlord, every Martian magos, every faction that wanted access to their gene-forges received the same response: silence, then refusal, then — if pressed — a demonstration that the Selenar could make what they pleased with living tissue, and that this applied to people standing outside their doors as readily as to anything inside the forge.

The empire's early expansion ran into this eventually. Unlike every other institutional body the empire encountered in its first centuries of consolidation, the Selenar were not obliterated or absorbed. They were too useful, and the empire's leadership was too pragmatic. The negotiation was long, unpleasant, and concluded with an arrangement that satisfied neither party: the Selenar would contribute gene-forging capacity to certain imperial projects in exchange for being left sovereign over their knowledge, their facilities, and — crucially — their continued practice of their own craft alongside whatever they contributed to imperial needs.

This arrangement is the point where the Gallihammer timeline diverges from what might have been. No conquest. No enslavement. No yoke placed on a tradition that had always treated external authority as an obstacle to be managed rather than a legitimate claim on their allegiance. The Selenar entered the imperial period with their autonomy intact and their philosophy uninterrupted, and had millennia of imperial stability in which to pursue the archetype project on their own terms while nominally contributing to imperial bioengineering programmes.

What the empire got from the Selenar was significant. What the Selenar learned from thirty centuries of access to imperial resources, imperial data, imperial genetic archives, and the full range of humanity that the empire assembled in one place — was more.

## The Archetype Project

The Selenar's central religious practice was resurrection through gene-forging. A cult member was not born in the conventional sense. They were made — crafted by gene-wrights according to formulae developed and refined over generations, their body constructed to embody a specific human archetype with as much precision as the current state of the craft could achieve. They would live that body's life, develop it, extend it, eventually exhaust it. Then the gene-wrights would study what they had produced, identify where the embodiment had succeeded and where it had fallen short, and craft the next iteration with those refinements incorporated.

The numbering system preserved in the Selenar's recorded matriarch lineages — Heliosa-54, Heliosa-78 — refers to exactly this. The fifty-fourth body crafted to embody the Heliosa archetype. The seventy-eighth. The soul, or whatever the Selenar meant by soul — the persistent pattern of identity that they believed survived physical death and that their resurrection process was designed to re-instantiate — carried from one iteration to the next, progressively more precisely expressed in each successive body.

The archetypes themselves were varied and contested. Different cults within the Selenar tradition had adopted different taxonomies. Some organised their archetypes as anthropomorphic figures, essentially a mythological pantheon translated into gene-forging practice. Some used numerical or mathematical systems, treating each archetype as a pattern that could be expressed as a formula. Some used symbolic or cosmological frameworks. All pursued the same underlying goal by different routes: to determine, through the discipline of gene-forging applied to living human subjects across multiple iterations, what human nature actually is at its deepest level, below the noise of circumstance and environment and individual accident.

Whether this goal was achievable was a theological question within the tradition. The gene-wrights who were most honest about it acknowledged that each iteration refined the expression of the archetype without necessarily converging on a fixed target — that the archetype itself might shift as the practitioner's understanding of it deepened, that perfect expression might be asymptotic, that the value of the project was in the practice rather than the destination. The ones who were less honest claimed convergence was happening and disputed each other's metrics for measuring it.

Over the centuries of the imperial period, the archetype project accumulated something that had never existed before: a comparative database. Different cults pursuing different archetypes, with access to each other's records for the first time, could observe patterns across traditions that no single tradition could have identified in isolation. What the archetypes shared. Where they overlapped. Whether the differences between cult taxonomies were fundamental or merely terminological. This comparative work was the most intellectually significant thing the Selenar did during the imperial period, and it remains the most fragmented portion of their heritage — because it was stored centrally, and central storage is what the collapse destroyed most thoroughly.

The Magna Mater — their name for the central repository of archetype source code, the most sacred object in the tradition — was hidden during the first serious threat to Luna's sovereignty and has never been recovered. What remains of the archetype database exists as fragments in the knowledge traditions of every lineage that descended from the Selenar, each holding different pieces of what was once a coherent whole, most no longer aware that the piece they hold is a piece of something larger.

## The Divergence

The empire's collapse did not happen quickly from the Selenar's perspective. They had been watching civilisations rise and overextend and fail for long enough to recognise the pattern. By the time the fracturing was visibly underway, the Selenar had already spent generations preparing for what they expected to follow.

The preparation was dispersal.

This was not a unified decision — the tradition had never been unified in that sense, and the cults that comprised it approached the crisis differently based on their own assessments of what was coming and what mattered most to preserve. But the broad shape was consistent: scatter, go underground, carry the knowledge in people rather than in facilities, and survive the collapse as a distributed network rather than a concentrated target.

Matriarchs sent out gene-wrights with memorised sequences, physical samples, preserved specimens, and the specific components of their practice most likely to be useful in whatever came next. Some lineages went together, maintaining cult structure through the collapse and the millennia after. Others dispersed as individuals, integrating into whatever communities survived, practicing what they could manage without infrastructure, waiting for conditions that never quite recovered enough to resume the full practice.

What came next was thirty thousand years.

## The Three Branches

The current biomancer tradition — and the biologis order within the Mechanicus — are products of three broad paths taken from the Selenar root in the immediate pre-collapse and post-collapse periods. The paths were not deliberate strategic choices at the time. They were responses to different circumstances by different components of a tradition that stopped being able to communicate with itself as the infrastructure that had connected it burned.

### The Hidden Cults

The largest portion of the Selenar by number chose concealment over integration. They went underground — literally, in many cases, occupying the kind of subterranean installations that the tradition had always favoured, now without the protection of Luna's political sovereignty or the resources of the imperial period. They maintained cult structure. They maintained the archetype project, or what they could still manage of it. They maintained the resurrection practice, though the scale and precision available to them contracted significantly as the infrastructure that had supported industrial-scale gene-forging was lost.

What the hidden cults preserved, they preserved almost entirely through people. The practice is the knowledge, in a tradition where the knowledge was never primarily stored in documents. Gene-wrights apprenticed to gene-wrights, sequences memorised and passed down through lineages that tracked their own continuity as carefully as the archetype iteration numbers had once been tracked. The knowledge degraded over thirty thousand years — it could not do otherwise, passed through human chains across that timescale without access to the material resources that would have allowed correction of accumulated error. But it degraded unevenly. Some lineages maintained specific competencies with extraordinary fidelity because those competencies were the ones they used every day. Others lost practices they had no occasion to perform and retained only the knowledge that those practices had existed.

The hidden cults are what most people mean when they use the word "biomancer." An independent practitioner, operating from a fixed location with a collection of compounds and specimens and tools accumulated over their career, serving the communities around them and maintaining an apprenticeship relationship with a student who will eventually carry the knowledge further. Their arcane component — the sensitivity to living systems that makes their work possible without intact imperial equipment — is understood within their tradition as an inheritance: something the Selenar had always cultivated, becoming more pronounced through thirty thousand years of selective pressure toward it. The practitioners who lack sufficient arcane sensitivity cannot do the work. The ones who have it train it. The knowledge and the arcane ability have co-evolved across the full span of the post-collapse period, each shaping the other.

What the hidden cults have almost entirely lost is the archetype project in any organised sense. Individual lineages maintain fragments of it — a specific archetype their founding gene-wright carried, pursuit of which has been embedded in the lineage's practice for as long as anyone can trace. But the comparative framework that would make those fragments legible as parts of a whole is gone. A hidden-cult biomancer who encounters another biomancer from a different lineage may not recognise that they are both pursuing aspects of the same foundational project, because neither has the vocabulary the project once used for itself.

The resurrection practice persists in vestigial form. A very few lineages still maintain it in something approaching its original structure — the crafting of successive bodies to progressively more precise archetype expression, the re-instantiation of the persistent identity pattern across deaths. These lineages are the most insular of an insular tradition, because what they are doing raises questions that the communities around them cannot comfortably absorb. More commonly, the resurrection tradition has collapsed into simpler practices: cloning without the archetype-refinement framework, or longevity modifications that extend a single body's useful span without the full resurrection sequence, or simply the deep familiarity with identity continuity and what it requires biologically that informs the tradition's approach to other procedures without being explicitly named as resurrectionist practice.

The hidden cults vary enormously. Thirty thousand years of isolation and divergence have produced traditions that share a root and share almost nothing else recognisable. A biomancer from one lineage encountering another may identify the family resemblance in their approach to living tissue — a similar intuition about what biology can be made to do, a similar relationship between technical knowledge and arcane sensitivity — without being able to converse meaningfully about the theoretical underpinning of either practice. The shared language is practical, not conceptual. Two biomancers of different lineages can collaborate on a procedure in ways that two biomancers of the same lineage collaborating on theology cannot.

### The Mechanicus Biologis

The second path was integration.

Not conquest. The Selenar who became the nucleus of the Mechanicus biologis order did so voluntarily, on terms they negotiated. This matters enormously to the biologis tradition's self-understanding and to the way it has been received within the Mechanicus more broadly: the biologis were not absorbed. They joined, and they joined as an institution bringing something the Mechanicus needed, not as a defeated faction being yoked to an external purpose.

What prompted the negotiation was the same thing that had always mediated the Selenar's relationships with external institutions: pragmatism on both sides. The early Mechanicus — emerging in the immediate post-collapse period from the same impulse that had produced the pre-collapse Martian Mechanicum, the conviction that imperial technical knowledge was sacred and its preservation was the most important work anyone could do — recognised that biological systems are machines. The most sophisticated machines ever produced. More complex than anything mechanical. And the people who understood them best were the gene-cults that had been studying them for longer than the Mechanicus had existed.

Some Selenar lineages made the same calculation from the other side: the Mechanicus had resources, infrastructure, and an institutional continuity that individual gene-cults could not achieve alone. Access to Mechanicus archives opened pathways to fragments of imperial bioengineering knowledge that the cults had lost or never possessed. The Mechanicus's commitment to preserving knowledge aligned with the Selenar's own priorities in ways that other external institutions' priorities had not.

The integration was not smooth and has never been fully resolved. The theological friction persists in every biologis tradition to this day.

The Mechanicus, as an institution, treats organic systems as machines: complex, self-replicating, sophisticated, but ultimately subject to the same analytical framework as any mechanical system. The biologis order inherited this framework as the price of institutional membership. It is a framework that produces excellent results in a narrow sense — organisms treated as machines are tractable objects of rigorous technical analysis — and that systematically excludes something the Selenar tradition had always maintained was essential: that living systems have a quality that is not fully captured by treating them as machines, that the practitioner's arcane sensitivity perceives something real about that quality, and that working with it rather than around it produces better outcomes.

This is the central internal argument within the biologis order and has been since the founding. The Mechanicus mainstream position is that arcane sensitivity is an epiphenomenon at best and a contaminating influence at worst — that a biologis who relies on arcane perception rather than rigorous technical procedure is doing bad science in ways they may not be able to detect, and that the tradition's Selenar origins are a historical fact to be acknowledged and then set aside as irrelevant to current practice. The biologis counter-position is that the Mechanicus mainstream does not fully understand what it is asking the order to set aside, that the technical and the arcane are not separable in the way that the mainstream assumes, and that the biological results achieved by biologis practitioners who work with their arcane sensitivity intact are consistently superior to those achieved by practitioners who have suppressed it in deference to institutional preference.

Both positions are represented across the biologis order. The institutions that emerged from the most thorough integration of Selenar practice into Mechanicus epistemology tend toward the mainstream position and produce work that is rigorous, reproducible, and conservative. The ones that maintained stronger Selenar heritage tend toward the counter-position and produce work that is more variable in quality, occasionally spectacular, and difficult to transmit through Mechanicus-standard documentation because the critical steps are ones that the practitioner feels rather than measures.

What the biologis order preserved from the Selenar that the hidden cults largely lost is the formal structure of the knowledge tradition. Documents. Systematic procedure. Comparative records. The biologis archive contains more of the pre-collapse Selenar framework than any individual hidden-cult lineage possesses, though the archive's version of that framework has been filtered through Mechanicus epistemology in ways that have sometimes preserved the form while losing the content. A biologis archivist examining a document describing Selenar archetype-refinement procedures will see a sophisticated cloning protocol with spiritual language surrounding it. A hidden-cult biomancer examining the same document — if they could read it — might see something quite different.

The biologis order knows it descended from the Selenar. This is institutional history, taught at initiation. What the order does not fully know — and actively debates — is how much of the Selenar tradition it actually preserved versus how much it transformed beyond recognition in the process of making the tradition legible to Mechanicus epistemology.

### The Lost Lines

The third path was neither concealment nor integration. It was simply what happened to the parts of the Selenar tradition that had neither the resources to maintain themselves as coherent cults nor the inclination to seek Mechanicus shelter.

They dispersed. They integrated into post-collapse communities not as biomancers but as people, carrying fragments of knowledge that degraded through generations of non-practice or partial practice or practice in conditions too limited to sustain real development. Some of what they carried vanished entirely within a few generations. Some survived in transformed shapes — medical traditions that contain compressed and unrecognised Selenar technique, agricultural practices built on bioengineering knowledge that the practitioners have no concept of as bioengineering, religious healing traditions whose effective interventions have Selenar gene-forging in their deep history.

Occasionally a fragment encounters conditions that allow it to resurface. A healer's child discovers they have unusual arcane sensitivity and an unusual facility with the biological techniques their family has practiced for generations. A community that has maintained a medical tradition for two thousand years produces a practitioner who begins to understand that the tradition is much more than it appears. An outsider biomancer encounters a local practice and recognises in it something they have only seen described in fragments of very old text.

These encounters are the setting's most reliable mechanism for the hidden-cult tradition to encounter pieces of the Selenar legacy it has lost. The lost lines are not a recoverable tradition — they are not a tradition at all, merely scattered fragments in the cultures that absorbed their carriers thirty thousand years ago. But the fragments are out there, in places that no organised biomancer lineage or biologis archive has catalogued, waiting for someone with enough knowledge to know what they're looking at.

## What This Means for the Current Practice

The biomancer tradition in the post-collapse setting is not one thing. It was never one thing — the Selenar themselves were always a confederation of cults that disagreed on fundamentals — and thirty thousand years of divergence have produced a landscape of practices that share a root and have almost nothing else in common.

What they share, that no other tradition shares, is a specific orientation toward living systems that comes from the Selenar founding. The conviction that living tissue is legible — that it has a pattern, that the pattern is meaningful, that understanding the pattern is the work. The coexistence of technical knowledge and arcane sensitivity as complementary rather than contradictory ways of approaching that pattern. The particular quality of relationship that a biomancer has with a subject they are working with — the intimacy of their perception, the weight they give to what they feel about what is happening alongside what they measure.

These characteristics are recognisable across every lineage that carries Selenar heritage, however diverged. They are the signature of the root. A sufficiently knowledgeable observer encountering any biomancer tradition — hidden cult, biologis order, or the scattered remnants of the lost lines — can identify the family resemblance.

What none of them know is the full shape of what they came from. No single tradition holds enough of the original framework to reconstruct it. The Magna Mater — the central repository that held the archetype source code, the comparative database, the formal documentation of what the Selenar had learned across millennia of practice — is gone. The hidden cults have their fragments. The biologis order has its archive. The lost lines have traces so degraded they are barely legible.

Whether the complete picture would be recoverable, given access to enough fragments from enough traditions, is a question that practitioners who have thought carefully about it tend not to discuss with anyone outside their immediate circle. The implications of what a fully reconstructed Selenar tradition could do are significant enough that possessing such a reconstruction is a different kind of problem from not possessing it.

The Magna Mater is, according to some of the oldest Selenar-adjacent texts that any biologis archive holds, not truly destroyed. Hidden. The founding matriarch who hid it deleted her own memories of where she had placed it, which is either the most thorough concealment possible or the most thorough loss, depending on what the Selenar believed about identity persistence across the deletion of specific memories.

Whether that distinction matters depends on whether the resurrection works the way the Selenar believed it did.

Whether anyone has been iterating toward that matriarch's archetype across thirty thousand years of hidden practice, waiting for the iteration that remembers what was hidden — is not a question the tradition has an official position on.

## Selenar Traces in the Current Setting

**The numbering convention:** Biomancer lineages that trace direct Selenar descent sometimes maintain iteration numbering for specific roles within the lineage — the current holder of a position is the Nth person to hold it according to the lineage's count, with the implication that they are in some sense a continuation of the first. This is most common in the most conservative hidden-cult traditions and has been progressively abandoned in others as the resurrectionist context for the numbering has been lost. A biomancer who signs themselves with a number is carrying a Selenar inheritance they may or may not understand.

**The matriarchal default:** Most biomancer lineages default to matriarchal leadership and most of the practitioner tradition's most significant historical figures are women. This reflects the Selenar's founding culture rather than any inherent quality of the practice. Male practitioners exist across all lineages and the tradition has no formal exclusion, but the weight of precedent is consistent across thirty thousand years and the lineages that have moved away from it tend to be the ones most integrated with outside cultures.

**The insularity:** Every biomancer tradition, regardless of lineage and regardless of how integrated with outside communities it has become in practice, maintains a strong default toward not sharing knowledge outside the lineage without careful consideration of who is receiving it and what they will do with it. This is explicitly taught in most traditions. It is the oldest surviving policy the Selenar had, predating even the archetype project, and it has been reinforced rather than eroded by thirty thousand years of watching what happens when powerful knowledge reaches people who do not handle it carefully.

**The silence:** The original Selenar responded to offers, threats, and entreaties from external powers with silence. Many biomancer lineages maintain a version of this: the specific practice of not responding to requests for access to their knowledge or their practice in ways that could be interpreted as negotiation. Silence is not refusal. Silence is not acceptance. Silence is the assertion that the question of whether to share has not been decided yet, and that deciding it is the lineage's prerogative and nobody else's.

The lineages that abandoned this practice are, without exception, the ones that have had the worst relationships with the external powers they dealt with.
