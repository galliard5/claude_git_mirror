# Reshaping Programme Scenario Framework

**Type:** Setting-agnostic scenario framework (Gallihammer universe)
**Relationship:** Institutional counterpart to the Remade Origin Framework
**Compatible Launch Points:** Dead Terra settlement, void ship, station, enclave, mining operation, warlord compound — any entity with operational needs and a biomancer on staff
**Campaign Style:** Open-world, long-form
**Starting Point:** Player arrives at intake/signup

---

## Core Premise

An entity — town, ship, mining concern, warlord, enclave — has operational roles it cannot fill with unmodified personnel. The work is too dangerous, too specialised, or requires capabilities that baseline biology simply doesn't offer. The entity runs a **Reshaping Programme**: an organised operation that takes in recruits through various channels, reshapes them into specialised biological workers, deploys them for a term of service, and (nominally) returns them to a functional baseline at the end.

The programme exists because the need is real. Guards who can see in the dark and shrug off small-arms fire. Miners who breathe toxic atmosphere and dig with purpose-built limbs. Maintenance crews that fit inside hull plating. Scouts that cross irradiated wasteland without protective gear.

The programme is also an institution, with everything that implies. Paperwork. Orientation. Staff who've done this before and will do it again. A culture that has formed around the process — sometimes professional, sometimes corrupt, sometimes both. The quality of the programme depends entirely on who's running it and who's watching them. Nobody is watching most of them.

The campaign begins when the player walks in the door.

## Programme Anatomy

Every instance of this framework shares the same structural skeleton. All specifics — names, culture, quality, corruption level — are variables determined by the commissioning body.

### The Commissioning Body

The entity that needs the workers and funds the programme. This is the single most important variable — it determines everything downstream.

**Types:**
- **Settlement/Town** — needs guards, labour, infrastructure maintenance. Programme is a civic function, possibly with conscription authority. May be well-run or deeply corrupt depending on local governance.
- **Mining/Industrial Concern** — needs specialised labour for hazardous extraction. Programme is a commercial operation. Workers are assets on a balance sheet. Efficiency is the governing principle; welfare is a variable.
- **Ship/Station** — needs crew the vessel can't recruit conventionally. Maintenance forms for the crawlspaces, warforms for boarding defence, recon for void-walks and hull surveys. Programme is internal, insular, shaped by the ship's own culture.
- **Warlord/Stronghold** — needs a personal army, a workforce, or both. Programme may be professional or may be one person's project with institutional trappings. Oversight is whatever the warlord says it is.
- **Enclave/Order** — religious, philosophical, or ideological community with specialised needs. Skitarii-equivalent combat forms, ritual workers, temple guardians. Programme may carry genuine spiritual weight or use ideology as window dressing for coercion.
- **Caravan/Fleet** — mobile operation, needs workers adapted to life on the move. Programme is small, tight, possibly informal. The biomancer might be the same person who does the cooking.

### The Programme's Name and Culture

Every programme names itself. The framework provides the slot — the commissioning body fills it.

**Naming conventions vary:**
- **Functional** — calls the workers exactly what they are. "Pit crew." "Hull rats." "Warforms." No pretension.
- **Patriotic/Honourable** — wraps the programme in civic virtue. "The Changed Watch." "Sworn Shields." "The Reforged." Workers are told they're serving something larger.
- **Clinical** — designation codes, batch numbers, operational classifications. Workers are inventory.
- **Colloquial** — whatever the locals started calling them, formalised through use. Often more honest than the official name.

A programme's name tells you what the commissioning body thinks of its workers. Whether the workers agree is a separate question.

## Intake Channels

How someone enters the programme determines how much choice they have, what tracks are available to them, and how the programme treats them. Multiple channels feed into the same programme simultaneously — a contracted volunteer and a condemned prisoner might go through intake on the same day, assigned to the same track, reshaped by the same biomancer, and deployed to the same unit. Their experiences of the process will be fundamentally different.

### Contracted Volunteer

**Entry:** Walks in, asks about openings, signs paperwork.

The programme's preferred intake. Contracted workers chose to be here — for pay, for opportunity, for adventure, for lack of better options. They receive full orientation: what the tracks are, what the reshaping involves, what the term of service looks like, what reversal entails. The information is mostly honest, though every programme has things it undersells.

Contracted workers get input into track assignment. Not unlimited choice — the programme assigns based on need and suitability — but a volunteer who wants recon over warform will usually be heard. They have a defined term. They have a contract that specifies reversal conditions. They are, in theory, the programme's most protected intake.

In practice, protection depends on the programme. A well-run civic guard programme honours its contracts. A mining concern may find reasons to extend terms. A warlord's programme may have excellent contracts that mean nothing.

**Reshaping range:** Standard. Moderate to substantial depending on track, but within the programme's established parameters. No experimental work. The volunteer gets what was described at signup, or close to it.

### Debt Indenture

**Entry:** Arrives with paperwork from a creditor. The programme has purchased or assumed the debt. Service clears the balance.

The same programme, different leverage. Debt indentures get orientation — they need to understand what's happening to function — but less choice in assignment. The programme puts them where it needs bodies. Track preference is heard but not prioritised. The term is defined by the debt, not by a service period, and the accounting is controlled by the programme.

The relationship between the programme and the debt indenture is transactional in a way that contracted work isn't. The volunteer is selling their service. The indenture is buying their freedom. The programme knows the difference and it shows — in assignment priority, in quality of reshaping, in how quickly grievances get addressed.

**Reshaping range:** Standard to heavy. The programme may push debt indentures toward less desirable assignments or more extensive reshaping than it would offer a volunteer for the same track. Not always — a well-run programme treats all standard intakes the same. But the latitude exists.

### Civic Conscript

**Entry:** Summoned. Mandatory service under local law or custom.

Conscription exists in settlements and enclaves that can't attract enough volunteers and have the legal authority to compel service. The conscript didn't choose this, but they're not being punished either — it's civic duty, like a draft. The programme treats them as legitimate workers with rights and a defined term.

Conscripts receive the same orientation as volunteers. Track assignment is based on need and suitability — the conscript may have less input than a volunteer but more than a debt indenture. The key difference is psychological: the conscript didn't walk in the door voluntarily, and the programme's culture around conscription determines whether that's treated as unremarkable (everyone serves a term) or as a mark of lesser status.

**Reshaping range:** Moderate. Conscription programmes tend toward conservative reshaping — the community expects its members back in recognisable condition. A programme that produces radical transformations from conscripted citizens faces political consequences. This constraint doesn't apply everywhere, but it's the norm.

### Condemned

**Entry:** Delivered under guard. Sentence has been passed. The programme is the sentence, or part of it.

The condemned arrive with no negotiating position. They learn what their sentence entails when they're told — which may be at intake, or may be when the biomancer starts working. Track assignment is the programme's decision entirely. The condemned go where no one else will: the extreme end of each track, the experimental forms, the hazardous deployments.

**Defined sentence:** If the conviction carries a term, the condemned has an endpoint. They serve, they get reversed (or offered reversal), they leave. The programme may honour this cleanly or may find reasons to extend. Depends on oversight — and there usually isn't much.

**Indefinite/Life sentence:** No endpoint. The reshaping may be permanent by design. The condemned becomes a programme asset for as long as the programme exists or they do. Reversal is not part of the arrangement.

**Reshaping range:** Full spectrum, including extreme. The condemned are the programme's access to forms that no other channel would accept. Radical body-plan restructuring, experimental techniques, forms that push the biomancer's capability. A condemned intake is the closest the institutional programme gets to the Remade origin's territory — a person reshaped into something with no resemblance to what they were, fully aware, with no guaranteed return.

**Cybernetic depth and the condemned:** Programmes that use cybernetic reshaping on condemned workers face a practical constraint that biological reshaping does not: reversal difficulty scales with augmentation depth, and the programme has to live with the consequences. A defined-sentence condemned worker pushed to integration or total conversion depth presents a reversal problem at end of term that is expensive, medically complex, and may not produce a functional result. For this reason, most programmes that intend to actually honour defined sentences keep cybernetic augmentation on condemned workers at moderate to heavy levels — deep enough to be effective, shallow enough that reversal remains feasible. Programmes that push condemned workers to extreme cybernetic depth are either life-sentence operations where reversal is not on the table, or programmes that have no real intention of reversing the work regardless of what the sentence paperwork says. The depth of cybernetic augmentation applied to a defined-sentence condemned worker is, in practice, a reliable indicator of whether the programme plans to let them go.

## Operational Tracks

The programme's output categories. Each track serves a specific operational need and has a reshaping spectrum from moderate to extreme. Where a given worker lands on that spectrum depends on their intake channel, the programme's needs, and how much the biomancer is willing to push.

All tracks share a core principle: **the mind stays intact.** A reshaping that destroys cognition produces an animal, and the programme needs workers who can follow orders, adapt to changing conditions, report what they observe, and function within a chain of command. Even the most extreme reshapings preserve the subject's ability to think, communicate, and understand what's expected of them.

Most forms across all tracks are **neuter by default** — reproductive biology is unnecessary for the operational role and adds complexity to the reshaping. Exceptions exist where breeding capability is part of the programme's long-term design, but these are uncommon and typically flagged at intake. At the conservative end of the reshaping spectrum, however, the biomancer may leave the subject's original sex intact simply because removing it would require more effort than ignoring it — if the reshaping doesn't touch that region of the body plan, there's no operational reason to add extra work.

### Voluntary Extreme Reshaping

Not everyone who walks through the door wants the standard option. Occasionally an intake — contracted or otherwise — requests a more radical reshaping than their channel and track would normally produce. Motivations vary: fascination with the extreme forms, desire to be maximally effective in the role, personal reasons the programme may or may not inquire about, or simply a willingness to trade more of themselves for more capability.

How the programme handles this is programme-dependent. Some treat it as an intake option — a box on the form, a conversation with the biomancer, a signed acknowledgment that the worker understands what they're agreeing to. Others refuse outright, reserving extreme reshaping for condemned intakes only. Others are flexible on a case-by-case basis.

For **debt indentures**, voluntary extreme reshaping may be offered as an accelerant — accept a heavier reshaping, take the assignments nobody else wants, and the debt clears faster. The programme benefits from a willing body in the extreme tier. The indenture benefits from a shorter term. Whether the trade is worth it depends on the individual, the programme, and how honest the accounting is.

The key distinction is consent. A condemned worker pushed to the extreme end didn't choose it. A volunteer who requests it did — and the programme's responsibility shifts accordingly. Reversal terms for voluntarily extreme reshapings should be explicit at intake, because coming back from a form that radical is harder, slower, and less certain.

### Labour Track

**Operational need:** Work in environments too hostile, too demanding, or too inaccessible for baseline personnel. Mining, construction, hazardous material handling, deep-ruin salvage, irradiated zone clearance.

**Moderate reshaping:**
- Reinforced skeletal structure, enhanced musculature
- Environmental tolerances — toxic atmosphere breathing, temperature extremes, radiation resistance
- Additional limbs or modified existing limbs for tool use, load-bearing, climbing
- Enhanced stamina, reduced sleep requirements
- Form is clearly modified but recognisably derived from the subject's original species

**Heavy reshaping:**
- Fully restructured body plan optimised for a specific work environment
- Specialised appendages replacing general-purpose limbs — drill structures, gripping arrays, manipulation clusters
- Respiratory systems rebuilt for specific atmospheres — the worker *needs* the toxic air, normal atmosphere becomes uncomfortable
- Sensory modifications for lightless, high-pressure, or otherwise hostile conditions
- Form is substantially inhuman but purpose-built and functional

**Extreme reshaping (condemned track):**
- Body plan designed from scratch around an industrial function
- May be unrecognisable as having ever been a person
- Sessile or semi-sessile forms for fixed installation work
- Biological integration with machinery or infrastructure
- The worker *is* the equipment

**Psychological texture:** Labour-form workers often report that the reshaping feels *efficient*. The body does what it was built to do without resistance. The horror, when it comes, is quiet — the realisation that the work feels natural, that the old body's limitations now seem like flaws rather than features. Well-run programmes address this with rotation and leave. Poorly-run programmes don't notice or don't care.

### Warform Track

**Operational need:** Combat, guard duty, territorial defence, boarding actions, hazardous-zone patrol. The programme needs things that fight, and fight better than baseline personnel with weapons.

**Moderate reshaping:**
- Enhanced senses — low-light vision, thermal sensing, motion detection, expanded hearing range
- Armour plating — subdermal or external, scaled to threat environment
- Combat reflexes, enhanced reaction speed
- Reinforced frame capable of absorbing damage that would cripple a baseline
- Natural weaponry — claws, spurs, bite force — supplementing conventional arms
- Form is clearly martial but retains a recognisable body plan. The worker looks like a soldier, not a monster.

**Heavy reshaping:**
- Body plan restructured for a specific combat role — ambush predator, heavy assault, siege-breaker, point defence
- Multiple limb configurations for simultaneous weapon use and movement
- Sensory arrays tuned for battlefield awareness — threat detection, friend-or-foe discrimination
- Integrated biological weaponry — venom delivery, acid projection, sonic organs
- Form is purpose-built for violence but retains humanoid-level manipulation for equipment use and communication

**Extreme reshaping (condemned track):**
- Purpose-built killing organism with no resemblance to origin species
- Body plan designed from scratch for a specific threat environment
- May have no recognisable head, face, or bilateral symmetry
- Sensory and combat systems that have no baseline analogue
- The worker is a weapon platform that happens to think

**Psychological texture:** Warforms deal with the fact that their body wants to do what it was built for. Combat instincts are part of the reshaping — the biomancer builds in threat response, aggression management, targeting reflexes. The mind has to decide how much to lean into what the body offers. Good programmes train their warforms to maintain cognitive control. Bad programmes just point them at problems. The worst moments are when a warform acts on instinct and it *works perfectly*.

### Utility Track

**Operational need:** Maintenance, repair, technical work, infrastructure management. Getting into spaces baseline personnel can't reach, doing work baseline hands can't do, surviving conditions baseline bodies can't tolerate.

**Moderate reshaping:**
- Reduced size for access to tight spaces — conduits, hull plating, internal infrastructure
- Enhanced manual dexterity — additional fingers, finer motor control, tool-specialised appendages
- Magnification vision, electrical sensing, vibration detection
- Environmental tolerances for the specific conditions inside the infrastructure — heat, vacuum exposure, chemical atmosphere
- Form is small, nimble, and specialised but clearly a modified person

**Heavy reshaping:**
- Body plan rebuilt for life inside infrastructure — elongated, flexible, capable of navigating spaces not designed for habitation
- Limbs replaced with specialised maintenance appendages — welding structures, cutting tools, diagnostic sensors built into the body
- Sensory systems tuned to detect structural faults, power fluctuations, material stress
- May be capable of extended periods in vacuum, toxic atmosphere, or zero gravity
- Form is more tool than person — recognisable as biological but not as any natural organism

**Extreme reshaping (condemned track):**
- Lives inside the infrastructure permanently
- Body plan is indistinguishable from the machinery it maintains
- May be integrated into ship/station/settlement systems as a biological component
- Sensory experience is the infrastructure itself — feels hull stress, tastes atmosphere composition, hears power flow
- Barely distinguishable from the systems it services, which is the point

**Psychological texture:** Utility workers inhabit a strange space. Their reshaping is less dramatic than warforms and less grinding than labour, but the intimacy with infrastructure creates its own kind of drift. A utility worker who spends months inside the walls of a ship starts thinking of the ship as their body and their body as a component of it. Well-run programmes rotate utility workers to the surface regularly. Neglected ones forget what sunlight looks like and stop missing it.

### Recon Track

**Operational need:** Scouting, surveillance, pathfinding, intelligence gathering. Going where others can't, seeing what others miss, and getting back to report.

**Moderate reshaping:**
- Reduced size and profile — smaller, lighter, harder to detect
- Enhanced sensory suite — expanded visual spectrum, directional hearing, chemical sensing, tremorsense
- Camouflage capability — chromatophores, texture shifting, scent suppression
- Enhanced speed and agility over varied terrain
- Communication adaptations — subvocal, pheromonal, or other low-detectability channels
- Form is small, fast, and subtle. Built to not be noticed.

**Heavy reshaping:**
- Body plan optimised for a specific reconnaissance environment — aerial, subterranean, aquatic, void
- Sensory systems that have no baseline equivalent — echolocation, electromagnetic field detection, radiation mapping
- Near-total environmental camouflage — the worker is effectively invisible in their operating environment
- Minimal physical presence — the body is a sensor platform with just enough locomotion to position itself
- Communication systems built into the biology — the worker transmits what they perceive through organic channels

**Extreme reshaping (condemned track):**
- Sensory organism with minimal physical agency
- Body plan exists to perceive and transmit, not to act independently
- May be deployed as a living sensor grid — placed and left, reporting continuously
- Perception is extraordinary; physical capability is deliberately minimal
- The worker sees everything, does nothing, and can't leave

**Psychological texture:** Recon workers are watchers. Their reshaping optimises for perception at the expense of presence — they become very good at knowing what's around them and very small in relation to it. The moderate end produces excellent scouts who enjoy the capability. The extreme end produces something closer to a living camera — aware, perceptive, and nearly helpless. The worst assignment for a recon worker is static deployment: placed somewhere to watch, indefinitely, unable to move, reporting to people who may or may not be listening.

## Reshaping Methodology

The framework accommodates two fundamentally different approaches to reshaping, and many programmes employ both.

### Biological Reshaping

The original and most common methodology. A biomancer transforms the subject's body — their own tissue serving as the medium, reshaped into the target form through biomantic technique. The result is a continuous biological organism: every cell descended from the subject's original biology, however radically restructured.

**Advantages:** Reversal is another reshaping — the material is present and workable. The body is self-sustaining once stable; no external maintenance infrastructure is required. The subject's biology handles healing, adaptation, and environmental response through organic processes. No external dependencies.

**Limitations:** Modification is holistic — changing one system affects the whole organism. Incremental adjustment is difficult; significant changes typically require a full reshaping session. Upgrading or reconfiguring a biologically reshaped worker means reshaping them again, with all the time, cost, and biological stress that implies.

### Cybernetic Reshaping

The mechanical alternative. A cyberwright augments the subject's body through removal and replacement — excising organic tissue and substituting engineered mechanical, electronic, or synthetic components. The result is a hybrid organism: biological core (brain, nervous system, whatever organic tissue the cyberwright preserved) operating within a mechanical framework.

**Advantages:** Modular. Components can be swapped, upgraded, or reconfigured without rebuilding the entire system. A cybernetically reshaped worker can be refitted for a different role by changing their loadout rather than reshaping their body. Damage to mechanical components can be repaired with parts and tools rather than biomantic intervention. Standardised components mean multiple cyberwrights can service the same worker — no single-practitioner dependency.

**Limitations:** Reversal is fundamentally harder (see Reversal section). Removed organic tissue is gone, not transformed — getting back to baseline requires biological reconstruction, not just reshaping. Maintenance is ongoing and mandatory; cybernetic components require power, calibration, and eventual replacement. This creates infrastructure dependencies that biological reshaping does not. A cybernetically reshaped workforce requires a supply chain. A biologically reshaped workforce requires a biomancer at intake and reversal, and is self-sustaining between.

Additionally, cybernetic reshaping introduces vulnerabilities that biological reshaping does not. Mechanical systems can be interfaced with, overridden, or compromised from outside. A biologically reshaped worker's body is their own in a way that a cybernetically reshaped worker's body may not be — the hardware responds to signals, and the question of who can send those signals is a security concern that well-run programmes take seriously and poorly-run programmes exploit.

### Hybrid Approaches

Some programmes use both methodologies on the same worker — biological reshaping for the body plan, cybernetic augmentation for specific capability additions. A labour-form worker with a biologically restructured frame and cybernetically enhanced sensory systems. A warform with organic armour plating and integrated electronic threat-assessment hardware. Hybrid reshaping requires either a practitioner trained in both disciplines or a biomancer and cyberwright working in concert, and the integration points between biological and mechanical systems are where most complications arise.

Hybrid approaches are more expensive and more technically demanding than either methodology alone, but they can produce results that neither can achieve independently. Programmes that employ hybrid reshaping tend to be well-resourced and technically sophisticated. Programmes that cannot afford it pick one methodology and commit.

## The Reshaping Process

### Staff Biomancers

Most programmes employ **staff biomancers** — specialists on permanent or long-term contract who handle the standard reshaping workload. A typical programme has one to three biomancers depending on throughput, with assistants and support staff.

Staff biomancers are professionals. They've done this before — dozens, hundreds, possibly thousands of times depending on the programme's age. They have standard procedures for each track and reshaping level. They know the common complications and how to manage them. They are efficient, competent, and often personally detached from the work in the way that any professional becomes detached from routine.

Their relationship with the intakes varies. Some are clinical — the work is the work. Some are reassuring — walking the intake through the process, explaining what will happen, managing expectations. Some are brusque. A few are cruel, though a well-run programme weeds those out (a poorly-run programme promotes them).

Staff biomancers handle **standard reshapings** — the established forms and modifications the programme uses regularly. They work from proven templates, adapted to the individual subject but not invented from scratch.

### Staff Cyberwrights

Programmes that employ cybernetic reshaping maintain **staff cyberwrights** — the mechanical counterpart to staff biomancers. A typical programme has one to two cyberwrights with technical assistants.

Staff cyberwrights work from standardised component sets and installation procedures. They know the programme's chassis designs, the standard loadouts for each track, the common integration complications and how to manage them. They are efficient, competent, and often more openly technical in their interaction with intakes than biomancers — the work is mechanical, and explaining what a component does and where it goes is more straightforward than explaining what a biomantic transformation will feel like.

Their ongoing relationship with reshaped workers is more sustained than a biomancer's. A biologically reshaped worker's body, once stable, is self-maintaining; the biomancer's involvement is front-loaded. A cybernetically reshaped worker requires periodic maintenance — calibration, component replacement, software updates — and the staff cyberwright who handles this sees the same workers repeatedly throughout their term of service. This creates a different dynamic: less intense than the biomancer's transformative involvement, but more continuous.

### Bespoke Biomancers and Cyberwrights

For **specialist commissions** — unusual requirements, unique forms, experimental techniques — the programme brings in a bespoke specialist. For biological reshaping, this is a bespoke biomancer. These are outside specialists hired for a specific job.

Bespoke work happens when the programme needs something its staff can't produce: a form that doesn't match any existing template, a reshaping that requires techniques the staff haven't mastered, a commission from the programme's leadership that goes beyond standard parameters. Bespoke biomancers are expensive, temporary, and often eccentric. They may have opinions about the programme's standard work that the staff biomancers don't appreciate.

The bespoke specialist's relationship with the subject is more personal by necessity — they're building something new, and they need to understand the raw material. This can be better or worse for the subject depending on the practitioner.

Bespoke cyberwrights are brought in for similar reasons — custom chassis designs, non-standard component integration, augmentation configurations that the staff cyberwrights haven't worked with. A bespoke cyberwright building a one-off combat chassis for a specific operational requirement is doing work that no standardised loadout can replicate.

### The Process Itself

**Duration:** Hours to days depending on the reshaping level and the biomancer's method. Moderate reshapings might be completed in a single extended session. Extreme reshapings are staged over days or weeks, allowing the body to stabilise between phases.

**Consciousness:** Variable. Some programmes sedate the subject for the duration. Others keep them aware — either because the biomancer needs feedback during the process, because the method requires it, or because the programme considers awareness part of the experience (for better or worse). Condemned intakes are more likely to be kept aware, either for practical reasons or punitive ones.

**Pain management:** Ranges from comprehensive (sedation, nerve blocking, post-procedure care) to minimal (the process hurts, deal with it). Correlates strongly with programme quality. Also correlates with intake channel — contracted workers typically receive better pain management than condemned.

**Orientation period:** After reshaping, the worker needs time to adapt to the new body. Learning to move, to perceive through modified senses, to use new appendages. Well-run programmes build in structured adaptation training. Poorly-run programmes deploy immediately and let the worker figure it out in the field.

## Reversal

### Biological Reversal — Contracted and Conscripted Workers

The programme promises a return to **functional baseline** at end of term. Not necessarily identical to the original body — reversal is a second reshaping, and perfect reconstruction is difficult. But functional: bipedal (if they were before), manually dexterous, sensorily normal, capable of living in baseline society without accommodation.

**Optional retention:** Workers may choose to keep specific modifications they find useful or desirable. Enhanced senses, increased resilience, minor structural modifications. This is presented as a benefit — "you earned these, keep what works for you." The programme is often happy to comply because partial reversal is cheaper and easier than full.

**Optional changes:** Reversal is a full reshaping in its own right, and a good biomancer can make adjustments along the way. Workers may request cosmetic changes during reversal — a different height, different hair or eye colour, a change of sex if the reshaped form removed it (or simply because the opportunity is there). The programme treats these as minor additions to the reversal process. Some programmes offer a menu. Others handle it informally — ask the biomancer, see what they say. The result is that workers sometimes come out of reversal looking noticeably different from how they went in, by choice. It's one of the quieter perks of service.

The quality of reversal depends on the programme and the biomancer performing it. The best reversals leave the worker feeling close to their old self. The worst leave them functional but *different* — proportions slightly off, senses not quite right, a persistent awareness that this body is a reconstruction, not the original. The original is gone. This is the second version.

### Cybernetic Reversal — Retention, Swap-Out, and the Harder Road

Cybernetic reversal is not always full extraction. In practice, most cybernetically augmented workers leaving a programme keep some of their hardware — and the programme often encourages this, because partial retention is cheaper and simpler than full reversal.

**Retention of conservative augments:** Workers commonly keep augmentations that are useful in civilian life and carry no operational stigma. Enhanced optics — eyes that see better than the originals — are the most popular retention. Neural interface jacks are nearly as common; a worker who has spent a term accessing data and communication through an NIJack rarely wants to go back to handheld devices. Basic prosthetic limbs, subdermal reinforcement, respiratory filters — anything that improves quality of life without marking the worker as visibly industrial or military tends to stay.

**Component swap-out:** For heavier augmentations — the industrial and combat hardware that defines the worker's operational role — programmes offer swap-out rather than full extraction. A labour worker whose arm was replaced with a power drill doesn't get their original arm back (it's gone), but the drill assembly is removed and a general-purpose prosthetic arm is installed in its place. A warform's integrated weapons are extracted and replaced with standard-capability prosthetics. The worker leaves with a functional body that is still partially cybernetic but no longer purpose-built for the programme's operational needs. Swap-out is faster, cheaper, and produces better results than full biological reconstruction, and for many workers it's the preferred option — they've adapted to having cybernetic components and would rather keep functional hardware than undergo a difficult organic rebuild.

**Hot-swappable components:** Well-designed cybernetic augmentation programmes build modularity into their standard loadouts from the start. Limb attachments use standardised interface sockets. Sensory packages plug into common mounting points. Tool-specific appendages connect through universal couplings that accept both industrial and civilian components. This means a worker's operational hardware can be swapped for civilian equivalents in hours rather than days — unplug the mining drill, plug in the prosthetic hand, recalibrate, done. Hot-swap capability is a design philosophy, not a universal feature; it requires forethought at the augmentation stage and standardised component architecture across the programme. Programmes that invest in it find reversal significantly easier and cheaper. Programmes that install bespoke or non-standard components for each worker find reversal proportionally harder.

The hot-swap principle has limits. It applies to external and semi-external components — limbs, tool attachments, sensor packages, modular armour. It does not apply to deep integration augmentations — neural architecture modifications, skeletal replacement, organ substitution. Those require surgical extraction regardless of how the programme designed its component standards.

### Full Cybernetic Reversal

When retention and swap-out are not sufficient — when the worker wants to return to a fully organic body, or when the augmentation is deep enough that swap-out doesn't cover it — full cybernetic reversal is fundamentally more difficult than biological reversal. This asymmetry is the single most important factor programmes must address when offering a cybernetic reshaping pathway.

Biological reversal is another reshaping — the biomancer works with tissue that is present, transforming it back toward baseline. The material is there. Cybernetic reversal requires *reconstruction*. The organic tissue that was removed during augmentation does not exist anymore. Getting back to baseline means growing, cloning, or fabricating new organic components to replace the mechanical ones being extracted, and then integrating that new tissue into a body that has adapted around the hardware.

This means cybernetic reversal requires a biomancer or chirugen in addition to the cyberwright — someone who can build the organic systems the hardware replaced. Programmes that offer cybernetic reshaping and promise reversal must either maintain biological reconstruction capability on staff or contract it at end of term. Programmes that promise cybernetic reversal without this capability are making promises they cannot keep.

**Reversal feasibility by depth:**

**Moderate augmentation** (utility and protective level) — reversal is feasible. Organic systems that were supplemented can usually recover once hardware is removed, with medical support. The worker gets most of themselves back. Scars, phantom sensations from removed implants, and minor functional differences are common but manageable.

**Heavy augmentation** (enhancement level) — reversal is a major undertaking. Organic systems that were fully replaced must be reconstructed. The result is functional but imperfect — a reconstructed body that works but doesn't feel quite like the original. The worker returns to a baseline that is technically their own but experientially unfamiliar.

**Extreme augmentation** (integration and total conversion) — reversal is theoretically possible but practically approaches a full biological reshaping in scope. The worker would need a new organic body built around their surviving biological components. This is not reversal in any honest sense; it is a third transformation. Most programmes that push workers to this depth either do not promise reversal or promise it with caveats that amount to the same thing.

**The consent implication:** Programmes that offer cybernetic reshaping have an obligation to be explicit at intake about the relationship between augmentation depth and reversal feasibility. A worker who signs up for a moderate cybernetic augmentation with full reversal promised is in a different position from one pushed to extreme augmentation where reversal is a legal fiction. Well-run programmes draw a clear line: augmentation beyond a specified depth requires additional consent because the reversal guarantee weakens past that point. Poorly-run programmes do not mention this until it matters.

### Debt Indentures (Both Methodologies)

Same reversal promise as contracted workers, contingent on debt clearance. If the debt is paid, the reversal happens. If the programme has extended the term through creative accounting, the reversal waits. The debt indenture's access to reversal is technically guaranteed and practically contingent.

### Condemned — Defined Sentence (Both Methodologies)

If the sentence has an endpoint, reversal is part of the release process. The condemned serves their term, gets reversed, and returns to society. The reversal may be less careful than what a contracted worker receives — functional but not prioritised. Some programmes reverse condemned workers to a minimum viable baseline and call it done.

For cybernetically reshaped condemned workers, the reversal challenge is compounded by the likelihood that they were pushed to heavier augmentation depths than volunteers. A condemned worker at integration level returning from a defined sentence faces a reversal process that is expensive, medically complex, and produces results that are at best approximate. Some programmes quietly offer cybernetically condemned workers retention of their augmented form as an alternative to a reversal that would be marginal at best. Whether this is kindness or cost-saving depends on the programme.

### Condemned — Indefinite / Life Sentence (Both Methodologies)

No reversal. The reshaping is permanent by design. The condemned worker serves until the programme no longer needs them, they die, or the programme itself ends. If the programme collapses or the commissioning body falls, the condemned worker is loose in the world in whatever form they were given, with no institutional pathway back.

For cybernetically reshaped condemned workers, the collapse scenario is particularly acute. A biologically reshaped worker loose in the world is self-sustaining — their body functions without external support. A cybernetically reshaped worker loose in the world without maintenance infrastructure faces degradation of their systems over time. Components wear. Power systems deplete. Calibrations drift. Without access to a cyberwright and a supply of compatible parts, the worker's augmented body slowly fails — not catastrophically, usually, but progressively, uncomfortably, and with no institutional recourse.

## Programme Quality Spectrum

The framework doesn't enforce quality. The same structural template produces wildly different programmes depending on who's running them and what constraints they operate under.

### Well-Run Programme

Contracts are honoured. Orientation is honest. Pain management is standard. Adaptation training is structured. Workers are treated as valued assets whose service has limits. Reversal is performed carefully. Workers leave the programme with functional bodies and whatever optional modifications they chose to keep. Alumni may speak well of the experience, or at least neutrally.

The programme still reshapes people into inhuman forms and deploys them into dangerous work. "Well-run" means the institution functions as promised, not that the experience is pleasant.

### Adequate Programme

Contracts are mostly honoured. Orientation covers the basics but undersells the difficult parts. Pain management exists but isn't always prioritised. Workers are treated as assets, emphasis on *assets*. Reversal happens on schedule but quality varies. The programme works, but nobody's inspecting it closely and it shows in the margins. Most programmes live here.

### Poorly-Run Programme

Contracts are flexible documents. Orientation is perfunctory. Pain management is inconsistent. Condemned workers are treated badly; debt indentures are treated as nearly-condemned. The biomancer staff may be competent but overworked, or incompetent but cheap. Reversal happens when it happens. Workers leave the programme functional but marked — physically, psychologically, or both.

### Corrupt Programme

The institutional structure exists to provide cover for what is effectively a Remade-origin operation with paperwork. Contracts mean nothing. Intake channels blur — a debt indenture who complains may find their accounting adjusted to indefinite. Condemned workers may include people whose convictions were manufactured to supply the programme with expendable bodies. The commissioning body treats workers as property and the programme as a machine that produces it.

The line between a corrupt programme and an outright Remade operation is thin. The programme still uses the institutional framework — intake channels, tracks, terms — but the framework serves the commissioning body's interests exclusively. A corrupt mining programme and a warlord's personal reshaping project may differ only in the quality of the paperwork.

## Session Zero: Building the Programme Instance

When starting a campaign with this framework, Session Zero establishes the specific programme the player is walking into.

### 1. The Commissioning Body

Who runs this? What do they need? How do they treat the people who provide it?
- Settlement, ship, mine, warlord, enclave, caravan — pick one
- What's the operational need that created the programme?
- Where does it fall on the quality spectrum?

### 2. The Programme

What's it called? What's the culture?
- Name and designations for each track
- How long has it been running?
- How many workers does it currently maintain?
- What reshaping methodology does it use? Biological, cybernetic, hybrid, or a mix depending on track and intake?
- What's the staff biomancer/cyberwright like? Is there more than one?
- If cybernetic: what maintenance infrastructure exists? Who services the workers long-term?

### 3. The Player's Intake

How did the player get here?
- Contracted volunteer — what brought them in? Pay? Duty? No other options?
- Debt indenture — what's the debt? Who held it before the programme?
- Civic conscript — is this normal here? How does the community view it?
- Condemned — what was the conviction? Is the sentence defined or indefinite?

### 4. Track Assignment

What does the programme need, and what does the player want?
- Contracted and conscripted workers get input — how much depends on the programme
- Debt indentures get less
- Condemned get told

The player may learn what's available and express a preference, or may be assessed and assigned. The programme's current needs may override preference — if they're short on warforms, that's where the new intake goes regardless of what they wanted.

### 5. The Reshaping Methodology and Level

What approach, and where on the spectrum?
- Biological, cybernetic, or hybrid?
- If cybernetic: has the programme been honest about reversal limits at this depth?
- Intake channel sets the range — contracted workers get standard, condemned get whatever the programme decides
- Track determines the specifics
- The biomancer/cyberwright may have opinions

### 6. Cohort or Solo

Is the player going through intake alone or with others?
- A guard programme might process two or three recruits together — instant NPC relationships
- A mining concern might bring in a new pit crew — five or six intakes, different channels, same destination
- A unique hire or condemned individual might go through alone
- Mixed cohorts (a volunteer and a condemned prisoner processing together) create immediate tension

### 7. The First Day

What does intake look like?
- Walking in the door. Who greets them? What's the space like?
- Paperwork, assessment, orientation — how much, how honest?
- Meeting the biomancer — when, how, what's the dynamic?
- Meeting existing workers — people already reshaped, already deployed, already living in the forms the player is about to receive

## Campaign Hooks (Post-Reshaping)

### Standard Service
The programme works as described. The player is reshaped, deployed, does the job, deals with the daily reality of living in a purpose-built body while serving a term. The drama is in the work, the relationships, and the slow accumulation of experience in a form that isn't what they were.

### The Contract Frays
Something in the arrangement isn't right. Terms shifting. Accounting that doesn't add up. Reversal being deferred. A programme that's sliding down the quality spectrum while the player is inside it.

### Condemned Among Volunteers
A condemned worker in the player's cohort or unit is being treated in ways the programme's own standards wouldn't permit. The player sees it happening. What they do about it depends on their position, their intake channel, and how much they're willing to risk.

### The Body's Capabilities
The reshaping produced something the biomancer didn't fully intend, or the player discovers capabilities the programme didn't mention. This can be advantageous, disturbing, or both.

### Programme Politics
The commissioning body's internal politics affect the programme. Budget cuts, leadership changes, shifting priorities. The programme is an institution, and institutions are subject to the forces that act on all institutions.

### The Old Workers
Long-term workers who've been in their reshaped forms for years. Some are well-adjusted. Some have drifted. Some have been in so long that reversal would be more disorienting than staying. They know things about the programme the new intake doesn't, and they have opinions about sharing.

### Other Programmes
The player's programme isn't the only one. Other entities run their own versions — different standards, different cultures, different levels of corruption. Contact between programmes can be collaborative, competitive, or hostile.

### When the Programme Ends
The commissioning body falls. The ship is lost. The mine plays out. The warlord dies. The programme ceases to exist — but the workers don't. They're loose, reshaped, and the institution that promised them reversal no longer exists to provide it.

## Relationship to the Remade Origin Framework

This framework is the **institutional counterpart** to the Remade Origin Framework. They describe different versions of the same fundamental act: someone is reshaped into something inhuman by someone else's decision.

The difference is structure. The Remade origin is personal, coercive, and unregulated — one patron, one specialist, one subject. The Reshaping Programme is institutional, procedural, and self-regulating (for whatever that's worth). The Remade origin produces singular horrors. The Programme produces them at scale, with paperwork.

A well-run programme and a Remade origin look nothing alike. A corrupt programme and a Remade origin look identical except for the filing system.

The condemned track is where the two frameworks converge most obviously. A condemned worker reshaped to the extreme end of any track is experiencing something structurally indistinguishable from a Remade origin — the institutional framing changes nothing about the reality of being reshaped into an unrecognisable form against your will with no guaranteed return. The programme calls it justice. The Remade framework calls it what it is.

The cybernetic pathway adds a specific inflection to this convergence. A condemned worker pushed to total conversion — brain in a chassis, original body gone — is in a position that is in some respects worse than the biological equivalent, because the road back is longer and less certain. A biologically reshaped condemned worker, if freed, retains a body that could theoretically be reshaped again by any competent biomancer. A cybernetically converted condemned worker, if freed, requires not just a practitioner but an entire reconstruction infrastructure — biomancer, cyberwright, medical support, compatible biological material — to have any hope of returning to an organic form. The institutional programme's collapse hits cybernetically reshaped condemned workers harder than biologically reshaped ones, because the dependencies run deeper.

Both frameworks exist in the same universe. A player who escapes a Remade origin might encounter a programme and see it as either salvation or a more organised version of what happened to them. A programme worker might hear about Remade cases and recognise the process, stripped of the institutional buffer that makes their own situation tolerable.
