VERITY BIOMEDICAL
==================

A boutique biomedical firm occupying two floors of a converted industrial building on the boundary between the Mid-Deck and the Upper Tier approaches of Caldera's Second District. The building is older than most of what surrounds it — brick and poured concrete from two construction generations back, renovated carefully rather than replaced. The sign outside is small. The work inside is not.

Verity does not advertise. It has never needed to.

---

THE FOUNDER
===========

**Dr. Maren Ashby**

Fifty-three. Tall, grey-streaked hair she doesn't do anything particular with, the kind of stillness in her movements that comes from thirty years of working around animals and people in equal measure. She holds doctorates in veterinary medicine and human pharmacology — the latter acquired specifically because the intersection between the two disciplines is where her actual work lives.

She developed Verity's integration protocol independently, working from the same foundational science that Fenrix commercialised and Nexagen later adapted. She was working on it first. She published nothing. She watched what Fenrix did with the technique when they got there ahead of her in the commercial space, and she decided that the answer was not to compete with them but to do it correctly and let the contrast speak for itself.

She is not warm in the way that invites familiarity. She is warm in the way that means she remembers details about every patient she has ever treated and will reference them accurately three years later without consulting notes. She has strong opinions and states them without drama, which some people find refreshing and others find uncomfortable. She does not adjust her opinions based on which category the person in front of her falls into.

She built Verity with private capital — her own savings, two research grants that predated corporate biotech dominance, and a long-term loan from a source she has never publicly identified. She has declined acquisition offers from Solace Medical Group (twice), from a Nexagen front company (once, and she knew it was Nexagen), and from a consortium she could not fully identify (which concerned her more than the ones she could).

Verity is hers. She intends to keep it that way.

**Speech quirks:** Uses precise clinical language as a default register, not as affectation — she thinks in those terms and speaks accordingly. Asks "what do you understand about what I've just told you?" rather than "do you have questions?" — she wants to know what landed, not whether you're comfortable asking.

WHAT VERITY DOES
================

**The core service:** Feral integration — the same foundational process as Nexagen and Fenrix, developed independently, executed under a consent and welfare framework that neither of those organisations has matched and neither has tried to.

**The scope:** Verity does not operate at scale. Ashby made this decision deliberately — she identified the maximum caseload at which she could maintain direct clinical oversight of every patient and did not exceed it. Current operational capacity is twelve active integration clients per year. There is a waiting list. It has never been shorter than four months.

**What the process looks like at Verity:**

*Pre-integration:* A minimum of four consultation sessions before any commitment. Ashby conducts the first one personally. The second and third address specific questions from the patient. The fourth is a structured review — what the patient understood, what they didn't, what has changed in their thinking. Consent documentation is generated after the fourth session, not before. It is also considerably shorter than Nexagen's 140 pages, on the grounds that documentation that requires a legal specialist to interpret is not informed consent.

The sex outcome is explained clearly, specifically, and early — session one, not buried in a packet. "If your donor is female, your result will be female. If your donor is male, your result may be either. Your own sex has no bearing on it. We will discuss your donor choices with this in mind."

*Integration:* Performed on-site, with Ashby present for the full procedure. Post-integration monitoring runs for a minimum of six weeks in Verity's residential wing. Patients are not discharged until Ashby is satisfied with stability.

*Post-integration:* Ongoing support relationship. Verity maintains a patient file on every individual they have integrated. Former patients can return for consultations, welfare assessments, augmentation review, and in one specific and critical way that Nexagen does not offer — **suppression suite alternatives.**

THE SUPPRESSION QUESTION
=========================

This is where Verity diverges most sharply from the Nexagen model, and where the political implications of Verity's existence become most visible.

Nexagen's suppression suite is handler-administered. Verity's pharmacological approach is patient-administered, titrated to the individual, and designed to reach a stable endpoint rather than requiring ongoing dosing indefinitely.

Ashby's position, stated plainly in the paper she published four years ago and that Nexagen's legal division reviewed with significant attention: the neurological interface between human cognition and donor animal template can be managed to a stable equilibrium. The ongoing suppression model is not clinically necessary for most integration outcomes. It is, however, operationally convenient for organisations that benefit from maintaining their patients' dependency.

She did not name Nexagen in the paper. She did not need to.

The paper was peer-reviewed and published. It has been cited forty-seven times. Nexagen has not formally responded to it. Nexagen's legal division has sent Ashby two letters on unrelated matters since its publication. She considers this a form of response.

**What this means in practice:** Verity's patients achieve stable neurological integration and manage their own suppression needs independently. They are not dependent on a handler or a corporate infrastructure to maintain cognitive coherence. This is better medicine. It is also, from the perspective of organisations whose operating model depends on Unit dependency, a significant problem.

Former Nexagen Units whose contracts have concluded sometimes contact Verity. Occasionally ones whose contracts have not concluded do too, through channels that cannot be easily traced. Ashby does not ask which category a patient falls into when they need help. She does ask whether they are safe, and she means the question.

AUGMENTATION SERVICES
======================

Verity does not manufacture augmentations. They source, review, and install hardware through a network of independent augmentation specialists — none of them corporate-affiliated — and they maintain modification and removal capability that Nexagen's programme does not offer its Units.

The vocaliser and optical augmentations Verity installs are functionally comparable to Nexagen's standard suite. The tracking architecture is not: Verity's patients carry no subdermal tracking. Ashby's position on this is not stated in the paper. It is stated clearly in session one: "You will know where you are. We will know where you are when you tell us. That is the correct arrangement."

The suppression implants Verity installs are designed to be self-administering, patient-controlled, and fully removable. The last point is significant. Verity is the only licensed medical facility in Caldera that has performed augmentation removal on former Nexagen Units. The number of times this has happened is small. Nexagen's awareness of each instance is believed to be complete, since the tracking went dark on removal. What Nexagen has done with that awareness is not something Ashby discusses.

POSITION IN CALDERA
====================

**Not a corpo.** Verity holds a medical practice licence and a pharmaceutical research certification. It does not have a security division, a legal army, or a lobby presence. It operates on revenue generated by its practice and two ongoing research grants — one from a civil liberties foundation, one from an anonymous source that Ashby has traced to a shell company she believes is ultimately Varex-adjacent, for reasons she has not fully worked out.

**Not a charity.** Verity charges for its services. The fees are significant — the work is expensive, the expertise is rare, and Ashby has made a deliberate choice not to price the service in a way that would require scaling to survive. Clients who cannot cover the full fee are assessed individually. Ashby has a sliding scale that she does not publicise and applies at her own discretion.

**Not neutral.** Ashby has opinions about Nexagen and Fenrix and she does not pretend otherwise. She is careful about where she states them and how — she is a small operation in a city where both of those organisations have considerably more leverage than she does, and she is not naive about what that means. But she did not build Verity to be comfortable, and she does not run it that way.

RELATIONSHIPS TO OTHER ENTITIES
=================================

**Nexagen Biosystems:** Adversarial, managed. Nexagen views Verity as a triple liability: a competitor in the high-end transformation market, a published critic of their suppression model, and a facility that has assisted former Units in ways that Integrated Assets considers a contractual matter. They have not moved directly against Verity because direct action against a licensed medical facility would surface the kind of regulatory attention that benefits nobody, and because Ashby is careful and well-documented and would be a difficult target. The restraint is strategic, not principled.

**Fenrix Bio-Systems:** Contemptuous indifference, mostly. Verity represents proof that the technique can be done correctly, which Fenrix finds neither threatening nor interesting — their market is clients who want transformation services without consent requirements, which is a different market from Verity's entirely. What Fenrix does find occasionally useful is that Verity's existence gives regulators something legitimate to point to, which absorbs attention that might otherwise land on Fenrix operations. Ashby is aware of this and finds it one of the more unpleasant ironies of her position.

**Solace Medical Group:** Formally collegial, substantively competitive. Solace wants Verity's research data and has wanted it since the suppression paper. They have approached Ashby through three different intermediaries with acquisition offers dressed as partnership proposals. She has declined each time with the same sentence: "Verity is not for sale and its data is not for licensing." Solace's Advanced Therapeutics division has been attempting to replicate her suppression protocol independently. They have not succeeded.

**Coldfront Logistics:** Pragmatic working relationship. Verity uses Coldfront for pharmaceutical supply chain on biological materials that require cold-chain handling. No grey-zone involvement — Ashby's documentation on her supply chain is meticulous specifically because she knows it will be reviewed and she intends it to be unimpeachable. Coldfront's operational staff like the Verity account because Ashby's team is professional, the paperwork is always complete, and nobody has ever asked them to move anything they'd prefer not to know about.

**Auris Security Solutions:** No formal relationship. Two former Auris employees are among Verity's patients — people who left the corporation and chose transformation through a channel that wasn't Nexagen. Auris is aware of this. No action has been taken. Whether this is because Auris's leadership considers it a private matter or because they are waiting to see if it becomes useful information is not something Ashby knows.

**The Low Streets:** This is the most complicated relationship, and the most important one. Verity's prices are not Low Streets prices. But Ashby maintains two pro bono slots per year, allocated at her full discretion, and over the past eight years she has used them to see patients from Low Streets communities — independent transformed individuals, Fenrix escapees who needed medical assessment, people who had been through integration processes that were not Verity's and were dealing with the consequences. She does not announce this. The Low Streets know anyway. Information about a doctor who will actually look at you tends to travel.

She has never asked a patient's legal status. She has never reported a patient's presence to anyone. She has received two inquiries from Nexagen's legal division that were, reading between the lines, asking whether she had recently treated anyone matching a description that would have made them a Nexagen asset. She responded to both inquiries with her standard privacy policy documentation and nothing else.

THE RESIDENTIAL WING
=====================

Six post-integration suites on Verity's upper floor. Designed for non-humanoid occupation — appropriate ceiling heights, non-slip flooring throughout, feeding and hydration infrastructure that does not require hands, climate management adjusted for species-specific comfort ranges. The window in each suite looks out over the Second District. The view is better than most patients expected.

The wing is staffed around the clock during active occupancy. The staff are not handlers — their role is welfare support and medical monitoring, not operational management. The distinction matters to everyone in the building and is communicated clearly to patients on arrival.

Patients in the residential wing can leave. Not unaccompanied in the first two weeks — the adjustment period is real and Ashby is careful about it — but with a staff member, and then independently once stability is confirmed. There are no locked doors in the residential wing except the ones patients can lock from the inside.

This is not how Nexagen's post-integration facilities work. Ashby knows this. She built the wing after the first time a former Nexagen Unit described their post-integration experience to her and she needed several hours alone before she could respond professionally.

---

WHAT VERITY REPRESENTS
=======================

A third option that the setting's institutional logic says shouldn't exist.

Nexagen has the contract. Fenrix has the black market. Verity has twelve patients a year, a four-month waiting list, a paper that Nexagen's legal team has read more carefully than any medical document they've encountered, and a founder who has been offered enough money to retire comfortably and has declined it every time on the grounds that comfort is not why she built this.

It is not a safe place in the sense that the corps cannot reach it. It is a safe place in the sense that the person running it has decided what her obligations are and is not negotiating on them.

For a Unit operating in Caldera — officially a Nexagen asset, tracking always on, suppression schedule in someone else's hands — Verity is the place that is not supposed to matter and keeps mattering anyway. Whether they ever walk through the door is another question. The door exists. That is not nothing.
