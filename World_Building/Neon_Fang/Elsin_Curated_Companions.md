ELSIN CURATED COMPANIONS
=========================

A boutique transformation services firm operating out of a private residence in Caldera's Upper Tier approaches — not a corporate tower, not a clinical facility. A building that looks, from the outside, like the kind of address that belongs to a very private, very wealthy family. The logo is a small typeset wordmark. The website, accessible only by referral, uses the word "companion" seventeen times and the word "transformation" once, in a subordinate clause.

Elsin does not advertise what it does. Its clients know what it does. The gap between those two facts is where the business model lives.

---

THE FOUNDER
===========

**Cassian Elsin**

Forty-six. Impeccably presented in the way that signals money old enough not to need to signal anything. He has the easy social fluency of someone who grew up in rooms where the right word at the right moment was the primary currency, and who is very good at deploying it. He is charming in the specific way that makes people feel attended to — he remembers your preferences, he asks the right questions, he makes you feel like the most interesting person in the room while learning everything about you that might be useful.

He did not come from biotech. He came from wealth management — a decade advising the kind of clients whose portfolios required discretion as much as expertise. He identified the market Elsin serves while watching what his clients did with their money and what they wanted that money couldn't straightforwardly buy.

He is not a scientist. He employs scientists. He does not need to understand the process; he needs to understand the client. This is, in his view, the more complex and more valuable skill.

He has no personal cruelty in the obvious sense — he does not enjoy suffering. He has simply made a deliberate and considered decision that the suffering of people who pass through his operation is not a variable he weights heavily against the satisfaction of his clients and the profitability of his firm. He has reasoned his way to this position and is comfortable with his reasoning. This is, in some respects, more unsettling than if he enjoyed it.

**Speech quirks:** Speaks in the register of someone who has never needed to raise his voice to be listened to — measured, warm, the kind of cadence that sounds like patience but is actually control. Refers to the individuals his firm processes as "companions" without irony or hesitation, in every context, including internal operational ones. Never uses clinical or transactional language in client-facing settings. Uses exclusively clinical and transactional language in operational settings, without any of the warmth.

WHAT ELSIN DOES
===============

**The stated service:** Elsin Curated Companions provides "bespoke companion placement" for high-net-worth clients seeking "a uniquely intimate and enduring bond." The companion is transformed to the client's specification. The placement is permanent. The companion will live in the client's residence, under the client's care, for the remainder of the arrangement.

**What this means in practice:** Elsin sources individuals, transforms them using a licensed variant of the feral integration process, and places the result with a paying client as a luxury companion animal — fully sapient, fully aware, legally classified as a transformed individual under a private care contract that concentrates every material decision about their life in the hands of the client.

The transformation is to the client's specification within the biological constraints of the process — species, colouring, size where the process allows variance, augmentation loadout. Elsin maintains a catalogue of donor animals. Clients select from it. The companion they receive will be a precise execution of what they chose.

**The consent architecture:**

This is where Elsin's legal sophistication is most visible.

Elsin does not traffic people in the straightforward sense. Every individual who passes through the process has signed documentation. The documentation is real. Whether the consent it records is meaningful is a question Elsin's legal team has engineered to be very difficult to answer definitively.

Sources vary. Some come through legitimate channels — people in financial extremity who are told they are signing a "long-term residential care contract" with an "elite household." The transformation component is present in the documentation. It is not the framing. By the time it becomes the reality, the contract has been executed.

Others come through intermediaries — fixers, debt consolidators, labour brokers operating in the Low Streets and lower Mid-Deck who have a working relationship with Elsin's sourcing team. These individuals are not told they are going to Elsin. They are told various other things. The documentation they sign can be traced, with effort, to a chain of shell companies that terminates at an Elsin subsidiary. The effort required has, to date, not been applied by anyone with the authority to act on the findings.

A small number are sourced through what the internal documentation calls "gift arrangements" — clients who have identified a specific individual they wish to have transformed and placed. These arrangements require additional legal architecture and command a significant premium. They also require that Elsin's sourcing team handle the acquisition of the individual directly, which is the closest the firm comes to something that cannot be dressed in documentation at all. Elsin is careful about how many of these it takes on per year.

THE AUGMENTATION SUITE
=======================

Elsin's augmentation package is not Nexagen's. It is bespoke, developed by a small team of independent biotech specialists who are well-compensated and do not ask questions about the end use of their work.

*VOCALISER* — Standard. Clear speech capability. Unlike Nexagen's model, the Elsin vocaliser can be client-controlled — muted, volume-limited, or restricted to specific vocabulary sets at the client's request. This is presented to clients as a "customisation option." What it means is that a companion can be made effectively non-verbal at the client's discretion, indefinitely, without the companion's agreement.

*NEURAL INTERFACE* — Not a HUD. Elsin's neural augmentation provides the client with a passive emotional readout — a small device the client carries that displays the companion's broad emotional state in real time. Calm, distressed, content, afraid. Framed to clients as enabling "deeper attunement." What it means is that the client can see when the companion is afraid and can choose what to do with that information.

*SUPPRESSION SUITE* — Client-administered. Framed as "stability maintenance." Functions identically to Nexagen's model in terms of dependency architecture. The dependency is a feature here in the most literal sense: it is one of several mechanisms by which the companion is kept in the arrangement.

*TRACKING* — Passive, client-accessible. The companion cannot access the tracking data. The client can. The client can also set a perimeter alert — a neurochemical response triggered if the companion moves beyond a defined boundary. This is described in the catalogue as "home-bonding support."

*COSMETIC AUGMENTATIONS* — Available at premium. Bioluminescent patterning, enhanced colouration, modifications to vocaliser tone and timbre. These serve no operational purpose. They exist because clients pay for them.

THE CLIENT BASE
===============

Elsin's clients are not a monolith. They share wealth — the base placement fee starts at a figure that exceeds most Mid-Deck residents' annual income by a factor of several — and they share the desire for something that cannot be acquired through legitimate means. Beyond that, the motivations vary.

Some clients want a companion in the most literal sense — they are lonely, they have resources, they want a presence in their lives that is constant and attentive and cannot leave. The fact that the companion cannot leave because of a suppression dependency and a perimeter alert is not something they examine closely. It is not something they are required to examine.

Some clients want status. A transformed companion is rare, bespoke, and expensive. It demonstrates a level of access and wealth that most objects cannot. They treat the companion well in the way they treat expensive things well — careful maintenance, pride of ownership, genuine but instrumental affection.

Some clients are worse than this. Elsin does not screen for which category a client falls into. The contract the client signs specifies their obligations to the companion's welfare. The obligations are thin. Enforcement is nonexistent — Elsin does not monitor placements after completion. What happens in a client's private residence is not Elsin's concern.

**The Haven Group** provides approximately thirty percent of Elsin's client referrals. This is not documented anywhere. It is an understanding between Cassian Elsin and two senior people at Haven whose names are also not documented anywhere. Haven's concierge service occasionally encounters clients with specific requests that Haven cannot fulfil through legitimate channels. Elsin is the referral. The fee for the referral is paid in continued access to Haven's social infrastructure, which is more valuable to Elsin than money.

LEGAL POSITION
==============

Elsin operates in genuine legal grey space and has spent significant money ensuring that grey space remains navigable.

The transformation process is licensed — Elsin holds a private biomedical practice certification obtained through a shell company and renewed without incident. The consent documentation is real in the technical sense. The care contracts that govern placement are registered with a civil notary. None of this makes what Elsin does legal in the substantive sense. It makes it difficult to prosecute.

Caldera's civil code does not have settled provisions for the legal status of transformed individuals under private care contracts. Nexagen's lobbying has ensured that this ambiguity persists — they have their own reasons for not wanting transformed individuals to have clear legal personhood. Elsin benefits from the same ambiguity without having paid for it, which is one of the few things about Elsin's operation that Nexagen finds genuinely aggravating.

The city enforcement division has received three complaints that can be traced, indirectly, to Elsin placements. Two were filed by individuals who claimed to know someone who had disappeared into an arrangement they hadn't fully consented to. One was filed by a former Elsin employee. All three were reviewed and closed without action. The review process for each was faster than average.

Varex Communications has, on at least one occasion, been asked by an unnamed party to ensure that certain data traffic associated with an Elsin shell company did not appear in a city enforcement data request. Varex complied. This is consistent with Varex's standard operating relationship with entities that can make it worth their while.

RELATIONSHIP TO OTHER ENTITIES
================================

**Nexagen Biosystems:** Complicated contempt. Nexagen dislikes Elsin for several reasons — Elsin uses a derivative of the same technique without Nexagen's consent architecture, Elsin's legal ambiguity creates regulatory conversations that sometimes catch Nexagen in the peripheral blast, and Elsin's existence makes the question of transformed individual legal personhood politically live in ways that complicate Nexagen's own position. They have not moved against Elsin directly because doing so would require surfacing the shared legal ambiguity in a public forum, which benefits nobody. There is also the Haven referral network, which touches several Nexagen executives' personal lives in ways they would prefer remain undisturbed.

**Fenrix Bio-Systems:** Working relationship at arm's length. Fenrix and Elsin overlap in the non-consensual end of the market and have, on specific occasions, referred clients or sourcing opportunities to each other. Fenrix finds Elsin's branding faintly ridiculous. Elsin finds Fenrix's operational roughness a liability it prefers not to be adjacent to. They maintain useful distance and occasional utility.

**Verity Biomedical:** Dr. Ashby knows Elsin exists. She has known for approximately three years, when a former Elsin companion made contact through a Low Streets intermediary seeking a welfare assessment. She treated the individual, documented everything she observed, and submitted the documentation to the city's medical oversight board. The board acknowledged receipt. Nothing further has happened. She has submitted twice more since. She keeps copies of everything. She is building something, methodically, and she is patient.

**The Haven Group:** Referral relationship, undocumented. Approximately thirty percent of Elsin's clients come through Haven. Haven's senior management considers this a discreet value-add to their concierge service. What it actually is is a degree of institutional involvement in Elsin's operation that would, if surfaced, constitute a significant problem for Haven's public-facing identity. Cassian Elsin is aware of this. He finds it useful.

**Coldfront Logistics:** Occasional use for discreet transport of sourced individuals pre-transformation. Coldfront's operational staff do not ask questions. Whether the specific staff members who have handled Elsin cargo know what they moved is not something Coldfront's management has investigated.

THE COMPANIONS
==============

They are sapient. They remember who they were. The vocaliser may or may not allow them to say so, depending on the client's preferences.

The suppression dependency is real and the doses are in the client's hands. The perimeter architecture means that moving beyond the residence triggers a response that most find too aversive to test more than once. The emotional readout means that distress is visible to the client in real time, and visible distress can be interpreted as a management problem rather than a human response.

Some companions adapt in the way that people adapt to situations they cannot change — they find the margins, they find what is bearable, they construct a life within the constraints. Some do not adapt. Elsin's post-placement documentation does not track outcomes. Cassian Elsin considers this appropriate — once placed, the companion is the client's responsibility.

A small number have escaped. The tracking architecture makes escape difficult. The suppression dependency makes sustained independence medically dangerous without access to a facility that knows how to manage the withdrawal. Verity knows how. The Low Streets have partial knowledge and variable capability. Fenrix-adjacent operators occasionally help, for reasons of their own that have nothing to do with the companion's welfare.

The ones who escape tend to end up somewhere in the Low Streets, navigating a city that does not have a category for what they are, with a body they did not choose and augmentations that were designed to keep them somewhere they have left. Some of them find Verity. Some of them find other things. Some of them are found by Elsin's sourcing team before they find anything.

---

WHAT ELSIN REPRESENTS
======================

The thing that money buys when it has already bought everything else.

The corps have their power structures and their leverage and their deniable operations and their asset classifications. Elsin is where that logic reaches its private endpoint — not a programme with an ethics board that never votes no, not a black-market operation with rough edges, but a boutique service that takes the transformation of a person into a permanent companion for someone wealthy enough to want one and wraps it in the aesthetics of luxury and the language of care and makes it feel, to everyone who doesn't look closely, like something that belongs in the world.

It belongs in this world. That is the point.
