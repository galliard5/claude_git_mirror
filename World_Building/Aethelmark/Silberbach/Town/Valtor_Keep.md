# Valtor Keep

The fortified seat of [[House_Valtor]], rulers of the River Valley. Located within the Old Town walls, on the high ground at the river bend's apex. The keep has been the Valtor seat for eleven generations.

[[Count_Elias_Valtor|Count Elias Valtor]] is in residence and rules from here. He holds weekly public petitions, hears legal disputes regularly, and remains the active executive authority over Silberbach and the broader river valley. Anthony Valtor's testimony has damaged his reputation but has not displaced him; the keep's posture under that damage is the *internal* story of the building right now.

---

## Approach

The keep sits inside the Old Town walls, accessible only via the Old Town gate at the end of the Silver Bridge. The watch at the gate clears all visitors to the keep before they cross. From the gate, a paved approach climbs steadily uphill for a quarter mile through the Old Town's grey-stone streets, past the Mayor's mansion and the temple complex, to the keep's outer wall.

The outer wall is real. Twelve feet thick at the base, twenty feet tall, with two flanking towers at the main gate and a slow defensive curve following the contour of the high ground. The gate is iron-bound oak, kept closed during the day and barred at sunset. A pedestrian wicket allows after-hours access for residents and authorised visitors.

Inside the outer wall: a courtyard, the main keep building, the garrison barracks along the east wall, the stable yard, a small chapel for the household's private use. Servants' quarters and kitchens occupy the lower north wing. The whole compound is built into the hillside, with the keep itself rising another forty feet above the courtyard.

---

## The Great Hall

Where the Count holds court. A long stone-walled chamber with a vaulted timber-and-iron ceiling, lit by tall narrow windows on the south wall and by iron candelabra that hang on chains from the rafters. A raised dais at the north end holds the Count's chair (heavy carved oak, grey-and-blue Valtor heraldry) and a smaller secondary chair for the chamberlain. Two long benches along the side walls accommodate observers and parties to a dispute. A central paved area between the dais and the entrance is where petitioners stand to speak.

The hall is decorated with the iconography of House Valtor: the river-and-tower coat of arms on banners along both side walls, portraits of past Counts in a single line down the east wall, weapons and ceremonial objects in glass cases along the west wall (the case-mounted display includes a sword that supposedly belonged to the founding Valtor and a chain of office that is, in fact, the actual chain of office and not a copy).

**Petitions:** Held every fourth-day, mid-morning. The hall is open to any subject of the river valley who wishes to bring a matter before the Count. Petitions are filtered first through the keep's chamberlain — most matters are redirected to other authorities (constables, guild courts, the temple) before reaching the Count, but a steady stream of legitimate appeals does reach the dais each week. The Count rules. His rulings are recorded by a clerk and posted publicly the following day.

**Court days:** Less public. Disputes between noble houses, complex commercial disagreements, matters touching imperial law. The hall is closed to general observers; only the parties, their counsel, and the keep's officers are present. The Count's rulings here are also recorded but not always posted.

## The Count's Chambers

Private quarters in the keep's upper levels, accessible via a guarded staircase. The Count's office, library, and bedchamber occupy the entire third floor. His personal guard stands at the staircase landing day and night.

These quarters are not visited by petitioners or general callers. Nobles of the appropriate rank may be received in his office for private conferral. Servants attend to the chambers under the Count's chamberlain — a small, vetted staff who have been with the household for at least a decade in every case.

---

## The Treasury

Heavily guarded. Stores tax revenues collected on behalf of the Count and (separately) on behalf of the Empire. Two locked doors, one keyed to the Count, one keyed to the imperial assessor who visits twice a year to audit the imperial portion. A small staff of treasurers maintains the books — currently shaken by the Anthony Valtor testimony, which exposed embezzlement of imperial funds.

The treasury operates under elevated scrutiny since the testimony. The imperial assessor's spring visit is overdue (politically delicate; the Count is delaying it where he can). The treasury staff are aware that they are now being watched both internally and externally and conduct their work accordingly.

---

## The Garrison

Barracks for the Count's personal guard along the east wall of the keep compound. Forty men under arms in normal times, expanded to sixty during periods of imperial tension or active disputes. The garrison commander is the Count's appointee and reports directly to him.

The garrison is not the same as the town watch. The watch reports to the constable and handles civil law enforcement; the garrison serves the Count's political and military interests specifically. The two cooperate at the constable's level but operate under separate command. The garrison handles keep security, ceremonial duties, escort of the Count when he travels, and any military operations the Count orders.

A handful of transformed members serve in the garrison — three trained warhounds, two warhorses, and a wolf scout. These are valuable specialised units; their handlers are senior garrison members. The transformed members wear the Valtor heraldry on their collars in addition to the standard military markings.

**Garret Brune** — a missing garrison regular, three weeks absent from duty as of present. His commander filed paperwork. His trail leads to the Harbor District and possibly the Crescent House. The garrison considers him AWOL; his commander considers him lost to a darker fate but does not yet have evidence. See [[The_Crescent_House]].

---

## The Dungeons

Below the keep, accessed via a guarded stairway from the garrison level. Cold stone, low ceilings, a half-dozen cells in good repair plus a larger holding chamber for short-term detention. Used rarely in peacetime; mostly for political prisoners and capital cases awaiting trial.

The dungeons are currently empty. They have not been heavily used in more than a decade. The Count's preferred instrument for political pressure is not detention; it is reputation, financial leverage, and selective application of imperial law. The dungeons exist as a final option, available, and the awareness that they exist is part of the Count's authority.

---

## The Servants' Wing & Kitchens

The lower north wing, set into the hillside. Kitchens (significantly larger than the manor's, designed to feed the entire household plus guests during major events), pantry, scullery, laundry, and the servants' quarters. A staff of approximately thirty maintains the keep — cooks, scullions, maids, valets, footmen, a small administrative staff for the Count's household specifically (separate from the treasury staff).

The household staff, like the treasury staff, is in a state of quiet adjustment since the Anthony Valtor testimony. The chamberlain has been trimming the staff slowly — letting the older members retire without replacement, declining to hire new staff except where necessary. The keep is running thinner than it did two years ago. Whether this is fiscal caution, political precaution, or both depends on who you ask.

---

## The Stable Yard

A working stable of twenty stalls plus a coach house, on the south side of the courtyard. Houses the Count's personal mounts, the carriage horses, and the warhorses (transformed and regular) of the garrison. A stable master and three grooms run it. The stable is well-maintained and the animals are in good condition.

---

## The Chapel

Small. Private. Inside the keep itself, off the great hall. Used by the Count for personal worship and household observances. Officiated by a chaplain provided by the Old Temple — a half-elven cleric named Ferran who has held the post for twenty years and who is, by all accounts, one of the few people in the keep the Count trusts unconditionally. The chapel is not open to the public.

---

## Atmosphere

**Visual:** Cold and grey. The keep was built for defense, not beauty, and centuries of additions have made it more functional rather than less so. Tapestries soften the great hall slightly; the Count's chambers are warmer and more comfortable; the rest of the building is honest stone, honest timber, and a great many candles.

**Scent:** Stone, leather, woodsmoke, the faint background of the kitchens, the iron-and-oil smell of weapons in the garrison. Very different from the Silver Eel's warmth or the manor's mixed domesticity. The keep smells like authority.

**Sound:** Echoing stone. Boot-falls carry. The garrison's drill yard generates a constant low percussion during morning practice. The Count's rooms are carpeted enough to muffle his approach when he chooses; his wing is the only place in the keep that sounds quiet.

**Tension:** Present. The household has been on a slightly heightened alert posture since the imperial testimony — not visible drama, just the small daily tells of an organisation watching itself. The chamberlain's increased vigilance over staff. The treasury's elevated scrutiny. The garrison commander's tighter morning briefings. Visitors who know what to look for can feel it. Visitors who don't, will sense the keep is *off* without being able to say why.

## The Count's Daily Routine

The Count is in residence and active. His routine, broadly:

- **Dawn:** Up. Brief morning meeting with the chamberlain on the day's schedule. Light breakfast.
- **Mid-morning:** Office work in his chambers. Correspondence, ledgers, political coordination.
- **Late morning:** Petitions on fourth-day; otherwise meetings as scheduled in the office or great hall.
- **Midday:** Meal in the chambers. Often with a single guest — an ally, an advisor, a household officer.
- **Afternoon:** Variable. Court days, riding, occasionally a visit to a holding within the valley. He has not visited Silberbach proper (descended into the New Town) in approximately a year — politically strategic.
- **Evening:** Meal in the great hall when guests are present, in his chambers when not. He still entertains — the Count is not in seclusion — but the guest list has narrowed since the testimony.
- **Late evening:** Reading. Correspondence. Sleep.

## Active Threads

- **The Anthony Valtor situation.** Ongoing. Anthony lives at Isalia's Manor as a free transformed retainer and imperial witness. The Count has not sought to have him moved or constrained, and is publicly silent on the subject. The political damage continues to accumulate slowly. The chamberlain is managing an elevated correspondence load related to nobles asking pointedly polite questions.
- **The imperial assessor.** Spring audit overdue. The Count is delaying. This cannot continue indefinitely. When the audit comes, the treasury will be under sharp imperial scrutiny.
- **Garret Brune.** Missing garrison regular. The garrison commander is building a case to take to the Count. When he does, the matter will reach the Crescent House through proper authority.
- **The Kaelen rivalry.** Baron Torvin Kaelen is using the political opening to consolidate reformist support. The Count is aware. Counter-moves are in development but not yet public.
- **Internal household tensions.** The chamberlain is trimming staff. The treasury is more careful. The garrison is more disciplined. None of this is dramatic; all of it is a household reading itself for damage and shoring up.

## Petition Procedure (For Players)

Players wishing to bring a matter before the Count proceed thus:

1. **Approach the gate.** State business to the watch. Be granted entry to the Old Town if not already a resident.
2. **Reach the keep.** Be received at the outer gate by a steward.
3. **Speak with the chamberlain.** Or his clerk. State the matter. The chamberlain will determine whether the matter merits the Count's attention or should be redirected. Most matters are redirected.
4. **If accepted:** Wait. Petitions are heard on fourth-day, mid-morning. A petitioner who arrives on third-day will wait. A petitioner who arrives early on fourth-day will wait less.
5. **In the great hall:** Stand in the central paved area. Speak when called. Be brief and clear. The Count rules. Disagreement after the ruling is unwise.

The Count is a competent, deliberate ruler. His rulings are usually consistent with established law and precedent — paranoia and political ruthlessness do not extend to the petition process, where his interest is in being seen as a fair authority. A petitioner with a legitimate grievance will receive a fair hearing. A petitioner with a political agenda hidden inside their grievance will be detected and the agenda will work against them.
