# Silberbach — Arrival Reference

A reference document for running scenes where someone arrives in Silberbach for the first time, returns after long absence, or moves between town and outlying areas (manor, river, valley road). Pulls together the sensory and procedural detail scattered across other files into a usable arrival-experience kit.

This is not a location file. It is a *transition* file — what the town feels like when you cross into it, in any direction.

---

## Approaching by River

Most travelers come in by water. The Angerap is the spine of regional commerce, and barges from the larger downstream cities arrive daily with goods and passengers.

**The first sight:** The bend itself. Coming upstream from the west, the river straightens for half a mile, then turns hard to the northeast — and as the barge rounds that turn, Silberbach opens up on the inner curve, all at once. The Old Town's grey stone walls along the high ground, the bristling line of dock pylons along the harbor, the spread of New Town roofs climbing inland. On a clear day the northern mountains show behind the town as a faint ridge.

**What you smell:** River water first — silty, weed-cool, fish. Then woodsmoke from the New Town hearths. As the barge nears the docks: tar from the boat-builders' yards, hemp from the rope-walks, the dye works' pungent eye-watering register if the wind is from the south. By the docks proper: sweat, fish gutting, hot pitch, ale.

**What you hear:** Long before sight — bells. The Old Temple rings the hours, and the sound carries. As the barge closes: the rhythmic creak of dock pylons, calls between bargemen and dockhands, the steady industrial drone of the mill race a half-mile inland. Gulls — yes, this far inland; the Angerap supports a river gull population.

**Docking procedure:** River Traders' Guild dockmaster meets every barge. Cargo manifest reviewed, dock fee paid (graduated by tonnage), berth assigned. Passengers disembark down a gangway onto the broad timber wharf. There is a small customs shed for noble or imperial cargo; ordinary passengers are not searched but are watched.

**First impression on the wharf:** Crowded. The Harbor District is the busiest part of town for most of the day, and the dock area itself is a churning current of porters, factors, dock laborers, and travelers. A new arrival not paying attention will be jostled at minimum and pickpocketed at worst. Dock laborers are mostly human; some halflings work the cargo lines (lighter and faster on narrow walkways); a few transformed boar-form workers handle the heaviest crates without supervision because they are trusted to.

## Approaching by Road

The valley road comes in from the southeast, climbing slowly out of the pastoral plains. Travelers from inland — caravans, mounted parties, foot travelers from the smaller villages — arrive this way.

**The first sight:** Smoke before stone. The town's chimneys rise above the rolling country a long way out, and the smoke is what announces it. Then the Old Walls become visible across the valley as a grey line, and at the right time of day the temple's bronze-capped tower throws back the sun. The river is hidden until you are close — it sits in its bend, lower than the surrounding land. You hear it before you see it.

**What you smell:** Pasture and dust on the approach. As you near the outer farms: woodsmoke, animal manure, hay. The dye works again, depending on wind. Inside the gates: bread baking, ale brewing, leather, charcoal forges, the persistent low note of human density.

**What you hear:** Cartwheels on packed earth. Cattle. Distant millstone. The temple bells from a mile out. As you near the New Town gate: the chatter of the marketplace if it is a market day, or the percussion of the smithies if it is not.

**Gate procedure:** The New Town has no proper gate anymore — the outer roads simply run into streets — but the Old Town gate at the end of the Silver Bridge is staffed continuously. Town watch on duty, two men at minimum, more during festival times. Visitors entering the Old Town on legitimate business (petitioners to the Keep, temple-goers, residents) pass without challenge. Visitors who look out of place get questioned: name, business, where staying. Polite but firm. A noble's livery passes without question; a cloaked stranger with no obvious destination does not.

**First impression on the road:** The valley narrows in the last half-mile, the road runs alongside the millrace, and the millrace runs alongside the smaller of the New Town's two grain mills. The first sight of actual town buildings is industrial — the mill, then the row of cooperages and brewhouses that sit on the millrace bank. By the time you reach Market Square, you have passed through the working district, which is by design. The town's wealth is not displayed at its edges.

## Approaching from the Manor

Isalia's Manor sits southeast of the town, a brisk hour's walk or twenty minutes by horse along the outer road. People moving between the manor and the town do so constantly — couriers, deliveries, staff on errands, clients arriving and departing.

**The route:** A well-maintained stone-paved road leaves the manor's main gate and joins the valley road a quarter-mile north. From there it is the standard valley-road approach into the New Town. A footpath through the river meadows offers a shorter route in good weather but is unreliable in spring (flooding) and winter (frozen ruts).

**Manor staff doing town errands** typically use the millrace road — it bypasses Market Square if the market is not the destination and reaches Merchants' Close, Weavers' Row, and the guild hall row directly.

**Manor-to-Keep specifically:** The route into the Old Town crosses the Silver Bridge. The watch at the Old Town gate knows the manor's regular couriers (Marek among them) by sight and waves them through.

## The Sound of the Town's Day

A reference for any "we arrive at hour X" scene:

- **First bell (dawn):** The temple rings the hour. Bakeries already at work; smoke is up. River gulls call. The town watch night shift hands off to the day shift.
- **Second bell (mid-morning):** Markets are in full swing. The Silver Bridge is crowded. Dock activity peaks. The day's first guild meetings are concluding and members are spilling into the streets.
- **Third bell (midday):** Temple bell again. The sound carries everywhere. Most workers pause for a midday meal; the Silver Eel and other taverns are full. Market Square thins briefly.
- **Fourth bell (mid-afternoon):** The afternoon's heaviest commerce. River barges that arrived in the morning are being loaded for departure. Smithies are at their loudest.
- **Fifth bell (early evening):** Markets break down, stalls cleared, the square emptying. Workers heading home or to taverns. The harbor district is at its second peak — evening barges arriving for the night.
- **Sixth bell (sunset):** Temple bell. Old Town gate closes for the night unless festival-period; pedestrian wicket remains accessible. New Town never closes but quiets.
- **Late night:** The Drowned Rat is loud. The Silver Eel runs softer. Old Town is quiet. The watch patrols are paired and audible — boots on stone.
- **Pre-dawn:** A few hours of genuine stillness. River barges loading for the dawn departure. Bakers starting. The temple bell does not ring overnight.

## First-Encounter Texture

For any new-arrival scene, useful texture pieces a GM can pull from:

**The watch:** Mixed force. Most are human or half-elf; a half-dozen transformed members work the night patrols (hounds for tracking, wolves for the harbor district, hawks who roost on the Old Town wall and watch from above). The transformed members wear the distinctive watch collar with the city sigil — visible at any reasonable distance. Citizens are used to them. Visitors sometimes stare. A polite traveler nods at watch members as they pass; a rude one does not, and is remembered.

**Children:** Underfoot, especially in the Harbor District. River-rat boys offer to carry baggage for a copper. A traveler who pays them fairly will find his name being repeated among the wharf children for days; one who does not, the same.

**Religious encounter:** The Old Temple is a mixed-faith institution. Travelers from a single-faith background sometimes find this disorienting — robed clergy of three or four different traditions on the same temple steps, on cordial terms. There are also small shrines tucked into corners throughout New Town: a saint of brewers behind the largest brewhouse, a river-spirit shrine on the Silver Bridge, a fertility shrine in a courtyard off Weavers' Row.

**Language:** Common is universal in town. The local Silberbach river-country accent is slightly slower than city Common, with warmer vowels (Marta_Halse speaks this register). Halflings speak Common with a faster cadence and clipped sentence ends. The trade languages of the larger downstream cities are heard at the docks but not elsewhere.

**Currency:** Silver-marked coin (the town's mint-mark is a stylised river bend) trades at par with imperial silver in the surrounding region. Vander's Currency Exchange handles foreign coin for travelers and for the noble houses.

**Where strangers go:** The Silver Eel is the default for travelers of merchant rank or above who want food, ale, and conversation. The Drowned Rat for travelers who want discretion and don't mind grime. There is no proper traveler's inn — a gap the town has not filled, and which guests of the noble houses are usually accommodated within the houses themselves.

**Where strangers don't go:** Old Town residential streets after sixth bell. The dye works at any time (the smell, mostly, but also the dye workers' guild has a long-standing dislike of sightseers). The Crescent House without specific reason and an introduction.

## Arrival Hooks

A few seeds for arriving scenes:

- **The dockmaster's question.** "Berth or pass-through? If pass-through, where to?" — a question that sounds routine but is actually the first piece of information someone is gathering on a new arrival. Dockmasters compare notes informally.
- **The watch nod.** A transformed wolf on the New Town side of the Silver Bridge, not patrolling — sitting on a stone bench by the gate, watching travelers come in. Citizens nod to her by name (the GM names her when needed). New arrivals usually don't, and don't realise they've been clocked.
- **The boy with the hand-cart.** A wharf urchin, eight or nine, offering to lead the new arrival to whichever tavern they name. He charges by destination — the Silver Eel is a copper, the Drowned Rat is two (because that's a rougher walk and his mother told him not to go there alone).
- **The temple bell at the wrong time.** If the temple bell rings at an unusual hour, every Silberbach resident notices and pauses. Visitors who don't notice it are visibly outed as visitors.
