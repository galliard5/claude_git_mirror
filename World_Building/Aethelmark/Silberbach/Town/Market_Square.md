# Market Square

The beating heart of the New Town. Permanent stalls cluster around a central stone fountain. On market days the square fills with farmers, craftsmen, and merchants from the surrounding countryside. The square is also the town's announcement point — the town crier's post sits at the fountain's south side, and any official news first reaches the public from that step.

---

## Layout

A roughly rectangular open space, perhaps eighty paces by sixty, bounded on four sides by:

**North:** The two-storey timber-and-stone facade of the merchants' colonnade — a permanent line of established storefronts, dry goods, fabric merchants, a candle and oil dealer, a leatherworker's frontage. Awnings extend over the colonnade walkway.

**East:** The Silver Eel's frontage and a row of smaller establishments — a charcuterie, a baker (the better of the New Town's two), a small wine merchant. The eastern alley behind these shops connects to Weavers' Row a block away.

**South:** Open to the street running toward the Silver Bridge — the square's primary entry for foot traffic from the Old Town side. A row of more transient stalls sets up here on market days.

**West:** The town hall's rear wall (the formal entrance is on the next street over) and a narrow passage leading to the millrace road.

**The Fountain:** A stone basin, twenty feet across, fed by a piped channel from the millrace. The central sculpture depicts three river spirits — a wading heron-figure, a salmon-bodied river deity, and an indeterminate water-otter figure carved later than the other two by an obviously different hand. The fountain is a popular meeting place ("by the fountain" needs no further specification in Silberbach), a watering point for the market animals, and on hot days a place where children play despite the watch periodically chasing them off.

**The Crier's Post:** A worn flagstone on the south side of the fountain, with an iron ring set into a nearby wall to which the crier ties a small lantern for evening announcements. The crier — a different person every shift — stands on the flagstone and rings a hand-bell before any announcement.

---

## The Permanent Stalls

The wooden stall structures around the fountain are leased annually from the town. Twenty-four stalls in total, ranging from large to small. The leaseholders rotate slowly — a large stall passes through a family for generations, a small stall might change hands twice a year. As of present:

- **Vegetables and fruit:** four stalls, run by river-valley farmers with regular rotations
- **Bread and baked goods:** two stalls, both supplied by Silberbach bakeries
- **Cheese and dairy:** two stalls, river-valley pastoral
- **Smoked and salted meats:** two stalls
- **Live poultry:** one stall (in a corner, by request of every other stallholder)
- **Fish:** three stalls, all supplied directly from the docks
- **Eggs and small dairy:** one stall, run by a halfling family
- **Herbs and dried preparations:** one stall, with an informal arrangement with the manor's apothecary side (some of the more unusual herbs come *from* the manor's garden surplus)
- **Pottery and earthenware:** one stall
- **Wooden goods (utensils, small carvings):** one stall
- **Cloth offcuts:** one stall, supplied by the Weavers' Row merchants
- **Sundries (pins, ribbons, small notions):** two stalls
- **Reserved for itinerant merchants:** three stalls held for travelling vendors, allocated weekly by the market clerk

A market clerk's small office sits at the south end of the colonnade — Heidrun the elder, a half-elven woman with a permanent ink stain on her right hand, runs the lease records and assigns the itinerant slots. She is not friendly. She is reliably honest.

## Daily Rhythm

**Pre-dawn:** Stallholders arriving with carts. The fish stalls go up first because the docks are working. Bakers' deliveries arrive. The square smells of woodsmoke and damp stone.

**Dawn:** Stalls opening. First customers — household servants doing the day's marketing for the noble houses and wealthy merchants. The square is quiet but functional.

**Mid-morning:** Peak commerce. The square is full. The fountain is the meeting point; couriers and messengers wait there for handoffs. The crier reads the day's announcements at second bell — petitions accepted at the keep, market regulations, lost property, occasionally a notice from the temple.

**Midday:** A brief lull. Stallholders eat. Customers dispersing for their own meals. The Silver Eel's lunch trade is at its loudest just east.

**Afternoon:** Second wave of commerce — workers running errands at the end of their morning shift, manor staff doing the day's purchases, river travelers stocking up before evening departure.

**Late afternoon:** Stalls beginning to break down. The flowers, herbs, and breads are usually sold out by now; the fish is moving fast or being marked down; the durable goods (pottery, wood) work later.

**Evening:** Cleanup. Sweepers come through. The square is empty by sixth bell except for the crier's stand, where the evening announcements are read.

**Night:** Quiet. The fountain still flows; the square is unlit; a lone watch patrol comes through every hour or two.

## Market Days

The weekly market is held on third-day. Sunrise to sunset. The square fills beyond capacity — itinerant vendors set up on every available surface, including spilling onto the streets that feed in from the south and east. Country folk arrive from the surrounding villages with carts, livestock, woven goods, fresh produce, and any specialty their village is known for.

Special markets through the year:

- **Wool market** (early summer): the wool harvest from the regional pastoral lands. Brings the Weavers and Fullers guilds out in force.
- **Harvest fair** (early autumn): three days, expanded into the streets, with food, music, and a temporary stage near the fountain for performers.
- **Winter market** (mid-winter): a smaller affair under awnings, focused on preserved goods, salted meat, dried fish, root vegetables, and the year's last imported wares before the river closes.

Market days draw the watch out — usually six members on rotation, including at least one transformed member (a hawk overhead is the most common; sometimes a wolf at street level for the more crowded hours).

---

## The Crier's Schedule

Official announcements are read at:
- Second bell (mid-morning) — the day's primary announcements
- Fifth bell (early evening) — closing announcements, lost-and-found, anything received during the day

Announcements include: keep proclamations, council notices, guild bulletins, lost property, marriage banns, occasional imperial notices, and the day's market regulations if changed. The crier is paid by the council and rotates among a pool of about six. They are professional, audible, and accurate — Silberbach's crier service is reliable enough that residents organize their day around the second-bell reading.

## Recurring Types Worth Naming When They Appear

(Per the *types not names* approach — these are the slots, names go in when the GM needs them in play.)

- **The market clerk** — Heidrun the elder (named, half-elven, ink-stained, runs the lease records)
- **The fish stallholders** — three of them, two old families and one newer; the newer one undercuts the others on price and the others resent it
- **The herb stall woman** — friendly with [[Alric_Dain|Alric]] from the manor; trades surplus both directions
- **The town crier on duty** — varies by shift
- **The watch patrol member** — varies; a transformed hawk often roosts on the colonnade roof during market hours
- **The wharf-boy with the hand-cart** — works the square in the late afternoon, connecting customers with porter services
- **The pickpocket** — always one in any Silberbach market day; the watch knows; she only works visitors

## Hooks

- **A petition rejected at the keep, then read out by the crier as a public notice the next day.** The gap between the private rejection and the public framing creates conversation in the square. Players who pay attention here learn what's politically inflammable.
- **A merchant whose stall has been "reassigned" by Heidrun under disputed circumstances.** Comes to the players if they look approachable. The stall in question is in a high-traffic spot. Someone with influence wanted it.
- **The herb stall woman has news from the manor.** Alric wants something the players might be best positioned to fetch. She is the messenger.
- **A market-day fight.** Two fish stallholders, the third one. It will spill into a wider scene if the players don't intervene.
- **The pickpocket lifts something she shouldn't have.** A noble item, a guild seal, a sealed letter. She knows immediately and is now in trouble. The watch hasn't caught her — but they will.
