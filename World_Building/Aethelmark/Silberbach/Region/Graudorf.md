# Graudorf

The village at the foot of [[House_Grauwald|Grauwald Manor]]'s road. Population approximately 350. Two days' ride southwest of Silberbach via the Pastoral Road. The pastoral heart of House Grauwald's territory and the principal residential settlement for the working families who staff the wool harvest and the broader sheep economy.

A small, quietly-functional place. The kind of village where everyone knows everyone, where the manor's economic gravity shapes daily life without being oppressive, and where the financial decline of the ruling house has begun to register without yet producing visible change.

---

## Layout

The village clusters at the base of the road that climbs to Grauwald Manor proper. The road enters from the northeast (the Pastoral Road from Silberbach) and continues southwest past the village toward the Cendrel border (twelve miles further). The village itself is built along this single principal road, with side lanes branching to residential clusters and the agricultural buildings.

**Village center:**
- The village green — a modest open space with a stone-walled well at its center, used for community gatherings, the autumn harvest celebration, and informal market days
- The Sheep-and-Crook — the village's only inn, run by a halfling family
- The village chapel-house — small, modest, mixed-faith but with a local tradition emphasising the saint of shepherds
- Master Halren's smithy (the village blacksmith — unrelated to Master Halren of the Fullers and Dyers Guild and to the late Halren Reinhart; common name)
- The village's small market — three stalls operating most days, expanded for periodic market events

**The Manor Road:**
- Climbs from the village green up the gentle slope to Grauwald Manor proper, half a mile away
- Lined with the homes of the more senior manor staff who live in the village rather than at the manor itself

**The Wool Lanes:**
- Side streets running away from the principal road, where the shepherding families live in a dense cluster of stone-and-thatch cottages
- The wool barns and shearing sheds at the lanes' ends — large, well-maintained working buildings
- The communal wool storage shed, where the spring and autumn harvests accumulate before transport to Silberbach

**The Outlying Farms:**
- A dozen smaller pastoral holdings sit within an hour's walk of Graudorf proper, connected to the village by farm tracks
- Each holding is operated by a family that pays a portion of its wool to the manor and sells the remainder through the village market or directly to Silberbach textile traders

## Economy and Daily Life

**Sheep, wool, mutton.** The economic foundation of Graudorf is comprehensive. Spring and autumn shearings are the village's two principal annual events; mutton and lamb are sold continuously to Silberbach and locally; cheese production from sheep's milk runs through the warm months; tanned hides go to the Silberbach Tanners Guild.

**Manor employment.** Approximately a third of the village's working population draws income from Grauwald Manor directly — shepherds, shearers, wool processors, manor household staff who live in the village. Wages are paid by the household; have been less reliable for the past five years as Adelaide's quiet financial management has compressed manor expenses.

**Limited commerce.** The village does not function as a regional trading center. Most goods are produced locally or come up from Silberbach via the manor's supply runs. Visitors are uncommon; the village is rarely more than a day's stop for travelers heading to or from the Cendrel border.

**The Sheep-and-Crook.** The village's only inn. Three rooms, plain food, the kind of place where everyone present knows everyone else and where strangers attract immediate attention without overt unfriendliness. Run by the Vohlen family — halflings, three generations in the village, the inn passed mother-to-daughter for the last two of those generations.

---

## Manor Relationship

Graudorf is structurally an extension of Grauwald Manor's economy. Most working-age village residents have either direct manor employment or commercial relationships with the manor's household. Count Aldwin and Countess Mareth are familiar figures — Aldwin is genuinely beloved, walks the village frequently, knows children's names, attends shearing celebrations; Mareth is respected but slightly more distant, regarded as the woman who actually runs things.

The slow financial decline of the household is becoming visible at the village level in small ways: a manor wage payment slightly delayed, a contracted purchase from a village supplier downsized, a maintenance project on the manor road postponed twice. Villagers notice. They are loyal enough not to discuss it openly. They notice anyway.

The Countess's recent quiet correspondence with Isalia's Manor (re: Lord Bertrand and the surrogate inquiry) has *not* reached the village. The travel to Silberbach was framed as wool-trade business; only Mareth, the manor steward, and one trusted maid know the truth. The village does not.

## Politics and Atmosphere

Graudorf is politically inert at the regional level — the village does not have its own opinions on Valtor versus Kaelen and would not be asked for them if it did. Internal politics are about manor employment, wool prices, the quality of the autumn harvest, and the household's slow financial situation.

The village's identity is bound up with House Grauwald to a degree that surprises visitors from more politically-active settlements. The villagers regard Aldwin with genuine affection and Mareth with respectful loyalty; there is no Cendrelais sympathy, no reformist sentiment, no investigation-related discomfort. The village is, in regional political terms, a quiet block of Grauwald-aligned support that is not currently being asked to do anything.

## In-Between Texture

**The Pastoral Road.** Graudorf's principal route. Two days northeast to Silberbach; twelve miles southwest to the Cendrel border via the Westgate route or by the smaller direct crossing two miles south of Graudorf (which is unmonitored and used primarily by local shepherds with sheep grazing on both sides of the border).

**Banditry:** Minimal in the immediate area. The pastoral country is thinly populated but also thinly traveled; opportunists prefer busier roads. The Sheep-and-Crook has not had a robbery in the inn's three-generation history.

**The Cendrel border** sits very close. Graudorf's residents have working relationships with Cendrelais shepherding families across the border; sheep stray both directions; local children grow up understanding that the border is more political than physical. The border garrisons two miles south accept this and document only formal commercial movement.

**Religious life:** The chapel-house serves the village's mixed-faith population. The local tradition of the shepherd-saint adds a specifically pastoral inflection that the broader Aethelmark religious framework accommodates without difficulty.

**Climate and seasons:** The pastoral hills are mild. Winters are cold but rarely severe; summers are long but rarely hot. The shearing seasons (early spring and early autumn) are the village's productive peaks; the rest of the year runs at a slower rhythm.

## Hooks

- **The Countess's secret reaches the village.** A village child overhears a conversation between Mareth's maid and a manor staff member; the secret of the surrogate inquiry begins to spread. Mareth must contain it.
- **A Cendrelais shepherd is found dead on the Aethelmark side of the border.** Murder or accident? Local resolution or imperial-Cendrelais incident? Aldwin must navigate.
- **A wool harvest fails.** A disease in the local flocks reduces the autumn harvest. Mareth's already-compressed finances cannot absorb the reduction. Decisions must be made.
- **A traveler arrives at the Sheep-and-Crook with too much money for a stranger of his apparent class.** Anneke notices. The traveler is asking about Bertrand.
- **A village wedding becomes a political event.** Two senior shepherding families' children marry; Aldwin attends, unexpectedly bringing Bertrand. The villagers treat Bertrand with carefully-managed affection that he does not entirely know how to receive.
