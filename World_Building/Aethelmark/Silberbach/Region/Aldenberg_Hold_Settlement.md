# Aldenberg Hold Settlement

The town that has grown around [[House_Aldenberg|Aldenberg Hold]] in the northern mountains. Population approximately 900, making it the third-largest settlement in the March. Half a day's ride north of Silberbach along the Mountain Road — a switchback climb from the lowland that takes most travelers two hours from the road's first ascent to the Hold's outer gates.

The settlement exists because the Hold exists, and the Hold exists because of the mines. Forges run continuously. Ore moves down to Silberbach in regular shipments under Aldenberg toll. The town's working population is dwarven, human, and a smaller half-elven community in roughly equal thirds — an unusual demographic mix produced by generations of forge-master apprenticeships drawing from across the wider region.

---

## Layout

The town wraps around the mountainside below the Hold, climbing the slope in tiers connected by switching streets. Three principal districts:

**The Forge Quarter** (lowest tier, where the air is thickest with smoke):
- Three primary forge complexes, all bonded to House Aldenberg
- The smaller specialty workshops — gemstone polishers and setters, fine metalwork shops, a brass and copper workshop
- The forge-workers' housing, dense and functional
- The Forge Quarter's chapel-house — small, dedicated primarily to dwarven traditions but accommodating other faiths
- The town's largest tavern, the Anvil-and-Cup, which serves the forge-workers and is loud through every working hour

**The Market Quarter** (middle tier):
- The town's market plaza — smaller than the Ford's, smaller still than Silberbach's, but functional
- The principal mercantile establishments handling the day-to-day commerce
- The town's two inns (one travelers', one workers')
- The toll-master's office where mountain-pass fees and ore-shipment levies are calculated
- The Aldenberg-affiliated banking house (for the more substantial commercial transactions)

**The Hold Quarter** (highest tier, just below the Hold's outer gates):
- The senior forge-masters' homes
- The Hold's commissary and supply quarters
- The administrative offices for the mountain pass tolls
- Two small but well-appointed visitor's residences for traveling Hold business
- The chapel-house of the Hold Quarter, slightly larger and more ornate

**Aldenberg Hold itself** sits above the town, accessible by a final switchback that leads to the Hold's outer gate. The Hold's walls and the iron-bound oak gate are its working defensive features; the town below has no walls of its own and depends on the Hold for its security.

## Economy and Daily Life

**The forges.** The settlement's central economic engine. Three primary forge complexes operate continuously — each producing a different specialty (one for blade-and-armor work, one for tool-and-implement work, one for finer specialty work including gemstone-set pieces). The forges supply Aldenberg's premium steel output, the Silberbach Blacksmiths' downstream demand, and the imperial garrison contracts.

**The mines.** Seven major mine operations sit within or adjacent to Aldenberg territory, plus a network of smaller workings. The mining workforce lives partly in the settlement itself (the forge-side mining roles) and partly in distant mountain camps (the deeper extractive operations). The Miners Guild's working master miners gather quarterly at the Hold's lower hall.

**The pass tolls.** Every shipment moving down to Silberbach pays an Aldenberg pass toll. The toll-master's office is one of the busiest of the town's establishments; the toll itself is a significant family revenue stream and one of the political levers Aldenberg holds over the broader regional economy.

**Specialty workshops.** Gemstone polishing and setting (a quiet specialty that serves both regional noble households and imperial commission), brass and copper work, fine metalwork. The specialty work is small in volume and high in margin.

**The market.** Smaller than the Ford's, mostly serving the town's own population and the visiting merchants who come up from Silberbach for forge-side business. Local food production is limited (the mountain terrain does not support much agriculture); most foodstuffs are brought up from the lowland on regular shipment schedules.

---

## The Anvil-and-Cup

The town's largest tavern. Located in the Forge Quarter — loud, smoky, with the rhythmic background pulse of forge-hammers reaching it through the walls. Run by a dwarven family (the Vrik family, four generations at the same establishment). Substantial food, dwarven ale brewed locally to forge-worker specifications, the kind of tavern that runs at three-quarters capacity through every shift change.

Merchants visiting from Silberbach who want to be visible to forge-master society take their meals here. Travelers who simply want a quiet meal go elsewhere.

---

## The Mountain Inn

The travelers' inn, in the Market Quarter. Run by a human family. Five rooms, decent food, sound walls, recommended by the toll-master to merchants traveling on Hold business. The Inn's principal clientele is the Silberbach commercial traffic — the visiting merchants, factor's-house representatives, and occasional noble visitors who come up for forge commissions.

---

## The Pickaxe

The workers' inn, also in the Market Quarter but a tier lower. Run by a half-elven man named Borrek. Plain food, plain beds, plain prices. Serves the working-population: visiting miners between shifts, junior forge-workers who do not have their own quarters, traveling craftsmen on smaller commissions.

## Politics and Atmosphere

The Aldenberg Hold Settlement is unusually internally cohesive for its size. The town's residents identify strongly with the Hold and with House Aldenberg's neutrality policy; outside political pressure does not penetrate the town easily. Baron Edmund and Baroness Helga are visible figures — both of them walk the town regularly, both of them know the senior forge-masters by name, both of them are accessible in ways that more distant nobles would not be.

The town is also notably internally diverse — dwarven, human, half-elven communities living and working alongside each other for generations has produced a working multiethnic culture that surprises visitors used to more homogeneous settlements. Religious life accommodates this; the chapel-houses run mixed services on a flexible schedule.

The town's biggest internal political tension is between the established forge-masters (whose families have been at the Hold for generations) and the newer arrivals (forge-workers, miners, specialty craftsmen who came in through Aldenberg's recruitment over the past two decades). The tension is not dramatic but it is real; rises and falls slowly; produces occasional internal council disputes that Edmund and Helga resolve carefully.

The current Corvel investigation shadow on the Hold (Aldenberg appearing on Corvel's client list) is *not* visible at the town level. Edmund's internal investigation is being conducted at the Hold itself, with great discretion. The townspeople do not know.

## In-Between Texture

**The Mountain Road.** The principal route, well-maintained through Hold territory. The switchback climb from the lowland is the route's defining feature. Above the Hold, the road continues into the deeper mountain passes and eventually to the smaller mountain settlements; quality decreases progressively.

**Banditry:** Minimal at the town and the Hold. More substantial in the mountain passes above the Hold, where road quality is poor and Aldenberg's enforcement reach attenuates. The miners' own security handles the worst of it. Travelers continuing past the Hold should travel armed and in groups.

**Climate:** The settlement is significantly colder than the lowland; winter brings real snow and the Mountain Road can become impassable for stretches. Summer is short. The forge-quarter's warmth is partly an industrial fact and partly a comfort.

**Religious presence:** The two chapel-houses serve the town's mixed faith communities. The smaller wayside shrines along the Mountain Road mark traditional pause-points for travelers blessing themselves before the climb or thanking the spirits for safe passage.

**The Hold's reach:** Aldenberg's legal authority over the town is comprehensive. The Marquess's authority reaches in only through the toll-master's office and the imperial assessor's visits. In practice the town runs on Aldenberg's law.

## Hooks

- **A forge accident reveals an irregularity.** A working forge produces a flawed batch of premium steel. The investigation reveals the irregularity was not a working error but a deliberate adulteration. The question is who and why.
- **A visiting merchant brings news that one of Aldenberg's gemstone-setters is on the Corvel client list.** Edmund's internal investigation may have its first concrete lead. He needs to investigate without alerting the suspect.
- **The pass toll is challenged.** A merchant from Silberbach contests the toll calculation; the dispute escalates; the political question is whether Aldenberg's toll authority is being respected.
- **A miner's death in the deeper mines.** Standards investigation, mutual aid demands, and the question of whether Aldenberg-concession or imperial-concession working conditions slipped first.
- **A diplomatic visit.** A Cendrelais delegation arrives at the Hold seeking specific commissions. Edmund's neutrality policy is tested.
