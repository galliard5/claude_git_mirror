# The March of Silberbach

The March of Silberbach is a borderland territory on the western frontier of the Empire of Aethelmark. A *march* (also called margraviate) is an imperial border-county granted elevated authority precisely because it sits on the empire's edge — its ruler is a marquess (sometimes addressed as margrave in older records, or count in informal speech) charged with both the routine administration of his territory and the additional imperial duty of guarding the frontier.

The March's western border is the Kingdom of Cendrel — a sovereign realm with its own crown, court, and laws. The March's eastern border opens onto the imperial heartland of Aethelmark proper. The territory between is the working county that encompasses Silberbach itself, the Angerap river valley, the northern mountain mining region, the eastern forest, and the southwestern pastoral hills.

Marquess Elias Valtor rules from Valtor Keep in Silberbach. He is addressed as *Marquess* in formal imperial contexts and as *Count* in informal speech and by lower nobility — both forms are correct HRE-equivalent practice; the distinction has become politically meaningful in his case, since which title a speaker chooses has begun to register their stance toward his current political situation.

## Geography

The March stretches roughly forty miles east-to-west and thirty miles north-to-south at its broadest. The terrain is more varied than its modest size suggests, organised around the spine of the Angerap River and shaped by three principal landforms.

**The river spine.** The Angerap enters the March from the northeast, flows southwest through a series of valleys, bends sharply at Silberbach, and continues westward toward Cendrel. The river is navigable by shallow-draft barges throughout the March; its current is moderate, its winter freeze runs roughly mid-Decembermid-March in normal years, and its bend at Silberbach is the single most important commercial geography in the region.

**The northern mountains.** Half a day's ride north of Silberbach, the land rises into a chain of mountains that produce the silver, iron, and gemstones that have given Silberbach its name and much of its wealth. House Aldenberg controls the principal mountain passes from Aldenberg Hold; the seven major mines and a network of smaller operations sit within or adjacent to Aldenberg's territory. Beyond the mountains lies a thinly-inhabited highland that runs into the imperial heartland by mountain trails.

**The eastern forest.** A day to two days' ride east of Silberbach, the open country gives way to dense forest under House Waldheim's authority. The forest extends approximately twenty miles eastward before thinning into the Eastern Marches, where House Rennic's territory begins. Within the forest are three logging settlements, the Sergovy holding (a Waldheim cadet branch), and the network of ranger paths the Waldheim corps maintains.

**The southwestern pastoral hills.** Two days' ride southwest of Silberbach, the river valley opens into rolling pastoral country. House Grauwald's territory occupies these hills — sheep country, light grain, the modest village of Graudorf at the foot of Grauwald Manor. Beyond Grauwald, the country opens toward the Cendrel border, which the March's southwestern boundary follows for approximately twelve miles.

**The southern and western plains.** The bulk of the March's productive agricultural land lies in the broad valleys around the lower Angerap. House Rothwyn's vineyards, House Steinfeld's pastoral estate, and a dense network of bonded farming hamlets occupy these stretches. The Angerap continues westward through this country toward Cendrel; the principal Cendrel border crossing is the Westgate, three days' ride west of Silberbach.

**The river crossing — the Ford.** Half a day's ride south of Silberbach, the Angerap narrows enough to be forded at low water and bridged at higher. House Kaelen's seat at Kaelen's Ford controls the crossing — the principal land route between the March's northern and southern halves and the most important toll point outside Silberbach itself.

**The coastal salt lands.** Far to the southwest, where the March's territory meets the wider imperial coastline, House Meerhold operates the salt evaporation works that supply both Aethelmark and (under licensed export) Cendrel. The coastal holdings are administratively part of the March but geographically distant — three days' hard ride from Silberbach. The relationship between the Meerhold lands and the rest of the March is more colonial than integrated; many in Silberbach have never been to the coast and never will.

## The Cendrel Border

The March's western and southwestern frontier with the Kingdom of Cendrel is approximately thirty miles of formally-delimited border, running from the highland north of the river down through the southwestern plains. The border was formalised approximately eighty years ago in the Treaty of the Westgate, ending a series of small-scale conflicts that had run intermittently for two centuries before that.

The border is operationally peaceful. Trade crosses regularly. Both sides maintain ceremonial garrisons at the Westgate and at two smaller crossings; both garrisons spend most of their time inspecting paperwork rather than fighting. The two realms have not been at war for the eighty years since the Treaty.

But the border is also politically meaningful in ways the operational peace does not show:

- **Cendrel is sovereign.** The Cendrelais crown does not recognise imperial overlordship. Imperial law does not extend across the border. A merchant who flees from Aethelmark imperial justice into Cendrel cannot be reached without the consent of the Cendrelais authorities; a Cendrelais subject who commits a crime in Aethelmark is dealt with by Aethelmark's law, but only if he can be caught before he crosses back.
- **The Cendrelais nobility is differently structured.** Where Aethelmark organises its territory through marches, counties, baronies under imperial authority, Cendrel's nobility operates under its own crown's grant — duchies, baronies, knightly fiefs that owe loyalty directly to the Cendrelais monarch rather than to any emperor.
- **Cultural difference.** Cendrel speaks Common with a distinct accent, has its own religious traditions (a pantheon overlapping but not identical with Aethelmark's mixed-faith), runs its own coinage (the *écu* and the *denier* rather than imperial silver marks), and produces wine that Aethelmark does not (the Rothwyn vintages compete with Cendrelais imports at the Silberbach Harvest Festival every autumn).
- **The Vellancourt connection.** [[Edouard_Vellancourt|Lord Edouard Vellancourt]], the patron whose foundation gave rise to the kennel hound programme that produced the campaigns at Camp Rochevaux, Maruvec, and Vauclair, is a Cendrelais noble. The kennel hound facilities are inside Cendrel territory, not in the March. Travel from Silberbach to those facilities crosses the border; characters traveling between the two settings are crossing between sovereign realms, not merely between counties.

The March's status as a *march* — rather than an ordinary county — exists structurally because of this border. The marquess's elevated authority, the larger garrison the keep maintains, the imperial assessor's regular visits, the diplomatic intermediary role that historically passed through Silberbach — all are downstream of the simple fact that this is the empire's western edge.

## The Wider Imperial Geography

Aethelmark itself is a substantial empire stretching from the Cendrel border in the west to the eastern empire's frontier (which Rennic's Eastern Marches face) in the east, and from the northern highlands down through the imperial heartland to the southern coastal regions.

**To the east** of the March, the road runs through the wider imperial heartland — a journey of approximately ten days from Silberbach to the imperial capital where Emperor Aldric holds court (and where Aldric Kaelen the Younger and Tomas R. Rennic both currently reside as students/pages). Rennic's Eastern Marches lie three to four days' ride east of Silberbach, on the way to but not on the way of the capital. Beyond Rennic's Marches, the empire's eastern frontier opens onto the **Kingdom of the East** — a separate sovereign realm, monarchical, with its own crown and court (loosely a Polish-equivalent kingdom in the project's HRE-and-neighbors framing). The two realms have not been at war for over a century; the long peace is held by the Treaty of the Eastern Plains and by the working diplomatic relationships that pass through Rennic Hall. The Easterners speak a closely-related but distinct register of Common, follow a partly-overlapping faith framework, organise their nobility through *starostas* (a regional governorship register) rather than imperial counts and barons, and run their own coinage. See [[Rennic_Hall]] for the working interface with this neighbor.

**To the south** of the March, the imperial coastline extends through several other counties before reaching the wider empire's southern provinces. The Meerhold lands sit at the western end of this coastline.

**To the north**, beyond Aldenberg's mountain holdings, the country becomes thin and difficult — passes that close in winter, settlements that depend on summer trade, eventually reaching the northern imperial provinces by mountain routes.

**To the west**, across the Cendrel border, lies the Kingdom of Cendrel and the broader region that contains it. The Cendrelais kingdom is itself substantial — Camp Rochevaux, Maruvec, Vauclair, and the broader kennel hound territory are spread across Cendrel's working countryside. From Silberbach, Cendrel's capital is roughly five days' travel by road or four by river-and-road combined.

## Routes Through the March

The March's road network is functional but not exceptional. Major routes:

**The Valley Road.** The principal land route through the March, running northeast-to-southwest along the Angerap. Connects Silberbach to the Westgate and onward into Cendrel; in the other direction, runs upstream past Halselund, past Reinheim, and continues into the imperial heartland. Maintained by the marquess's authority through annual road-tax assessments on the bonded farming hamlets along the route.

**The Mountain Road.** From Silberbach north toward the Aldenberg passes. The route climbs steadily for a half-day's ride to reach Aldenberg Hold and continues from there into the high mountain settlements. The road is in good condition through Aldenberg territory (the Hold has a vested interest in keeping ore movement smooth) and worsens above the Hold.

**The Forest Road.** From Silberbach east toward the Waldheim Lodge and the eastern forest's logging settlements. Maintained intermittently — Waldheim's rangers patrol it, the forest itself encroaches on it, the road is more like a series of forest tracks than a single route.

**The Pastoral Road.** From Silberbach southwest toward Grauwald and Graudorf, then onward toward the Cendrel border via the pastoral hills. Lightly trafficked, lightly maintained, but reliable in good weather.

**The Coastal Road.** From Silberbach west-and-southwest toward the Meerhold coastal holdings. A long route — three days at fastest pace — through territory that becomes progressively less settled as it approaches the coast. Used primarily for salt shipments and occasional administrative business.

**The River.** The most important route. Barges run continuously along the Angerap throughout the navigable season (mid-March through mid-December). Upstream traffic is mostly passenger and luxury goods; downstream traffic is mostly bulk commodities (grain, wool, timber, ore). The river's bend at Silberbach is the principal commercial node; secondary harbors operate at Halselund, Reinheim (for upstream traffic from there onward), and the Westgate (for Cendrel-bound trade).

## The In-Between

The territory between the named locations of the March is largely working countryside — bonded farming hamlets, riverside fishing communities, isolated woodcutters' settlements at the forest edge, mining camps in the lower mountains. Most of this territory is unremarkable to a traveler making a single journey through; lived in by people whose names do not enter the keep's records except through tax rolls.

A few characteristics that matter for traveling-through scenes:

**Banditry exists but is moderate.** The march's roads are not safe in the way the heart of an established empire's roads are safe, but they are not as dangerous as a contested frontier. Travelers in groups are rarely troubled; lone travelers occasionally are, particularly on the more remote routes (the Forest Road, the Pastoral Road, the upper Mountain Road). The marquess's garrison conducts periodic sweeps when banditry concentrates in any one area; Waldheim's rangers handle the forest fringes; the Aldenberg miners' own security handles the mountain routes. The river is patrolled by the River Traders Guild's authority and is the safest transit method by a substantial margin.

**Inn-keeping is uneven.** Silberbach has the Silver Eel and the Drowned Rat as named establishments; the larger settlements (Halselund, Kaelen's Ford-town, Aldenberg Hold's settlement, Graudorf) each have at least one functioning inn or guest-house; the smaller settlements have neither, and travelers either accept household hospitality from a sympathetic farmer or sleep rough. Waldheim Lodge accommodates visiting hunts in proper guest quarters, but only by prior arrangement.

**Religious presence is dispersed.** The Old Temple in Silberbach is the largest religious institution in the March, but smaller shrines and chapel-houses appear throughout the territory — at the Ford, at major road junctions, at the entries to the forest. The mixed-faith framework of Aethelmark means most of these accommodate multiple traditions in ways that look chaotic to outsiders and run smoothly to locals.

**Refugee and movement texture.** The Cendrel border generates intermittent low-level movement of people in both directions — merchants, transient labor, occasional people fleeing one realm's law for the other's. The border garrisons document what they can; some movement passes outside their records. People appearing in the March's interior with Cendrelais accents and uncertain documentation are not unusual.

## Active Conditions

*(As of 20 April 1650 — last known state. Conditions may have moved.)*

**The marquess's political situation.** Marquess Elias Valtor's reputation has been damaged by the imperial testimony of his son Anthony. The damage has not displaced him from his title; the title has additional weight as a march (rather than ordinary county) and the imperial authority has been measured in its public response so far. Whether this measure holds, and for how long, is the central political question of the year.

**The imperial assessor's spring audit.** Overdue. The marquess has been delaying. The audit, when it occurs, will inspect both the keep's treasury and (less heavily but inevitably) the mining concessions in the north. The mining audit is technically a separate operation conducted by a different imperial functionary, but in practice the two will be coordinated.

**The Cendrel border generally peaceful.** No diplomatic crises in progress as of present. Trade flows. Border garrisons routine. The longer-term political question — how long the present generation of imperial-Cendrelais peace will hold — is a topic of polite speculation in the masters' council chambers but no immediate concern.

**Banditry baseline.** Normal seasonal range. Late winter and early spring bring slightly elevated activity on the more remote roads (the standard pattern); summer's heavier traffic discourages most banditry; autumn's harvest movement brings opportunistic activity around the wealthy harvest moves; winter quiets everything because traveling itself becomes hard.
