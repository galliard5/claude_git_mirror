# Halselund

The riverside hamlet six miles upstream of Silberbach along the Angerap. Population approximately ninety souls across fifteen households. Reached by either the Valley Road (the upstream route) or by river itself — barges that travel between Silberbach and the upper Angerap pass through Halselund regularly and most of the hamlet's external commerce moves on the water.

A modest fishing-and-farming community. The kind of place where everyone is related to everyone else within three or four marriages, where the mill at the upstream end has been in the same family for four generations, and where a daughter who left to work in Silberbach is talked about with quiet pride and slow expectation that she might come back someday.

[[Marta_Halse]] (the manor's kitchen assistant) is from Halselund. Her mother and three younger siblings still live here. She visits one Sunday a month.

---

## Layout

The hamlet stretches along approximately a quarter-mile of riverbank, with a small mill at the upstream end and the principal cluster of cottages at the dock end. The Valley Road passes through the hamlet on the bank-side; the river itself is the other principal feature.

**The Mill:**
- A working watermill at the upstream end, taking power from a small stream that joins the Angerap there
- Run by the Mehler family, four generations
- Grinds grain for the local farms and (under contract) for some of the outlying holdings within a half-day's reach
- The mill's pond doubles as the hamlet's swimming hole in summer

**The Cottages:**
- Approximately fifteen households, mostly stone-and-thatch, single-story or with a small loft
- Clustered along the road and the river, with kitchen gardens behind each house
- The senior families' compounds (Halse, Mehler, two others) are slightly larger and better-maintained

**The Common:**
- A modest open green near the dock, with a single ancient oak tree and a stone-walled well
- Used for community gatherings, the spring planting blessing, the autumn harvest celebration

**The Dock:**
- A wooden working dock for the small fishing boats and for the barges that occasionally moor here
- Larger barges from Silberbach do not stop unless prearranged; smaller traders and the upriver passenger barges call here regularly

**The Fishing Beach:**
- A short stretch of bank just downstream of the dock where the local fishing community lays out catches for sorting and salting
- Smells of fish more reliably than anywhere else in the hamlet; the local olfactory marker

**The Chapel-house:**
- A small mixed-faith chapel beside the common
- Serves the hamlet's religious life with the standard Aethelmark accommodation; emphasises the river-spirits and the fishing-saint locally

## Economy and Daily Life

**Fishing.** The principal economic activity. The Angerap supports a stable population of river fish; the hamlet's fishing community works the local stretches and supplies the local market plus a small surplus that goes to Silberbach via the regular barge connections.

**Smallholding farming.** Approximately a third of the hamlet's working population farms small bonded holdings under inherited tenure. Modest grain production, kitchen gardens, a few cattle and sheep distributed across the families. The bonded landlords are minor — small noble holdings or church-affiliated tenure rather than any of the major March houses.

**The mill.** The Mehler family's mill processes most of the local grain and provides the principal non-agricultural employment in the hamlet. The mill's apprentice lineage runs through the family.

**Limited commerce.** The hamlet is too small for a permanent market; informal trading happens at the common during high-traffic days. A traveling tinker passes through every few months. The Halse family runs a small home-based operation that sells preserved foods, smoked fish, and herb preparations to passing travelers and to Silberbach's market via Marta's mother's irregular shipments.

**The river bread starter.** Halselund is the source of the wild yeast that produces the distinctive Halselund bread starter — a working culture that the local bakers maintain and that Marta brings to the manor's kitchen monthly. The starter is a quiet local pride; the manor's bread carries the Halselund yeast as a result.

## The Halse Household

The home of Marta Halse's family. Marta's mother (a widowed woman in her early fifties), her two younger sisters (one married within the hamlet, one unmarried at twenty-one), and her younger brother (eighteen, working the family's small smallholding alongside the cousin who lives next door).

The family is genteel-poor — well-respected within the hamlet, comfortable enough to feed themselves reliably, not comfortable enough to weather an unexpected disaster. Marta's wages from the manor are sent home most months and represent a meaningful portion of the family's discretionary income.

Marta's monthly Sunday visits are one of the regular events in the household's life. The family knows what she does at the manor in general terms (kitchen work, cooking for nobles) and has not asked for detail; they take her quietly elevated cooking confidence at face value when she helps with the family meals.

## Politics and Atmosphere

Halselund is politically nonexistent at the regional level — the hamlet does not register on the masters' council, has no political opinions worth recording, and would be ignored if it tried to advocate for any. Internal politics are entirely about local concerns: the mill's grain rates, the river's fishing rights, the chapel-house's seasonal observances, the small marriages and disputes that organise daily life.

The hamlet is not on any regional faction's map. The bonded landlords' authority is felt indirectly through tithe collection rather than through political pressure. The Marquess's authority is essentially abstract — Marquess Valtor is a name people know but not a person who affects their daily lives.

The hamlet is friendly to outsiders in the working-class register — pleasant, cautious, generous if approached politely. A traveler stopping at the dock for the day will be met with helpful courtesy. A traveler trying to settle in unannounced would be met with watchful but not hostile observation.

## In-Between Texture

**The Valley Road through Halselund.** The road runs through the hamlet's center along the riverbank. Light traffic — most upstream travelers go further before stopping; downstream travelers from the upper Angerap usually go all the way to Silberbach. The road is in adequate repair through the hamlet's stretch.

**The river.** The hamlet's principal route is the water itself. Barges pass several times a day; small fishing boats are continuous. River-skill is a basic life skill for most hamlet children.

**Banditry:** Essentially nonexistent. The hamlet is too small to attract opportunists, too close to Silberbach to escape attention if anything happened, and the local fishing community is collectively numerous enough to deter most hostile interest.

**Climate and seasons:** Mild river-valley conditions. The river's freeze in winter restricts the fishing economy; the spring thaw produces minor flooding that the hamlet has built around for generations. Summer is the most prosperous season.

**Religious presence:** The chapel-house serves the hamlet with the typical mixed-faith Aethelmark accommodation. The fishing-saint and the river-spirits get particular emphasis here; the bonded farming families' agricultural observances overlap.

## Hooks

- **A bargemaster requests an unusual layover.** A barge from upriver wants to stop at the dock for two days, ostensibly for repairs. The cargo is unmarked. The hamlet does not have the authority to refuse but does have the eyes to notice.
- **Marta brings news from the manor.** During one of her monthly visits, she shares something with her mother that the broader family overhears. The information is sensitive. How does it move in the hamlet?
- **A child goes missing.** Most likely drowned in the river, the standard tragedy. Possibly something else. The community responds; the response is one of the hamlet's most testing collective moments.
- **A traveler with too many questions.** Asking about the manor staff. The hamlet does not have a name to place; Marta's mother is uncertain whether to acknowledge that her daughter works there.
- **The mill's grain rate dispute.** A bonded farmer claims the Mehler mill is overcharging. The dispute is small but concentrated; the hamlet's social fabric depends on resolution.
