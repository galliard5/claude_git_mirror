House Grauwald
==============

Barony. Small territory in the southern hill country, known for sheep farming and wool production. The wool harvest is regionally respected; the family finances are not what they were a generation ago. The current Countess is actively pursuing solutions to a household problem that has reached the point where outside help is required.

---

## Identity

**Crest:** A white sheep on a grey hill
**Colors:** Grey and white
**Motto:** "Patient as the Flock"
**Seat:** Grauwald Manor — a modest stone manor house at the centre of the family's hill territory, two days' ride southwest of Silberbach.

---

## Holdings

**Primary Seat:** Grauwald Manor in the southern hills
**Territory:** Approximately twelve square miles of hill pasture and a small village (Graudorf) at the base of the manor's road. Five subordinate shepherding holdings in the surrounding country pay tithe to the family.
**Vassals:** None of noble rank. The five tributary holdings are run by senior shepherding families who hold customary rather than legal title.
**Income:** Wool harvest twice a year (spring and autumn shearings), mutton and lamb sold to the Silberbach market, modest cheese production, and a slowly diminishing reserve of family silver that has been quietly converted to operating capital over the past decade.

---

## Standing & Influence

Grauwald is a minor house — small territory, small population, small income, small political weight. Their wool is genuinely good and the textile guilds of Silberbach (Weavers, Fullers) work regularly with their factor. The family attends regional functions when invited and is taken as an unremarkable presence at most of them.

What Grauwald has retained is reputation. The line is old. The wool is reliable. The family has not embarrassed itself in living memory. None of these are politically powerful, but they are politically *clean*, and that cleanness is itself an asset for a family that increasingly cannot afford to be tarred by association with anyone in active controversy.

The house has no current rivalries because no one finds it worth the effort to be Grauwald's rival.

---

## Key Members

- **Count Aldwin Grauwald** — Current ruler. Late sixties. A genuinely decent man whose practical capacity has not kept pace with the family's accumulating financial problems. Spends most of his time on the estate among the flocks and the shepherds; leaves administrative and political matters increasingly to his wife. Not stupid; simply not equipped for the present situation. Adored by the household staff.
- **Countess Mareth Grauwald** — Aldwin's wife. Mid-fifties. A determined woman from a slightly less distinguished line who married up and has been managing the family's actual operations for the past fifteen years. Knows precisely how poor they have become. Has been running the slow conversion of family silver to working capital, in private, without informing her husband of the full picture. She is the household's effective head and has been for some time. *(See Current Situation below.)*
- **Lord Bertrand Grauwald** — Heir, early thirties. Quiet, scholarly, more interested in the estate's wool sales records than in courtly life. Has not married. The family has been seeking a match for him for six years without success — every promising prospect has discovered the family's finances during the negotiation phase and withdrawn. Bertrand knows this. He has resigned himself to it without bitterness, which his mother finds simultaneously admirable and frustrating.
- **Lady Anke Grauwald** — Bertrand's younger sister, mid-twenties. Unmarried. Unlike her brother, she actively wants out of the house's financial trajectory and has been the most enthusiastic about her mother's recent strategic shifts. The Countess regards her as the family member most likely to eventually save them, by which the Countess means *outlast them.*
- **Lady Hilde Grauwald** — Aldwin's elderly aunt, late eighties. Lives in the manor's east wing. Owns most of the family's remaining unencumbered jewelry. Will not part with any of it. Has expressed her intentions about its disposition after her death in language that could be read several ways and is being read in all of them.

## Politics & Secrets

**Alliances:**
- Modest commercial ties to **the Silberbach Weavers' Guild** through their wool factor (a cousin of the household, working on a salaried basis rather than a kinship one — the family pays him because they cannot afford informal arrangements).
- Cordial relations with **House Rothwyn** — the two families exchange letters and mutual support; both are minor, both are quietly struggling, both maintain dignity for similar reasons. They are not allied politically because neither has anything to bring to such an alliance.

**Rivalries:**
- None active.

**Secret Agenda:**
The Countess has been quietly seeking a discreet arrangement with [[Isalias_Manor]] for the past six months. The reason is not financial in the conventional sense — it concerns Lord Bertrand. The family's heir has confided to his mother (and only to his mother) that he has no interest in marriage of any kind, and specifically that the prospect of being expected to produce an heir has begun to affect his health. The Countess does not consider this a moral problem. She considers it an inheritance problem.

She is approaching the manor to discuss whether one of the manor's surrogate arrangements might be made to work for the family's situation — a solution that would produce a Grauwald heir without requiring Bertrand to enter a marriage he cannot sustain. The arrangement she is contemplating is unusual, expensive in ways the family cannot quite afford, and absolutely the kind of thing the manor handles. She has discussed it with no one. Her husband does not know.

**Relationship to Manor:**
Currently nascent. The family has had no prior dealings with the manor. The Countess's pending visit (within the fortnight of 20 April 1650) will be the first formal consultation. She has been corresponding indirectly with Isalia through a third party for two months to establish that the conversation can be had at all.

## Current Situation

*(As of 20 April 1650 — last known state. Conditions may have moved.)*

The Countess is preparing for her visit to the manor. The journey to Silberbach is not difficult but the political care she must take in being seen there is considerable — Grauwald cannot afford to be seen approaching the manor in any way that suggests scandal or financial desperation, so the visit will be framed as a wool-trade matter. The Countess will arrive with a small retinue, stay one night at a respectable inn (likely the Silver Eel, where Mira's manor arrangement extends courtesy to other noble visitors), and proceed to the manor on a stated business pretext.

The actual consultation will take longer than the cover story permits. The family is therefore likely to need a second visit, or a return correspondence, before any arrangement can be finalised. Mareth has thought about this carefully. She intends to bring Anke with her on the second visit if the first goes well — partly because Anke can be trusted, partly because the daughter's eventual marriage prospects depend on the family's solution to the heir problem, and Mareth wants her to understand the situation directly.

Aldwin remains uninformed. The Countess intends to bring him in only when the arrangement is structurally settled and presentable as a *fait accompli*. She is prepared to absorb his disappointment in her concealment as the cost of having handled the matter.

Bertrand knows his mother is acting on his behalf and has not asked for the details. He has expressed gratitude in a letter he asked her not to read until the matter was concluded. She has the letter and has not opened it.

## Atmosphere & Place

Grauwald Manor is a working estate that has been worn slowly thin by maintenance deferred over a generation. The stone is sound; the roof is sound; the curtains are old; the servants' wages are six months behind in places, with the staff paid in kind from the larder when coin runs short. The household is loyal and unhappy — they understand the situation and they stay anyway because the family treats them well in every way that does not require silver.

The manor's great hall would not impress a visitor. The wool barns and the shearing sheds, on the other hand, are immaculate. The family invests where the family's income comes from, and lets the rest sit.

Visitors arriving from the valley road see hills, sheep, and the manor's grey stone roof rising from a low rise. The approach is undefended. The family has not had a garrison in living memory.
