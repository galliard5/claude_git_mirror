Decanter of Endless Horse Semen
================================

A novelty item. Functions exactly as described and is otherwise useless. Isalia has kept it for years.

---

## Description

An ordinary-looking decanter — dark glass, simple stopper, the kind of thing that sits on a shelf without drawing attention. Nothing about its appearance suggests what it contains. It is full when you pour from it. It is full when you pick it up again. It does not empty.

The contents are equine semen. This is not approximate or metaphorical. Whatever enchantment the thing runs on produces the genuine article in unlimited quantity, at a consistent temperature, with no degradation over time. From a purely technical standpoint it is an impressive piece of work.

---

## Origin

Purchased from a shop selling novelty enchanted items — the kind of establishment that stocks a Robe of Useless Things, a Moveable Rod, items designed to inconvenience or bewilder rather than serve any practical function. It was on a shelf with similar items. Isalia bought it. Whether the shopkeeper knew what they had is not recorded.

Who made it and why are unknown. That someone put genuine effort into enchanting a decanter to produce this specific output in infinite quantity suggests either a very particular sense of humour or a commission that history has not preserved.

---

## Estate Testing

Early in the estate's history, when the decanter was newer and its output had obvious theoretical applications to a breeding operation, Isalia had it tested. The results were unambiguous: sterile. Every sample, consistently, across multiple tests. The enchantment produces the appearance and composition of equine semen with complete fidelity in every respect except the one that would make it agriculturally useful.

It was shelved after that. The estate uses conventional breeding stock.

---

## Current Use

Isalia keeps it in her quarters. It has not left since the testing concluded.

The evening drink is deliberate — when the work is done and the ledgers are closed, she pours from it and drinks it the way another person might drink brandy. Chosen, savoured, a known pleasure at the end of the day.

The morning pull is different. She takes it before going downstairs, before dressing for business, as part of no stated ritual. The stopper comes out, she tips it back, she puts it back on the shelf. She does not appear to register it as a conscious act. If asked, she would likely say she simply likes the taste — which is true. Whether that fully accounts for the automaticity is a question she has not asked herself and shows no sign of finding interesting.

She is not unaware that she does this. She has assessed it, found no harm in it, and moved on. The self-possession is genuine. So is the habit. Both things are true simultaneously, which is characteristic.

Most staff who have been in her quarters have seen it — a decanter on the shelf, dark glass, some pale liquid inside. Few have asked what it is. Fewer still know. It is not the kind of thing that comes up.
