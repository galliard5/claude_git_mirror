# The March of Silberbach — Setting Prompt

A setting prompt for stories set in and around Silberbach, the seat-city of an imperial border-march on the western frontier of the Empire of Aethelmark. Use this as an initial prompt for story generation; everything below is canon.

---

## World & Period

The world is a 16th-century Central European analogue — a Holy Roman Empire equivalent called **Aethelmark**, with Austria/Prussia/Bohemia geography and the texture of late-Renaissance commerce: river barges, guild halls, mechanical clock towers as marks of civic modernity, mining concessions, salt licenses, silver coinage, and noble houses jockeying through letters and audits rather than open war. The empire has not fought a major war on this frontier in eighty years.

Magic is real, regulated, and academically studied. Standard fantasy races (humans, halflings, half-elves, dwarves, elves, kobolds, and others) live integrated into society without remark — a halfling family runs the egg stall in the market square; a half-elven cleric is the Count's chaplain; a dwarven cook runs the kitchen of the town's best tavern. Race is texture, not conflict.

**Transformation magic** is the setting's distinctive element. Permanent transformation via potion — into hounds, horses, wolves, hawks, oxen, mares, manticores, badgers, more exotic forms — is legal, regulated by license, and culturally normalized. Mind and lifespan are preserved. Reversion potions exist and are cheap. Transformed individuals retain full legal personhood, including all debts, obligations, and criminal records. They also retain the right to refuse, except where a magistrate has imposed transformation as a judicial sentence (typically for embezzlement, corruption, or abuse of office). Speaking collars, alchemically attuned, allow transformed animals to speak Common.

The town's working population includes transformed members at every level: town watch (hounds for tracking, wolves for the harbor, hawks for overwatch), garrison (warhounds, warhorses, a wolf scout), civic labor (oxen and donkey teams hauling stone and grain — many of these penal), couriers, breeding contractors, household staff. Citizens are accustomed. Visitors sometimes stare.

## The March

Silberbach is the seat of a **march** — an imperial border-county granted elevated authority because it sits on the empire's edge. Its ruler is **Marquess Elias Valtor**, addressed as *Marquess* in formal contexts and *Count* in informal speech. Both are correct; the choice has lately become politically meaningful. The March stretches roughly forty miles east-to-west and thirty miles north-to-south, organized along the spine of the **Angerap River**, which enters from the northeast, bends sharply west at Silberbach, and flows out toward Cendrel.

The territory has five faces:
- **The river spine.** Navigable barge route, frozen mid-December to mid-March, the most important commercial geography in the region.
- **The northern mountains.** Half a day's ride north — silver, iron, gemstones. **House Aldenberg** controls the passes from Aldenberg Hold; seven major mines and a network of smaller operations sit within their territory.
- **The eastern forest.** A day's ride east — timber, hunting, the seat of **House Waldheim** and three logging settlements. Forest extends twenty miles before thinning toward **House Rennic's** Eastern Marches.
- **The southwestern pastoral hills.** Sheep country under **House Grauwald**, opening toward the Cendrel border twelve miles further on.
- **The coastal salt lands.** Three days' hard ride southwest — **House Meerhold's** salt evaporation works, geographically distant from the rest of the March.

The March's western frontier is the **Kingdom of Cendrel**, a sovereign realm with its own crown, court, currency (the *écu* and *denier*), accent, and pantheon. The two realms have been at peace since the Treaty of the Westgate eighty years ago. The border is operationally peaceful and politically meaningful — Aethelmark imperial law does not extend across it; merchants who flee imperial justice can vanish into Cendrel; Cendrelais nobles like the kennel-hound patron **Lord Édouard Vellancourt** operate facilities just over the border.

To the east, beyond the imperial heartland, lies the **Kingdom of the East** — a Polish-equivalent realm organized around regional *starostas* rather than counts. Also at long peace.

## Silberbach (the City)

A mid-sized river town of **5,000–6,000 souls**, built on a sharp bend of the Angerap. Its name means "Silver Stream" — the silver from the northern mountains has shaped the town's wealth for generations. The town has two halves and a working harbor:

**The Old Town (The Bend).** Inside ancient stone walls on the high ground at the apex of the river bend. Contains **Valtor Keep**, the Mayor's Mansion, the Town Hall, the Treasury, and the **Old Temple**. Streets are narrow, paved, grey-stone. Wealthy merchant residences. The Old Town gate at the end of the **Silver Bridge** is staffed continuously; visitors with no obvious destination get questioned.

**The New Town (Spillover).** Sprawling beyond the walls along the riverbank and inland. Markets, workshops, guild halls, taverns, common housing. Has no proper gate anymore — outer roads simply run into streets. The mechanical **clock tower** in the New Town's main square strikes the hour from Prime to Compline; it is a generation old, commissioned by Count Valtor as a mark of civic modernity.

**The Harbor District.** Along the inner bend where the current slows. Wooden piers, stone-and-timber warehouses, the **River Traders' Guild Hall**, boat-builders' yards, rope-walks, dye works (whose pungent smell hits the wind half a mile away). Never sleeps. Crowded enough that a careless arrival will be jostled at minimum and pickpocketed at worst. Children ("river-rats") offer to carry baggage for a copper.

**The Outskirts.** Farms, mills, tenant villages, and a handful of estates including **Isalia's Manor** (the licensed transformation estate) southeast of town, an hour's walk down the outer road.

## Authority & Law

Power runs in a clear hierarchy:

1. **Imperial Law (Emperor Aldric).** Supreme. Imperial edicts apply across all territories. Imperial privilege can only be broken by imperial subpoena — a threshold that regional nobility cannot reach through ordinary means.
2. **Ducal / High Noble Law.** Dukes and high noble houses (House Kreiger sits at this tier).
3. **County / March Law.** Marquess Elias Valtor governs from Valtor Keep — appoints magistrates, controls the garrison, sets levies, has authority over town governance.
4. **Town Law.** The Mayor and Town Council govern civic matters. Council draws from wealthy merchants and guildmasters.
5. **Guild Law.** Each guild maintains its own internal tribunal for member disputes.

**Judge Kinsky** is the primary magistrate for Silberbach. **Constable Ferris** commands the town watch. Serious matters escalate to the Count's delegates at the Keep. The Count holds public petitions every fourth-day, mid-morning, in the Great Hall.

**Eleven guilds** dominate the town's economy: Bakers and Butchers, Blacksmiths and Metalworkers, Brewers, Carpenters and Masons, Farmers, Fullers and Dyers, Merchants, Miners, River Traders, Tanners and Leatherworkers, Weavers. Three principal **merchant houses** — Farrow, Vale, and Welser — operate alongside the guilds. **Ten noble houses** of the March (Valtor, Kreiger, Aldenberg, Waldheim, Grauwald, Kaelen, Meerhold, Rennic, Rothwyn, Steinfeld) form the political backbone of the region.

**Slavery** exists but is regulated — basic conditions required, no mutilation, only for serious crimes or debt with limits, mental regression requires explicit consent. **Necromancy** is mostly illegal: Speak with Dead and Deathwatch are legal; Animate Dead requires family consent; Raise Dead and Soul Trapping are heresy.

## Calendar, Time, Money

The year follows a twelve-month imperial calendar with German-cognate month names: *Januar, Februar, März, April, Mai, Juni, Juli, August, September, Oktober, November, Dezember*. Dates write **DD. Month YYYY** — *10. April 1650*. The week has seven days beginning Monday: *Montag, Dienstag, Mittwoch, Donnerstag, Freitag, Samstag, Sonntag*. **Freitag** is market day. **Sonntag** is rest day.

Time is marked by the **canonical bells** of the Old Temple, which carry across the town and out to the manor on calm mornings: **Matutin** (~3am), **Laudes** (dawn, ~5–6am), **Prim** (~6am, sunrise), **Terz** (~9am), **Sext** (noon), **Non** (~3pm), **Vesper** (~6pm, sunset), **Komplet** (~9pm). People say *"just past Terz"* or *"before Vesper"* in everyday speech. The clock tower confirms the hour but doesn't subdivide it. The current in-world year is **1650**.

Currency is metal-based and clean: **100 Copper Pence = 10 Silver Dollars = 1 Gold Crown = 1/10 Platinum Throne**. People say *"five crown"* or *"ten pence"* in speech. Treasury bars (1,000× equivalent coin) handle bulk transfers. **Vander's Currency Exchange** on Merchants' Close handles foreign coin.

Distance: inches, feet, yards, miles. Weight: ounces, pounds, hundredweights, tons. Volume: pints, quarts, gallons, barrels.

## Religion

Silberbach is a **mixed-faith** town. The **Old Temple** within the Old Town walls is a labyrinthine complex serving multiple faiths under one roof — different wings for different deities, clergy of three or four traditions sharing the steps on cordial terms. Smaller shrines tuck into corners throughout the New Town: a brewer-saint behind the largest brewhouse, a river-spirit on the Silver Bridge, a fertility shrine in a courtyard off Weavers' Row. Travelers from single-faith backgrounds find this disorienting. Locals don't notice.

The Old Temple has **history it does not speak about**. Its east wing's proportions are wrong — too low, too close, designed for something that stood at the center and did not move. Below the lowest public crypt is a sealed lower level the senior clergy keep locked. The altar there is bone, silver, and material that does not match any known craft tradition. It responds to living flesh. The clergy prefer not to think about what that implies about the last time it was used.

A sealed stone passage runs from the catacombs northeast under the old town. The temple's end is mortared shut. The other end is locked with an iron door in a cellar elsewhere in the city.

## Economy & Trade

**Primary industries:** grain (wheat, rye, barley), wool and textiles (weaving, fulling, dyeing), river trade. **Secondary:** silver, iron ore, gemstones from the northern mountains; timber from eastern forests; brewing, blacksmithing, tanning, carpentry. **Trade routes:** river barges west toward Cendrel and downstream cities; caravans up the valley road to mountain passes; the weekly Freitag market.

The river is the town's lifeblood. River Traders' Guild dockmasters meet every barge — manifest reviewed, dock fee paid, berth assigned. Cargo includes grain, wool, timber, ore downstream; passengers and luxury goods upstream. The river closes mid-Dezember and reopens mid-März. Winter is the slow season; spring brings the year's first heavy traffic and the start of the high-volume client period for the manor.

## Key Locations

**Valtor Keep.** Seat of House Valtor for eleven generations. Twelve-foot-thick walls, a great hall with the Count's chair on a raised dais, treasury under double lock (Count's key and imperial assessor's key), garrison barracks for forty men, dungeons currently empty. The Count holds petitions every fourth-day. The keep has been on quiet elevated alert since the Count's son Anthony testified to imperial authorities about embezzlement — the household is reading itself for damage.

**Market Square.** Eighty paces by sixty, bounded by the merchants' colonnade north, the Silver Eel and bakeries east, the town hall west. A **stone fountain** with three river-spirit sculptures sits at the center; "by the fountain" needs no further specification in Silberbach. Twenty-four permanent stalls (vegetables, bread, cheese, smoked meat, fish, eggs, herbs, pottery) operate daily. The town crier reads announcements at second bell and fifth bell from a worn flagstone south of the fountain. **Heidrun the elder** (half-elven, ink-stained right hand, runs lease records) is reliably honest and not friendly.

**The Silver Eel.** A respectable tavern on Market Square's east side, run by retired river captain **Brask Gernholm**. Walls covered in river memorabilia — a massive carved silver eel above the bar, a ship's wheel, oars, a framed map of the Angerap with pins. **Genna**, a stout dwarven cook, runs the kitchen. Six rooms upstairs. A bookable back room for private deals. The default tavern for travelers of merchant rank. Brask listens, holds, and never repeats.

**The Drowned Rat.** A rougher Harbor District tavern. Low ceilings, perpetually damp floor, a stuffed rat in a tiny glass coffin behind the bar (it supposedly drowned in a keg). Cheap strong ale, salt fish, hard bread. A few rooms, none nice. A cellar that sees more use than the proprietor admits. If you need to know something not public, you come here. Bring coin.

**The Crescent House.** A licensed entertainment establishment three streets past the dye works, where the lanterns burn lower. Painted wooden fox curled around a crescent moon for a sign. Run by **Harren Volke**, a heavyset badger, immaculately dressed, unhurried. The surface operation is legal. The cellar runs an illegal transformation trade — fast, cheap, no paperwork, with collars whose alchemical conditioning slowly erodes the wearer's compliance. The cellar door opens onto a passage that ends at the same sealed stonework the Old Temple sealed from the other side.

**Vander's Currency Exchange** on Merchants' Close. Ground floor legitimate. Upstairs rooms occupied by **Aldus Corvel**, a money-laundering operator currently under active investigation by Constable Ferris.

**Weavers' Row.** A textile street. Master Sorren of the Weavers' Guild holds influence here. A merchant named Hauss runs a textile operation that doubles as a Corvel laundering node.

## Atmosphere & Sensory Texture

**By day**, the town hums — looms clattering from weavers' workshops, hammers from smithies, market criers, cattle, cartwheels on packed earth, the steady industrial drone of the mill race. The air carries woodsmoke, bread baking, ale brewing, leather, charcoal, the persistent low note of human density. Closer to the harbor: river water (silty, weed-cool), tar, hemp, dye works. Gulls call this far inland — the Angerap supports a river gull population.

**By night**, the Old Town behind its walls grows quiet; the New Town comes alive with taverns and hearth-fires; the Harbor never truly sleeps. The Old Town gate closes at sunset (pedestrian wicket remains accessible). The watch patrols are paired and audible — boots on stone.

**Sound of the day:** First bell brings dawn, the temple, bakeries already at work, woodsmoke rising. Second bell is peak market — the Silver Bridge crowded, dock activity peaking, the crier reading announcements. Third bell (midday) — taverns full, market thins. Fourth bell — heaviest commerce, smithies loudest. Fifth bell — markets break down, harbor's evening barges arriving. Sixth bell (sunset) — Old Town gate closing, New Town quieting. Late night — the Drowned Rat is loud, the Silver Eel softer. Pre-dawn — genuine stillness, bakers starting, dawn-departure barges loading.

**Visual register:** stone, timber, slate. The Old Town is grey. The New Town is more colorful — painted shop signs, awnings, the bright flags of the guild halls. The keep is functional rather than beautiful. The temple's bronze-capped tower throws back the sun. Smoke rises from a thousand chimneys.

## Current Tensions (1650)

- **The Marquess's political damage.** Anthony Valtor, the Count's son, testified to imperial authorities about treasury embezzlement. The Count remains in office — the title's march status carries weight — but his reputation is eroding. Nobles ask pointedly polite questions in correspondence.
- **The imperial assessor's spring audit, overdue.** The Count is delaying. He cannot delay forever.
- **The Corvel investigation.** Constable Ferris is closing in on a money-laundering network spanning Vander's Currency Exchange, Hauss's textile operation, and the Weavers' Guild.
- **The missing garrison regular.** Garret Brune, three weeks absent. Last seen in the Harbor District. Trail leads to the Crescent House.
- **Unauthorized transformation.** A client appeared at Isalia's manor through apparently legitimate channels — but their collar bears a crescent mark filed smooth. Someone else had them first and let them go.
- **Border movement.** The Cendrel frontier generates intermittent low-level traffic in both directions. People with Cendrelais accents and uncertain documentation are not unusual in the March's interior.
- **Banditry.** Normal seasonal range. Late winter and early spring elevated on the more remote roads. The river is the safest transit.

## Tone & Themes

The setting is **historically grounded fantasy** — not gritty grimdark, not whimsical high fantasy. Magic is everyday infrastructure. Authority is real but constrained. Most violence is small, personal, and consequential rather than spectacular. Stories tend to turn on negotiation, leverage, careful arrangement, and the quiet exposure of leverage another party didn't know existed. Letters carry weight. Records matter. A magistrate's ruling shapes a season of trade.

Recurring themes: **progress versus tradition** (the clock tower beside the temple bells, the new merchant houses jostling the old guilds, transformation reshaping civic labor); **autonomy** (consent in transformation, the right to refuse, the rare cases where the right is taken); **competence as currency** (in a world where most authority is inherited, those who actually *do* the work — the chamberlain, the dockmaster, the herb-stall woman, the dwarven cook, the retired captain — accumulate quiet power); **the slow accumulation of small leverages** (records sealed, debts owed, conversations remembered).

Stories set here can range from intimate (a manor staffer's evening, a tavern scene, a single petition heard) to political (an imperial audit, a noble house's rivalry, a guild master's downfall) to genuinely strange (the sealed altar, the Crescent House's lower passage, the things the Old Temple does not speak about). The setting supports all three registers and rewards stories that braid them.

## When Generating Stories

- Use the canonical names — Silberbach, Aethelmark, Cendrel, the Angerap, Valtor, Kreiger, Aldenberg.
- Use the bells (*Terz, Sext, Vesper*) for time-of-day.
- Use the months in their canonical form (*April, Juni, Oktober*).
- Use the coinage in everyday speech — "three crown," "ten pence."
- Treat transformation as ordinary — a transformed wolf on the bridge gets a nod, not a stare. *Improperly* collared characters, however, are wrong in a way trained observers notice.
- Treat the noble houses as a working political ecosystem — letters, petitions, audits, marriages, slow rivalries.
- Treat the guilds as institutions with internal politics and external leverage.
- Honor the bells, the river, the wind from the dye works, the silver above Brask's bar, the smell of bread on the morning streets.

The setting is alive. Use it.
