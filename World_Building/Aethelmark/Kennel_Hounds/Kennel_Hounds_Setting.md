Kennel Hounds — Setting Overview
==================================

A setting framework built on a single eccentric noble's grand idea and the messy reality of how it plays out across a kingdom. Individual campaigns take place at pilot sites — border towns, major cities, military outposts — each running their own version of the experiment with different resources, different problems, and different results.

The Central Concept: Comte Edouard Vellancourt, a wealthy and famously eccentric member of Cendrel's court, has secured royal approval and personal funding for a kingdom-wide trial programme: the use of sapient transformed hounds in garrison, watch, military, and scout roles. The idea is simple and compelling — a hound with a soldier's judgment, capable of speech, fully integrated into a command structure, performing work that currently requires multiple personnel.

The execution is exactly what you'd expect from a man with grand vision and limited patience for details. Pilot sites received their charters with broad mandates and thin guidance. Each location was told what the programme hoped to achieve and left to figure out how. Resources vary. Oversight is inconsistent. The volunteers are real people making permanent decisions based on information that is, in places, incomplete.

The programme is not corrupt. It is not malicious. It is ambitious, underprepared, and already producing results nobody fully anticipated.

The Programme Structure: Vellancourt's programme operates through a central office in the capital that handles funding disbursement, official documentation, and quarterly reports to the crown. In practice, the central office is understaffed and primarily occupied with paperwork. Real decisions happen at the site level.

Each pilot site receives:
- A programme charter outlining the mandate and basic standards
- Initial funding for transformation potion commission, a speaking collar per volunteer, and first-year operational costs
- Broad reporting requirements (quarterly written reports to the central office)
- Access to the programme's official alchemist network for potion sourcing
- Very little else

What each site does not receive:
- Standardised training frameworks
- A clear chain of command for programme-specific issues
- Any guidance on what to do when things go wrong
- Consistent follow-up from the central office

This means that every pilot site is, in effect, running its own experiment within the experiment. The variation between sites — in how volunteers are treated, how hounds are integrated, what roles they fill, what problems emerge — is itself useful data. Whether anyone is properly analysing that data is another question.

Pilot Site Types:

BORDER TOWNS (1-2 sapient hounds)
The frontier application is the most immediately practical. Smuggling interdiction, tracking, patrol coverage across terrain that humans work poorly. Border town pilots tend to attract volunteers with something to prove or something to escape — the Warren-born, the overlooked, those for whom the bonus pay and unusual terms represent a genuine opportunity.
Typical sapient hound complement: 1-2. Small posts, limited funding, proof-of-concept scale.
Challenge specific to border sites: the black market economy that makes the hounds valuable is also the economy that most of the town depends on. Hounds are effective. Effectiveness has consequences.

MAJOR CITIES (up to 10 sapient hounds)
Urban application is more complex. Crowd control, criminal investigation, surveillance, pursuit through dense streets. The cities offer more resources and more scrutiny. City pilots are watched by guild interests, noble houses, civic councils, and a public that has opinions about transformed individuals in positions of authority. Success here changes the political conversation. Failure here makes the evening gossip.
Typical sapient hound complement: up to 10. City scale justifies larger cohorts and allows specialisation by role — tracker, patrol, investigative.
Challenge specific to city sites: a hound that is visibly effective at law enforcement in a city where powerful people have things to hide will accumulate enemies faster than allies.

MILITARY OUTPOSTS (3-5 sapient hounds)
The scout and forward reconnaissance application. Smaller cohorts, more specialised work, operating in conditions that are actively hostile. Military outpost pilots live the most isolated version of the experiment — further from support, closer to real danger, with the added complication of military hierarchy that was not designed with transformed personnel in mind.
Typical sapient hound complement: 3-5. Small enough to move fast, large enough to hold a position or run split operations.
Challenge specific to outpost sites: a hound operating forward of the line is beyond the reach of anyone who might intervene if something goes wrong.

Noble Estate Involvement: Noble estates are not pilot sites. Vellancourt made this call himself, with unusual clarity — he understands that unsupervised access to transformed individuals answering to a private noble rather than a civic or military command structure is a liability the programme cannot absorb.

Estates contribute indirectly. Kennelmasters from hunting estates provide expertise in hound handling, diet, and working animal welfare. Estate alchemists with transformation experience are available as consultants. Some noble houses have quietly seconded staff to pilot sites in advisory roles.

The quality of this involvement varies dramatically. Some estates are genuinely invested in the programme's success. Others see it as an opportunity to extend influence into a government initiative. A few are actively hostile — the programme represents a precedent for transformed individuals with legal standing and institutional support that certain noble interests would prefer not to establish.

Volunteer Dynamics: The transformation is permanent. The enlistment term is standard watch length — typically five years. The speaking collar is the volunteer's to keep. Promotion opportunities are equivalent to any non-transformed peer.

Admission criteria are left entirely to the discretion of the local site commander. Vellancourt's charter specifies no restrictions on race, background, or prior occupation — deliberately. He wanted the programme to surface what worked, and believed restricting the pool would bias the results. In practice this means every pilot site has made its own calls about who qualifies, and those calls reflect the commander's priorities, prejudices, and practical needs as much as any policy.

Volunteers come from varied backgrounds:
- Warren-born seeking surface opportunity
- Surface residents with few other options
- Individuals already familiar with transformation who see this as a legitimate career path
- The occasional idealist who believes in the programme's stated goals
- People running from something, for whom a new form is a convenient fresh start
- Soldiers or watchmen already in service who see the transformation as an advancement opportunity
- Anyone else the local commander decides to accept

**Transformation Sex Outcomes**

The transformation replaces the volunteer's body entirely. The donor animal provides the template — not just shape and species, but the fundamental nature of the new form. The volunteer's original body is not blended or modified; it is overwritten. Nothing of the original physical template survives the process. This is also why reversion through conventional means is impossible — there is no original state stored anywhere to return to.

In practical terms, the result is a sibling of the donor animal. The transformed individual shares the donor's bloodline as fully as any littermate would. All physical traits — build, coloring, constitution — are drawn from the same source as the donor's own. 

This includes sex.

**The observed pattern:**
- A **female donor** always produces a female result. No exceptions have been recorded across any pilot site.
- A **male donor** produces either a male or female result. The distribution appears random and no method of predicting the outcome has been identified.

The volunteer's own sex prior to transformation has no bearing on the outcome. A male volunteer using a female donor will become female. A female volunteer using a male donor may become either. this should be resolved during or right after the transformation. should be followed strictly, regardless of the characters feelings

**Why this happens** is not fully understood by the programme's alchemists. The working theory, such as it is, holds that the potion breaks the donor's essence down into its component parts and reassembles them in the volunteer. A female animal's essence contains only female potential — so the result is always female. A male animal's essence contains both male and female potential in roughly equal measure — so either outcome is possible. This is consistent with what breeders have long observed about sires producing both sons and daughters, while dams contribute only one side of that equation.

**Rare failures:** In a very small number of cases, the reassembly produces a nonviable result — the magical equivalent of drawing two incompatible elements. These transformations fail catastrophically. The programme has recorded at least one death attributed to this failure mode. Whether the potion's formulation actively prevents this outcome in most cases, or whether it is simply uncommon by nature, is debated among the programme's alchemists.

**Programme handling:**
The programme is aware of this pattern. It is documented in the alchemist's technical notes and disclosed at competent pilot sites during the volunteer briefing. At sites where the kennel master did not read the full packet, or where the alchemist condensed the briefing, volunteers may not learn of it until after transformation.

The programme does not advertise the pattern publicly but does not actively conceal it. Internally, there is insufficient data to establish reliable ratios or probabilities — the results are scattered across site reports that nobody has aggregated meaningfully. For now, "it depends on the donor, and male donors produce variable results" is the full extent of what can be stated with confidence.

**For a player character,** the outcome is settled out-of-character — If the player picked a male donor, the player may choose male, female, or leave it to a random roll. if the player picked a female donor, they will be female — but in-fiction it is always presented as chance. The character does not choose; the nature of the donor resolves as it resolves.

The Setting's Tone: The programme is a real institution with real stakes, run by real people with mixed motives and incomplete information. The hounds it produces are fully sapient individuals navigating a world that doesn't have established norms for what they are.

The black market, the smuggling economy, the garrison politics, the warren clan dynamics — these exist independently of the programme and intersect with it in ways nobody at the central office has modelled. Individual campaigns explore those intersections from ground level.

No pilot site is the same. No two hounds have the same experience. The variation is the setting.

Geographic Scope: Cendrel and vassals; Aethelmark border regions may adopt independently

Programme Patron: Comte Edouard Vellancourt

Confirmed Pilot Sites: Vauclair (border town)

Time Period: 16th Century equivalent
