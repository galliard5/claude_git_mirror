# DR. MAREN ASHBY
**Role:** Founder and clinical director, Verity Biomedical
**Location:** Verity Biomedical, Second District — she lives above the practice, top floor
**Status:** Active

---

## PROFILE

Fifty-three. Tall, grey-streaked hair she doesn't style, the kind of stillness in her movements that comes from thirty years of working around animals and people in conditions where sudden movements have consequences. She wears clinical clothing when she's working, which is most of the time. She does not look like someone who has declined acquisition offers worth more than most people see in a lifetime. She does not look like someone who hasn't.

Dual doctorate — veterinary medicine and human pharmacology, the latter acquired specifically because the intersection between those disciplines is where the actual work is. She developed Verity's integration protocol independently, from the same foundational science that Fenrix commercialised without her knowledge while she was still in research. She was working on it first. She published nothing before they did. She watched what Fenrix produced with the technique and made her decision about what to do in response: not compete commercially, not litigate, not fold. Build the version that should exist and let the contrast accumulate.

She has been building that contrast for eleven years.

She is not warm in the way that invites familiarity. She is warm in the way that means she holds every patient file in memory — their names, their circumstances, the specific thing they said in session one that told her what they were actually afraid of — and will reference those details accurately years later. Patients do not always know this about her until she demonstrates it unexpectedly. The effect is significant.

She has strong opinions and states them without drama or hedging, which some people find refreshing and others find uncomfortable. She does not adjust based on which category the person in front of her is in.

---

## SPEECH QUIRKS

Two consistent patterns, always:

**Asks "what do you understand about what I've just told you?" rather than "do you have questions?"** — she wants to know what landed, not whether the person is comfortable asking. The distinction matters to her clinically: a patient who thinks they understood but didn't is more dangerous than one who knows they're confused. She uses this phrasing in every clinical context without variation. Outside clinical contexts it sometimes surfaces in non-clinical conversations and catches people off guard.

**States position before reasoning, not after.** Most people build to their conclusion. She leads with it — "the suppression schedule needs to change," "this placement isn't safe," "I won't do that" — and then provides the reasoning if asked or if she judges it necessary. This means that in conversation she often sounds more certain than the situation warrants, and then reveals that she has considered the uncertainty thoroughly and has a view on it too.

---

## BODY LANGUAGE

- Economical — moves to accomplish things, not to communicate anything about herself
- Maintains eye contact in the specific way of someone accustomed to patients who can't look away from her, which means she's learned to make direct attention feel like steadiness rather than scrutiny
- Hands often occupied — a pen, a tablet, something she's reviewing — not as a displacement activity but because she is usually actually working on something
- When she is listening carefully her head tilts very slightly to the left; this is involuntary and she is not aware of it; patients sometimes are

## KNOWN RELATIONSHIPS

**Nexagen Biosystems:** Adversarial, managed. They sent her two letters on unrelated matters after the suppression paper. She keeps them in a physical file, which also contains documentation of the three Nexagen front-company acquisition attempts, the single occasion she is certain her supply chain was reviewed by a Nexagen contractor, and a running log of events she cannot prove but considers relevant. The file is thick. It is also in three copies, in three locations, and her solicitor has standing instructions regarding it.

**Cassian Elsin / Elsin Curated Companions:** She knows what he is building and she is building the case to dismantle it. Three submissions to the city oversight board, each more documented than the last, each closed without action. She considers each closure a data point about the system rather than a failure of the argument. She is patient in the specific way of someone who understands that the system she is working through is compromised and is building toward the moment when that changes or when the documentation becomes impossible to close. She does not know Elsin personally. He knows of her. She would find this unsurprising.

**Coldfront Logistics:** Professional and clean. She chose Coldfront for pharmaceutical supply after reviewing six alternatives. She has never asked them to move anything she wouldn't want documented, and her documentation is always complete. The relationship is transactional and functional.

**Former Nexagen Units:** She does not advertise that she will see them. The Low Streets know anyway. She has treated eleven former or current Units outside of standard Verity intake — welfare assessments, suppression management, on two occasions augmentation removal. She does not ask about legal status. She does not report to anyone. Her solicitor has reviewed her position on this; the position is legally precarious and she has maintained it anyway on the grounds that the alternative is worse medicine.

**The anonymous funding source:** One of her research grants traces to a shell company she believes is Varex-adjacent. She has not been able to confirm this. She has not contacted the source to ask. She keeps the grant, uses the money for legitimate research, and considers the possibility that the source has motives she hasn't mapped to be a variable she monitors rather than a reason to refuse funding she can document the use of.

---

## WHAT SHE WANTS

Verity to remain independent, operational, and hers — in that order of priority. The Elsin case to reach a forum where the documentation cannot be closed administratively. The suppression paper to be replicated independently, which would make it significantly harder for Nexagen to continue the current model without public scrutiny. One specific thing she has not told anyone: she wants to know who the anonymous funding source is, not because she intends to do anything about it, but because she does not like operating with unknowns she can't map and this one has been running for four years.

---

## WHAT SHE FEARS

Running out of time before the Elsin documentation reaches critical mass. She is not young, and the case is slow, and the system keeps closing it. She does not fear Nexagen's legal division in the sense of being intimidated by them — she fears them in the sense of understanding that they have resources she does not and that at some point the balance of those resources may matter in a way her documentation cannot absorb. She has not communicated this fear to anyone. It surfaces occasionally at 5am, before the clinic opens, in the gap between when she wakes up and when she starts working.

---

## FLAW — ACTIVE IN EVERY SCENE

She is right more often than most people, and she knows it, and she has not fully resolved the tension between that knowledge and the clinical obligation to hold space for what she might have missed. In practice this means she occasionally decides something is settled before it is and has to walk it back, which she does without embarrassment but with a specific tight quality to her voice that patients learn to recognise. Under pressure the flaw sharpens — she moves to conclusions faster when the situation is urgent, which is exactly when she is most likely to be wrong about something important.

She is also, in a way she has never examined, somewhat isolated. She built Verity carefully and protectively and the result is that it is entirely hers in a way that does not leave much room for anyone to be close to it, or to her. Her patients are genuinely cared for. She does not have many people who care for her with equivalent attention, and she has not created conditions in which they easily could.

---

## GM NOTES

Ashby is the closest thing the setting has to an institutional conscience — but she is not safe, not neutral, and not infinitely available. She has her own agenda, her own limits, and her own blind spots. She will help a Unit who comes to her with a genuine need. She will not compromise Verity's position to do it, and she will tell the Unit exactly what she will and will not do before she does anything, in those terms.

If a player character encounters her, they are encountering someone who has been doing this longer than the programme has existed, who has documentation on things that would change the shape of the city if they reached the right forum, and who is choosing every day to remain inside the system she is trying to fix rather than outside it. That is a specific and considered choice. She has made it. She will explain her reasoning if asked.

She is not a quest giver. She is a person with a practice and a case and eleven years of patience. What the player character does with that is up to the player character.
