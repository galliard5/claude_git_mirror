CALDERA CORPS
==============

The megacity runs on corporate infrastructure. Power, water, transit, communications, food distribution, medical access — every essential system in Caldera is owned, operated, or licensed by a corporate entity. The corps do not govern Caldera. They have simply made governance redundant.

What follows are the major players. None of them transform people. All of them have, at some point, picked up a phone and called Integrated Assets.

---

TIER 1 — MAJOR CORPS
=====================

These are the names on the skyline. They have their own security divisions, their own legal systems, their own residential and commercial zones in the Upper Tiers. They do not need Nexagen for most things. They call Integrated Assets when they need something that cannot appear on a budget line.

---

**KORRIGAN DYNAMICS**

*Industry:* Heavy infrastructure, transit, civic utilities

Korrigan built Caldera's elevated transit network, owns the water treatment contracts for six of the city's eight districts, and holds the primary power grid licence through a subsidiary called Korrigan Municipal Solutions — a name chosen to sound like a public entity without being one. Half the physical infrastructure of the Mid-Deck and Upper Tiers was constructed under Korrigan contract. The other half was built by competitors using Korrigan materials.

Korrigan is old money by corporate standards — second-generation leadership, conservative strategy, deeply embedded in city politics through a tangle of consulting contracts, charity foundations, and personal relationships between executives and city council members. They do not move fast. They do not need to. The city cannot function without their infrastructure and all parties know it.

**Why they call Integrated Assets:** Korrigan's leverage depends on nobody looking too closely at how their contracts were awarded or maintained. Investigative journalists, city auditors, and rival corps who have figured out where the bodies are buried — these are Korrigan problems. The operations are intelligence and suppression work: finding out what someone knows, discrediting or relocating them before it becomes a public event, occasionally something harder. Korrigan does not ask for details. They specify outcomes.

**Relationship with Nexagen:** Long-standing client. One of Integrated Assets' most reliable revenue sources. Korrigan pays well and asks clean questions. What they ask the questions about is less clean.

**Texture:** Old-money restraint on the surface. The executives dress conservatively, speak carefully, and never raise their voices. The decisions made in those quiet voices are not conservative.

**SOLACE MEDICAL GROUP**

*Industry:* Healthcare, pharmaceuticals, biotech research

Solace is Caldera's dominant medical provider. They operate seven hospital campuses across the Upper Tiers and Mid-Deck, hold the pharmaceutical supply contracts for the city's emergency services, and run a research division — Solace Advanced Therapeutics — that is the public-facing legitimate end of a biotech pipeline with a considerably less public back end.

Solace occupies an unusual position: they are the company Caldera residents interact with most directly when they are most vulnerable. Birth, illness, injury, death — Solace is present. They have translated this structural intimacy into genuine public trust that is, by any honest measure, not fully deserved.

Solace Advanced Therapeutics has been aware of the feral integration process since Fenrix's early operational period. They did not develop it. They have not replicated it. They have, however, filed fourteen patent applications in the past six years covering adjacent techniques, and their research division receives quarterly briefings on Unit Programme augmentation outcomes through a data-sharing arrangement that is not documented in any Nexagen filing.

**Why they call Integrated Assets:** Medical research produces data. Data has competitors. Solace's Advanced Therapeutics division has lost research leads to corporate espionage on three confirmed occasions and an unknown number of unconfirmed ones. They use Integrated Assets for counter-intelligence work — tracking leaks, identifying moles, occasionally retrieving research that has already moved. They have also, once, contracted a Unit for something that was described to the handler as "compliance assistance" regarding a research subject who had developed reservations about their participation. That contract file has been reviewed by Nexagen's legal division and determined to be within acceptable parameters. The legal division's definition of acceptable parameters is flexible.

**Relationship with Nexagen:** Client and adjacent competitor simultaneously. Solace wants access to the Unit Programme's medical data. Nexagen wants access to Solace's distribution network for the suppression suite pharmaceuticals. The relationship is functional and cautious and neither party fully trusts the other.

**Texture:** Clinical. The public face is warm — patient-centred, community-focused, the kind of branding that hires good designers. The internal culture is metrics-driven in the specific way medical institutions become when they are primarily businesses: outcomes are tracked, costs are managed, and the distinction between a patient and a revenue event has blurred past the point of easy recovery.

**VAREX COMMUNICATIONS**

*Industry:* Telecommunications, data infrastructure, media

Varex owns the communications backbone. The fibre running under Caldera's streets, the satellite uplinks, the encrypted corporate network that the Upper Tiers runs on, the consumer data network that the Mid-Deck lives on, and the patchy unlicensed signal that leaks into the Low Streets from overloaded relay towers — all of it routes through Varex infrastructure at some point.

This means Varex knows things. Not because they read the content — that would be illegal and they would never — but because metadata is not content, and traffic analysis is not interception, and the legal distinctions between what Varex is permitted to observe and what they actually observe have been litigated extensively and resolved in Varex's favour each time because Varex can afford better lawyers than the city's civil liberties office.

Varex is also Caldera's dominant media provider through a subsidiary called Meridian Broadcasting. Meridian produces the news, the entertainment, the social platforms, and the advertising infrastructure that shapes what the Mid-Deck thinks about and how. This is not sinister in any operational sense. It is simply the consequence of one company owning the pipes and the content simultaneously, which is a situation that regulatory bodies have noted with concern in five separate reports and acted on in zero of them.

**Why they call Integrated Assets:** Varex's leverage depends on their data advantage remaining exclusive. When a competitor develops independent infrastructure, when a city auditor starts asking questions about data retention practices, when a journalist gets hold of internal traffic analysis — these are Varex problems. They also use Integrated Assets for competitive intelligence: Units are very good at physically accessing locations that digital intrusion cannot reach, and Varex has a recurring interest in what their competitors' server rooms contain.

**Relationship with Nexagen:** Transactional. Varex has no particular loyalty and shops contracts across multiple security providers. They are valuable to Integrated Assets primarily because Varex's communications infrastructure is useful to Nexagen in ways that have nothing to do with the contract fees.

**Texture:** Slick, fast, perpetually relaunching. Varex's culture rewards cleverness and punishes sentimentality. They were the first major corp to fully integrate AI into middle management and they are proud of this. Their offices are open-plan and loud. Their executives are young and move fast. Their decisions have a half-life of about eighteen months, which is how long it takes for the consequences to surface.

TIER 2 — MID-TIER CORPS
========================

Smaller than the skyline names but operationally significant. These corps do not have their own residential zones or full legal infrastructure. They operate in the spaces the Tier 1 corps do not bother with — specialised markets, niche services, grey-zone industries that the majors prefer to keep at arm's length.

---

**COLDFRONT LOGISTICS**

*Industry:* Cold-chain transport, perishables, pharmaceutical distribution

Coldfront moves things. Medical supplies, biological materials, food stock, and — in a line of business that does not appear in their public filings — materials for clients who need physical transfer without Korrigan transit infrastructure, without Varex network logs, and without questions.

The legitimate operation is real and substantial. Coldfront holds pharmaceutical distribution contracts with Solace and two other hospital chains, runs refrigerated transit across the Mid-Deck and Low Streets through a fleet of vehicles that the Korrigan transit network was not designed to accommodate, and employs a large proportion of their workforce from the Low Streets communities that their routes pass through. They are not a good company in any deep sense, but they are better than average on wages and they do not use identity verification for employment, which makes them a significant employer of people who do not have clean corporate records.

The grey-zone operation runs parallel. Coldfront's vehicles can move through checkpoints that stop most commercial transport. Their drivers know routes that do not appear on any official map. They move things — biological, chemical, and occasionally human — for clients who cannot use conventional logistics. The Unit Programme has used Coldfront on at least two occasions for asset transfer that could not be documented through Nexagen's standard contracting process.

**Why they call Integrated Assets:** Coldfront's grey-zone operation makes them a target for both corporate intelligence operations and city enforcement. They need security assessment, leak detection, and occasionally the kind of problem resolution that their own in-house security is not equipped to handle. They pay in kind as often as in credit — favours, access, and the occasional movement of something that Nexagen needed moved without a paper trail.

**Texture:** Working-class pragmatism at the operational level. The people who drive the trucks are not thinking about the grey-zone contracts. The people who manage the grey-zone contracts are not thinking about the truck drivers. The man who runs the company thinks about everything and sleeps fine.

**AURIS SECURITY SOLUTIONS**

*Industry:* Private security, enforcement, armaments licensing

Auris is Nexagen's closest thing to a direct competitor in the security services market, and the relationship between the two companies is accordingly complicated. Auris provides conventional private security — guards, surveillance systems, enforcement contracts, close protection — to clients across the corporate and mid-tier spectrum. They are larger than Nexagen in headcount and smaller in strategic leverage.

Auris knows about the Unit Programme. They have been aware of it for approximately three years, through a source inside Integrated Assets that they have not been able to identify or corroborate. What they know is fragmentary — enough to understand that Nexagen has a capability they cannot match, not enough to understand exactly what it is or how to replicate it.

This makes Auris simultaneously a competitor and a potential client. They have contracted Integrated Assets twice for operations that fell outside their own capability envelope. Both times they found the experience uncomfortable — not because anything went wrong, but because hiring Nexagen to fill a capability gap reminded Auris's leadership of exactly how large that gap is.

**Why they contract Integrated Assets:** Operations requiring feral-form capability in environments their human operatives cannot access effectively — long-duration surveillance, infiltration of spaces with non-standard access control, tracking in dense urban terrain or the Low Streets where human operatives are visible and a large animal is not, paradoxically, always remarkable. The contracts are specific, arms-length, and accompanied by a lot of internal Auris documentation about how this is a temporary arrangement while they develop in-house capability.

**Relationship with Nexagen:** Wary. Nexagen knows Auris has a source and is not certain who it is. Auris knows Nexagen knows. Both companies continue doing business because the alternative is a direct confrontation that neither is confident of winning cleanly.

**Texture:** Military culture grafted onto corporate structure — ranks, protocols, a strong institutional identity built around competence and discipline. The problem is that corporate security is not the military, and the people who joined Auris for the culture often find themselves doing work the culture wasn't designed to justify.

**THE HAVEN GROUP**

*Industry:* Hospitality, residential management, private social infrastructure

Haven operates the Upper Tiers' premium residential and hospitality layer — private residential towers, exclusive dining and social venues, and a concierge infrastructure that provides Caldera's wealthiest residents with everything from personal security to discreet medical services to the management of problems that are better resolved quietly.

Haven is, in the obvious sense, not a security company. They are a hospitality company. Their staff are trained in service, their properties are architecturally prestigious, and their public identity is built entirely on exclusivity and discretion.

The discretion is real. Haven's client roster includes senior executives from every Tier 1 corp in Caldera, several city council members, and a number of individuals whose wealth and influence derive from sources that do not withstand scrutiny. Haven knows things — not because they surveil their clients, but because wealthy people are careless around staff they have categorised as furniture, and Haven staff are very well trained not to appear to be paying attention.

**Why they call Integrated Assets:** Haven's value proposition is discretion. When something threatens that — a blackmail situation, a client whose activities have attracted investigative attention that could splash onto Haven, a competing hospitality group that has gotten hold of client data — Haven needs it resolved without any visible connection to Haven. They also, occasionally, need something retrieved or suppressed that their own internal security is not positioned to handle. Haven does not like using Integrated Assets. They find the relationship distasteful. They use it anyway because the alternative is worse.

**Texture:** Surface warmth, operational coldness. The staff are genuinely good at hospitality. The management is genuinely good at protecting the business. The gap between those two organisational layers is where most of Haven's ethical compromises live, unexamined by either.

THE LOW STREETS — INDEPENDENT OPERATORS
=========================================

Not corps. Not clients. Present anyway.

The Low Streets have their own economy and its own power structures — fixer networks, unlicensed augmentation shops, black-market biotech operators running Fenrix-derived technique without Fenrix affiliation, and communities of transformed individuals who exist entirely outside the corporate framework.

These are not organised. They do not have names on buildings. They matter because Units operating in the field will encounter them, and the encounters do not follow the rules that corporate relationships follow. A Low Streets fixer does not care about the Nexagen asset classification. A street-level augmentation tech does not have access to Solace's suppression suite but might know things about the suppression suite's chemistry that Nexagen's internal pharmacology team would find alarming. A community of Fenrix escapees is not an enemy and is not an ally and is something considerably more complicated than either.

The Low Streets are where the setting's institutional logic breaks down. That is why they matter.
