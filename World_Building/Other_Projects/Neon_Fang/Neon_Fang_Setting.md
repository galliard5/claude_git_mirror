NEON FANG — SETTING OVERVIEW
==============================

A corporate megacity in the late 21st century. The skyline belongs to the corps. The streets belong to whoever can hold them. The space in between — the grey zone where contracts expire and oversight looks the other way — belongs to people like the Units.

---

THE WORLD
=========

**The City: Caldera**

Caldera is the setting's primary arena. A sprawling megacity built in overlapping construction waves across two centuries — older districts compressed under newer ones, infrastructure patched over infrastructure, zoning laws rewritten so many times that the current city map is a palimpsest of six different urban visions, none of them fully realised.

The city divides broadly into three zones:

**THE UPPER TIERS** — Corporate towers, controlled airspace, climate-regulated plazas. The physical and legal domain of the major corps. Residents here live under corpo law, not city law. Security is private, dispute resolution is contractual, and the streets are clean because someone is paid to keep them that way. Access requires credentials. Most people don't have them.

**THE MID-DECK** — Where the city's functional population lives. Skilled workers, mid-tier employees, licensed vendors, semi-legitimate operators. Policed by a city force that is nominally independent and practically underfunded. The Mid-Deck is not dangerous so much as tired — a population that works corpo-adjacent jobs, consumes corpo-produced media, and is one restructure away from sliding into the Low Streets.

**THE LOW STREETS** — Below the city's formal infrastructure. Not underground — physically present at street level, but below the elevated walkways and skybridge networks that the Mid-Deck uses to navigate. The Low Streets get the rain, the runoff, and the noise. They also get the freedom: unregistered vendors, unlicensed augmentation shops, fixer networks, black-market biotech, and communities of people who have been transformed by processes the corps don't acknowledge. Some of them are Fenrix escapees. Some are independent — transformed by operators who never had a corporate charter and never wanted one.

---

THE TWO CORPS
=============

**NEXAGEN BIOSYSTEMS**

Nexagen is a mid-tier megacorp — large enough to have its own security division and legal infrastructure, not large enough to dictate city policy alone. Its primary product line is biotech: gene therapies, augmentation suites, pharmaceutical contracts. The Unit Programme is Nexagen's black-budget project, run through a division called **Integrated Assets** that appears on the org chart between Research Applications and Security Solutions.

Nexagen's public face is polished. Their recruitment materials are glossy. Their volunteers sign contracts. The programme has an ethics board — three members, meets quarterly, has never rejected a proposed protocol in its operating history.

The Unit Programme is not the most profitable thing Nexagen does. It is, however, the most strategically useful: Integrated Assets contracts Units to client corporations for deniable operations, intelligence work, and enforcement actions that cannot be traced to a human employee. The revenue is secondary to the access it buys — Nexagen gains leverage over the clients who use the service, because both parties know what the service actually is.

**Nexagen's position in the market:** Legitimate face, institutional structure, real contracts, real consent process — thin in places, buried in subsections in others, but legally defensible. They are not Fenrix. They will remind you of this frequently.

---

**FENRIX BIO-SYSTEMS**

Fenrix predates the Unit Programme by nearly a decade. They developed the core feral integration process — the technique for overwriting a human body with an animal template and hardwiring cybernetic augmentations into the result — without regulatory approval, without a consent framework, and without much interest in either.

Fenrix's operation is nominally a research corporation. In practice, it is a black-market biotech firm that sells transformation services to clients who cannot or will not use legitimate channels, runs experimental protocols on subjects who did not meaningfully consent, and produces augmented feral units that it deploys, leases, or sells depending on the arrangement.

Fenrix does not have Nexagen's infrastructure. They have something more dangerous: a decade of operational data, a willingness to experiment past the ethical limits the Unit Programme's board would never approve, and results that Nexagen's own scientists study with a mixture of envy and discomfort.

The Isandar incident — an F-9 classified subject who formed an independent Alpha-bond with his integration template and escaped containment — represents Fenrix's most significant operational failure and most scientifically interesting result. The corporation is actively hunting F-9 and the pack. They are also, quietly, trying to replicate the outcome under controlled conditions.

**Fenrix's position in the market:** No legitimate face, no public contracts, real capability. They are what Nexagen would become if the ethics board stopped meeting and the HR department was replaced with a procurement function.

---

**THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN THE TWO CORPS**

Nexagen and Fenrix do not have a formal relationship. Officially, Nexagen considers Fenrix a liability — an unregulated operator whose failures generate regulatory attention that affects the entire biotech sector.

Unofficially, the relationship is more textured:

- Nexagen recruited at least two former Fenrix researchers. Neither recruitment is documented.
- Several of the Unit Programme's augmentation protocols share design signatures with Fenrix-developed tech. The patents are held by a shell company that neither corp acknowledges.
- Nexagen's legal division has, on at least one occasion, quietly ensured that a city investigation into Fenrix stalled before it produced public findings. The reason is not altruism — a serious investigation into Fenrix would surface connections Nexagen cannot afford to have surfaced.
- Fenrix has a standing buyer for Nexagen Unit data. They have never successfully acquired any. They keep trying.

The corps are competitors, not allies. They are also, in specific ways that neither will acknowledge, entangled.

---

THE UNIT PROGRAMME
==================

**What It Is**

Nexagen's Unit Programme converts human volunteers into fully feral animal bodies hardwired with a corporate augmentation suite. The result is called a **Unit** — a permanent transformation producing a non-humanoid animal form with full sapience, full speech capability, and full integration into Nexagen's operational infrastructure.

The stated purpose: elite deniable operatives with capabilities no human augment can match — tracking, infiltration, sustained field operation, and a legal identity that has been formally dissolved into a corporate asset classification.

**The Transformation**

The process is adapted from the foundational feral integration technique — the same core mechanism Fenrix developed, refined through Nexagen's more controlled conditions and larger sample set.

The volunteer's human body is overwritten. Nothing of the original physical template survives. The donor animal provides the complete biological template — species, build, colouring, sex, and constitution are all drawn from the donor. The volunteer's mind, memory, and voice survive. Their hands do not.

*Sex outcome:*
- A female donor always produces a female result.
- A male donor produces either a male or female result — the distribution appears random and no predictive method has been identified.
- The volunteer's original sex has no bearing on the outcome.
- This is disclosed during briefing at well-run intake sites. At others, volunteers learn after.

The form is fully feral — not a chimera, not a digitigrade compromise. A wolf is a wolf. Fine manipulation is zero. Coarse manipulation is limited to pushing, batting, or pressing. The animal body's capabilities are real: speed, endurance, sensory range, and sustained field performance exceed anything a human augment achieves. The animal body's limitations are also real, and permanent.

**The Augmentation Suite**

The cybernetics are what separate a Unit from a Fenrix subject. Nexagen's integration process includes:

*VOCALISER* — Synthetic voice modulator implanted at the larynx. Produces clear human-range speech from an animal anatomy. Can be muted remotely by an authorised handler. Mute events are logged. Reasons are not always given.

*NEURAL HUD* — Heads-up display overlaid on the Unit's vision. Displays mission parameters, biometric readouts, target flags, navigation data, and the corpo comms feed. Updated by handler or division ops. Can be used to push information the Unit did not request. Cannot be disabled by the Unit.

*SUBDERMAL TRACKING* — Passive location beacon, always active, always logged. Range is city-wide via Nexagen infrastructure, global via satellite relay. Nexagen knows where every Unit is at all times. There is no off switch. This is in the contract.

*SUPPRESSION SUITE* — Neurochemical regulation implants that manage the interface between the Unit's human cognition and the donor animal's neurological template. Without regular suppression doses, the animal neurology begins asserting itself — instinct bleeds into decision-making, threat responses become harder to override, identity coherence degrades over extended periods. The doses are administered by the handler. Units cannot self-administer. This is not explained as a control mechanism in the contract. It is described as "neurological stability maintenance."

*ROLE AUGMENTATIONS* (variable by assignment) — Additional hardware fitted to the specific operational profile. Reinforced skeletal frame for close-quarters work. Extended-range optical mods for surveillance. Scent analysis suite for tracking and detection. Tactical hardpoints — physical mounting points integrated into the body structure that allow equipment to be attached to a form that cannot carry it by hand. The loadout is assigned by Integrated Assets, not chosen by the Unit.

**The Contract**

Enlistment is voluntary. The contract is real. It is also approximately 140 pages, written in legal language, and presented during a briefing that runs two hours.

Key provisions:

- *Identity dissolution:* Upon transformation completion, the volunteer's legal human identity is suspended and transferred to a Nexagen asset classification. The Unit does not legally exist as a person. They exist as corporate property with contractual protections — which is not the same thing.
- *Term:* Standard enlistment is five years from transformation date. Early termination provisions exist. They are not favourable.
- *Compensation:* Paid monthly to a Nexagen-managed account in the Unit's name. Accessible at term completion or approved leave periods. Not accessible during active deployment.
- *Welfare provisions:* Nexagen is contractually obligated to provide adequate nutrition, veterinary care, maintenance of augmentation systems, and the suppression suite. What constitutes "adequate" is defined by Nexagen.
- *Reversion clause:* Not present. There is no reversal process. This is disclosed. It is disclosed on page 94.

INTEGRATED ASSETS — THE DIVISION
===================================

**Structure**

Integrated Assets sits in the Nexagen org chart between Research Applications and Security Solutions. It reports to a Senior Vice President who reports to the Chief Operations Officer. The SVP has never visited a deployment cell. The COO knows the division exists and prefers not to know more.

The division operates through **deployment cells** — small operational units of two to six Units, each attached to a handler.

**Handlers**

Handlers are human. They manage logistics, interface with division leadership and client contacts, administer the suppression suite, and are technically responsible for their Units' welfare. The role attracts a specific personality profile: people who are good at managing complex assets, comfortable with moral ambiguity, and able to maintain professional distance from the fact that their assets are people.

Handler quality is the single largest variable in a Unit's quality of life. The division does not have a standardised handler selection process. Handlers are recruited from corporate security, military contracting, and occasionally from Fenrix — quietly, and without that background appearing in their Nexagen HR file.

A good handler understands that a Unit who trusts them performs better than one who doesn't, and acts accordingly. A mediocre handler manages Units competently and thinks about them as little as possible outside working hours. A bad handler has too much unsupervised authority over people who cannot easily report abuse and whose complaints route through a division that has strong institutional incentives to resolve them quietly.

**Clients**

Integrated Assets contracts Units to client corporations — other megacorps, mid-tier firms, occasionally civic institutions with black-budget lines. The service is deniable: if an operation surfaces, there is no human employee whose identity connects it to the client. The client gets capability without accountability. Nexagen gets revenue and leverage.

The client contracts specify the operation type. They do not specify methods. The handler interprets the gap.

**Division Culture**

Integrated Assets presents itself internally as a professional operation — rigorous, results-driven, operating within clearly defined parameters. The parameters are defined by the division. The rigour applies to outputs, not to the welfare of the people producing them. The professionalism is real in the sense that paperwork is completed accurately and operations are planned carefully. It is not real in the sense that anyone at division level loses sleep over what the work costs the Units.

UNITS — GROUND LEVEL
======================

**Daily Reality**

A Unit's daily life depends almost entirely on their cell, their handler, and their current deployment. There is no standard Unit experience — the variation between a well-run cell with a decent handler on a manageable contract and a poorly-run cell with an indifferent handler on a client contract with no ethical floor is enormous.

What is consistent across all Units:

- The suppression suite requires regular doses. Missing a dose produces noticeable effects within 18-24 hours — sharpening instincts, narrowing focus, increasing threat sensitivity. Missing two produces significant behavioural shift. The handler controls the schedule.
- The Neural HUD is always on. The tracking is always running. Privacy does not exist in any technical sense.
- The animal body requires specific nutrition, requires rest in a space designed for a non-humanoid form, and requires assistance for tasks that hands accomplish.
- Communication with people outside the cell requires either the handler's knowledge or careful management of the communication record.

**Identity**

The Unit Programme strips the volunteer's legal identity and issues them a designation — typically an alphanumeric that serves as the internal asset reference. Most cells use names in practice; the designation exists for paperwork. Whether a handler uses the name or the designation says something about the handler.

The question of what a Unit is — legally, philosophically, socially — has no settled answer in Caldera. Nexagen's position is that Units are assets with contractual protections, not persons. The city's position is that Units are a Nexagen internal matter and not covered under general civil code. The Low Streets' position is more varied: some communities treat transformed individuals as people without qualification. Others have their own hierarchies and complications.

Units who make contact with the Low Streets — through field operations, through careful unofficial movement, through the fixer networks — encounter a world that did not sign the contract and does not necessarily accept its terms.

**The Fenrix Question**

Every Unit knows Fenrix exists. Most know the broad shape of what Fenrix does. A Unit operating in Caldera will, eventually, encounter a Fenrix subject — an escapee, a current operative, someone transformed by an independent operator using Fenrix-derived technique.

The encounter produces complicated feelings. The Fenrix subject did not consent. The Unit did. The Fenrix subject is hunted by their corp. The Unit is tracked by theirs. The Fenrix subject may be more free in specific ways. They are also more exposed, less supported, and operating with less reliable augmentation.

The easy narrative — Nexagen is the good version, Fenrix is the bad version — does not survive contact with specifics.

THEMES
=======

**Institutional legitimacy vs. actual ethics.** Nexagen has a consent framework, a contract, an ethics board. Fenrix does not. Nexagen is not therefore ethical — it is legally defensible. The distance between those two things is where the stories live.

**Dependency as control.** The suppression suite is not presented as a leash. It is presented as medical necessity. The distinction matters to Nexagen's legal team. It matters less to the Unit who notices that their handler is two days late with the dose and is not answering comms.

**Identity under institutional pressure.** The contract dissolved the volunteer's legal identity. The transformation dissolved their physical one. What remains is human cognition in an animal body in a corporate asset classification. The question of who you are when all the external markers have been stripped is not rhetorical — it is the operational condition.

**The org chart as horror.** Nobody at Nexagen is twirling a moustache. Decisions that are devastating at the Unit level are routine at the division level — a cost management call, a contract extension, a suppression schedule adjustment that makes operational sense from a logistics standpoint. The machinery is impersonal. That is not an excuse. It is what makes it hard to fight.

**Street contact as anchor.** The Low Streets do not care about the contract. Fixer contacts, Low Streets vendors, Fenrix escapees, independent transformed individuals — these relationships are unofficial, often prohibited, and the most unmediated human contact a Unit gets. They are also the relationships most likely to surface information the division does not want surfaced.

---

SETTING TONE
=============

Robocop meets The Insider. The violence is real and has weight. The corporate machinery is enormous and impersonal. The horror is not the monsters — it is the org chart, and the people who implemented it without losing sleep.

Action sequences exist and matter. So do the conversations that happen in the handler's office at 11pm when the comms feed is technically off. So do the moments in the Low Streets when a Unit realises the person they're talking to has no idea what a Nexagen asset classification means and is just treating them like a person.

Quiet scenes earn the loud ones. Loud ones should feel earned.
