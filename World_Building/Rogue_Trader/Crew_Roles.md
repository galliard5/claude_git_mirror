# Rogue Trader — Crew Roles

## Overview

The ship runs on a defined hierarchy of essential roles — the key personnel without whom the vessel cannot function — supported by a general working crew. Roles are drawn from the full range of what a long-running independent operation requires: command, navigation, technical maintenance, trade, security, ground operations, and the spiritual life of the ship itself. Most roles can be filled by PCs, NPCs, or a mix of both depending on the campaign group.

The line between officer and crew is practical. Titles matter less than competence, and the ship has been running long enough that everyone aboard knows the difference between someone who holds a position and someone who *does* the job.

---

## Command Tier

**The Captain**
Ultimate authority aboard the ship. Responsible for contracts, crew welfare, strategic decisions, and the ship's reputation — which in this business is as valuable as its hull. The captain does not need to be the best fighter, the best pilot, or the best anything. They need to be the person the crew follows when things go wrong, and the person other captains and factors negotiate with when things go right. A good captain delegates to people better than them and takes responsibility for the outcomes.

**The Seneschal**
The captain's right hand for everything that isn't flying or fighting. Trade negotiations, contract assessment, cargo management, crew payroll, supply acquisition, information brokerage. The seneschal knows what things are worth, what people want, and what it costs to make problems go away quietly. On a ship that runs trade, smuggling, and mercenary contracts simultaneously, this role is never not busy. A good seneschal is worth more than a working weapon battery.

## Navigation and the Warp

**The Navigator**
The single most critical and most protected individual aboard the ship. Without a Navigator, the ship cannot make warp transit. With a bad or incapacitated Navigator, it can attempt transit and not arrive. Navigators are individuals with a rare sensitivity to the warp — inherited, developed, or occasionally just present without explanation — that allows them to perceive the currents of warp space and guide a vessel through it without losing the crew to whatever lives in there.

They are protected not merely because they are valuable but because the crew is genuinely, practically dependent on them in a way that is difficult to overstate. A ship that loses its Navigator is stranded. The Navigator knows this. So does everyone else, and the relationship that results is complex — part employer/employee, part mutual dependency, part something that doesn't have a clean name.

Navigators are strange, as a rule. Extended warp exposure leaves marks. Most crews learn not to ask about certain things and to accept certain behaviours as simply part of the arrangement.

**The Void-Master**
Helm, navigation in real-space, sensor interpretation, and orbital mechanics. The Void-Master gets the ship where it needs to go once it has arrived somewhere, manages approach and departure, handles docking, and makes the calls during combat manoeuvring. They work closely with the Navigator on transit planning and are often the first to notice when something is wrong with the ship's movement or the space around it.

## Technical

**The Explorator**
Chief engineer, system maintainer, tech-specialist. Responsible for keeping the ship running — drives, weapons, life support, power distribution, all of it. On a ship this old, this means managing the relationship between modern replacement systems and the imperial infrastructure they're bolted onto. The Explorator is the person who knows which modern systems are load-bearing for the old ones, which old systems nobody fully understands but also nobody will let them remove, and which compartments in the lower decks are best left alone.

Explorators tend toward a technical religiosity — treating the ship's systems as something between machinery and living thing. On this ship, given the age of the hull, this attitude is not entirely irrational.

**Technical Crew**
Supporting the Explorator is a small team of specialists: drive technicians, weapons loaders, systems monitors. They know their sections of the ship intimately and are deeply suspicious of anyone who doesn't. Losing a specialist is a crisis. The Explorator spends significant effort cross-training crew against exactly this eventuality.

## Operations

**The Arch-Militant**
Security chief, weapons officer, and commander of any ground-side military operations. Responsible for ship defence, crew discipline in extremis, and leading or overseeing combat contracts. The Arch-Militant manages the ship's armed complement and maintains the armoury. On a vessel that takes mercenary contracts, this role overlaps significantly with the ground team leads.

**Ground Teams**
The ship maintains a complement of combat-capable crew for surface operations — recovery contracts, mercenary work, security details. Drawn from Only War and Deathwatch traditions, these are specialists in varied roles:

- *Squad Leaders* — small unit command, tactical assessment, keeping people alive under fire
- *Heavy Weapons* — crew-served weapons, anti-armour, area denial
- *Combat Medic* — field medicine, triage, keeping the team operational when things go wrong
- *Infiltrator/Scout* — advance reconnaissance, stealth operations, extraction
- *Demolitions* — breaching, sabotage, clearing obstacles of the structural variety
- *Close Quarters Specialists* — boarding actions, confined space fighting, the work that happens inside hulks and tight corridors

Ground teams are not soldiers in a formal military sense. They are capable professionals who have learned that preparation and restraint keep them alive longer than aggression, and that the best combat contract is one where nothing goes wrong enough to require their full capabilities.

## Information and Trade

**The Factor**
Where the Seneschal manages the ship's finances and internal economy, the Factor manages relationships — contacts in ports, intelligence on trade conditions, knowledge of who wants what and who has what and who would pay to keep those two facts from meeting. The Factor is the crew member who knows everyone, owes favours, is owed favours, and maintains the network of connections that makes the difference between a good contract and a missed opportunity.

**The Astropath** *(if present)*
Warp-sensitive individuals capable of sending and receiving communications across interstellar distances — the only reliable long-range communication method available. Astropaths are rarer than Navigators and considerably more fragile. A ship with a resident Astropath has a significant advantage in information and contract acquisition. A ship without one relies on ports, relays, and whatever messages were waiting when they arrived.

## The Ship Cult

The ship is old enough that nobody alive remembers a time before the rituals. They are simply part of how things are done — practices accumulated over generations, formalised in some cases, informal in others, observed by almost everyone aboard without particular examination of why.

**Daily Observances**
The engines are spoken to when powered up and thanked when powered down. This is not metaphor — specific words, specific cadence, the same every time. The Explorator maintains the formal liturgy; the drive techs have their own versions. Both work, or at least nothing has gone wrong yet when they're followed.

Before warp transit, certain sections of the ship go quiet. No unnecessary movement in the lower decks. The Navigator's route through the ship to the helm is kept clear. Those crew members who have tokens, icons, or personal observances perform them. This is not required. It is simply what everyone does.

When a crew member dies aboard, their name is added to the roll kept in the lower decks near the oldest section of the hull. Nobody decided this was where it should be kept. It has always been kept there.

**The Deeper Observances**
Beyond the daily rituals are the ones less easily explained — practices around specific compartments, specific dates, specific sounds the ship makes. Some crew members take these more seriously than others. The ones who have been aboard longest tend to take them most seriously, which the newer crew note without necessarily understanding.

The Navigator is central to the cult's more significant observances, whether they wish to be or not. Their role in keeping the ship and crew alive through warp transit has accreted meaning over generations. Before a significant transit, certain things are done. After a safe arrival, certain things are acknowledged. The Navigator participates in these with varying degrees of personal belief. What matters to the crew is the participation, not the belief.

There is no single theology. No organised priesthood, no holy text, no agreed doctrine. What there is instead is a body of practice — things that are done, things that are not done, things that happened once and were noted and have been observed ever since. It functions. It holds the crew together in a way that hierarchy alone does not. And on a ship this old, in a universe this strange, the line between superstition and practical caution is not always easy to locate.
