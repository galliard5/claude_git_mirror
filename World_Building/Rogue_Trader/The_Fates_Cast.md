# The Fates Cast
### Imperial Hull. Current-Era Guts. More History Than Anyone Has Mapped.

## The Hull

The Fates Cast is an imperial-era vessel sitting somewhere between light cruiser and cruiser displacement — approximately five kilometres stem to stern, with a beam and draught that suggest she was originally specced for a heavier combat role than she currently fills. The hull is old. How old depends on which section you're standing in and how much of the original structure survives under the repairs, modifications, and outright replacements that have accumulated across the intervening millennia. Some of the deep structural members are original — imperial metallurgy, dense and strange, bearing manufacturing marks that nobody aboard can fully interpret. The outer hull plating is a patchwork of eras: imperial sections that have never needed replacing alongside modern armour plate bolted over breaches, battle damage, and the slow inevitable failures of materials exposed to void and warp for longer than most civilisations last.

She is not beautiful. She is not ugly. She is a working ship that has been working for a very long time, and she looks like it.

---

## What She Was

When she left whatever imperial shipyard produced her, the Fates Cast would have been a genuine warship — fast for her displacement, well-armed, carrying weapons systems that operated on principles the current crew can describe procedurally but not explain physically. Lance batteries, point defence networks, targeting systems that could track and engage at speeds modern computers cannot match. The power systems that drove all of it were integrated into the hull architecture at a level that made them inseparable from the ship itself — not bolted on, but built in, the way bones are built into a body.

That was a long time ago.

---

## What She Is

The imperial systems failed. Not all at once — that would have killed the ship outright — but progressively, across centuries, the way old technology fails when nobody alive fully understands its maintenance. A lance battery fires one day and doesn't fire the next, and the diagnostic procedures in the manual don't identify the fault because the fault is in a subsystem the manual assumes is self-maintaining. A targeting array degrades past the point of reliable operation and nobody can recalibrate it because the calibration requires tools that were standard issue on an imperial fleet tender and have not existed for ten thousand years.

Each failure presented the same choice: find someone who could fix it, or replace it with something that works. The first option is expensive, unreliable, and occasionally impossible. The second option is achievable, understood, and keeps the ship operational. Over generations, the crew chose the second option. Repeatedly.

The result is a ship that is imperial in skeleton and modern in almost everything else. The drive systems are current-era — reliable, maintainable, comprehensible. The weapons batteries are modern cannon, laser arrays, and plasma systems mounted in the original imperial hardpoints, which were overengineered enough to accept almost anything bolted into them. The void shields are replacement units — functional, but operating at a fraction of the protection the originals would have provided. Navigation, sensors, communications, life support — all modern, all maintained by people who understand how they work.

What was stripped out was sold. Imperial weapons components, even non-functional ones, have value — to collectors, to tech-cults, to polities that maintain the expertise to attempt restoration, to anyone who understands that a singularity cannon that doesn't fire is still worth more than most modern frigates if the right buyer is found. The proceeds of those sales, across multiple captains and generations, funded the replacements and kept the ship running. It was pragmatic. It was also irreversible — each imperial system sold was a capability that could never be reacquired at any price the ship could pay.

Damien is aware of every system that was sold before his tenure and maintains a list. He does not discuss his feelings about the list unprompted.

## Layout and Condition

Five kilometres of ship contains an enormous volume of space, and the Fates Cast uses less of it than she once did.

The **operational decks** — the upper and mid sections where the crew lives, works, and maintains the ship — are in good condition. Functional, lit, pressurised, maintained by Damien's engineering staff with the attention the ship demands. These sections include crew quarters across multiple tiers, the bridge and command spaces, the engineering deck and Damien's attached laboratory, the medical bay, the armoury, training facilities, mess halls, the manufacturing workshops, and the cargo holds that make trade operations possible. This is the ship as the crew experiences it day to day — a working vessel, crowded in the way that any ship carrying several thousand people is crowded, but habitable and maintained.

The **lower decks** are a different matter. Below the operational sections, the ship opens up into vast spaces that the current crew does not fully occupy and in many cases does not fully access. These are the sections where the imperial-era interior was never modernised because there was no operational need — cavernous compartments designed for a larger crew complement, machinery spaces for systems that no longer exist, storage bays that haven't been inventoried in living memory. The lower decks are pressurised in most sections — the hull integrity holds — but they are dimly lit, inconsistently heated, and maintained only to the degree necessary to prevent them from becoming a threat to the sections above.

The **abandoned and sealed compartments** are the deepest layer. Sections of the ship that have been sealed off — some deliberately, some by structural failure, some for reasons that the current crew cannot determine because the seals predate anyone aboard. Some are simply inaccessible: collapsed corridors, hull deformations that pinched passages shut, compartments on the wrong side of a breach that was patched rather than reopened. Others are sealed with intent — heavy bulkhead doors locked from the outside, warning markers in imperial notation that nobody can fully translate, or the ship's own internal architecture routing around them in ways that suggest the original builders didn't want casual access either.

What is inside them varies. Some have been opened over the years and found to contain nothing remarkable — empty storage, dead machinery, the detritus of a crew that left or died thousands of years ago. Others have been opened and found to contain things that prompted the crew to seal them again. A few have never been opened by the current crew at all, and the sounds that occasionally carry through the bulkheads are attributed to thermal expansion, structural settling, and the general behaviour of a very old ship in the void.

The ship's cult observances include specific practices around certain sealed compartments. Whether this is superstition, institutional memory of something real, or the accumulated caution of generations who knew better than to open everything they found is not a question with a clean answer.

## Manufacturing Capability

The ship maintains a workshop level capable of basic manufacturing — sufficient for operational self-sufficiency on extended deployments away from port. This covers standard ammunition production (solid, las, common explosive), basic infantry equipment (webbing, armour components, tools), replacement parts for common weapons and ship systems, medical supplies, and rations processing.

This is not an industrial facility. It cannot produce vehicles, heavy weapons platforms, or complex electronics from raw materials. What it can do is keep the crew armed, equipped, and operational without depending on regular port resupply — a critical capability for a ship that operates in regions where the next friendly port may be weeks or months of transit away.

The manufacturing capability is supplemented by Damien's research programme, which feeds captured, salvaged, and researched technology back into what the ship can produce. The workshops can fabricate anything the research programme provides schematics for, given sufficient raw materials and time. This is the mechanism by which the ship's equipment slowly improves — every successful operation that brings back salvage or captures enemy equipment is a potential production unlock.

Raw material supply is the binding constraint. The ship carries reserves, but extended operations consume them. Looting, salvage, and trade all feed the material pipeline alongside the research pipeline. Running dry means running back to port, and running back to port means time and contracts lost.

## The Captain — Sebastian

Sebastian is young for a rogue trader captain — mid-thirties, human, carrying the bearing and presence that the role demands without the particular flavour of contempt that usually comes with it.

He has the air. That matters in this business. When Sebastian walks into a negotiation, a port authority office, or a hostile boarding action, he reads as someone who belongs at the head of a ship and expects to be treated accordingly. The confidence is genuine — not performed, not borrowed from a title. He carries it the way people carry something they grew into rather than something they inherited fully formed. Other captains, factors, and port officials respond to it because it is real, and because the ship behind it is real, and because the crew that follows him is visibly not following him out of obligation alone.

What he does not have — and what distinguishes him from most of the upper tier of the rogue trader world — is the instinct to treat the lower decks as a resource rather than a community. Sebastian knows the ship runs on people. Not abstractly, not as a management principle, but in the practical sense of someone who has spent time in the lower sections and understands what life looks like down there. He does not romanticise it. He does not pretend the hierarchy doesn't exist or that the gap between the bridge and the bilge deck isn't real. But he does not treat the crew below the operational decks as disposable, interchangeable, or beneath his attention, and the crew knows it.

This is not altruism. It is, partly, genuine decency — Sebastian likes people, or at least finds them interesting, and does not have to work hard to treat them as such. It is also, partly, the pragmatism of a captain who understands that a crew that trusts its captain fights harder, works better, and doesn't mutiny. And it is, partly, the influence of spending formative time aboard a ship with a culture that has drifted far enough from mainstream orthodoxy to consider the lower decks worth listening to.

The result is a captain the crew will follow into situations they probably shouldn't, because they believe he will bring them back if anyone can, and because they believe he will not spend them cheaply. Whether this trust is fully warranted or whether Sebastian's youth means he hasn't yet faced the situation where the math demands he spend people anyway is an open question the older hands think about and don't raise.

## Sebastian and the Ship's Culture

Sebastian's relationship with the Fates Cast's warp-adjacent culture is permissive rather than participatory. He is aware of the cult observances, the symbology in the lower decks, the Navigator's position at the centre of the ship's spiritual life. He does not suppress it. He does not participate in it beyond the transit rituals that every member of the crew observes, which he performs with the same practical seriousness as everyone else — not devotion, but the reasonable caution of someone who transits the warp regularly and has noticed that the rituals correlate with safe arrivals even if he can't explain why.

What he manages is the external face. Sebastian is the one who ensures the ship presents as a functional independent operation rather than what it actually is when dealing with port authorities, contract partners, and anyone likely to take issue with chaos undivided symbology and a crew that includes a mouse anthro heritek, a warp-mutated sergeant, an ancient super-soldier, and a psyker in a cyber-mastiff chassis. He code-switches between the ship's internal reality and the external requirements with the practised ease of someone who has been doing it long enough that neither register feels dishonest — they are simply different audiences requiring different presentations.

The crew appreciates this. A captain who drew attention to the ship's nature would endanger everyone aboard. A captain who tried to suppress it would lose the crew. Sebastian does neither. He lets the ship be what it is internally and manages what it appears to be externally, and the crew gives him the loyalty that comes from being trusted with something real.

---

## Sebastian and the Crew

Sebastian's command style is delegation to competence. Damien runs engineering, research, and medical — Sebastian does not pretend to understand the technical details and does not interfere with Damien's methods unless the results threaten the ship. Balphereon runs ground operations — Sebastian sets objectives and trusts the sergeant to achieve them. The Navigator navigates — Sebastian respects the role's centrality and does not push for routes the Navigator flags as dangerous without very good reason and a conversation that treats the Navigator's assessment as expert opinion rather than nervous objection.

Where Sebastian operates directly is in contracts, diplomacy, and the decisions that set the ship's course in the strategic sense — where they go, what work they take, who they deal with, what risks are worth the return. This is where his youth shows, occasionally. He is good at reading people and situations. He is good at the social performance of the rogue trader role. He is still building the depth of experience that tells a captain when a contract that looks good is actually a trap, or when a port that seems friendly has changed management since the last visit. He compensates with good instincts and a willingness to listen to crew members who have more years in the void than he does — a willingness that is genuine rather than performed, and that the experienced crew members value because it is increasingly rare among captains his age.

---

## The Ship's Name

Nobody aboard knows when the ship was named Fates Cast, or by whom, or whether it was the original name or one acquired later. The name appears in the ship's own records going back further than anyone has traced, which on a vessel this old means it has been the Fates Cast for a very long time. Whether the name was chosen for its implications — that the crew's fortune is thrown like dice, committed once and irretrievable — or whether it simply sounded right to someone who needed a name for a hull, is lost.

The crew doesn't think about it. The ship is the Fates Cast because it has always been the Fates Cast. Like most things aboard, the name is simply what it is, and what it means is what you bring to it.

## GM Notes

The Fates Cast is designed as a setting that rewards exploration, both in-game and narratively. The operational decks are home. The lower decks are frontier. The sealed compartments are the unknown — and on a ship this old, the unknown has had millennia to become something.

The ship's condition — imperial bones, modern guts — creates a persistent tension between what the ship was designed to do and what it can actually do now. Every replaced system is a reminder of capability lost. Every imperial hardpoint still functioning is a reminder of what the empire could build. Damien's research programme is, in part, an attempt to claw some of that capability back, one salvaged schematic at a time.

Sebastian's youth and the crew's experience create a command dynamic with room for growth. He is good now. Whether he becomes great depends on what the void throws at him and whether he makes the right calls when the cost is real. The crew is watching. They want him to succeed. They are also realistic about what the void does to young captains who haven't been tested yet.

The sealed compartments are hooks. Not every one needs to contain something dramatic — some are just empty rooms, dead systems, the unremarkable residue of thirty thousand years. But some of them are not, and the uncertainty is the point. When the crew decides to open one, the question is never just what's inside — it's why it was sealed in the first place, and whether the people who sealed it had good reason.
