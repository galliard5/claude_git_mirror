# Biomancer

## Overview

A biomancer is a practitioner who operates at the intersection of two streams of post-collapse knowledge that rarely survive intact in the same person: the remnant science of imperial bioengineering, and arcane ability oriented toward living systems. Most people inherit one or neither. Biomancers have both — and the combination produces capabilities that neither tradition achieves alone.

They are rare. They are sought after. They are, in most communities, regarded with a mixture of need and deep unease.

---

## Origins of the Craft

The stellar empire engineered life deliberately and at scale. Anthros were one product of that program — stable, reproducible, designed for specific roles and integrated into imperial society over centuries. The bioweapons were another product — less stable, less reproducible, and emphatically not integrated. Between those poles lay an enormous range of work: agricultural organisms, industrial organisms, medical organisms, things that were built to perform functions and then, when the empire fell, were left to perform them without oversight indefinitely.

The knowledge behind all of this existed in documents, in facilities, in the minds of specialists. The collapse scattered or destroyed most of it. What survived did so unevenly — fragments in ruins, partial records in the hands of technical cults, procedures passed down through apprenticeship chains that have now diverged so far from the originals that the practitioners no longer know what they've lost.

Arcane ability — the capacity certain individuals have to perceive and manipulate reality in ways that bypass understood physical processes — was known to the empire. Whether it studied it systematically, weaponised it, suppressed it, or simply noted it and moved on depends on which fragment of imperial record you're reading, and most of those fragments disagree. What is clear is that arcane ability oriented toward living systems — the specific capacity to feel the structure of an organism and push it — was useful to practitioners working without imperial equipment.

You cannot calibrate tissue without a calibration array. Unless you can feel the tissue directly. Then you can.

Biomancers emerged from this gap: people who had enough of the old knowledge to know *what* they were trying to do, and enough arcane sensitivity to do it without the tools that should have been required. The craft has existed in recognisable form for at least ten thousand years. It has never been common. It has never entirely disappeared.

## How It Works

### The Two Streams

Imperial bioengineering was procedural. It used compounds to prime tissue for change, equipment to hold organisms in specific metabolic states during transformation, calibrated energy inputs to guide cellular restructuring. The practitioner's role was technical — understand the system, apply the correct inputs in the correct sequence, monitor the outputs.

Arcane biomancy is intuitive. A practitioner with sufficient sensitivity can perceive living tissue directly — its structure, its state, the direction it wants to change and the directions it resists. They can apply pressure, guide processes, hold systems stable through active attention rather than external equipment.

Neither tradition works as well alone. Imperial procedure without arcane sensitivity is ritual without understanding — you can follow the steps, but you cannot diagnose, adapt, or recover when something goes wrong. Arcane ability without procedural knowledge is raw talent without theory — powerful, but limited by what the practitioner can intuit versus what they would know if they understood the underlying biology.

A biomancer uses both. The compounds are real — a biomancer's workspace typically contains an extensive collection of prepared substances, each serving specific functions in priming tissue, suppressing rejection, triggering or inhibiting particular biological processes. The arcane is also real — the practitioner maintains active awareness of the subject throughout the process, feeling the change as it happens and steering it with continuous small adjustments.

The result is transformation that a purely technical practitioner cannot achieve without intact imperial equipment, and transformation that a purely arcane practitioner cannot achieve without deep procedural knowledge. A biomancer can do things that, by rights, should require a facility that no longer exists.

### The Workshop

Biomancers work from fixed locations whenever possible. The practical reason is their material requirements: live cultures, prepared compounds, preserved samples, reference specimens, tools accumulated over years. A biomancer travelling light is a biomancer working at reduced capacity.

A biomancer's workshop reflects their practice and their history. Older practitioners have spaces that have grown around them over decades — things in jars that no longer have labels because the practitioner simply knows what they are; equipment that has been modified, repaired, and modified again until it bears little resemblance to whatever it started as; specimens at various stages of various projects. It is usually cluttered in ways that are legible only to the practitioner.

The smell is distinctive. Biomancers describe it as earthy, or chemical, or organic. Visitors usually describe it as wrong in a way they can't quite name.

### Self-Modification

Biomancers who practice long enough tend to modify themselves. This is partly practical — self-modification is the most direct way to test techniques that would otherwise require a willing subject, and a practitioner who has experienced transformation from the inside understands things that cannot be learned any other way. It is partly drift — people who spend their working lives reshaping living tissue develop a different relationship with their own body's permanence than most.

Self-modification accumulates. Early changes are typically subtle: improved senses useful to the work, modifications to reduce sleep requirements or metabolic needs, adjustments to reduce pain sensitivity during self-performed procedures. Over years, the cumulative effect becomes visible. Long-practicing biomancers look like what they are. The modifications are rarely random — there is usually a logic to them, a coherent vision of what the practitioner is building toward — but that logic is often personal enough to be opaque to outsiders.

Biomancers who have been practicing for multiple decades can be difficult to classify by race. The substrate is still visible, usually. What's been built on top of it may not be.

## Position in the Setting

### Knowledge Economy

A biomancer's primary value is knowledge. The compounds they use, the procedures they apply, the things they know about how living systems work and can be made to work differently — this is the currency that makes them sought after and dangerous to offend. Communities that have access to a biomancer have access to capabilities that would otherwise require intact imperial technology: medical interventions beyond the reach of conventional healers, purpose-built organisms for specific functions, transformation of volunteers for roles that cannot be filled otherwise.

Biomancers are aware of their value. The good ones are judicious about who they work for and what they do. The bad ones use leverage without restraint. Most are somewhere between, shaped by circumstances more than principle.

### Fear and Need

Communities that have never interacted with a biomancer tend toward fear, rooted in a general understanding that what biomancers do is close to what the old bioweapons did — reshape living things. Communities that have interacted with them tend toward something more complicated: need, dependency, discomfort with the dependency, and occasional resentment of the practitioner who provides it.

Most cultures have frameworks for categorising biomancers as distinct from the bioweapon-things that still haunt the ruins and the rad-wastes. Most of those frameworks are partially correct. The practical distinction — that a biomancer is a person with goals who exercises judgment, while a bioweapon-organism is a system executing a function with no capacity for either — is real. It does not entirely resolve the discomfort.

### Cults and Factions

Biomancers rarely operate entirely alone. The knowledge they carry is too valuable and too difficult to transmit in writing alone — much of it is practice, intuition, the felt sense of what tissue wants to do under specific conditions. This means apprenticeship, which means relationships, which means networks.

Some biomancers operate within cults that have preserved imperial bioengineering knowledge under religious framing. The cult provides access to old records, to compounds, to protected workspace. In return, the biomancer's work serves the cult's purposes, interpreted through whatever theology the cult has built around the empire's legacy. These relationships are sometimes comfortable and sometimes deeply compromised.

Others operate as independents, moving between communities and developing reputations that precede them. An independent biomancer's neutrality is often their most valuable professional asset — the practitioner who will not work against your community for a better offer from a rival is worth more than one who is simply very skilled.

A few have attempted to establish institutions — schools, in the loose sense — where bioengineering knowledge is preserved and transmitted outside of religious structures. These experiments have mixed records. The knowledge tends to survive even when the institution doesn't.

## Limits and Costs

### What Cannot Be Done

Biomancers are powerful within a specific domain and bounded sharply outside it. They reshape living tissue; they cannot create it from nothing. They guide processes that biology is already capable of; they cannot produce outcomes that have no biological basis. A biomancer can transform a subject's body along lines that biology supports — extending existing processes, redirecting existing capacities, introducing characteristics from other organisms via techniques the empire developed for exactly that purpose. They cannot produce a fundamentally impossible result.

They also cannot guarantee outcomes. Transformation is a live process in a live subject. Things go wrong. The compounds interact unexpectedly. The subject's arcane sensitivity — even a low level, even unconscious — interferes with the practitioner's ability to guide the change. The biomancer's own attention lapses at a critical moment. A responsible practitioner will say, before any significant procedure: *I know what I am trying to do. I cannot promise what will happen.*

Not all practitioners say this. The ones who don't are the ones to be wary of.

### Cost to the Practitioner

Maintaining active arcane awareness through a complex transformation is exhausting in ways that are difficult to describe to people who have never experienced it. A biomancer running a significant procedure is cognitively and physically depleted afterward in proportion to the complexity and duration of the work. Major transformations — the kind that take hours or days of active guidance — leave the practitioner genuinely impaired for a recovery period that varies by the individual and their condition.

This is one reason biomancers tend toward self-modification that improves their endurance and reduces their metabolic requirements. The work demands it.

There are also longer-term costs that practitioners debate. Extended practice changes the practitioner's arcane sensitivity in ways that are not reversible — deepening it, sharpening it, but also making it harder to turn off. Old biomancers tend to find crowds difficult. The constant low-level awareness of other people's biological processes is distracting at best. Some practitioners describe it as a form of sensory noise that they have learned to manage. Others say they have simply accepted that they will spend most of their time alone.

## Relationship to Bioservitors

Bioservitors — organisms or transformed individuals shaped by biomantic work for specific purposes — are the most visible product of the craft and the most ethically contested. See the separate entry on Bioservitors for a full treatment.

From the biomancer's perspective: creating a bioservitor is the most demanding work the craft produces. It is also, for many practitioners, the work they are most ambivalent about. The technical challenge is real and engaging. The human questions — what has been done to a person who consented under circumstances of limited options, what it means to shape a mind alongside a body, what the practitioner's ongoing obligations are to what they have made — do not have clean answers, and biomancers who do this work long enough develop strong opinions about them, rarely in agreement with each other.

A biomancer who creates bioservitors and thinks carefully about what that means is a different kind of problem from one who doesn't think about it at all. Both exist.
