# Remade Origin Framework

**Type:** Cross-setting campaign origin framework
**Compatible Settings:** Rogue Trader, Dead Terra, Aethelmark, any setting with biological reshaping
**Campaign Style:** Open-world, long-form
**Starting Point:** Post-capture, pre- or post-transformation

---

## Core Premise

Someone with no power has their rights purchased by someone with too much of it. They are handed to a specialist — biomancer, alchemist, hedgewitch — and remade into something inhuman. Not killed, not broken. *Reshaped.* Fully aware.

The patron wants the subject's mind intact. The reasons vary. The specialist complies, but leaves a crack in the cage.

The campaign begins when the crack widens enough to matter.

**Degree of reshaping:** The patron has a taste for the extreme. These are not subtle modifications — not a person with claws or scales. The results are *completely inhuman*. Wrong body plans. Too many limbs, or not enough. Insectoid, vermiform, sessile, chimeric — forms that have no recognisable relationship to what the subject was before.

Even features one might consider fundamental are optional. Heads, faces, eyes, hands, bilateral symmetry — none of these are guaranteed. Senses may be replaced entirely: antennae or pits instead of eyes, vibration-sensing plates instead of ears, chemoreceptors instead of a nose. The subject still perceives the world — possibly more effectively than before — but through organs that bear no resemblance to what they had. For gameplay purposes, the player may still functionally "see" and "hear," but the *experience* of perception is alien. They feel the shape of a room through air currents, or taste the emotional state of someone nearby, or sense movement through substrate vibration.

The player should be prepared to inhabit something that cannot pass as human, cannot pass as any natural creature, and may struggle to perform basic tasks their old body handled without thought. The mind is the only thing that survived intact. Everything else is gone.

## The Origin Sequence

Every instance of this framework follows the same skeleton, with all specifics as variables:

### 1. Capture / Acquisition
How the subject lost their autonomy. This establishes who they were before.

**Variables:**
- Debt bondage — sold to cover obligations they may or may not have incurred
- Criminal sentencing — handed over as punishment, possibly unjust
- War captive — taken in conflict, rights forfeit under local law
- Indentured contract — signed willingly under duress or deception
- Abandoned / unclaimed — no one to object, no one to notice
- Sold by family or community — traded for survival, favour, or spite

### 2. Commission
The patron specifies what they want. This defines the transformation target and reveals the patron's character.

**The patron provides:**
- The desired form or function (warform, labour, livestock, aesthetic piece, other)
- The cognizance requirement — always present, motivation varies (see Patron Motivations below)
- Control expectations — how they intend to maintain obedience
- Timeline and budget constraints

### 3. Reshaping
The specialist performs the work. This is the transformation itself — the process, the duration, the experience of it.

**Variables:**
- Duration (hours to weeks depending on setting and method)
- Whether the subject is conscious throughout
- Whether the process is staged or singular
- Pain management (if any)
- The specialist's demeanour during the work

### 4. The Flaw
The specialist's quiet rebellion. The control mechanism has a weakness — deliberate, deniable, possibly unknown even to the subject.

See **Control Flaws** section below for types.

### 5. Escape or Discarding
How the subject gets free. Not always dramatic — sometimes the patron simply loses interest or the control degrades over time.

**Variables:**
- Active escape — the subject exploits the flaw deliberately
- Passive failure — the control erodes and the patron doesn't notice until too late
- Discarding — the patron considers the result a failure and disposes of the subject
- Catastrophic event — external disruption (attack, disaster, regime change) creates an opening
- Release — rare; the patron or their heirs simply let go, possibly unaware the subject is still cognisant

## Reshaping Archetypes

These are categories, not fixed builds. Each implies different capabilities, different social responses, and different psychological weight the PC carries into the open world.

### Warform
Built for combat, but not built *like* anything natural. Not a bigger, stronger human — something with a body plan designed from scratch for killing. The form follows the combat role: a serpentine ambush predator, a centipedal siege-breaker, a flying thing with too many joints, a heavily armoured battering organism with no recognisable head. Sensory arrays that don't map to human perception. The patron wants a thinking weapon — something that can assess a battlefield, not just charge across one — and they want it to look like nothing their enemies have ever faced.

*Psychological weight:* The body does what it was built for instinctively. The mind has to decide whether to let it. The worst moments are when it feels *right*.

### Labour-Form
Optimised for work in environments no unmodified person could survive. This isn't a stronger human — it's a biological machine with a completely restructured body plan. Limbs replaced with specialised appendages, respiratory system rebuilt for toxic atmospheres, skeletal structure redesigned for load-bearing or tunnelling or zero-gravity. May be unrecognisable as having ever been a person.

*Psychological weight:* The most insidious archetype. The body feels efficient. It's easy to forget what it used to feel like. The horror is quiet — and the worst part is that the form *works*. It does exactly what it was built to do, and doing it feels natural.

### Livestock
Reshaped into a production animal. Milk, fibre, eggs, breeding stock — the form follows the function. Rarely recognisable as any natural animal — the patron wants yield, not aesthetics, and the specialist builds for output. The result might be sessile, limbless, or shaped around a single biological process the patron values. The cognizance requirement here is almost always cruelty or control — a thinking animal is easier to manage if it understands commands, and the patron may simply enjoy the dynamic.

When a patron commissions more than a few livestock, the specialist typically works from a **standard template** — a proven design repeated across multiple subjects. This means livestock-form escapees may encounter others with identical or near-identical bodies, which has implications for community formation, mutual recognition, and the unsettling experience of meeting someone wearing the same shape you were forced into.

*Psychological weight:* Maximum. The subject's entire existence is instrumentalised at the most basic biological level.

### Trophy / Aesthetic Piece
Reshaped for display. Beautiful, grotesque, or alien by design — often all three simultaneously. The patron's taste dictates the form, and the patron's taste runs *extreme*. Insectoid symmetry, bioluminescent structures, forms that exist at the intersection of sculpture and organism. May be functional (a living art piece that serves as furniture or decoration) or purely ornamental. Cognizance is the point — a trophy that knows what it is satisfies something in the patron that a statue cannot.

*Psychological weight:* Identity destruction through aestheticisation. The subject becomes a reflection of the patron's desires, not their own. The form may be genuinely beautiful, which makes it worse.

### Hybrid / Bespoke
Doesn't fit the categories above. The patron wants something specific and unusual — a living security system with sensory organs covering every surface, a biological computing device that processes information through neural tissue, a multi-limbed maintenance organism that can access spaces no human could reach. The biomancer may be working from reference or inventing as they go. These commissions tend to produce the most alien results because no archetype constrains the design.

*Psychological weight:* Variable, but the sheer specificity can be its own horror. The subject wasn't reshaped into a type — they were reshaped into someone else's *idea*. There may be no others like them anywhere.

## The Patron

A role, not a character. The patron is whoever has enough wealth, authority, or leverage to purchase a person and commission their reshaping without meaningful consequence.

### Patron Motivations for Cognizance

This is the variable that sets the tone for the entire origin:

**Pragmatic** — "A warform that can think is worth ten that can't." The patron sees cognizance as a feature, not cruelty. They may genuinely not understand why anyone would object. The subject is a tool, and the best tools are adaptable.

**Sadistic** — "The beauty of the form is BECAUSE it's aware of what it is now." The cognizance is the point. The patron derives satisfaction from the subject's awareness of their condition. This doesn't require active torture — the situation itself is the cruelty.

**Paternalistic** — "You were nothing. Now you have purpose." The patron believes the reshaping is an improvement. They may be kind, generous even, within the framework of absolute ownership. The subject's awareness is necessary because the patron wants gratitude, or at least acknowledgment.

**Indifferent** — The patron specified cognizance because that's the standard commission for this type of work. They haven't thought about it. The subject's inner life is not a consideration — not out of cruelty, but out of genuine disinterest.

**Paranoid** — A cognisant subject can report threats, monitor other servants, serve as a witness. The patron wants eyes and ears in a form no one suspects of intelligence. Control is everything.

### Patron Resources & Reach

The patron must be powerful enough that the specialist cannot meaningfully refuse the commission. This sets the stakes for post-escape — the patron has the resources to pursue, and the social standing to treat the subject as property, not a person.

## The Specialist

A role, not a character. The specialist is whoever performs the reshaping — biomancer, alchemist, hedgewitch, gene-wright, flesh-sculptor. Their methods vary by setting but their structural position is the same: skilled enough to do the work, compromised enough to accept the commission, and professional enough to have opinions about how it should be done.

### The Specialist's Position

They can't refuse. The patron is too wealthy, too connected, too dangerous. But they have latitude in execution — the patron specifies the outcome, not the process. The specialist controls the *how*, and that's where the sabotage lives.

### Specialist Attitudes

**Reluctant professional** — Does the work cleanly, hates the commission, plants the flaw out of conscience. May not even admit to themselves what they've done.

**Curious craftsman** — Finds the commission technically interesting. The ethical dimension is secondary to the challenge. Plants the flaw out of professional pride — no one should have total control over their work.

**Bitter servant** — Resents the patron specifically. The flaw isn't for the subject's benefit — it's revenge. The subject is collateral benefit.

**Sympathetic** — Actively wants the subject to escape. May take risks the other types wouldn't — leaving more capability intact, building in more obvious weaknesses in the control layer. Dangerous if caught.

**Complicit** — Agrees with the patron, does the work enthusiastically, but is simply not skilled enough to build perfect control. The flaw is accidental. The most unsettling variant — no one is on the subject's side.

## Control Flaws

The crack in the cage. The thing the patron doesn't know about, or doesn't know is a problem. The subject may not know either — discovering the flaw can be early gameplay.

### Flaw Types

**Chemical dependency** — The control requires a substance the patron must keep supplying. If supply is interrupted — travel, siege, shortage, negligence — the control weakens. The subject may not know why they feel clearer on certain days.

**Proximity lock** — The control only functions within a certain range of a device, location, or the patron themselves. Distance equals freedom. The subject may notice the compulsions weakening when the patron travels.

**Shallow conditioning** — The obedience layer looks solid from outside but has no depth. The subject can comply performatively while thinking freely underneath. Discovery is gradual — the subject realises they *chose* to obey last time, not that they *had* to.

**Sensory channel** — The specialist left a perception pathway the patron doesn't know about. The subject can see, hear, or sense something the control was supposed to block. Information is the first step to escape.

**Degrading architecture** — The control is structurally sound but not self-maintaining. It erodes over time — weeks, months, years. The patron would need to bring the subject back for maintenance they don't know is required.

**Kill switch reversal** — The patron has a kill switch. The specialist wired it wrong, or wired it to do something other than kill. Activating it might free the subject, or hurt the patron, or simply do nothing.

**Identity anchor** — The specialist preserved something the patron wanted erased — a memory, a sensory association, a name. It's buried deep, but it's a thread the subject can pull.

## Post-Origin: The Open World

The origin sequence ends. The PC is loose, reshaped, aware, and carrying a body built for a purpose they've rejected. The campaign is what they do with that.

### Immediate Survival Pressures

**The patron's response** — Do they pursue? How hard? A pragmatic patron might write off the loss. A sadistic one will hunt obsessively. A paternalistic one might be *hurt*, which is worse.

**The body's demands** — The reshaped form may have needs the subject doesn't know how to meet. Dietary requirements, environmental tolerances, biological processes they didn't have before. A labour-form built for a specific atmosphere might struggle to breathe normal air.

**Social invisibility** — The subject doesn't look like a person anymore. Depending on the setting, they may be treated as a monster, an animal, an escaped commodity, or simply something no one has a category for.

### The Escaped Population

Others have come through this process. Not all kept their minds. Not all escaped — some were discarded as failures. They exist in the margins.

**Wasteland / Wilderness communities** — Loose groupings, often clustered by shared function (labour-forms from the same operation) or shared territory. Not necessarily friendly. Not necessarily hostile. Just *there*, surviving in forms that weren't meant to be free.

**Ship / Station warrens** — Tighter, more dangerous. The escaped live in the infrastructure — ventilation, maintenance corridors, disused sections. Too many and the crew notices. Too few and they can't help each other. The patron has incentive to hunt them, because every escapee in the walls is proof the leash doesn't hold. That's not a moral problem for the crew — it's a safety problem.

**Solitary drifters** — Some don't group. Warforms especially may be too dangerous, too territorial, or too broken to coexist. They become local hazards, rumours, things the patrol avoids.

### Community dynamics depend on cognizance retention:
- Fully cognisant escapees can organise, communicate, plan
- Partially cognisant ones may follow routines from their commissioned purpose
- Feral failures are unpredictable — the reshaping worked but the mind didn't survive intact

## Setting Adaptations

### Rogue Trader / Void Setting
- **Specialist:** Biomancer, gene-wright, magos biologis
- **Method:** Biological reshaping, gene-grafting, vat-growth, surgical reconstruction
- **Patron type:** Rogue Trader, sector governor, cartel head, naval officer with private budget
- **Escape environment:** Ship bowels, station underbelly, void-hulk, orbital debris
- **Escaped population:** Warren-dwellers in the bilges, cargo-deck ferals
- **Crew dynamic:** The crew tolerates the patron's hobby until the escapees become a problem. Too many loose reshapings and good crew start looking for other postings.

### Dead Terra / Wasteland Setting
- **Specialist:** Biomancer, wasteland flesh-crafter, bunker-surgeon
- **Method:** Biological reshaping adapted to post-collapse resources, possibly cruder
- **Patron type:** Warlord, settlement tyrant, caravan boss, bunker-state autocrat
- **Escape environment:** Wastes, ruins, underground, irradiated zones the reshaped form can survive but pursuers can't
- **Escaped population:** Wasteland communities, ruin-dwellers, territorial ferals
- **Unique element:** The wasteland may actually suit the reshaped form better than civilisation. A labour-form built for toxic environments is *at home* in the ruins.

### Aethelmark / Fantasy Setting
- **Specialist:** Alchemist, hedgewitch, unlicensed potion-brewer, rogue biomancer
- **Method:** Transformation potions, ritual reshaping, bound enchantments
- **Patron type:** Noble, wealthy merchant, minor lord, guild head operating outside the law
- **Forms available:** Unusual or magical animals, chimeric forms, beast-forms that don't match any natural creature
- **Escape environment:** Forests, moors, ruins, the wild places between settlements
- **Escaped population:** The too-clever fox in the woods. The herd animal that won't stay penned. The thing in the old mine that the locals tell stories about.
- **Unique element:** In Aethelmark, institutional transformation (kennel hound programmes, Isalia's manor) exists as a *regulated* version of this. The Remade origin is the unregulated, black-market, no-oversight version — the thing the institutions exist partly to prevent or at least control.

## Campaign Hooks (Post-Origin)

These are starting threads for open-world play after the origin sequence:

**The Flaw Unravels** — The control is degrading. The PC doesn't know why they're thinking more clearly. Discovering the flaw and understanding it is the first arc.

**The Patron Hunts** — Recovery teams, bounty hunters, or the patron personally. Survival pressure that forces the PC to move, hide, or fight.

**The Warren / Community** — The PC finds others. Some are coherent. Some aren't. They have information, resources, and problems of their own.

**The Specialist Resurfaces** — The biomancer/alchemist who did the work appears again. Are they offering help? Covering their tracks? Working on a new commission?

**The Body Rebels** — The reshaped form has needs, instincts, or capabilities the PC is still discovering. Some are useful. Some are horrifying. Some are both.

**Reversal** — Is it possible? At what cost? Who would do the work? Would the result even be "going back" or just another reshaping?

**The Patron Falls** — Political change, death, bankruptcy. The patron's assets are redistributed — including their property. Including the PC, if anyone knows they exist.

**Others Like You** — Other patrons, other specialists, other subjects. The PC isn't unique. The system that made them is still running.

## Session Zero: Building the Origin

When starting a campaign with this framework, Session Zero should establish:

1. **Who were you before?** — Name, background, how you lost your autonomy
2. **Who bought you?** — The patron, their motivation, their resources
3. **What did they want?** — The reshaping archetype and any specific requirements
4. **Who did the work?** — The specialist, their attitude, their method
5. **What's the flaw?** — May be known to the player but not the character, or unknown to both
6. **How did you get free?** — The escape, the discarding, the crack that opened
7. **Where are you now?** — The starting environment, immediate threats, first decisions

These can be played as a prologue session or established collaboratively before play begins.
