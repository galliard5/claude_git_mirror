"I'm not going," I hissed, claws digging into the dirt as I backed away from the hound. Its yellow eyes gleamed in the torchlight, saliva dripping from jaws that could snap my spine like a twig. The village witch hadn't even bothered to explain why *I* had to be the one, just pointed her gnarled finger and muttered about "the old ways."

The hound lunged. I scrambled, heart hammering, tail lashing. Then I felt it, a shudder under my foot, like stepping on a wasp's nest. My heel crossed an invisible line, and the air turned thick, tasting of iron and burnt hair. The hound whined, suddenly cowed, ears flattening as it retreated.

Behind me, the witch's laughter was a dry rattle. "Too late, little scale-licker. The boundary's marked you now." The ground beneath my feet darkened, veins of something black and glistening spreading like spilled ink. My chest tightened. I'd heard stories, kobold pups vanishing into the marsh, their bones spat back weeks later, polished clean.

The witch's staff tapped the earth, and the black tendrils shot up, wrapping my ankles in coils colder than winter stone. I thrashed, but they clung like living shadows, dragging me backward toward the circle of torches where the village waited. Faces I knew, Grikk the tanner, old Mother Yiss with her milky eye, watched without blinking. Their silence was worse than the hound's growl.

The altar was just an old millstone, stained darker than rust. The witch hauled me onto it with a strength her bony arms shouldn't possess. "Hold still, or I'll let the hound have your tail first," she hissed, pressing a clawed thumb between my eyes. The world swam. My limbs locked as if frozen mid-leap, muscles screaming against invisible binds. Above me, the sky was a sickly green, the same color as the witch's fire when she brewed her curses.

They started chanting then, low and guttural, a sound that made my scales prickle. Someone tossed a handful of crushed bone into the air; it stung my nostrils, sharp as piss on hot iron. The witch leaned down, her breath reeking of rotten eggs and myrrh. "Don't fret, hatchling," she crooned, her cracked lips splitting into a grin. "The old gods like their sacrifices... lively." Her fingers trailed down my belly, and the meaning of her words hit me like a kicked rock. The hound whined again, but this time, it sounded hungry.

Her chant shifted, syllables twisting into something that slithered between my ribs. My vision doubled, no, tripled, and suddenly I wasn't just *on* the stone. I was above it, watching my own body writhe, my muzzle stretched in a silent scream. The witch's claws sank into my chest, but there was no blood, only a shimmering thread of silver, coiled around her wrist like a fishing line. She yanked. My soul came loose with a sound like tearing parchment.

From this new, floating vantage, I saw everything. The way my body jerked when she drove her dagger into its heart, the way the blood didn't spurt but oozed, thick and black as tar. The villagers didn't wince. Grikk the tanner licked his lips. Someone had already started a fire.

They ate me methodically, stripping scales first, "crispy," someone muttered, then the meat beneath. Mother Yiss sucked the marrow from my finger bones with wet, smacking noises. The witch kept my skull, whispering into the hollows where my eyes had been. My soul, tethered to her by that glistening thread, could only watch as they smeared my fat on their faces like war paint, as they passed my liver around like a wineskin.

The thread tugged. The witch was walking now, my soul bobbing behind her like a child's kite. Behind us, the feast roared on, drunken and laughing. Ahead, the marsh waited, humming with a sound I'd never heard before. Not frogs. Not insects. Something older. Something that recognized the taste of kobold souls.

Her hut stank of dried herbs and wet fur, the rafters hung with charms made of teeth and feathers. She tied my thread to a tiny green gem dangling from the ceiling, then spat on it. The world twisted, and suddenly I was *inside* the gem, the hut distorted through its faceted edges. "Better view," she cackled. Her hands worked at her belt, and for a horrified moment I thought, but no. She pulled out a fat, blind mole rat, its pink nose twitching. The rest of the night was wet, animal sounds and her occasional grunts. I tried to close eyes I no longer had.

Days blurred. Sometimes she'd pluck the gem from its string, murmuring spells that made my essence burn like cheap oil in a lamp. Once, she rubbed me along the belly of a pregnant goat while chanting. The kid came out wrong, too many teeth, but the village praised her magic. After each use, I'd fade, my vision dimming until the world was a smudge of shadows. Then, slowly, I'd wake again, trapped in the same green prison.

Years crawled by. The hut's walls sagged. The witch's hair thinned to greasy wisps. I watched her teach a scrawny apprentice, a human girl with bitten nails. They argued over how much mercury to add to a love potion. The apprentice poisoned her by midsummer. Stupid. Predictable. The new witch, barely sixteen, never noticed the green gem among the old one's trinkets. She hung it over her own bed, next to a stolen cavalry banner.

Decades. The village burned twice. Raiders came and went. The witches changed. The gem stayed. I learned their secrets, which ones fucked sheep, which ones cried after curses. One, a half-orc with a lazy eye, used me to scry on her rivals. She'd stroke the gem absently while whispering, and I'd feel her hunger like a hook in my ribs. Afterward, I'd sleep for weeks.

Then the raid. Not the usual drunken thugs, these were armored, organized. The witch screamed as they dragged her out by her braids. A scaled hand, claws chipped from battle, snatched me from the collapsing rafters. A kobold, but taller than any I'd known, her eyes slitted gold like coins in the dark. She tossed me to a companion, a broad-shouldered human with a nose broken three times. "Pretty bauble," he grunted, biting my chain between his teeth to test the metal.

They traded me for salt in a smoke-choked caravan town. The merchant's wife wore me around her neck while she bedded her husband's guards, my facets smeared with sweat and seed. When plague took her, I was tossed into a strongbox with her other jewels, forgotten until a tax collector cracked the lock. He sold me to a tanner's daughter for a night in her bed, and she, in turn, lost me in a dice game to a one-eyed minstrel who sang off-key ballads about dead kings.

Centuries folded like old parchment. Cities rose and crumbled. I passed through thieves' markets strapped to the wrists of corpse-robbers, dangled from the belts of mercenaries who pissed on their dead. Once, a scholar pried me from a mummy's grip, his fingers trembling as he muttered about "pre-Dusk artifacts." He died feverish, babbling of green fire, and his apprentice pawned me for drinking money.

The hermit found me in a tin box at the bottom of a rain barrel, left there by some fleeing bandit. He was a wiry thing, all elbows and knees, his beard knotted with clay and old bread. "Ah," he breathed, holding me up to the weak sun slanting through his rotten roof. "A spirit-cage." His cabin stank of mold and the greasy smoke of his endless little fires. He talked to me as if I could answer, rambling about omens in crow tracks, about the "true names" of storms. At night, he'd press me to his sunken chest, his heartbeat thumping through the gem like a moth in a jar.

One morning, he didn't stir. The crows came first, then the rats. My chain snapped when his collarbone gave way. I rolled into the damp earth beneath his floorboards, and there I stayed, listening to the slow chew of worms, to the hiss of roots threading through his ribs. Above, the world moved on. Below, the earth whispered secrets only the dead should know.

Seasons passed in patches of light and dark. The shack collapsed inward, timbers groaning like tired beasts. Rain pooled in my hollow, freezing and thawing until the gem's chain rusted to red dust. I dreamed of the marsh, of the taste of my own liver on Grikk's tongue, but even memories fade when buried deep enough.

Then, the digging. Claws scraped wood, then soil, frantic and eager. A wet black nose, quivering, shoved into my hollow. The direwolf's breath was hot as forge smoke, its yellowed teeth flecked with old blood. It snorted, pawing deeper, not for me, but for the fat rabbit cowering beside my gem. The rabbit squealed once before teeth crushed its spine. In the frenzy, a massive tongue lapped me up with a mouthful of dirt, the world tilting violently as the wolf tossed its head back to swallow.

Darkness. Heat. The rhythmic pulse of the wolf's gullet squeezing me downward. I tumbled into its stomach with the half-chewed rabbit, acid searing my facets dull. The wolf didn't notice; its belly was full of better meat. It loped back to the den, where pups yipped and gnawed on a deer's shattered femur.

Seasons turned inside that fleshy prison. I watched through the gem's fading glow as the wolf gorged on spring fawns, summer hares, autumn's fattened boars. Its belly became my world, the churn of digestion, the groan of full guts, the stench of meat dissolving into slurry. Sometimes, when the wolf slept, I'd press against its ribs from within, imagining I could feel moonlight through layers of muscle and fur.

Then came the rut. The stink of musk thickened the air, male wolves snarling outside the den. My host, no, my *home* now, growled low when the alpha mounted her, her claws scoring the earth as he bit her scruff. The gem rattled inside her with each thrust, wedged between loops of intestine.

When the male finished, his seed flooding her, something in the gem *cracked*. A fissure spiderwebbed across my prison, green light bleeding through. The wolf yelped as if bitten, bucking violently. Then, shattering. Not just the gem, but *me*, fracturing into a thousand shimmering shards that swirled downward with the hot spill of seed.

I expected oblivion. Instead, a different darkness swallowed me, thick and wetter than the gut had been. The wolf's womb pulsed around my scattered essence, drawing the shards together like beads of quicksilver. Around me, other lights flickered, new eggs, fresh life kindling in the heat. I felt myself drawn to one, my fractured soul stitching itself into its soft membrane as the wolf panted above us, licking her mate's jaw.

When the last shard settled, I *was* the egg. Not watching it, not trapped against it, but woven through its yolk and sinew. The wolf's heartbeat above us was a drum, her breath the wind that would shape our lungs. Her belly, which had been my tomb, was now my cradle. The irony tasted bitter, even without a tongue to taste it.

The others pulsed around me, three, no, four, their warmth pressing close in the dark. They had no memories, no stolen glimpses of witchfires or corpse-thieves. But I did. I remembered the way fear smelled when a knife pressed into flesh, the sound of bones cracking between teeth. My siblings wriggled, blind and dreaming, while I lay still, stitching ancient horrors into my marrow.

Time passed in stretches of heat and hunger. The wolf gorged herself, her ribs swelling with milk and meat, her teats dripping as we nursed. My jaws were the first to find them, my teeth the sharpest when they cut through gum. The others whined; I *remembered*. The taste of my own liver. The witch's breath. I bit harder, gulping the wolf's milk like it could scald the memories away.

Black fur sprouted from my skin, thick as marsh reeds, sleek as spilled ink. I grew faster than my siblings, my paws broader, my howl deeper. The alpha male snapped at me once, testing, but I bared teeth still stained with yolk. He backed down, tail flicking. The pack smelled the difference, not just direwolf, but something older, something that had been swallowed whole and spat back half-digested.

I remembered my kobold parents too, their scales dull from tannery work, their claws worn from digging. They'd been dead before the sacrifice, crushed under a wagon hauling witchroot. The wolves didn't mourn. They ate, slept, fucked, killed without guilt. In their simplicity, I found a kind of peace. No prophecies. No altars. Just the hunt, the feed, the den.

My first heat came with the thaw, a gnawing fire in my belly that made me pace the territory’s edge, scraping bark from trees with restless claws. The alpha caught my scent first, his nostrils flaring as he circled me, testing. I let him mount me against a lightning-struck oak, the rough bark scraping my belly as he bit my scruff. Pain and pleasure tangled like roots in marsh mud, but I didn’t fight it. When his seed took, I howled, not in submission, but in triumph.

The pups came under a fat summer moon, squirming bundles of fur and hunger. Four survived. Two carried my old, stolen knowledge in their marrow, their eyes too sharp, their howls too precise. They left before winter, as wolf-blood demanded. The pack watched them go without whines. Only I caught the way one turned east, toward the drowned marsh where my bones had once been spat onto blackened reeds.

Hunters trespassed often, their stinking hides wrapped in boiled leather, their blades dull with rust. The pack tore them apart, but I lingered over the bodies, pressing my muzzle to their throats to catch final words. It didn’t matter if they begged in Common or snarled in Orcish, I understood. One, a red-haired woman with a pox-scarred face, gasped a prayer to some dead god. I licked the blood from her lips before the alpha snapped her spine.

Seasons turned. Litters came and went. The alpha’s muzzle greyed, his haunches sagging. I stayed sleek, my fangs white as birch bark, my howl echoing farther than any wolf’s should. The pack noticed. They always did.

By the seventh winter, I was the only one who remembered the marsh stink of witchfire, the way Grikk’s teeth had crunched my finger bones. The others aged. I didn’t. The pups I bore grew old and died. I remained, curled in the den’s deepest shadows, tasting the wind for the scent of rotten eggs and myrrh.

The hunters came on a morning crusted with frost, not the drunken poachers we tore apart for sport, but silent shapes in oiled leather, their crossbows strung with gut that didn’t creak. The alpha caught the first bolt in his throat. The pack surged, but the hunters had dug pits lined with sharpened stakes, their horses trained not to flinch at wolf screams. I took three down before a weighted net pinned me, the lead pellets sewn into its edges cracking my ribs like brittle kindling.

They skinned us alive. I felt every strip of flesh peeled from muscle, every tendon severed with their bone knives. But I didn’t scream. I remembered the gem. The way silence made witches careless. My pelt, black as a starless night and twice as large as any direwolf’s, brought a murmur of awe even from the stone-faced huntmaster. They tossed the rest of me into the swamp, my flayed carcass splashing into water thick with algae and the floating bones of things that had drowned screaming.

The swamp took its time. Leeches swarmed my exposed meat, their bodies swelling to bursting before dropping away, bloated. Fish nibbled my ligaments clean, their bellies glowing faintly green afterward. Seasons cycled above me, the water turning from icy sludge to summer’s warm soup, then back again. Years, maybe. The silt settled over my bones like a shroud.

Then, one night when the frogs fell silent mid-croak, I *itched*. Not with nerves I no longer had, but with something deeper, older. The black ooze from the witch’s boundary all those lifetimes ago seeped up through the swamp bed, threading through my ribs, knitting flesh back to bone with the patience of a spider weaving its web. My claws found purchase in the muck. I rose, water sluicing off fur that hadn’t been there before, muscles rippling under skin unmarked by knives. The swamp’s creatures fled. My reflection stared back from still water, a direwolf’s body, yes, but the eyes… The yellow was gone. In its place, a quiet, hungry green, the same shade as the gem that had held me, the same glow as the witch’s fire the night she ate my liver.

I shook the water from my pelt. Somewhere, a village was dreaming of safety. Somewhere, a witch was whispering to a skull. The marsh knew my scent again. And this time, I was the thing that slithered in the dark.

Discarded bolts littered the mud where the hunters had stood, snapped shafts slick with moss and rust. Their scent lingered, oil, sweat, the acrid bite of fear-soured leather. Beneath it all, something else: tannin and rotten herbs, the stink of a village that had forgotten its debts. My claws sank into the earth, the same earth that had swallowed my bones centuries ago.

This wasn't the marsh of my sacrifice, that lay east, drowned under poisoned waters, but the wind carried whispers of a settlement northwest, nestled between blighted oaks. The details fit like a knife in a sheath: bark-roofed huts, a stone altar newer than mine but just as stained, the reek of frightened livestock. A different village, different witches, same hunger. My pack's killers had smelled of it too, their blades honed on the whetstone of stolen prayers.

I loped through the trees, my strides eating the miles, the forest bending away from my passage. The trees here remembered. Their roots had tasted the blood of my kin, kobold slaves dragged screaming to freshen some warlord's foundations. The memory curled like smoke behind my ribs, igniting the ember of that old, borrowed rage. My skull still rested in the witch's hut back east, hollow eyes watching generations of fools repeat the same mistakes. Tonight, I'd teach them a new one.

Dusk painted the village in shades of rot, goldenrod fading to corpse-gray. I crouched in a thicket of witchroot, its leaves shivering against my flank. Below, the villagers moved like ants on spoiled meat: women hauling water from a well too deep for kobold arms, men oiling blades already slick with something darker than rust. The tanner's yard reeked of fear and urine, hides stretched too tight over frames. My ears twitched at the rhythmic thump of flesh on flesh from a nearby hut, some guard taking his payment in sweat and whimpers. Familiar. Predictable. The rhythm of oppression never changed, only the hands wielding the whip.

The witch emerged as the last light bled from the sky, her silhouette hunched like a vulture on a branch. Younger than mine had been, but the same crooked fingers plucking at the air as if testing the wind for weakness. She paused by the altar, running a palm over its surface, her lips moving in silent communion with whatever filth she called divine. Behind her, a kobold child scuttled with an armload of kindling, scales dull with malnutrition. The witch didn't glance down when she kicked the pup's legs out from under him. The firewood scattered. Laughter rippled through the watching guards.

Midnight. The torches guttered, drunkards slumped against fence posts, their snores syncopated with the chirp of marsh frogs. The witch's hut exhaled a thin plume of smoke, the scent of burnt myrrh threading through the stench of livestock. My muscles coiled. The boundary stones here were newer, their carvings crude imitations of the old ways, but magic hummed in them all the same. I'd crossed one line unwillingly. Tonight, I'd carve my own. The first scream would taste like justice. The last would taste like home.

Silent as a shadow cast by dying embers, I slunk past the watchman slumped over his spear, his breath reeking of sour ale. A hound chained near the tannery stiffened, nostrils flaring. Its whimper died in its throat when my gaze locked onto its trembling flanks, some primal instinct recognizing what its master's dull eyes could not. The boundary stone loomed ahead, its surface slick with rain and something darker, the grooves filled with generations of pressed offerings: kobold claw clippings, tufts of direwolf fur, drops of virgin's blood.

I pressed one paw against the stone. The magic recoiled, a ripple of nausea crawling up my leg like centipedes beneath the skin. Different from the marsh's curse, but similar in its hunger, this boundary wasn't meant to keep danger out, but sacrifices in. The stone's carvings shimmered faintly green where my claws scraped the surface. Behind me, a goat in its pen shuddered and collapsed, froth bubbling between its teeth. The magic recognized me. Not as wolf. Not as kobold. As something that had already died on an altar once.

A twig snapped. The watchman stirred, scratching his groin with a grunt. I melted into the lee of a tannery vat, my breath still, my heart slower than a winter stream. Above, the moon slid behind tattered clouds. The village didn't know it yet, but their witch had been wrong. The danger wasn't coming. It was already here, licking its chops in the dark.

I skirted the boundary line, my paws leaving no prints in the mud. The goat pen's gate creaked open with a nudge of my muzzle. The animal inside froze, pupils blown wide. Its terror smelled sweet, almost familiar. One snap of my jaws, and its head vanished down my throat, the bones crunching like brittle pottery. Its body slumped silently. My claws unspooled its entrails in a looping pattern, three spirals, one inverted, just as the first witch had carved into my scales before the feast. The offal steamed in the chill air, the sigil glistening under the indifferent stars.

Beyond the huts, the hunter's trail was fresh, his bootprints deep with the weight of stolen pelts. He'd pissed against an oak recently, the ammonia stink cutting through the loam. Fool. Wolves weren't the only things that tracked by scent. His path led northwest, toward a game trail matted with crushed ferns. Not fleeing. Scouting. Perhaps his village expected more wolves to skin.

The wind shifted, carrying the metallic tang of blood from his direction. Not his, something smaller, furred. A hare, maybe, gutted and left to drain. Amateur. A real hunter would've hung the carcass to keep predators from ground scent. I loped after him, my belly tight with the goat's meat, my mind tighter with memory. His village would wake to the sigil, to the headless goat, to the silence where their watchman's snores should've been. Let them whisper. Let them remember. I had a different prayer to teach tonight, and it required a congregation.

His campfire glowed ahead, a pinprick in the gloom. He'd stacked his crossbow bolts neatly by his bedroll, the steel tips polished to a dull gleam. How thoughtful. I'd return them. One by one.

The hunter woke choking on his own blood, my claws already deep in his windpipe. His hands flailed, for a knife, for prayers, for anything, but I pinned him with a paw the size of a dinner plate. His eyes rolled white in the firelight, recognition dawning too late. Not a wolf. Not just a wolf. His lips shaped a word that might've been "witch" before I raked downward, splitting him from sternum to pelvis like a gutted trout.

His intestines spilled hot over my forelegs, the stench of bile and half-digested hare thick in the night air. He gurgled, twitching, his fingers scrabbling at the dirt as if digging his own grave. Pathetic. I remembered the way the first witch had gathered my soul like a spinner pulling silk, her fingers slick with my liver's grease. His soul shimmered above the ruin of his ribs, a fraying silver thread. I exhaled a breath that wasn't quite breath, more marsh gas and old magic, and his essence coiled like smoke around my muzzle.

The swamp would have him. Not the peaceful dark of natural decay, but the hungry ooze that had rebuilt me bone by bone. I blew the captured soul eastward, watching it twist through the trees like a wounded sparrow. By dawn, it would be screaming in the black water with the others.

His hide peeled away easily, the muscle beneath still twitching. I worked with the precision of a creature who'd watched a hundred skinning rituals, folding the pelt flesh-side up to keep the blood from staining the fur. His carcass I strung from an oak branch by the ankles, the same way he'd hung his wolf kills. The entrails dangled artfully, just so. Let his village see how pretty he looked in death.

Beneath the body, I arranged his own crossbow bolts in careful lines. Four pointing inward. One pointing east. The message was crude, but the witches would understand. They always did.

His hide I left at the boundary stone, neatly folded beneath the carved grooves where kobold claws had once bled. A gift. A promise. The first of many.

Morning came damp and reluctant, the sun straining through marsh mist as I crouched in the shadow of a blighted oak. The village stirred slow as a wounded beast, first the slaves, their scaled backs bent under water buckets, then the guards yawning into their fists. The hunter’s absence went unnoticed until a boy stumbled upon his stripped corpse swinging like a grisly pendulum. The scream that followed was satisfyingly shrill.

The witch emerged last, her robes clinging to bony hips, her fingers crusted with old blood under the nails. She didn’t gasp at the mutilated body, didn’t flinch at the stench of opened bowels. Her nostrils flared once, testing the air like a snake tasting venom, before her gaze snapped directly toward my hiding place. Green eyes met green. Recognition crackled between us, an unspoken understanding older than altars.

She moved first, murmuring to the trembling guards as she traced the pattern of spilled entrails with her toe. A summoning circle, inverted. Clever girl. Her lips curled as she snapped orders, double the watch, burn sage at the boundaries, bring the youngest kobold to her hut at dusk. The last command slithered through my teeth like a live eel. Some traditions never died.

I watched her all day, this new witch with old tricks. She painted wards in goat’s blood on doorframes, tucked charms of knotted hair into the thatch. Once, alone in her hut, she pressed a gnarled hand to her own chest where a green gem should’ve hung, whispering words that made my hackles rise. Not fear. Anticipation.

By sunset, the village stank of burned herbs and unwashed fear. The witch stood at the altar, her fingers twitching toward a knife sheathed in her sash. Behind her, a kobold child trembled, his claws chipped from digging witchroot. The resemblance to my own stolen flesh was uncanny.

I let the shadows swallow me whole. Tonight, she would learn the difference between sacrifice and slaughter.

The witch’s knife glinted in the torchlight, her incantation a hollow mimicry of the old tongue. She thought herself cunning, dangling the pup before the villagers as proof of their “protection.” But I had watched centuries of witches ply their trade, knew the way their fingers trembled just before the blade bit, how their throats bobbed when they swallowed the first metallic taste of fear. Performance. All of it. The altar, the chants, the way she cupped the kobold’s chin like a doting grandmother before positioning him over the stone.

Her mistake was in the details.

She’d used goat’s blood for the wards instead of virgin’s. Hadn’t noticed the third spiral in her summoning circle was lopsided. And oh, how she’d forgotten the oldest rule: never let your sacrifice see you sweat. The pup whimpered, his claws scraping the altar as she raised the blade. Behind them, the villagers’ breath hitched in unison. Perfect.

I struck as the knife descended.

My jaws clamped her forearm just above the wrist, teeth shearing through flesh and bone with the wet crunch of snapping kindling. The blade clattered harmlessly against the altar as her severed hand flopped onto the pup's chest, fingers still twitching. The witch gaped at the bloody stump, her scream curdling into a guttural choke as the kobold child scrambled away, wide-eyed.

Behind her, my glowing eyes reflected in the horrified faces of the villagers. They saw me now, not a wolf, but the marsh's vengeance given form. The witch staggered back, her remaining hand fumbling for the gem at her throat. Too late. My claws raked upward from the shadows, splitting her spine at the waist with a sound like a rotten melon bursting. Her torso toppled forward, intestines spilling over the altar in a steaming heap. Her legs remained standing for a breathless second before crumpling.

Silence. Then chaos.

Villagers scattered like roaches from a kicked nest. The pup froze, his nostrils flaring at the stench of witch-blood soaking into the stone, thicker than human blood, darker than kobold, reeking of spoiled milk and burnt hair. I nudged the child aside with my muzzle before stepping onto the altar. The witch's upper half still breathed, her lips working soundlessly as greenish froth bubbled from her mouth. Her fingers scrabbled at the gem around her neck, smearing the carving with her own bile.

I lunged, my jaws closing around the gem with a crack like breaking ice. Emerald shards bit my tongue as the trapped soul inside burst free, some ancient kobold ancestor, his essence writhing midair before dissolving into mist. The witch shrieked, her severed spine arching off the altar as her magic unraveled.

Her head snapped back violently as my second bite found purchase, teeth sinking into the meat behind her jaw. I wrenched upward, vertebrae popping like overripe fruit until her skull tore free with a wet rip, trailing a glistening rope of spinal cord. Her body convulsed once before slumping into the gore.

Above the corpse, her soul writhed like a hooked eel, formless yet shrieking silently. The village boundary stones groaned, their carvings splitting as the magic binding them inverted inward. I exhaled a breath thick with marsh-rot and watched the witch’s essence coagulate into a new gem, smaller than mine had been, murky as swamp water. It landed in the pulp of her ruined throat with a dull thud.

I crushed it beneath my paw.

Shards embedded themselves in my pads, humming with trapped fury. Good. Let her scream inside my flesh as I had inside hers. The kobold child hadn’t fled, smart pup, his nostrils flaring at the stench of ruptured spells. I nudged the altar’s edge with my muzzle until it tipped, spilling witch-guts across the trampled earth in a glistening landslide. The message was clear: altars break. Traditions rot.

Dawn found me nose-deep in a hunter’s ribcage, my claws working free the last stubborn scraps of lung tissue. His crossbow lay splintered beside him, the stock chewed to pulp. Six huts away, his brother’s entrails decorated a tannery vat in precise loops, the pattern matching the scars on my spirit. They’d hunted my pack with these same bolted arrows, skinned my mate while she still breathed. Now their pelts would hang from the same trees.

By midday, twelve corpses swayed from the blighted oaks, each dressed as the first, guts cascading like macabre garlands, bolts arranged beneath in accusatory lines. The village stank of voided bowels and copper, but no one screamed anymore. They’d learned. The surviving hunters cowered in a root cellar, their sweat sour with the realization that wolves weren’t the only things that remembered.

I saved the alpha for last.

He’d been clever, hiding beneath the witch’s hut where the stench of her herbs masked his fear. But the floorboards groaned under his weight, and oh, how sweetly the green gem shards in my paws sang when they scented his terror. When I dragged him out by his ankle, he fought like a stag in a snare, his blade scoring my flank once before I pinned him to the tannery’s blood-caked table.

The flaying knife was still sharp.

It whispered through the alpha’s hide with the same practiced ease he’d once used on my pack, peeling muscle from bone in long, trembling strips. He screamed until his vocal cords frayed, until his lungs collapsed like punctured bl skins. I left his face intact, eyes wide, mouth gaping, so the witches in their next life would recognize him when his soul joined theirs in the dark.

The hut’s floorboards splintered under my claws, revealing hollows stuffed with green glass bottles and cracked clay jars. Inside each, a soul pulsed like a trapped firefly, their dim glow casting sickly shadows on the witchroot dangling overhead. The newest gem held the alpha’s essence, murky with his terror, already clouding at the edges as the swamp’s influence seeped in.

I crushed them all between my teeth.
