The morning sun filtered through the leaded glass windows of Isalias Manor, casting fractured patterns across the polished oak floors. Isalia stretched lazily on one of the plush divans in the reception area, her blue-scaled limbs catching the light like polished lapis. The scent of freshly brewed chicory coffee and the distant clatter of hooves in the courtyard mingled with the crisp autumn air drifting through an open window. She exhaled, watching her breath curl visibly for just a moment before dissipating, a small, private amusement from her draconic heritage.

Across the room, the receptionist, a young human woman named Liora, sorted through a stack of parchment with practiced efficiency. "Another request from House Kaelen," she murmured, tapping the edge of the paper against the desk to straighten it. "Their usual discreet phrasing, but it’s definitely another transformation inquiry. Third one this month." Isalia chuckled, the sound low and rich. "At this rate, we’ll need to expand the east wing before winter," she mused, rolling onto her side to better face Liora. The divan groaned slightly under her weight; kobolds were compact, but Isalia carried herself with the density of someone who’d spent decades in full plate armor.

Liora glanced up, her quill pausing mid-stroke. "You’re in a good mood today," she observed, one eyebrow arching. Isalia grinned, flashing sharp teeth. "Why wouldn’t I be? The stables are full, the ledger’s balanced, and Count Valtor hasn’t sent any threatening letters in at least a fortnight." She plucked a sugar-dusted pastry from a tray on the side table, popping it into her mouth whole. "Small victories," she added around the crumbs.

Outside, the rhythmic *clop-clop* of hooves approached the main entrance, followed by the creak of the heavy door. Isalia turned and with a quiet sigh got to her feet as the visitor approached. wouldnt do to be lazing about when a client walked in, after all.

The newcomer was a tall woman wrapped in a heavy woolen cloak, her face hidden beneath the deep hood. She moved with the deliberate grace of someone accustomed to fine silks but trying very hard not to show it. The scent of lavender and something sharper, fear, perhaps?, wafted in with her. Isalia’s nostrils flared imperceptibly.

"Welcome," she said smoothly, brushing pastry crumbs from her scales. "You’ve come to Isalias Manor. How may we serve you?"

The woman hesitated, fingers tightening around the edges of her cloak. When she spoke, her voice was softer than expected, almost wavering. "I, I was told you could… help. With… changes."

Isalia tilted her head, studying the way the woman’s throat worked as she swallowed. "Changes of all kinds are our specialty," she said, gesturing toward one of the plush chairs near the fire. "Would you care for tea while we discuss particulars?"

The woman didn’t move. "I need to be certain of discretion."

A slow smile spread across Isalia’s face. "My dear, if discretion were a currency, this estate would be drowning in gold."

She extended a clawed hand toward the hallway. "Come with me to my office. We can talk there." The woman hesitated only a breath before following, her boots whispering against the floorboards.

The office smelled of aged parchment and the faint metallic tang of enchanted ink. Isalia closed the door behind them with a soft click, then traced a claw along the doorframe. The wards flared to life with a hum, threads of blue light weaving through the woodgrain before fading. "There," she said, turning to face her guest. "Now we’re as private as a confessional." She gestured to the chair opposite her desk, its velvet cushions worn smooth by decades of nervous clients.

The woman finally lowered her hood, revealing delicate features framed by honey-blonde hair. Her fingers trembled slightly as she untied her cloak. "They call you the Alchemist of Flesh," she murmured, folding the garment over her lap with excessive care. "I need, " Her voice cracked.

Isalia poured two cups of steaming jasmine tea from the ever-warm pot on her desk. "Start with what you *want*," she said, sliding one cup across the polished oak. "Not what you need. Desires are far more interesting."

The woman’s knuckles whitened around the teacup. "I want to disappear."

A beat of silence. Then Isalia leaned back, her tail curling around the chair leg. "You’re Baroness Elfriede von Aldenberg’s youngest, aren’t you?" At the woman’s sharp inhale, she chuckled. "Your mother commissioned a fertility potion from us last winter. You’ve got her cheekbones."

The baroness’ daughter, *Mira*, Isalia remembered now, swallowed hard. "The marriage contract’s signed. To Count Valtor’s nephew."

Ah. That explained the fear-scent. Isalia swirled her tea thoughtfully. "And you’d prefer to become someone, or something, else entirely."

Mira’s eyes glistened. "Before the wedding night."

Isalia considered shrewdly, claws tapping the teacup’s porcelain edge. "I heard mention of the betrothal," she murmured. "The Count’s nephew... Tell me about him. Why?"

Mira’s shoulders tensed. She stared into her tea as if divining answers from the leaves. "He collects things," she whispered. "Things that don’t belong to him. My sister, " Her voice broke.

Ah. Isalia exhaled through her nostrils, the scent of Mira’s distress sharpening. The nephew had a reputation, then. Not just a political match, but a predator’s trophy hunt. She set her cup down with deliberate softness.

The fire crackled. Somewhere beyond the warded door, a stable hand laughed. The ordinary sounds of the manor pressed against the silence between them.

Mira lifted her chin. "I won’t be collected."

Isalia traced a claw along the rim of her teacup, considering the tremor in the young noblewoman’s voice, not just fear, but fury simmering beneath. She nodded once. "Understandable. So what do you intend to do about it?"

The baroness’ daughter drew a slow breath through her nose, fingers tightening around her own cup. "I’ve studied your contracts. The transformations are permanent unless reversed by your hand." Mira’s teacup rattled as she set it down. "Something strong," she whispered. "Something that could run forever."

A stallion’s distant whinny echoed from the stables, as if summoned by the words. Isalia’s tail twitched. "We’ve had clients choose equine forms before," she mused. "Though usually for... less tragic reasons." Her claws tapped parchment. "You’d need stamina to outpace his hounds. A mare’s build would serve, but, "

"No." Mira’s interruption was sharp as a blade. "Not a mare."

The kobold’s nostrils flared. Ah. Of course. She inclined her head. "A stallion, then. You’d trade softness for strength."

Mira’s jaw set. "I’d trade anything."

Isalia studied her, the firelight casting shifting patterns across her scales. "Becoming a stallion won’t erase your past," she said quietly. "Only reshape your future. Have you considered, what do you intend to do afterwards? Once you're a stallion?"

"Far from here. Across borders, through forests, anywhere his hounds can’t follow." Her gaze flicked toward the window, where the distant pastures stretched beyond the manor walls. Isalia’s tail twitched thoughtfully against the chair leg. "A wild stallion is a rare thing," she mused, claws tapping the desk. "Wherever you go, you’d draw attention. Mira reached for the quill, then hesitated. Isalia’s clawed hand closed over hers, stopping her mid-motion. The kobold’s grip was surprisingly warm. The fire popped. Somewhere beyond the door, a stableboy whistled as he mucked out stalls. Mira inhaled the scent of leather and ink, feeling the weight of the quill between her fingers. She thought of silk gloves traded for iron shoes, of her mother’s tear-streaked face the morning the betrothal was announced.

Isalia’s tail twitched. "That’s your choice. Something simple for the stables, Storm, perhaps, or Ash. Viktor’s steward will register you under whatever you pick." She leaned back, claws laced over her scaled abdomen. "But once the ink dries, Baroness Mira von Aldenberg ceases to exist in any ledger but ours."

Mira’s thumb brushed the quill’s feather. Storm suited her, wild, untamed, something that couldn’t be caged. She dipped the nib into the inkwell, watching the black liquid cling to the metal. The first stroke of her new name flowed across the parchment, bold and unhesitating.

Isalia watched the signature take shape, her expression unreadable. When Mira set the quill down, the kobold lifted the parchment, blowing gently to dry the ink.
