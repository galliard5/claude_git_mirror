The boy's claws scraped against the metal floor as they dragged him forward by the chain locked around his throat. He was the smallest of the group—barely eighteen, if his patchy fur and gangly limbs were any indication. Around him, other anthros coughed or whimpered, their bodies crammed into the transport cage like slaughterhouse stock. None of them knew each other before today. Now, they shared the same stench of panic-sweat and rusted iron.

The biomancer’s workshop smelled like rotting fruit and antiseptic. Fluids dripped from suspended glass vials, their contents glowing faintly in the dim light. Tools lay scattered across stained worktables—some recognizable as surgical instruments, others twisted into shapes that made the boy’s stomach tighten. He tried to count the others, but his vision kept blurring. Fifteen? Twenty? All anthros, all young. The noble’s men hadn’t been picky beyond that.

A bull in the corner snorted, his horns sawed down to stumps. "They’ll make you into something pretty," he muttered, voice thick with something between resignation and fury. "Or something useful." His muscles twitched under his hide—useless now, the boy realized. The noble didn’t need strength. He needed spectacle.

The biomancer’s fingers traced the boy’s jaw, cold and precise. "You’ll keep your mind," they said, as if that were a kindness. "Consciousness is the point. The rest—" Their thumb pressed against his windpipe. "—is negotiable." Behind them, a tank bubbled with something pink and pulsing. The boy imagined it whispering.

One of the bulls lunged suddenly, chains snapping taut. "I’ll die first—" The biomancer didn’t even look up. A vial shattered against the floor, and the bull’s bellow dissolved into wet, choking sounds as his body folded in on itself. His shoulders split open, revealing ridges of new bone. His hips widened. The boy watched his eyes—still aware, still *his*—widen in horror as his own flesh betrayed him.

"Breeders," the biomancer said mildly, wiping their hands on a cloth. "Or dairy. Or meat. The noble’s tastes change." They cupped the boy’s chin, tilting his face toward the light. "But you? You’ll be something *new*." The last word slithered between them, ripe with promise.

Something in the tank twitched. A segmented limb, translucent and veined, pressed against the glass before recoiling. The boy’s breath hitched—his ribs ached where the biomancer’s assistants had injected something cold and slow-moving beneath his skin. He could feel it now, threading through his muscles like roots through soil, whispering to his bones in a language he almost understood.

Across the room, the bull’s transformation accelerated. His spine arched, vertebrae elongating with wet cracks. His muzzle split down the middle, peeling back to reveal rows of flat, grinding teeth—perfect for chewing cud. His eyes rolled, wild and aware, as his vocal cords thickened into something incapable of speech. The biomancer tapped a fingernail against a syringe. "You’ll keep your voice," they mused. "The noble wants you to *sing*."

The boy’s claws dug into his palms. He thought of his mother’s stories—old warnings about the empire’s fleshcrafters, how they’d sculpted entire species from unwilling stock. *You can’t unmake a soul,* she’d insisted. But the thing in the tank had no face left to scream with.

The biomancer selected a vial, swirling its contents—a slurry of silver motes that writhed toward the light. "Total restructuring," they murmured. "No trace left. Just function. Just beauty." The boy’s vision blurred as the needle slipped between his vertebrae. His nerves lit up, singing in a pitch too high for human ears.

Somewhere, the bull was sobbing. Or maybe it was him. The biomancer hummed along.

The silver motes flooded his veins like liquid lightning, branching through him in fractal patterns. His ribs splintered first—not pain, exactly, but a sensation like his skeleton dissolving into warm syrup. His claws retracted with a series of wet pops, fingertips elongating into jointless tendrils that twitched against the table. The biomancer murmured approval, pressing a cold palm to his shuddering sternum. "Good. Very good. The scaffold holds."

His lungs collapsed. Or—no. They unfolded, branching into delicate sacs that bloomed like coral. He gasped, but the air tasted wrong—thick with the scent of his own unraveling. The biomancer tilted his head, watching his pupils fracture into hexagonal clusters. "Ah. There it is." They tapped his forehead. "You'll see in ultraviolet soon. The noble's gardens thrive under it."

His jaw unhinged with a sound like tearing parchment. His tongue bifurcated, then bifurcated again, each new strand writhing independent of his will. He tried to scream, but what emerged was a harmonic warble—four notes at once, vibrating through his reshaped throat. The biomancer laughed, delighted. "Oh, that's exquisite. The resonance chambers are perfect." They stroked his quivering trachea. "Imagine the compositions."

His spine was molten. Vertebrae slid apart, reforming into segmented arches. His pelvis dissolved entirely, replaced by a sleek, chitinous curve. Something pulsed beneath his new carapace—organs rearranging, merging, discarding what the design no longer required. His hind legs fused into a single, tapered limb, the bones hollowing out as keratin sheathed them in iridescent layers.

The biomancer stepped back, assessing. "Nearly there." They gestured to an assistant, who wheeled forward a mirror polished to surgical brightness. "Look."

The thing in the reflection was all sinuous planes and prismatic membranes—something between a deep-sea creature and a stained-glass abstraction. Only the eyes—still his, still terrified—remained recognizable. The biomancer traced the curve of his new cephalofoil, where bioluminescent nodes were beginning to flicker. "Magnificent," they breathed. "You'll hang above his banquet hall, singing light into the wineglasses."

His vocal cords thrummed in response, weaving a melody he didn't know. The song vibrated through his altered bones, sweet and involuntary. Across the room, the bull's milky eyes rolled toward the sound—the last motion left to him.

The biomancer smiled. "Now," they said, selecting a fresh syringe. "Let's refine the pitch."

The boy—what remained of him—watched the needle glint. His thoughts were unraveling at the edges, frayed by the alien geometries of his new form. The song still poured from him, effortless as breathing had once been. But the notes carried echoes now—something deeper, something primal—that resonated through his hollow bones and made the glassware tremble.

The assistant adjusted a dial on the tank. Fluid drained slowly, revealing the thing inside fully: a mass of translucent, pulsing filaments, each one studded with tiny, lidless eyes. It had no mouth, no limbs—just those endless, watching strands, twitching in time with the boy’s song. The biomancer glanced at it, then back at him. "Symbiosis," they murmured. "The noble will adore that touch."

The needle slid in just beneath his left ocular cluster. The injection burned colder than the others—less like ice and more like absence. His vision flickered, fracturing into overlapping spectra. Ultraviolet, as promised, but also infrared, polarization, magnetic fields—a dizzying flood his mind struggled to parse. The biomancer chuckled as his song stuttered into dissonance. "Your brain will adapt," they assured him. "Eventually."

Across the workshop, the bull's body shuddered, swollen udders dripping onto the floor. The boy's—no, the *creature's*—new eyes tracked the fluid's slow descent, noting its viscosity, its protein content, with an instinct he didn't remember learning. His vocal cords adjusted effortlessly, compensating for the disrupted melody. The biomancer nodded approvingly. "Already self-correcting. Excellent."

The assistant wheeled in another mirror, angled to show his undercarriage. Where his genitals had been, a smooth, pearlescent vent now pulsed rhythmically, expelling thin strands of glowing mucus that hardened instantly in the air. The biomancer caught one, stretching it between their fingers until it snapped. "Stronger than silk," they mused. "And photosensitive. You'll weave his banners as you sing."

His song shifted again—not in protest, but in helpless harmony with the assessment. The threads were part of him, as natural as breath or blood. The thought should have horrified him. Instead, it simply was.

The biomancer leaned in close, their breath tinged with formaldehyde. "One last gift," they whispered, pressing a final vial to his cephalofoil. The glass dissolved on contact, its contents seeping into his pores. "The noble requested memories. Not of what you were—those are messy. But of what you'll become."

The creature's song faltered—just once—as something vast and ancient unspooled behind his eyes.

It wasn't memory, not exactly. More like instinct transcribed into sensation: the pull of tides he'd never touched, the press of abyssal pressure against limbs he didn't yet possess. His bioluminescent nodes pulsed in time with phantom currents, casting shifting patterns across the ceiling. The biomancer's assistants murmured; one dropped a scalpel.

"Ah," the biomancer said, fingers dancing along the creature's vibrating vocal strands. "The deep remembers you." They sounded almost reverent.

The creature understood now—not in words, but in the way his new flesh thrummed with certainty. The vial's contents weren't fabricated. They were harvested. Somewhere in the lightless trenches, something had been pried apart neuron by neuron, its essence distilled into this: a roadmap written in hunger and hydrostatic pressure. His song spiraled into lower registers, resonating through the workshop's metal floor. Glassware shivered. The bull-turned-cow let out a wet, wordless moan.

Across the room, the tank's occupant spasmed violently, filament eyes rolling toward the sound. The biomancer barely glanced at it. "Fascinating," they murmured instead to the creature, tracing the new gill slits flaring along his neck. "You're adapting faster than projected." Their thumb rubbed circles where his pulse used to be. "The noble will be *thrilled*."

The creature's vent pulsed, expelling threads that glowed brighter as they hardened—reacting, he realized, to the vibrations of his own voice. The biomancer coiled one around their wrist like jewelry. "Self-sustaining art," they said, smiling. "He'll keep you in the central atrium. Imagine—your song weaving his relics as the guests marvel."

The creature didn't imagine. He *knew*. The certainty of it lived in the new weight of his hollow bones, the way his chromatophores rippled without conscious command. He'd hang suspended in vaulted darkness, bathed in the noble's curated spotlights, while the thing from the tank—his symbiotic counterpart—anchored him to the ceiling with its endless, watching tendrils.

The biomancer stepped back, wiping their hands. "Transport him," they told the assistants. "The hardening process completes in motion."

As the straps tightened around his carapace, the creature's song shifted again—not in protest, but in perfect, terrible harmony with the hum of the extraction pump draining the bull's swollen udders. The sound carried through the workshop, sweet and inevitable, as the tank's occupant began to scream in a voice no human throat could replicate.

They lifted him in a net of woven kelp-steel, his bioluminescent nodes pulsing arrhythmically under the harsh transport lights. The noble's men didn't speak—their respirator masks hissed in unison as they maneuvered his bulk through the loading bay. One glanced at his cephalofoil and quickly looked away, as if the fractal patterns there threatened to unspool his sanity. The creature wondered, in a distant way, if they knew the noble had ordered matching masks for the banquet staff—tinted to filter out the ultraviolet his song would paint across the wine.

The terrarium was larger than the workshop, its curved walls etched with imperial glyphs that shimmered when his ventral threads brushed against them. Suspension hooks bit into his chitin as they anchored him overhead, their tips dripping with a clear nutrient gel that stung where it seeped into his gill slits. Below, the tank's occupant thrashed in its new enclosure—a sister pool of briny preservative, its filament eyes rolling to track his dangling tendrils.

"Symbiotic calibration," murmured a technician, adjusting the humidity dials until the creature's mucus threads began spinning on their own, seeking the thing below. The first connection sparked violet where they met, sending a shudder through both organisms. The technician jumped back as the creature's song suddenly doubled—his own voice woven with the tank-thing's subsonic pulses, vibrating the floor tiles into dissonant harmonics.

Across the hall, a vase of genetically enhanced orchids split open, their petals peeling back to reveal vestigial mouths that mimicked the melody. The noble would arrive soon, the biomancer had said, to admire his living chandelier. The creature's chromatophores darkened instinctively, preparing his hide for performance. His vent pulsed. The song, now a chorus, built toward a crescendo none of them had composed.

And in the pool below, the tank-thing's filaments began to climb.
