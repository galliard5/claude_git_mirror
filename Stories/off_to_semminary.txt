Tomas Rennic's boots scuffed against the worn oak floorboards as he hesitated just inside the manor's entrance hall. The air smelled faintly of leather and something herbal, mingling with the distant chime of harness bells from the stables beyond. He flexed his fingers against the strap of his travel pack, throat dry. The woman behind the desk hadn't looked up yet, too busy smoothing a ledger page with claws that glinted blue in the lamplight.

"Services," he blurted, then winced at his own voice cracking.

The kobold's ears twitched. She lifted her gaze with deliberate slowness, golden eyes narrowing as they traveled from Tomas's scuffed boots to his flushed face. "You're at Isalias Manor," she said. Her voice was flatter than he expected. "We have several." A claw tapped the ledger. "Would you like to browse the catalog, or are you here for something specific?"

Tomas swallowed. The pamphlet he'd stolen from his uncle's study had mentioned *transformations* in smudged ink, but the reality of standing here made his pulse thump unevenly. The hall was warmer than he'd anticipated. He could hear muffled laughter somewhere deeper in the building, the sound punctuated by a sharp, pleased whinny.

"Specific," he managed. His palms were sweating. "I, there was a price list. For, uh. Permanent alterations."

The kobold's claw stopped moving. She blinked once, slowly, like a cat assessing prey. "Ah." She reached beneath the desk without looking, producing a slim ledger bound in dark leather. "You'll want Alric, then. Though I should warn you, " Her nostrils flared slightly as she sniffed the air between them. ", he won't proceed without a full consultation. Including *why*." The last word landed with deliberate weight.

Somewhere beyond the hall, a door creaked open. The scent of crushed herbs and something metallic drifted in, followed by footsteps. A human man with gray-streaked hair and hands stained indigo paused mid-stride, glancing between them. "New client, Lyra?"

The kobold, Lyra, tilted her head toward Tomas. "Claims he wants permanence. Didn't specify what kind."

Tomas's throat tightened. The alchemist's gaze was uncomfortably perceptive, lingering on the fresh scratches along Tomas's knuckles, the way his shoulders hunched forward. "I see." Alric's voice was quieter than Lyra's, but no less measured. "Come through to the lab. We'll discuss options." He didn't wait for agreement, already turning back the way he'd come.

Lyra's claws clicked against the ledger. "You'll want to leave your pack here," she said, not unkindly. "Alric's workspace isn't kind to loose fabric."

Tomas fumbled with the straps, his fingers clumsy. The laughter from deeper in the manor had faded, replaced by the rhythmic sound of hooves on packed earth. Closer now, almost beneath his feet. He wondered if they had basements here, or if the stables extended underground. The thought sent an unexpected shiver down his spine.

Alric was waiting by an arched doorway, one stained hand resting against the frame. The corridor beyond smelled sharply of alcohol and something bitter, like burnt coffee. "This way," he said, and the shadows swallowed them both.

Tomas followed, fingers tightening around the crumpled letter in his pocket, the seminary acceptance, embossed with the sigil of Saint Verena’s. Four years of dawn prayers and late-night recitations had earned him that parchment, and his father had wept when the messenger arrived. Now the wax seal felt like a brand against his thigh with every step. The alchemist’s boots scuffed against stone as they turned into a narrower hall lined with cabinets, their glass fronts fogged with condensation. Behind them, jars of murky liquids glowed faintly in the dim light.

"You're young," Alric said abruptly. He didn’t turn around. "Most who come for permanence are older. Or running from something." A pause. "Which are you?"

The question knifed between Tomas’s ribs. He thought of the rector’s study last week, the way the man’s fingers had lingered too long on his wrist during scripture lessons. The hissed Latin verses afterward, as if prayer could scour the heat from his skin. "Neither," he lied, then swallowed hard when Alric glanced back with one raised eyebrow. The alchemist’s silence stretched until Tomas’s fingers dug into his thighs through the fabric of his trousers.

"I... want two things." The words tumbled out in a rush, lower than he intended. "I was just accepted to seminary school." His throat worked around the next confession like it was made of thorns. "And I won’t have another chance at this." A pause. Somewhere down the hall, glass clinked against metal. "I want to be transformed. But just partially." He gestured vaguely at his own body, the motion jerky. "A rabbit. On two legs. Walking, talking like a man, but... rabbit."

Alric stopped walking. The air smelled suddenly of crushed mint and something darker, like iron filings. "And the second?"

Tomas’s fingers found the letter again, crumpling it further. "I... want you to remove my parts." His voice cracked on the last word. "So I remain pure."

The alchemist exhaled through his nose. When he turned fully, his indigo-stained hands hung loosely at his sides, but his gaze was sharper than any seminary master’s. "You realize," he said slowly, "that purity isn’t a physical state."

Somewhere beyond the stone walls, a horse snorted. Tomas flinched. "It is for them," he whispered.

Alric studied him for another heartbeat before nodding toward a brass-bound door at the corridor’s end. "Inside. We’ll discuss feasibility." His tone implied the discussion was already over. "But understand, " He caught Tomas’s wrist as they moved, his grip startlingly warm. ", this isn’t a confessional. We don’t deal in penance here."

The door creaked open on its own, revealing a workbench strewn with vials and a single, long-necked flask glowing faintly gold. Tomas’s pulse hammered at his throat. He wondered if rabbits could blush.

Alric gestured to a stool. "Sit." He didn’t wait, already reaching for a ledger bound in cracked leather. His fingers left faint blue smudges on the pages as he flipped through them. "we have partials on hand, but none of them are rabbit.. it'll take about an hour to brew up. the change itself another hour."

Tomas swallowed, fingers twitching against his thighs. "And... the other thing?"

"Dr. Korr handles surgical alterations," Alric said without looking up. He scribbled something in the ledger, the quill scratching against parchment. "She'll assess you after the transformation. Won't proceed unless you're fully coherent post-change." His gaze flicked up, assessing. "Rabbits are skittish by nature. You'll be more so."

Somewhere beyond the door, footsteps approached, sharp, precise. A woman’s voice cut through the herbal haze before she appeared in the doorway: dark-haired, gloved hands clasping a clipboard. "Alric. You’re due in the, " Her sharp eyes landed on Tomas, narrowed slightly. "Ah. New procedure?"

"Partial rabbit," Alric confirmed. He tapped the ledger. "With surgical follow-up."

Dr. Korr’s nostrils flared. She stepped fully into the room, the scent of antiseptic clinging to her sleeves. "Consent verified?"

Tomas nodded too quickly. His pulse throbbed in his throat.

She studied him for a long moment before scribbling something on her clipboard. "Post-transformation evaluation, then. No earlier than two hours after completion." Her tone brooked no argument. "And you’ll spend the night in recovery. I don’t discharge patients who can’t walk straight."

Alric grunted approval, already measuring powders into a crucible. The mixture hissed faintly, emitting a scent like crushed clover.

Dr. Korr turned to leave, then paused at the threshold. "One more thing." Her voice softened, just slightly. "You’ll want to undress before drinking that. Fur doesn’t grow well through fabric."

The door clicked shut behind her. Tomas’s face burned.

Alric didn’t glance up from his work. "She’s right. The boots especially." A pause. "You can keep the smallclothes until the transformation’s done. Korr will handle the rest."

The crucible bubbled, gold swirling into green. Tomas stared at his hands, still human, for now, and wondered if rabbits ever felt this heavy.

"You can pace if you need to," Alric said without looking up. "Won’t bother the process."

Tomas stood abruptly, legs shaky. The stone floor was cool under his socked feet as he circled the workbench. His shadow stretched long in the flickering lamplight. The scent of clover thickened, clinging to the back of his tongue.

"So," Alric said after three precise stirs counterclockwise. His stained fingers hovered over the crucible. "Why are you doing this?"

Tomas's pacing stuttered. His pulse rabbited in his throat, fitting, now. "I told you. The seminary, "

"No." The alchemist set down his stirring rod with deliberate care. "You told me what you’re running from. Not why this," He gestured at the glowing brew, then at Tomas’s hunched shoulders, "is your solution."

Somewhere beyond the stone walls, a horse stamped twice. Tomas’s fingers twitched toward his missing pack. "They say rabbits don’t... couple. Not like people." The words came out too soft, sticking to his teeth. "That they’re clean."

Alric’s exhale smelled of iron and rue. "Rabbits breed faster than any mammal on earth." He lifted the crucible, swirling the contents gently. Gold flecks spiraled downward like falling stars. "What you’re paying for isn’t purity. It’s distance. From your own skin."

Tomas’s next step faltered. His bare toes curled against a groove in the stone. "Does it matter?"

Alric’s stained fingers tightened around the crucible. "Since this is just partial, and you're keeping nearly full functionality as a person," he said, slower now, like measuring drops into a beaker, "the transformation won’t be as thorough. Nowhere near as much as if you’d requested full rabbit." A pause. The mixture bubbled, releasing a scent like wet grass after rain. "If you had, we would’ve told you to think about it. Come back later. If not outright denied it." His gaze flicked up, sharp as a scalpel. "The other part, though, why remove your parts?"

Tomas’s pulse hammered against his ribs. His shadow rippled on the wall, distorted by the flickering lamplight. "Because," he whispered, "if I don’t, I’ll never stop wanting." The confession tasted like copper on his tongue. "And wanting is sin."

The alchemist’s exhale was slow, measured. He set the crucible down with a soft clink. "Sin’s a word," he said at last. "Like ‘purity.’ Neither has much to do with bodies." He reached for a clean vial, the glass glinting in the low light. "But if you’re certain, we’ll proceed. Just know, " His fingers stilled. ", once done, it can’t be undone. Not fully."

Tomas nodded, throat tight. The scent of crushed mint filled the room, sharp and clean. Somewhere beyond the stone walls, a horse whinnied, the sound muffled but insistent.

Alric poured the glowing liquid into the vial with practiced precision. Gold swirled against green, coalescing into something that looked almost alive.

"Drink this in the infirmary," he said, pressing the vial into Tomas's trembling palm. His fingers lingered for half a second too long, warm against Tomas's clammy skin. "Dr. Korr will monitor the transformation. You'll want to be lying down when it hits." His lips quirked at Tomas's startled blink. "Trust me. First-timers always forget about balance shifts."

The corridor smelled sharper on the way back, more alcohol, less herbs. Tomas's bare feet slapped against stone still warm from the day's heat. Somewhere ahead, a door creaked open before they reached it, revealing Dr. Korr leaning against the frame with crossed arms. Her gaze flicked to the vial, then to Tomas's hunched shoulders. "Infirmary's third door down," she said, already turning. Her braid swung like a pendulum behind her. "Strip to your smallclothes. Gown's on the cot."

The infirmary smelled overwhelmingly of lavender and something metallic. Tomas's fingers fumbled with buttons while Dr. Korr scribbled notes at a desk in the corner, her pen scratching loud in the quiet. The cot's sheets were crisp, starched stiff enough to hold their shape when he climbed in. He clutched the vial like a holy relic, its warmth seeping into his palm.

"Ready?" Dr. Korr didn't wait for an answer before plucking the vial from his grip. She held it to the light, watching the liquid swirl. "This will burn going down. Don't panic." Her fingers were unexpectedly gentle when they tilted his head back. "Bottoms up."

The potion tasted like crushed mint and lightning. Tomas gasped as it hit his stomach, heat blooming outward in waves. His vision swam. Dr. Korr's hand pressed firm against his sternum when he convulsed, her voice steady through the roaring in his ears: "Normal. Breathe through it."

Then the world twisted sideways. His bones creaked, joints popping like greenwood in a fire. Fur sprouted along his arms in patches, soft and downy at first, then thicker, white with brown streaks. His spine arched as his tailbone lengthened, vertebrae shifting beneath skin that prickled with strange sensitivity. He tried to clutch the sheets, but his fingers were shrinking, nails darkening into delicate claws. A whimper escaped him, too high, too thready, as his jaw realigned with audible clicks.

Dr. Korr's clipboard tapped against her thigh. "Ears next," she murmured, clinical but not unkind. Tomas barely had time to register the pressure at his temples before they erupted upward, furred and twitching, funneling every rustle of fabric into deafening clarity. His nose wrinkled uncontrollably, whiskers bristling as new scents flooded in: lavender undercut with iron, the starch of the sheets, the faint musk of his own rising panic.

When he tried to speak, his teeth felt wrong. "I, " The word came out lisping around elongated incisors. His tongue dabbed at them instinctively, heart rabbiting behind his ribs. He glanced down. His body was still roughly human-shaped, but covered in plush fur, his feet elongated into digitigrade paws. He wiggled his toes, no, his claws, and the sheets rasped against sensitive pads.

"Balance," Dr. Korr warned, too late. Tomas tumbled sideways when he tried to sit up, his new center of gravity betraying him. The world lurched as she caught him by the scruff, his entire body going limp in her grip. A rabbit instinct, he realized with distant horror. His pulse thundered in his ears, long ears now, twitching at every sound.

Dr. Korr's gloved fingers parted the fur along his abdomen, checking vitals. "Partial transformations always feel strangest," she said, matter-of-fact. "Too much left to compare against." She tilted his chin up, examining his dilated pupils. "Coherent?"

Tomas nodded, then shuddered as the motion sent his ears flopping. His whole body felt alien, too light and too heavy all at once. He could feel his own heartbeat in his whiskers.

The doctor scribbled a note. "Good. Now, " The door creaked open. Alric stood silhouetted against the hallway light, his stained hands gripping a tray of instruments. Tomas's nose twitched at the scent of alcohol and steel.

Dr. Korr nodded toward the cot. "Rest an hour," she said, already turning to confer with Alric. Her voice dropped, words indistinct except for "metabolic strain" and "partial adaptation." Then, louder: "Close your eyes. Concentrate on what you're feeling." She tapped her clipboard against her thigh. "You'll learn to recognize the instincts, and overcome them."

Tomas curled his too-light body into the sheets, ears flattening against his skull. The scent of his own fur, warm, musky, unnervingly animal, filled his nostrils. Beneath it, the sharp tang of fear. His claws flexed against the mattress, the unfamiliar tendons moving with eerie precision.

Dr. Korr paused at the door. "I'll be back in a couple hours," she said, fingers tightening around the knob. "To discuss part two. If you still want it." The click of the latch echoed like a verdict.

Alone, Tomas pressed a paw to his chest. His heartbeat fluttered against his ribs, rapid and shallow. Rabbit-pulse. He squeezed his eyes shut. The infirmary air carried whispers of lavender and iodine, but beneath it, hay. Distant, but unmistakable. His nostrils flared without his consent, whiskers quivering. Somewhere beyond the stone walls, a horse snorted. His ears swiveled toward the sound before he could stop them.

Footsteps approached. The scent hit first, steel and rosemary, Lyra’s oiled claws clicking against the floorboards. She paused at the cot’s edge, her golden gaze sweeping from his twitching ears to the way his toes curled reflexively against the sheets. “Partial rabbit.” Her tone gave nothing away. “Alric says you’re coherent.”

Tomas nodded, ears bobbing. The motion felt absurd.

Lyra’s nostrils flared. She set a tray on the bedside table, water, a crust of bread, a sprig of something green. “Eat. Transformation burns calories.” Her claw tapped the bread. “You’ll taste it differently now. Try.”

The bread smelled like dust. Tomas lifted it with clumsy paws, nibbled. His teeth, his new teeth, sliced effortlessly. Flavor exploded, rich and earthy. His ears shot upright.

Lyra’s lips twitched. “Told you.” She folded her arms, studying him. “You’re scheduled with Korr after dusk. She’ll assess your lucidity.” A pause. The distant whinny came again, closer. Tomas’s shoulders hunched.

“Second thoughts?” Lyra’s voice was quieter now.

Tomas’s whiskers trembled. He stared at the half-eaten bread, its rough texture suddenly overwhelming on his tongue. “No.” The lie tasted bitter.

Lyra exhaled through her nose, a soft hiss. She reached into her belt pouch and produced a smooth river stone, warm from her body heat. “Hold this.” She pressed it into his paw. “Grounds you.”

The stone’s weight was solid, real. Tomas curled his fingers around it, claws clicking against the surface. His pulse slowed, just slightly.

“Listen.” Lyra leaned in, her scent sharp with ink and ozone. “That letter in your pocket, seminary seal. I smelled the wax when you walked in.” Her golden eyes held his. “They teach you guilt before they teach you grace.”

Tomas’s ears flattened. His tail, a puff of fur he hadn’t noticed until now, twitched against the sheets.

Lyra straightened. “Rest. Korr’s precise, not cruel.” Her claws tapped the tray. “Eat the mint. Settles the stomach.”

She left without another word. The door clicked shut. Tomas lifted the mint sprig to his nose, the scent now impossibly vivid, like crushed leaves after rain. He nibbled, shuddering as the flavor burst across his tongue, cool, electric, overwhelming. His ears twitched at the crunch.

Time blurred. The infirmary’s single window cast amber rectangles across the floor as the sun dipped lower. Tomas dozed between waves of disorientation, his body adjusting in fits and starts. Once, he startled awake to find himself gnawing absently at the sheet’s edge, teeth sawing through starched linen before he caught himself. He recoiled, then flushed beneath his fur at the realization that no one had seen.

The scrape of boots outside jolted him fully awake. Dr. Korr entered, her braid unraveling slightly at the temples, sleeves rolled to reveal forearms streaked with something dark. She smelled of antiseptic and exhaustion.

"You’re lucid." Not a question. She pulled the stool close, her knees cracking as she sat. The clipboard landed on her lap with a practiced flick. "Partial transformation stabilized at ninety-two percent. Higher than expected." Her pen hovered. "Still want the surgery?"

Tomas’s claws dug into the river stone. He nodded.

Dr. Korr exhaled sharply through her nose. "Total penectomy and castration," she recited, eyes on her notes. "Urethral reroute through perineum. Standard procedure prevents urinary tract complications post-op." The pen scratched. "Do you still want sensation down there?"

The question punched through him. His ears flattened. "I, what?"

"Erotic sensation." She tapped the clipboard with her pen. "We can preserve nerve clusters. Or sever them entirely." Her gaze lifted, clinical. "Your choice."

Tomas’s mouth went dry. He stared at his lap, where fur now covered what had been, what was still, human skin beneath. The seminary’s hissed Latin echoed in his skull. "Sever them," he whispered.

Dr. Korr’s pen stilled. "Irreversible."

"I know." His voice cracked.

She scribbled another note. "Long-term effects," she continued, tone flattening into routine, "include loss of sexual function, obviously. Reduced testosterone production may soften your features over time. Some clients report mood stabilization. Others..." Her lips pursed. "Regret."

The infirmary air smelled suddenly of damp fur and iodine. Tomas’s whiskers trembled. "How long to heal?"

Dr. Korr snapped her fingers, summoning a tray of gleaming instruments from the side table without looking. "Three days," she said, adjusting the angle of a scalpel. "We use healing draughts to speed the process." Her gloves creaked as she pulled them tight. "But you'll still need to be off your feet."

Tomas curled his toes against the sheet, the claws catching threads. Rabbit instinct screamed at him to bolt when Alric entered bearing a steaming vial, its contents swirling like molten copper. The scent hit first, honey and iron, thick enough to coat his tongue.

"Drink." Alric’s stained fingers pressed the vial into his paw. "This’ll dull the worst of it." His thumb lingered against Tomas’s furred knuckles. "You’ll feel warmth. That’s normal."

The liquid burned going down, settling heavy in his gut. Heat radiated outward, softening the edges of the room. Dr. Korr’s voice came muffled through the haze: "On your back. Legs bent."

Her hands were impersonal as she arranged his limbs, the gloves cool against his inner thighs. Tomas stared at the ceiling beams, counting knots as the draught’s warmth turned syrupy. Distantly, metal clinked. Alric murmured something about pressure points, his palm a steady weight on Tomas’s shoulder.

"Breathe in," Dr. Korr ordered. The scalpel flashed. Tomas gasped, not from pain, but from the sheer wrongness of sensation without hurt, his nerves lighting up in confused protest. His claws flexed, scoring the sheet. Somewhere beyond the ringing in his ears, Lyra’s oiled footsteps paused at the door.

Alric’s grip tightened. "Look at me." His breath smelled of rosemary and exhaustion. "Not down. At me."

Tomas focused on the flecks of gold in Alric’s irises as the sounds continued, soft snicks of scissors, the wet schlick of something being set aside in a basin. His pulse rabbited behind his ribs, too fast, too shallow. Dr. Korr’s forehead gleamed with sweat when she leaned into his sightline, blocking the lamp’s glare.

"Halfway done." She dabbed at his abdomen with a soaked cloth. The liquid smelled sharply of witch hazel. "Draught’s holding. Your vitals are stable." Her thumb pressed below his navel, making him jerk. "Try not to tense."

Outside, hooves clattered on cobblestones. Tomas’s ears swiveled toward the sound instinctively, twitching at each strike. Lyra’s shadow moved across the wall, her claws tapping a restless rhythm against the doorframe.

Dr. Korr snapped her fingers again. The tray floated closer, revealing neat coils of black thread. "Closing up," she announced. Her needle gleamed. "Don’t watch."

Tomas obeyed, fixing his gaze on the ceiling’s rough beams. His body felt distant now, limbs heavy with the draught’s influence. The tug of sutures registered as faint pressure, rhythmic and oddly soothing. Alric’s thumb traced slow circles against his shoulder, grounding. Somewhere beyond the infirmary walls, Sable’s voice murmured to a horse, the words indistinct but the tone low and reassuring. Tomas’s ears flicked toward it despite himself.

The last stitch pulled taut. Dr. Korr’s gloves peeled away with a sticky sound. "Done." She pressed a linen pad against his groin, the fabric already darkening with some alchemical salve that smelled of comfrey and frost. "No weight on it for twelve hours. Then light movement only." She snapped the bloody gloves into a disposal chute. "Pain will spike when the draught wears off. Take this." A smaller vial clinked onto the bedside table, its contents swirling like liquid dusk.

Alric released Tomas’s shoulder with a final squeeze. "You’ll sleep now," he said, not unkindly. His stained fingers brushed Tomas’s forehead, lingering just long enough to check for fever.

The infirmary door creaked wider. Lyra entered bearing a folded blanket, its weave tight enough to trap warmth without weight. She draped it over Tomas’s legs with uncharacteristic gentleness, avoiding the bandaged area. "Eat when you wake," she instructed, nodding toward a fresh tray, this one holding a bowl of broth, its steam carrying the scent of thyme and bone marrow.

Tomas’s whiskers twitched. His mouth watered despite himself.

Dr. Korr stripped off her apron, revealing sweat-darkened sleeves beneath. "Someone will check on you in four hours," she said, scrubbing a hand over her face. "Yell if anything feels wrong."

Then they were gone, their footsteps receding down the hall, leaving Tomas alone with the muffled sounds of the stable yard and the slow, creeping awareness of his altered body. The river stone lay cool in his palm. He curled his claws around it, clinging to its solidity as the draught’s warmth pulled him under.

Somewhere beyond the window, a horse snorted. His ears twitched toward the sound even as his eyelids fluttered shut. The seminary letter rustled in his discarded robe pocket, forgotten for now.

Dawn light painted stripes across the infirmary floor when Tomas woke, his body stiff and strangely light. The ache between his legs pulsed in time with his heartbeat, sharp but dulled by whatever remained of the draught. He flexed his claws against the sheet, the sensation still foreign. The river stone had slipped from his grasp overnight, leaving faint indents in the mattress where his fingers had clenched.

The door creaked. Lyra entered balancing a tray, her nostrils flaring as she took in Tomas’s hunched posture. "Eat," she commanded, setting down a bowl of porridge studded with diced apple and, his nose wrinkled, carrots. The scent was overwhelming now, earthy-sweet. His stomach growled despite himself.

Lyra’s claws tapped the tray. "Korr will be here soon." She lifted the blanket with clinical efficiency, barely glancing at the bandages. "We’ll need to see you void to make sure everything’s working."

Tomas’s ears flattened. He stared at the porridge, its steam carrying the ghost of cinnamon.

She exhaled through her nose. "It's medical, not moral." The porridge spoon clinked against the bowl as she Lyra nudged it closer. Tomas's whiskers quivered at the rising steam, his elongated ears twitching toward the stable yard where hooves crunched fresh straw. The scent of turned earth and wet fur seeped through the window cracks, thick with morning dampness.

Dr. Korr arrived buttoned into a fresh apron, her braid coiled tightly at her nape. She examined the bandages without touching, lips pressed thin. "Sit up slowly," she instructed. When Tomas winced at the shift in pressure, she caught his elbow, brief, impersonal, then snapped for the bedpan. Her fingers hovered near his abdomen, applying precise pressure that made his new nerves sing with confused signals. "Breathe out."

The first attempt failed, his rabbit instincts recoiling from the vulnerability. Korr's sigh carried the sharpness of starched linen. "You're not the first ascetic we've patched up," she muttered, adjusting the angle. Tomas's claws dug into the mattress as his body reluctantly complied, the process clinical, humiliating, and strangely painless. Korr's nod was curt. "Functional."

Lyra vanished with the pan, returning with a damp cloth that smelled faintly of lemon verbena. Tomas wiped his paws, *hands*, he corrected himself, while Korr measured a green-tinged tonic into a tin cup. "For swelling," she said, watching his nostrils flare at the bitter scent. "Drink it fast."

Beyond the window, Sable's low voice murmured to one of the horses, the cadence steady as a heartbeat. Tomas's ears swiveled toward the sound before he could stop them. Lyra's claw tapped the windowsill. "You'll hear everything now," she said, not unkindly. "Takes getting used to."

Korr checked the sutures one last time, her fingers deft. "Walk this afternoon," she ordered, "but no running." Her pen scratched across the clipboard. "And eat those carrots. You'll crave them."

The door shut behind her with a click. Lyra lingered, her golden eyes tracking the way Tomas's ears twitched at every distant clatter from the kitchens. She said nothing. The seminary letter peeked from his folded robe on the chair, its broken seal glinting in the dawn light like a half-healed wound.

The days blurred. Tomas learned to walk again in cautious shuffles, each step measured, his rabbit-light body adjusting to the absence between his legs. The first attempts left him panting against the infirmary wall, his claws scrabbling for purchase on the polished floorboards. Dr. Korr timed his laps with a pocket watch, her expression unreadable as his gait smoothed from jerky hops to something approximating grace. By the third day, he could circle the room without pausing, though his ears still flicked nervously at every unexpected sound.

Alric visited with tonics that tasted of dandelion roots and regret, watching Tomas sip with eyes that missed nothing. "Your body remembers," he said when Tomas startled at a sudden birdcall, his whole frame tensing. "Instincts don't care about your theology." The words weren't cruel, just factual, like the way Tomas's nose now twitched at the scent of fresh greens before he could stop it. He hated it. Hated the way his teeth ached for crunchy stems, hated how his ears pricked toward laughter from the courtyard.

Lyra brought him tasks, sorting herbs, mending tack, anything to keep his paws busy. The work was menial, but Tomas found solace in the rhythm of braiding leather, his claws surprisingly deft. The stable smells no longer made him flinch; instead, he caught himself inhaling deeply, sorting hay from hoof oil from the musk of horses with alarming precision. Sable paused once, her dark eyes noting the way Tomas's nostrils flared as she passed with an armload of oats. She said nothing, just nodded, as if welcoming him into some unspoken fellowship of those who understood scent as language.

At night, lying stiffly on his cot, Tomas listened to the manor breathe. The seminary letter remained folded in his robe pocket, the wax seal cracked like an overripe pod. Sometimes, when the moon silvered the infirmary floor, he'd catch himself thumping a hindfoot against the mattress, a rabbit's warning drum, only to freeze, ashamed. Then the sound would come again, softer this time, tentative. His own pulse echoing in the dark.

On the seventh morning, Dr. Korr examined the healed incision with clinical detachment. "Functional," she pronounced, snapping her gloves off. "Avoid strenuous activity for another fortnight." Her pen scratched across the discharge parchment. Tomas's new ears caught the subtext in her pause. "The seminary's three weeks' ride northeast."

Lyra arrived with his belongings, human clothes altered for his changed form, the seams let out around his elongated thighs, the sleeves tapered to accommodate narrower wrists. The robe smelled of lye and faint regret. "Your boots won't fit," she said, nodding toward his paws. Tomas flexed his toes, the claws clicking against the floorboards. He'd have to go barefoot.

Alric met him at the courtyard gate with a pouch of dried mint and a vial of something that smelled like rain on hot stone. "For the instincts," he said, pressing it into Tomas's palm. His stained fingers lingered. "It'll dull the edges." Beyond them, Sable led a dappled mare through morning drills, her voice low and steady. Tomas's ears swiveled toward the sound before he could stop them.

The road unfurled like a pale scar through the autumn woods. Tomas walked light now, his body lean under the robes, his steps soundless on the damp earth. At dusk, when the first rabbit bolted across his path, his entire frame locked, not in fear, but in a recognition that punched through him like a poorly brewed potion. The creature froze too, nose quivering, black eyes wide. For a heartbeat, they mirrored each other. Then it fled, leaving Tomas alone with the seminary's distant spire piercing the twilight, and the terrible understanding that his body had begun telling truths his soul still feared to name.
