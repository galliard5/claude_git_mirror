The boy's claws scraped against the metal floor as they dragged him forward by the chain locked around his throat. He was the smallest of the group—barely eighteen, if his patchy fur and gangly limbs were any indication. Around him, other anthros coughed or whimpered, their bodies crammed into the transport cage like slaughterhouse stock. None of them knew each other before today. Now, they shared the same stench of panic-sweat and rusted iron.

The biomancer’s workshop smelled like rotting fruit and antiseptic. Fluids dripped from suspended glass vials, their contents glowing faintly in the dim light. Tools lay scattered across stained worktables—some recognizable as surgical instruments, others twisted into shapes that made the boy’s stomach tighten. He tried to count the others, but his vision kept blurring. Fifteen? Twenty? All anthros, all young. The noble’s men hadn’t been picky beyond that.

A hand clamped around the boy’s muzzle, forcing his head up. The biomancer’s fingers were cold, the skin oddly smooth, like something grown in a vat. "You’re the wolf," the biomancer murmured, more to themself than to him. Their eyes—too large, too dark—flicked over his frame. "Lean. Good. Less waste that way."

The noble’s voice cut in from somewhere behind them, bored and syrupy with wine. "I want them unrecognizable. No paws, no fur, no *faces*. Just meat and milk and breeding stock." A pause. "And make sure the person is still in there at the end. Aware of everything."

The biomancer exhaled through their nose. The boy felt the chain around his throat loosen, then fall away. He didn’t move. He’d seen what happened to the ones who ran.

The first cuts were careful. The biomancer’s fingers pressed into his ribs, tracing the bones beneath his fur as if reading a map. Then came the needles—long, curved things that slid under his skin without breaking it, pumping something thick and warm into his veins. His muscles spasmed. His vision went white.

Somewhere to his left, a bull anthro screamed as his body began to twist, his shoulders splitting open in wet cracks. The biomancer didn’t look up. "Breeders will be made female," they said, almost absentmindedly. "Dairy and meat stock get neutered. No point in aggression where there’s no need for it."

The boy tried to speak. His tongue was heavy, his jaw locked. He felt his bones soften, his spine curling inward like a fetus. His last coherent thought was of his mother, years dead, telling him to run fast and never look back.

Then the pain swallowed him whole.

Something in the needles' payload bypassed his nerves entirely, attacking the blueprint of his body instead. His bones dissolved first—not breaking, but *unraveling*, the calcium struts melting like wax under a flame. His muscles spasmed violently as they lost their anchor points, fibers snapping and reknitting in wrong configurations. His vocal cords tore themselves apart mid-scream, leaving only wet, bubbling clicks in his throat.

The biomancer's hands never stopped moving. They pressed thumbs into the collapsing hollow where his hips had been, guiding the flow of dissolving tissue like a potter shaping clay. "Spine needs reinforcement before elongation," they muttered, plunging a glass pipette into the mess of his lower back. The boy felt—not pain, but a terrible *pressure* as something crystalline seeded itself along his neural cord, branching out in fractal patterns.

His vision returned in fractured bursts. The workshop's ceiling swam above him, its rusted metal beams now impossibly distant. His body was spreading, flattening, his limbs absorbed into a shuddering mass of raw biomass. Only his head remained partly intact, skull thinning like parchment as the biomancer's fingers sculpted what would soon become his new—face? Face wasn't the word. There would be no jaws here, no eyes, just smooth planes of living tissue with the barest indentation where sensory nodes clustered.

Across the room, the bull anthro had stopped screaming. His torso had split down the middle like overripe fruit, ribcage splayed open to form a bony cradle around the swollen udders now budding from his chest. The biomancer flicked a glance his way. "Viable. Proceed with neural mapping."

Then they turned back to the boy—no, the *thing* he was becoming—and pressed both palms against its quivering surface. "Now," they whispered, "we graft the awareness."

The boy tried to beg. His tongue was gone. His thoughts were next.

A final needle slid home behind his ear. The world dissolved into perfect, endless white—and when it cleared, he saw himself from outside. Saw the glistening, barrel-shaped body now studded with lactating ports. Saw the nest of tendrils where legs should be, each tip pulsing with the rhythm of a second, embedded heartbeat.

Worst of all, he *understood* it. Understood the purpose thrumming through every reshaped cell.

Somewhere, the noble laughed.

The sound reached the boy—no, the thing—through membranes that had once been ears, distorted into a wet, clicking vibration. The noble’s voice oozed through the workshop’s stale air, thick with satisfaction. "Perfect. Like a living cask." 

The biomancer’s hands were still inside him. Not hands—tools. Fingers elongated into probes, splitting at the tips into fine, branching filaments that threaded through his liquefying musculature. Each movement sent ripples of understanding through him, as if his nerves had been rewired to comprehend anatomy textbooks. He felt his pelvis dissolve, the hollow where his hips had been expanding into a smooth, rounded chamber. Felt his ribs soften and splay outward, forming a latticed cradle around the swelling sacs of mammary tissue. 

A new pulse thrummed through him—not a heartbeat, but a slow, rhythmic contraction deep in his core. He *knew* its purpose before the biomancer spoke. "Milk production initiates in three hours. Neural mapping is stable." 

Stable. The word echoed through him. He had no mouth to scream. No lungs to fill. But his mind—trapped, pristine—clawed at the walls of his new flesh. The noble wanted him aware. Wanted him to *know*. 

Across the room, the bull anthro’s body had finished its metamorphosis. What remained was a swollen, barrel-shaped mass of veined flesh, its surface studded with teats that glistened under the workshop’s flickering lights. Its—*his*—head was gone, absorbed into the biomass save for a single, lidless eye embedded near the base. It stared blankly at the ceiling, pupil dilated in perpetual, sightless panic. 

The biomancer followed his gaze. "Differentiation is necessary. Milk stock requires docility. Breeding stock needs heat cycles." Their fingers withdrew from his body with a wet slurp. "But you—you’re the showpiece." 

The noble’s shadow fell over him. A hand—soft, manicured—traced the curve of his new form. "Exquisite. Like something the empire would have kept in its private gardens." 

The boy—the thing—the *showpiece*—felt the last of his humanoid structure give way. His skull flattened, his face spreading into a smooth, featureless plane. His thoughts didn’t fracture. Didn’t fade. They simply… rearranged. 

When the noble leaned closer, whispering promises of audiences and admirers, he understood every word. 

And worst of all, he agreed.
